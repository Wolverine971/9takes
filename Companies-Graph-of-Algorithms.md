- **Author**: Daniel Miessler

## Cleaned Article

### Core Thesis

Miessler's argument is that a company can be understood as a connected set of workflows, and each workflow can be broken into smaller steps. Those steps are effectively algorithms. Once AI can map and reason across that graph, every business function becomes a candidate for optimization, consolidation, or automation.

### 1. A Company Is a Stack of Workflows

The article opens with a simple example business that receives old photos, restores them, stylizes them, captions them, and returns digital or printed outputs. The point is not the photo business itself. The point is that what looks like a company from the outside can be decomposed into a sequence of actions.

Each of those actions can then be broken down again:

- intake
- scanning
- quality review
- repair
- stylization
- captioning
- delivery

And those sub-steps can be broken down further. Miessler's framing is that it is algorithms all the way down.

### 2. The Business Is Bigger Than the Product Pipeline

The product workflow is only one part of the graph. The article expands the model to include:

- company setup
- hiring
- taxes
- infrastructure
- marketing
- support
- and everything else needed to operate

Once all of those are represented as connected workflows, the company becomes a full graph rather than a single assembly line.

### 3. Transparency Creates Leverage

Miessler argues that simply articulating these workflows is already valuable, even before automation. Once the business is made legible, leaders can see:

- where work is duplicated
- where steps are unnecessary
- where coordination is slow
- where waste exists
- where one workflow should be merged into another

His core claim is that transparency is the fuel that makes AI optimization possible.

### 4. Consultants Will Productize This

One of the sharper parts of the article is the prediction that large consultancies will sell AI-driven workflow decomposition to executive teams.

The pitch, in effect, is:

- we will interview the company exhaustively
- we will map all workflows
- we will identify human and automated tasks
- we will locate redundancy and waste
- we will recommend elimination, consolidation, or redesign

The expected outcome is a leaner company with fewer people required to operate it.

### 5. Optimization Will Become Continuous

The article argues that this is not a one-time exercise. Once a company has been rendered as a machine-readable graph of work, AI can continuously inspect that graph and keep asking:

- why are humans still doing this part?
- why does this take a month instead of a day?
- why are these handoffs manual?
- why is idea generation batched rather than ongoing?
- why are there so many approval layers?

Miessler uses marketing, customer support, and hiring as examples, but the broader point is that every department is exposed to the same form of analysis.

### 6. AI Will Not Care That Your Company Feels Special

The article explicitly pushes back on the common objection that a given company is too nuanced or too complex to model this way.

Miessler's response is simple: complexity does not invalidate the model. It just means the graph is larger. The fact that the graph is too large for a human to mentally hold does not mean it is too large for AI to inspect and optimize.

### 7. The Human Response Should Be Preparation

The closing message is not pure techno-optimism. Miessler says this shift will produce real gains in productivity and make new businesses easier to start, but it will also eliminate many easy-to-automate jobs.

His advice is that both business owners and workers should prepare now by understanding their company as a graph of workflows before AI consultants or internal AI systems do it for them.

## Analysis

### Main Topics

1. **Businesses can be decomposed into workflows.** The article treats the company as a network of connected process nodes rather than as departments, titles, or culture.
2. **Transparency is a precondition for automation.** AI cannot optimize what has not been made legible.
3. **AI will move from task support to structural optimization.** The target is not just worker productivity but the redesign of entire workflows.
4. **Consulting and executive decision-making will accelerate adoption.** Large firms will operationalize this faster than many internal teams.
5. **Optimization pressure will be continuous.** Once the graph exists, it can be re-evaluated constantly rather than occasionally.

### What the Piece Gets Right

- It correctly identifies that many businesses are far less coherent than leaders think, and that making workflows explicit is itself a major strategic advantage.
- It captures a real shift from isolated AI use cases toward organization-wide process inspection.
- It is strong on the idea that complexity does not protect a business from automation pressure; complexity often just hides inefficiency.
- It gives operators a useful question: if an outsider mapped our work honestly, what would look wasteful or fragile?

### Where the Piece Is Weak or Incomplete

- It treats explainability and decomposition as if they capture most of the company's value, when some of the hardest value lies in taste, trust, power, timing, and narrative.
- It assumes the objective function is obvious. Optimizing for speed or cost can destroy quality, brand, or community trust.
- It reduces humans mainly to labor cost and process friction, which is too narrow for creative, relational, or community-centered businesses.
- It says little about governance, misoptimization, or second-order effects once AI starts changing workflows continuously.
- It underplays that many organizations are not just graphs of tasks; they are also containers for conflict, ambiguity, politics, and meaning.

### Compared to the Block Essay

This piece is related to the Block article, but it sits one level lower.

- **Miessler's lens**: the company is a graph of workflows that AI can analyze and optimize.
- **Block's lens**: the company should become an intelligence system with world models, capabilities, and an orchestration layer.

Miessler is stronger on decomposition and optimization pressure. Block is stronger on what a future operating model might look like after decomposition.

## Relevance to You and 9takes

### Where It Applies

- **It is very useful as an internal audit lens.** 9takes can map content creation, question sourcing, moderation, answer ingestion, personality tagging, summaries, SEO production, internal research, and distribution as an operational graph.
- **It helps expose hidden bottlenecks.** Anywhere work waits on context transfer, manual copy-paste, inconsistent tagging, or loosely defined editorial decisions should become visible.
- **It supports capability thinking.** Once the graph is visible, you can separate reusable capabilities from one-off labor.
- **It is highly relevant for small-team leverage.** A small company benefits more from removing coordination waste than from abstract org-theory debates.

### Where It Does Not Transfer Cleanly

- **9takes is not just a process machine.** It is also a meaning-making and community-shaping product. That means quality, tone, trust, and psychological safety matter as much as throughput.
- **Some of your best value may look inefficient.** Deep analysis, careful framing, nuanced moderation, and original editorial angles can appear slow even when they are the right choice.
- **The model can produce false confidence.** Just because a workflow can be diagrammed does not mean it should be optimized purely for speed or headcount reduction.

### The Best Translation for 9takes

The most useful way to apply this is:

1. Map the graph of work across 9takes.
2. Mark which nodes are repetitive, bottlenecked, expensive, or low-leverage.
3. Separate what should be automated from what should remain human and judgment-heavy.
4. Build AI support around routing, summarization, extraction, and pattern detection before trying to automate meaning itself.

- question ideation

- personality-type signal extraction

- SEO enrichment
- distribution and recirculation

### Practical 9takes Implications

1. **Create an internal workflow graph.** Not a vague whiteboard. A real map of how ideas become questions, questions become answers, answers become insights, and insights become content or product improvements.
2. **Measure handoff cost.** Every time work moves between tools, people, or contexts, treat that as a candidate for AI assistance.
3. **Protect the high-judgment zones.** Editorial angle selection, psychologically sensitive interpretation, moderation escalation, and trust-sensitive UX should not be flattened into simple optimization problems.
4. **Automate the scaffolding first.** Metadata cleanup, clustering, summaries, routing, and draft generation are safer and more valuable first targets than trying to automate community meaning.
5. **Use the graph to sharpen strategy.** If a workflow exists but does not contribute to user understanding, differentiation, or compounding data advantage, question whether it should exist at all.

### Bottom Line

This article is relevant to 9takes, but mainly as a **workflow decomposition and internal leverage framework**.

The strongest takeaway is:

> Map 9takes as a graph of workflows, then decide which nodes deserve automation, instrumentation, or removal.

The weakest takeaway is:

> Treat the entire business as if its value is fully captured by process optimization.

For 9takes, the opportunity is not just to cut steps. It is to use AI to make the right steps more coherent while preserving the human parts that create trust, insight, and differentiation.
