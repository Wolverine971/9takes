## Cleaned Article

### Core Thesis

At Sequoia, speed is treated as the best predictor of startup success. Most companies are using AI as a productivity enhancer. Far fewer are using AI to rethink how work is coordinated in the first place. Block presents itself as an example of that second path: using AI to redesign the organization so speed becomes a compounding advantage.

### 1. Hierarchy as an Information-Routing System

Two thousand years before the first corporate org chart, the Roman Army solved a problem that every large organization still faces: how do you coordinate thousands of people across vast distances with limited communication?

Their answer was a nested hierarchy with a consistent span of control at every level. The smallest unit was the contubernium, eight soldiers who shared a tent, equipment, and a mule, led by a decanus. Ten contubernia formed a century of eighty men under a centurion. Six centuries made a cohort. Ten cohorts made a legion of roughly 5,000. At each layer, a named commander held defined authority, aggregated information from below, and relayed decisions from above.

The structure (8 -> 80 -> 480 -> 5,000) was an information-routing protocol built around a simple human limitation: a leader can effectively manage somewhere between three and eight people. The Romans discovered this through centuries of warfare. Even today, the US Army's hierarchical chain follows a similar pattern. We now call it "span of control," and it remains the governing constraint of every large organization on earth.

### 2. How Management Evolved

The next big change came from Prussia. After Napoleon destroyed the Prussian forces at the Battle of Jena in 1806, reformers led by Scharnhorst and Gneisenau rebuilt the military around an uncomfortable truth: you cannot depend on genius at the top. You need a system.

They created the General Staff, a trained class of officers whose job was not to fight but to plan operations, process information, and coordinate across units. This was effectively middle management before the term existed: professionals whose purpose was to route information, pre-compute decisions, and maintain alignment across a complex organization. The distinction between "line" and "staff" also comes from this tradition.

Military hierarchy then entered business through the American railroads in the 1840s and 1850s. West Point-trained engineers brought military organizational thinking into railroad companies. Daniel McCallum's org chart for the New York and Erie Railroad formalized layers of authority, reporting lines, and structured information flow for a system that had grown too large for informal management.

Frederick Taylor later optimized work inside that hierarchy through measurement, specialization, and task design. The result was the functional pyramid organization: a structure built for efficiency inside a larger information-routing system.

### 3. Attempts to Break Functional Hierarchy

The Manhattan Project was a major test of whether cross-functional coordination could outperform rigid functional hierarchy. Oppenheimer organized Los Alamos into functional divisions but repeatedly forced collaboration across them. When the implosion problem became critical in 1944, he reorganized around the problem itself rather than preserving the org chart.

After World War II, larger and more global companies exposed the limitations of functional design. McKinsey helped popularize the matrix organization by combining functional specialties with divisional units. Later frameworks like McKinsey's 7-S argued that structure alone was not enough; shared values, skills, staff, and style mattered too.

In recent decades, tech companies tried alternatives:

- Spotify pushed cross-functional squads.
- Zappos attempted Holacracy.
- Valve operated with a flat structure.

The article's conclusion is that all of these were partial experiments. Once organizations reach enough scale, they tend to fall back into hierarchy because no better coordination mechanism existed.

The underlying tradeoff stayed the same: a narrower span of control adds layers, and more layers slow information flow.

### 4. What Block Thinks AI Changes

Block argues that the old assumption can now be challenged: organizations do not have to stay hierarchically organized if humans are no longer the only coordination mechanism.

The article contrasts two uses of AI:

- **Copilot mode**: AI helps people inside the existing structure work a bit faster.
- **Organizational redesign mode**: AI replaces some of the coordination work that hierarchy was invented to perform.

Block claims AI is finally capable of maintaining a continuously updated model of the business and using that model to coordinate work without relying on humans to pass context up and down layers of management.

### 5. The Two Models Block Thinks Matter

Block says this requires two things:

1. A **company world model**
2. A **customer world model**

The company world model is possible because Block is remote-first and artifact-heavy. Decisions, code, designs, plans, and discussions already exist in machine-readable form. In theory, AI can continuously assemble those artifacts into a living picture of what is being built, where things are blocked, and how work is progressing.

The customer world model depends on a strong signal. Block argues that financial transactions are unusually honest because spending, saving, sending, borrowing, and repaying are real actions rather than survey answers or ad clicks. Its claim is that seeing both sides of millions of transactions gives it a uniquely rich behavioral dataset.

### 6. The Four Layers of the New Company

Instead of organizing around traditional product teams and roadmaps, the article says companies like Block should build four things.

#### Capabilities

These are the atomic primitives: payments, lending, card issuance, banking, BNPL, payroll, and other core financial infrastructure. They are not products. They are durable building blocks with reliability, compliance, and performance requirements.

#### World Model

This has two sides:

- The **company world model**, which tracks operations, priorities, blockers, and internal state
- The **customer world model**, which tracks customers, merchants, markets, and over time moves from raw transactions toward causal and predictive understanding

#### Intelligence Layer

This is the system that composes capabilities into solutions for specific customers at specific moments. The article's examples include:

- proactively offering a merchant financing package when cash flow looks tight
- reconfiguring a consumer's financial setup after detecting a likely move to a new city

In this model, the system recognizes the moment and assembles the solution. The roadmap becomes less about guesswork and more about observing where composition fails because a needed capability does not yet exist.

#### Interfaces

Square, Cash App, Afterpay, TIDAL, bitkey, and proto are framed as delivery surfaces rather than the primary locus of value creation. The real value, in this view, sits in the model and the intelligence layer.

### 7. What Human Roles Become

If the system carries more of the intelligence, then people move toward the edge where judgment and real-world contact still matter.

The article argues that humans remain essential for:

- sensing things the model cannot yet perceive
- handling cultural context and trust
- making ethical calls
- navigating novel and high-stakes situations

Block says the org should normalize around three roles.

#### Individual Contributors (ICs)

Specialists who build and operate capabilities, the world model, the intelligence layer, and the interfaces.

#### Directly Responsible Individuals (DRIs)

People who temporarily or persistently own cross-cutting problems and outcomes. A DRI can pull resources across multiple teams to solve a problem.

#### Player-Coaches

Builders who still do craft work but also develop people. In the article's framing, they replace traditional managers whose primary function was information routing.

The conclusion is explicit: if AI can coordinate the work, then a permanent middle-management layer is no longer structurally necessary.

### 8. Final Claim

Block says every company will eventually have to answer a hard question: what does your company understand that is genuinely hard to understand, and is that understanding getting deeper every day?

If the answer is "nothing," then AI is mostly a cost-cutting story. If the answer is "something deep and compounding," then AI reveals what the company actually is.

Block's answer is the economic graph: merchants, consumers, transactions, and real-time financial behavior. The article claims that this understanding compounds continuously, and that companies organized as intelligence rather than hierarchy will reshape how many organizations operate.

## Analysis

### Main Topics

1. **Hierarchy is mostly about coordination, not status.** The article's core move is to reinterpret org charts as information-routing systems built around human cognitive limits.
2. **AI's biggest upside may be organizational, not just individual.** The piece argues that the real leap is not "everyone gets a copilot" but "the company itself becomes more intelligent."
3. **Data quality determines whether this works.** Block's model depends on machine-readable internal artifacts and unusually rich customer signal.
4. **Products may become compositions instead of fixed roadmaps.** Capabilities, models, and an intelligence layer replace parts of traditional product management.
5. **Human work shifts toward judgment, trust, and edge cases.** The article does not claim people disappear; it claims coordination-heavy management becomes less necessary.

### What the Piece Gets Right

- It correctly identifies that much of management exists because information is fragmented, delayed, and filtered as organizations scale.
- It makes a useful distinction between AI as a local productivity tool and AI as a redesign of the operating system of a company.
- It offers a concrete architecture instead of staying at the level of vague futurism.
- It is strongest when treated as an operating-model essay rather than a labor-replacement essay.

### Where the Piece Is Weak or Incomplete

- It under-specifies governance. Featherwit's reply is the right pressure test: audit, drift detection, and rollback cannot be an afterthought.
- It assumes the internal artifact layer is complete and legible. In reality, important context still lives in heads, side chats, politics, and timing.
- It downplays how much product intuition, editorial judgment, and strategy are upstream of any model.
- It likely fits companies with dense proprietary data much better than companies whose signals are thinner, noisier, or culturally mediated.
- It frames middle management mainly as routing overhead, when some of it also performs conflict containment, coaching, and institution-building.

## Relevance to You and 9takes

### Where It Applies

- **The world-model idea is relevant.** 9takes already produces machine-readable artifacts: questions, answers, comments, moderation events, drafts, SEO work, analytics, personality tags, and research notes. That is the raw material for a company and community world model.
- **Capability-based product thinking fits.** Instead of thinking only in pages or features, 9takes can think in reusable capabilities: ask a question, answer before reading, classify by type, cluster perspectives, summarize disagreement, recommend follow-up questions, surface cross-type patterns, and turn high-signal threads into articles.
- **AI coordination is useful for a small leverage-focused team.** Not because you need to remove management layers, but because you can route editorial, research, moderation, SEO, and product work more intelligently.
- **The article matches your existing direction toward structured perspective data.** 9takes is already built around comparative viewpoints, which is a better substrate for AI than a generic social feed.

### Where It Does Not Transfer Cleanly

- **9takes does not have Block's transaction graph.** Block's argument works because money is a very strong signal. 9takes needs a different "honest signal."
- **You probably do not have a hierarchy problem yet.** A small company usually does not need a grand org redesign. The more useful takeaway is data architecture plus workflow orchestration, not abolishing middle management you do not really have.
- **Community products depend heavily on trust and tone.** Over-automating personalization, moderation, or identity inference can damage the very honesty the platform is trying to create.

### The Best Translation for 9takes

If Block's honest signal is transaction behavior, 9takes' honest signal is closer to **answer-first behavior plus revealed preference**:

- what people answer before seeing others
- which questions they return to
- which perspectives they dwell on
- which type filters they use
- where disagreement clusters
- what they save, share, or continue discussing

That is weaker than payments data, but it is still real behavioral signal. It could support a meaningful 9takes world model if captured carefully.

### Practical 9takes Implications

1. **Build a community world model.** Track users, question intent, answer patterns, personality lenses, topic clusters, and unresolved tensions across the platform.
2. **Turn features into capabilities.** Separate reusable functions like summarization, comparison, recommendation, moderation triage, and editorial extraction from the surface UI.
3. **Let failed composition generate roadmap ideas.** If the system repeatedly cannot answer a user need, moderate a pattern, or connect a discussion to relevant follow-up content, that failure should inform what you build next.
4. **Keep humans on sensitive edges.** Mental health-adjacent interpretation, moderation escalation, identity-sensitive personalization, and anything that could misread a person's motives should stay human-reviewed.
5. **Use AI to increase coherence, not just output.** The biggest gain for 9takes is likely better routing across content, community, product, and SEO rather than just faster drafting.

### Bottom Line

This piece is relevant to you and 9takes, but mostly as a **product-and-operations architecture lens**, not as a literal blueprint for company structure.

The strongest takeaway is:

> 9takes should think about building an intelligence layer on top of question, answer, and personality data.

The weakest takeaway is:

> 9takes should copy Block's org ideology wholesale.

Block is arguing from a high-signal fintech environment. For 9takes, the opportunity is narrower and more practical: use AI to build a better model of community behavior, surface more meaningful perspective differences, and route work more intelligently across a small team.
