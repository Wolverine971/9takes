## Core Philosophy & Framing

Your overarching frame for every issue should be:

> **"Individual liberty, fiscal responsibility, and limited government produce better outcomes than centralized control and expanding bureaucracy."**

Secondary frames to weave in:

- **Results over intentions**: "We judge policies by their outcomes, not their stated goals."
- **Empowering people, not institutions**: "We trust individuals and communities to solve problems better than distant bureaucracies."
- **Sustainability**: "Compassion without fiscal discipline is a promise you can't keep."
- **Pragmatism**: "What actually works vs. what sounds good on paper."

## Main Talking Points

### 1. Fiscal Responsibility & the National Debt

**The argument**: The federal government is spending at an unsustainable pace that threatens future generations.

- The national debt hit **$37.6 trillion** as of September 2025 - up $2.2T in a single fiscal year
- Debt-to-GDP ratio reached **99.8%** - nearly double the 50-year average of ~51%
- Interest payments on the debt reached **$1.2 trillion** in FY2025 - more than we spend on defense
- The Treasury spent **$276 billion on interest in Q4 2025 alone** - up $30B from a year prior
- CBO projects deficits exceeding **$2 trillion annually** over the next decade

**Key line**: "We're now spending more on interest payments than on national defense. Every dollar we pay in interest is a dollar we can't invest in infrastructure, education, or tax relief. This is a bipartisan failure, but only one side is seriously talking about spending discipline."

**Anticipated counter**: "Republicans also increase the debt - Trump's tax cuts added trillions."

**Rebuttal**: "That's fair - fiscal discipline needs to be bipartisan, and Republicans haven't been perfect. But the solution isn't more spending. The solution is growth-oriented policy combined with spending restraint. Tax cuts that stimulate economic growth can increase revenue - the 2017 tax cuts led to record federal revenue collections. The problem has always been spending, not revenue."

---

### 2. Border Security & Immigration

**The argument**: Enforcing immigration law is both a national security imperative and a matter of fairness to legal immigrants and American workers.

- Under the Biden administration, CBP recorded approximately **2.9 million nationwide encounters** in FY2024
- Border crossings peaked at **249,700 in December 2023**
- After the Trump administration's enforcement policies took effect, southwest border apprehensions dropped **85%** vs. the same period in 2024
- By May 2025, illegal crossings at the southwest border fell to **8,725** - a **93% decrease** from May 2024
- FY2026 started with the **lowest number of encounters for the start of a fiscal year in history**

**Key line**: "The numbers prove that enforcement works. When you signal that laws will be enforced, illegal crossings plummet. When you signal leniency, they surge. This isn't about being anti-immigrant - it's about being pro-legal-immigration and pro-rule-of-law."

**Anticipated counter**: "These policies are cruel and separate families."

**Rebuttal**: "What's cruel is an open-border policy that incentivizes dangerous crossings where people die in the desert, enriches cartels, enables human trafficking, and undercuts wages for the most vulnerable American workers. A compassionate immigration policy is one that's orderly, legal, and actually enforced. Every country in the world enforces its borders - this shouldn't be controversial."

---

### 3. Energy Independence & Affordable Energy

**The argument**: American energy production creates jobs, lowers costs for families, and strengthens national security.

- US became the **first country to export over 100 million metric tons of LNG** in a single year (2025)
- US natural gas production and demand **both hit record highs** in 2025
- A **16% increase** in recoverable supply helped meet rising demand
- The BLM approved nearly **6,000 drilling permits** - a **55% increase** from 2024-2025
- Alaska crude oil production is expected to **grow in 2026 for the first time since 2017**

**Key line**: "American energy dominance means lower prices at the pump, hundreds of thousands of good-paying jobs, and the ability to supply our allies in Europe with clean natural gas instead of letting them depend on Russia or the Middle East. Energy independence is economic policy, national security policy, and environmental policy all in one."

**Anticipated counter**: "Fossil fuels cause climate change."

**Rebuttal**: "We can pursue an all-of-the-above energy strategy. But killing domestic production doesn't reduce global demand - it just shifts production to countries with worse environmental standards. US natural gas has actually been the single biggest driver of emissions reductions, displacing coal. Innovation and market forces are reducing emissions faster than mandates. Meanwhile, we need affordable energy today - families can't wait 20 years for a transition that may not work as planned."

### 4. Government Efficiency & Accountability

**The argument**: The federal bureaucracy is bloated, wasteful, and in need of serious reform.

- DOGE investigations uncovered **$382 million in fraudulent unemployment payments** - including to people with birthdates in the future and infants
- **$312 million** in business loans issued to children 11 and under
- **$80 million** in wasteful spending at the Department of Defense
- The EPA itself identified **$20 billion** in wasted taxpayer dollars within the agency

**Key line**: "Whether you agree with every DOGE decision or not, the principle is right: taxpayers deserve to know where their money goes, and government agencies should be accountable for results. Finding hundreds of millions in payments to people who don't exist isn't partisan - it's basic stewardship."

**Anticipated counter**: "DOGE has been chaotic and its savings claims don't add up."

**Rebuttal**: "Government reform is messy - entrenched bureaucracies resist change. The AP found that some contract cuts didn't produce immediate savings, and that's a fair critique of execution. But the underlying mission is correct: the federal government spends $6.7 trillion a year. Even finding 1-2% in waste and fraud saves tens of billions. The question isn't whether DOGE is perfect - it's whether the status quo of zero accountability was acceptable. It wasn't."

### 5. Education & School Choice

**The argument**: Parents - not bureaucracies - should choose the best education for their children.

- **16 states** created new or expanded school choice programs in 2025
- Participation jumped **25%**, with roughly **1.3 million K-12 students** participating
- **23 of 31 research analyses** find positive outcomes from school choice; **none** found overall negative effects
- A 2025 Urban Institute study found voucher students were **significantly more likely to enroll in college**, especially four-year institutions
- Arkansas, Indiana, and Iowa have reached the **8% participation threshold**

**Key line**: "If the local public school is great, parents will choose it. But if it isn't, they shouldn't be trapped by their zip code. School choice doesn't defund public schools - it gives every family the same options that wealthy families have always had. That's equity in action."

**Anticipated counter**: "School choice defunds public schools and lacks accountability."

**Rebuttal**: "Funding should follow the child, not the institution. When public schools lose students, that's a signal to improve, not an argument to trap kids in failing schools. And the research is clear: 23 of 31 studies show positive outcomes, zero show negative. As for accountability - parents are the ultimate accountability mechanism. They'll leave bad schools faster than any bureaucratic review process."

### 6. Free Speech & First Amendment

**The argument**: Government should not be in the business of policing speech, and the trend toward censorship - whether by government or through government-tech collusion - is dangerous.

- **65% of Americans** say they are afraid to speak freely
- Federal employees were found to have directed social media platforms to censor legal speech
- The Free Speech Protection Act (S.188) was introduced to prohibit federal employees from directing platforms to censor protected speech
- Europe's Online Safety Act and Digital Services Act give bureaucrats sweeping power over online content - a cautionary tale

**Key line**: "The First Amendment exists precisely to protect speech that some people find uncomfortable. A government that decides which ideas are 'misinformation' is a government that can silence anyone. The answer to bad speech is more speech, not censorship."

**Anticipated counter**: "Misinformation is dangerous and platforms should moderate harmful content."

**Rebuttal**: "Who decides what's 'misinformation'? The government said the lab leak theory was misinformation - until it wasn't. They said natural immunity didn't work - until it did. The problem isn't individual moderation decisions; it's the principle that government officials can pressure private platforms to suppress legal speech. That's the definition of censorship, and it should alarm everyone regardless of party."

### 7. Second Amendment Rights

**The argument**: The right to bear arms is a constitutional right that serves a practical purpose - self-defense.

- Estimates of defensive gun use range from **500,000 to 1.67 million incidents annually**
- A 2025 JAMA Network Open study found **4.7%** of Americans have shown a firearm to deter a perceived threat in their lifetime
- **56%** of gun owners cite personal protection as their primary reason for ownership
- The Heritage Foundation's database documents **thousands** of verified defensive gun use incidents

**Key line**: "Guns are used defensively far more often than they're used in crimes. Law-abiding citizens have a constitutional and moral right to protect themselves and their families. Every gun control law should be evaluated by a simple test: will it stop criminals, or will it only burden law-abiding citizens?"

**Anticipated counter**: "The US has far more gun deaths than other developed countries."

**Rebuttal**: "When you break down the data, roughly 60% of gun deaths are suicides - a mental health crisis that gun bans don't solve (countries with strict gun laws still have comparable suicide rates using other methods). Of the remaining homicides, the vast majority are concentrated in a handful of cities with the strictest gun laws in the country. The problem isn't legal gun ownership - it's gang violence, mental health, and under-enforcement of existing laws."

---

### 8. Healthcare - Market-Based Reform

**The argument**: Free-market competition, transparency, and choice will lower healthcare costs better than government-run systems.

- Sanders' Medicare for All was projected to cost **$25-35 trillion over 10 years**, or roughly **$2.5 trillion per year** (Urban Institute estimate)
- Harris' version was projected at **$43.9 trillion** over 2026-2035
- Administrative cost savings from single-payer could be achieved through **payer-provider contract reforms** without overhauling the entire system
- Association Health Plans allow small businesses to pool together for **affordable coverage without new federal spending**

**Key line**: "Every government-run healthcare system in the world has rationing, wait times, and bureaucratic inefficiency. Instead of replacing the entire system, let's fix what's broken: allow insurance to be sold across state lines, require price transparency, expand Health Savings Accounts, let small businesses pool together for coverage, and crack down on pharmaceutical middlemen."

**Anticipated counter**: "Other countries have universal healthcare at lower cost."

**Rebuttal**: "Those countries also have months-long wait times for surgeries, fewer treatment options, and benefit from American pharmaceutical innovation that their systems don't fund. The US develops more new drugs and medical technologies than any other country precisely because we have a market-based system. If we adopt single-payer, who picks up that innovation? Also, those countries have populations the size of a single US state - scaling is fundamentally different."

### 9. Trade & Tariffs

**The argument**: Strategic tariffs protect American manufacturing and national security, and rebalance unfair trade relationships.

- Trade deficits are shrinking and **investment announcements are surging**
- **81% of CEOs** plan to bring supply chains closer to home
- US-China trade deal framework suggests tariffs may be reduced to **10-20%** in exchange for IP protections and agricultural purchases
- Strategic tariffs on steel and aluminum protect industries critical to national defense

**Key line**: "Free trade works when it's fair trade. For decades, China manipulated its currency, stole our intellectual property, and subsidized state-owned enterprises to undercut American manufacturers. Tariffs are leverage - they brought China to the negotiating table and are reshoring critical supply chains."

**Anticipated counter**: "Tariffs are a tax on consumers and hurt the economy."

**Rebuttal**: "In the short term, yes - there are adjustment costs. But the question is: what's the cost of dependence? COVID showed us what happens when 90% of our pharmaceuticals and critical minerals come from China. National security requires a manufacturing base. The tariffs have already brought China to negotiate, and any trade deal should be judged on whether it achieves lasting structural reform, not just short-term price effects."

## Strongest Issues

These are the issues where the data is most clearly on your side and public opinion tends to favor the right-leaning position:

### 1. Border Security (STRONGEST)

- The before/after data is devastating: 249,700 crossings/month vs. 8,725/month
- Public opinion strongly favors enforcement
- The "enforce existing law" framing is common-sense and hard to argue against

### 2. Government Waste & Accountability

- Specific, shocking examples (loans to children, payments to nonexistent people) are powerful
- "Accountability" is universally popular - no one defends waste

### 3. School Choice

- Research data is overwhelmingly positive (23/31 studies positive, 0 negative)
- The equity argument ("giving poor families the same options as rich families") is devastating when used against the left
- Participation is surging, suggesting satisfaction

### 4. Energy Production

- Record production numbers, record exports, lower prices
- National security angle (not depending on adversaries) is strong
- LNG exports helping Europe reduce Russia dependence is a strong talking point

### 5. Free Speech

- 65% of Americans afraid to speak freely is a powerful stat
- Government-tech censorship collusion is well-documented
- "Who decides what's misinformation?" is a question the left struggles to answer

## Weakest Issues & How to Handle Them

### 1. Healthcare (WEAKEST)

**Why it's weak**: Most Americans want some form of universal coverage. Saying "the market will fix it" sounds hollow to people drowning in medical debt.

**Strategy**: Don't defend the status quo. Acknowledge the system is broken, then pivot to specific market-based reforms: price transparency, cross-state competition, HSAs, cracking down on PBMs (pharmacy benefit managers). "I'm not defending the current system - I'm saying the solution is more competition, not less."

### 2. Climate Change

**Why it's weak**: The scientific consensus is clear. Dismissing climate change entirely loses credibility with moderates and younger voters.

**Strategy**: Don't deny the science. Instead, argue about solutions: "The question isn't whether climate change is real - it's whether crushing American energy production and spending trillions on mandates is the best way to address it. US emissions have actually declined thanks to natural gas displacing coal. Innovation beats regulation."

### 3. Tariffs & Trade

**Why it's weak**: Manufacturing jobs haven't returned as promised. Blue-collar employment actually fell by 59,000 jobs. Prices went up. Only 2% of companies have completed reshoring plans despite 81% saying they want to.

**Strategy**: Acknowledge the transition takes time: "Reshoring doesn't happen overnight - it takes years to build factories and train workers. But the investment announcements are real, and the alternative - continued dependence on China for critical goods - is unacceptable. We're playing a long game."

### 4. National Debt (Partially Weak)

**Why it's weak**: Republicans have also contributed significantly to the debt. Trump's tax cuts added trillions. Hard to claim fiscal discipline when your side also spends.

**Strategy**: Own it honestly: "You're right that both parties share blame. That's exactly why we need structural reform - spending caps, balanced budget requirements, and sunset clauses on programs. But the solution to too much debt is never more spending."

### 5. DOGE Execution

**Why it's weak**: 40% of DOGE's claimed contract cuts don't actually save money. Senate investigations found DOGE itself wasted $21.7B. Chaotic execution undermines the credibility of the mission.

**Strategy**: Separate mission from execution: "The mission of rooting out waste is right even if some of the execution has been imperfect. The question is: do you want a government that never audits itself, or one that tries - imperfectly - to find and fix waste? I'll take imperfect accountability over comfortable complacency."

## Anticipated Left-Wing Arguments & Rebuttals

### "Trickle-down economics doesn't work"

**Rebuttal**: "That's a straw man - nobody advocates 'trickle-down.' What we advocate is creating conditions where businesses invest, hire, and grow. After the 2017 tax cuts, unemployment hit historic lows, wages rose fastest for the bottom quartile, and federal revenue actually increased. Supply-side economics isn't about helping the rich - it's about growing the pie so there's more for everyone."

### "Republicans only care about the rich"

**Rebuttal**: "The 2017 tax cuts doubled the standard deduction, benefiting every middle-class family. The Trump administration saw the fastest real wage growth for the bottom 25% of earners. School choice gives poor families options. Energy production lowers costs for working families. These are policies that help people who don't have lobbyists."

### "You want to ban abortion"

**Rebuttal**: "The Dobbs decision returned this issue to the democratic process - to voters in each state. That's not banning anything; it's letting people vote on it rather than having nine unelected judges decide for 330 million people. And polls show most Americans support some restrictions - the Democratic position of abortion until birth is actually the extreme one."

### "Trump is a threat to democracy"

**Rebuttal**: "Which democratic institution has been destroyed? Congress is functioning, courts are issuing rulings against the administration (and those rulings are being followed), the press is operating freely, and elections are happening on schedule. Meanwhile, it was the previous administration that pushed for censorship of political speech and weaponized federal agencies against political opponents. Democracy is messy - that's not the same as it being under threat."

### "You're racist/anti-immigrant"

**Rebuttal**: "Legal immigration to the US remains among the highest in the world. Enforcing immigration law isn't anti-immigrant - it's pro-immigrant. The biggest victims of illegal immigration are legal immigrants who waited years and followed the rules, and minority communities whose wages are undercut by an illegal labor market. We want more legal immigration and less illegal immigration."

### "Universal healthcare works in Europe"

**Rebuttal**: "It 'works' with 6-month waits for knee replacements, cancer treatment delays, and fewer MRI machines per capita than the US. The UK's NHS is in perpetual crisis. Canada's wait times are getting worse every year. And those systems free-ride on American pharmaceutical innovation. If the US stops developing new drugs because we destroy the profit incentive, the whole world suffers."

### "Climate change is an existential crisis"

**Rebuttal**: "Even if we accept the most aggressive projections, the US eliminating all emissions tomorrow wouldn't solve the problem - China and India would still be increasing theirs. The practical answer is innovation: nuclear energy, carbon capture, natural gas as a bridge fuel. The Paris Agreement let China increase emissions until 2030 while we hamstrung our own economy. That's not a climate plan - it's economic disarmament."

### "Gun control saves lives"

**Rebuttal**: "Then why do the cities with the strictest gun laws have the highest gun violence? Chicago, Baltimore, DC - all have extreme gun restrictions and extreme gun crime. Because criminals don't follow gun laws. Meanwhile, states with high legal gun ownership often have lower crime rates. The data suggests that enforcement of existing laws and addressing root causes (gangs, mental health, poverty) is more effective than new restrictions on legal owners."

### "Inequality is getting worse"

**Rebuttal**: "The question isn't whether some people have more - the question is whether everyone is doing better. Under pro-growth policies, median household income hit all-time highs, poverty hit all-time lows, and unemployment hit all-time lows before COVID. The rising tide lifted all boats. The left's approach of redistribution has never created prosperity - it's only ever redistributed scarcity."

### "Defunding government agencies hurts people"

**Rebuttal**: "Reforming isn't defunding. Nobody is saying we don't need environmental protection or education. We're saying the agencies doing those jobs have become bloated and inefficient. The Department of Education's budget has grown 200% in real terms since 1980, while test scores have flatlined. More spending doesn't equal better outcomes. Let's fund results, not bureaucracies."

## Debate Tactics & Strategy

### Opening Statement Framework

"I believe in an America where the government works for the people, not the other way around. Where families keep more of what they earn, where parents choose their kids' schools, where our borders are secure, where energy is affordable, and where every citizen can speak their mind without fear. That's not radical - that's the founding vision of this country."

### Rules of Engagement

1. **Lead with data, not emotion**: Let your opponent be the emotional one. You be the one with the numbers.
2. **Acknowledge valid criticism**: "You're right that Republicans have also contributed to the debt" - this builds credibility and disarms your opponent.
3. **Reframe, don't defend**: Never defend the indefensible. If pressed on something awkward, pivot to the principle: "The principle is right even if the execution was imperfect."
4. **Use the "who benefits" test**: "Who benefits from school choice? Poor families. Who benefits from the status quo? Teachers' unions. Who should we prioritize?"
5. **Ask questions**: "If enforcement doesn't work, why did crossings drop 93%?" Questions are more powerful than statements.
6. **Avoid culture war traps**: If your opponent tries to drag you into culture war issues (trans athletes, pronouns, etc.), briefly state your position and pivot back to economics, border, or energy. Those are your strongest ground.
7. **Cite left-leaning sources when possible**: Urban Institute, JAMA, CBO - these carry more weight than Heritage or Cato when you're persuading a skeptical audience.

### Closing Statement Framework

"We can argue about policy all day, but let me leave you with this: the communities that have embraced school choice are seeing kids thrive. The border communities that finally have enforcement are safer. American energy workers are setting production records. These aren't theories - they're results. I believe in policies that empower people, not policies that empower government. And I believe the results speak for themselves."

## Strategic Advice (Post-Analysis)

_Added after cross-analyzing against the opposing debate prep material._

### 1. Healthcare Is Your Achilles' Heel -- Prepare Much More

Your own prep correctly labels this your weakest issue, but your current response is insufficient. Your opponent will lead with: _"We pay $14,885 per capita vs. the OECD average of $7,371. We pay double and 36% of Americans skip care due to cost."_ Your current counter -- "wait times in Canada" and "American pharmaceutical innovation" -- is decades old and audiences know it.

**Fix**: Stop defending the status quo. Get aggressive on specific reforms with projected savings:

- _"Price transparency alone could save families 20-40% on procedures. Right now hospitals charge wildly different prices for the same surgery and you can't find out until you get the bill. That's not a market -- that's a racket."_
- _"Cracking down on pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs) -- the middlemen who take a cut of every prescription -- could save $100+ billion."_
- _"Association Health Plans let small businesses pool together -- same coverage, no new bureaucracy, no trillion-dollar price tag."_

Have specific dollar amounts for each reform. Your opponent has specific numbers; you need them too. Saying "the market will fix it" without specifics loses to "we pay double for worse outcomes" every time.

### 2. The Inequality Data Will Hurt You -- Don't Dismiss It

Your opponent has devastating numbers: top 1% owns 31.7% of wealth, bottom 50% owns 2.5%, billionaires pay an 8.2% effective rate vs. 13% for average Americans. Your current rebuttal -- "A rising tide lifts all boats" and "median household income hit all-time highs pre-COVID" -- hasn't matched lived experience for most Americans in decades.

**Fix**: Acknowledge the problem, then compete on solutions: _"You're right that the system is tilted -- I agree. Where we disagree is the fix. My opponent wants to redistribute. I want to create more paths up. School choice so a poor kid in Baltimore can get the same education as a rich kid in Bethesda. Deregulation so a single mom can start a business without a lawyer. Energy jobs that pay $80K without a college degree. Opportunity zones that bring investment to the communities that need it most."_

The key: don't deny the inequality. Contest who has the better solution.

### 3. Prepare for the Tax Rate Sound Bite

"Billionaires pay 8.2% while nurses pay 13%" is your opponent's single best line. You need a direct answer, not a pivot.

**Fix**: _"That statistic includes unrealized capital gains -- paper wealth that hasn't been sold. No country in the world taxes unrealized gains as income, because it's not income until it's sold. But I agree the tax code is broken. It's 74,000 pages of loopholes written by lobbyists. I want to simplify it -- close the loopholes, lower the rates, and make it fair for everyone. My opponent wants to add complexity. I want to reduce it."_

This acknowledges the problem, offers a credible counter to the specific stat, and pivots to your reform narrative.

### 4. Your Climate Position Needs Substance, Not Just Framing

"Innovation beats regulation" is a viable frame, but it's too vague. Your opponent has $38 trillion in projected annual costs by 2049. You need equally specific proposals.

**Fix**: Get concrete on the innovation agenda:

- _"I support doubling our nuclear energy capacity. Nuclear is zero-emission, reliable, and France gets 70% of its electricity from it."_
