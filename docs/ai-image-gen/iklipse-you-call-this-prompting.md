## Beat skeleton

Hook (accusation: "you call this prompting?") → diagnosis frame ("fails for the same four reasons") → **negative 2×2 grid** (the 4 tells of fake) → before/after contrast ("same AI, different outcome") → **positive 2×2 grid** (the 4 fixes) → rule ("prompt for intention, not vibes") → comment CTA.

The engine is a **mirrored 2×2 diagnosis/fix pair**: slide 3 names the four failure traits in a grid, slide 5 answers each with a fix in the _same_ grid layout. The visual symmetry makes the lesson feel complete and save-able. The **"driver not the car"** caption metaphor is the memorable reframe (it's the _direction_, not the _tool_).

## Highest-value extractables

- **Mirrored 2×2 diagnosis ↔ fix grids** — name 4 failure modes, then 4 fixes in the identical layout. Extremely legible, screenshot-friendly. The strongest _layout_ template in the set.
- **"It's the driver, not the car."** — clean, quotable reframe for "the skill matters more than the tool." Reusable across any tool-anxiety topic.
- **"Prompt for intention, not vibes" / "X, not Y" rule** — sharpens a fuzzy instinct into a directive.
- **"Same AI, different outcome"** — before/after with the variable held constant (only direction changed) — a persuasive proof device.

## 9takes translation

"**You call this reading people?**" Mirrored grids:

- **Why your read falls flat (2×2):** Surface judgment · Generic labels · No curiosity · Your own projection.
- **To actually understand someone (2×2):** Notice the reaction · Ask what they're protecting · Name the situation · Hold the type loosely.
  Rule: **"Don't read for a verdict. Read for the why."** Reframe (the "driver not the car"): **"It's not the personality test, it's how you use it."** CTA: "Comment your type."

The mirrored-grid layout is the single most reusable _design_ idea from this deck for 9takes — a "what misreading looks like / what understanding looks like" diptych maps perfectly onto the give-first teaching frame. The "driver not the car" line is worth lifting almost verbatim to counter "isn't personality typing just a gimmick?" skepticism.
