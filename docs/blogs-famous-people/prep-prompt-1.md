# Prep Prompt 1: Deep Research for Personality Analysis

## Objective

Research **{PERSON}** to find the material that makes a great personality analysis possible: the central contradiction, the signature details, the unguarded moments, and the testimony from people who know them. This is not a biography exercise. It is a hunt for psychological insight.

## What You're Looking For

The research phase determines whether the blog will be compelling or forgettable. Generic career summaries produce generic blogs. The goal is to find material that reveals who someone actually is, not just what they've done.

### 1. The Central Contradiction (MOST IMPORTANT)

Every fascinating person has something that doesn't quite add up. Find it.

- What does this person preach vs. what do they struggle with?
- What does the public see vs. what do people close to them describe?
- What contradiction in their behavior has never been satisfactorily explained?
- What would surprise a casual fan the most about who they really are?

**Examples of great contradictions found through research:**

- Peter Thiel: Lectures against surveillance while co-founding the most powerful surveillance company on earth
- Paris Hilton: Packaged as a sex symbol while coping with sexual trauma that made her "almost asexual"
- Blake Lively: Claims her identity comes from internal stability while spending enormous energy controlling every external detail
- Hasan Piker: Dismissed as "just a rage streamer" when the anger is actually how he expresses care

**If you can't find a contradiction, the research isn't deep enough. Dig more.**

### 2. Signature Details (3-5 Minimum)

The most powerful evidence is never the headline accomplishment. It's the small, specific, seemingly minor detail that cracks open who someone really is.

Hunt for:

- **Offhand comments** in long-form interviews that reveal more than any prepared answer
- **Childhood anecdotes** told in passing that connect to adult behavior
- **Habits and rituals** that seem trivial but illuminate a pattern
- **Moments of failure or crisis** where the mask slipped
- **Objects, spaces, or choices** that reveal psychology (Thiel's parachute, Thiel's Tolkien company names, Blake's three years on drink recipes)

**The pattern to find:** Small moment → years of silent impact → revealing consequence. This is what makes readers feel they're seeing the real person.

### 3. The Subject's Own Words

Direct quotes are the structural material of a great personality analysis. Paraphrasing is the fallback, not the default.

**Priority sources (in order):**

1. **Long-form podcast appearances** — Search "[Person] podcast interview," "[Person] long form interview." Major shows (Joe Rogan, Lex Fridman, Diary of a CEO, Tim Ferriss, Call Her Daddy, etc.) plus niche podcasts in their field. These are gold because people are unscripted and reveal things they'd never say in a press junket.

2. **Memoirs and books they've written** — Direct window into thinking patterns.

3. **YouTube interviews** — Search "[Person] interview YouTube," "[Person] talks about [childhood/struggles/motivation]." Long-form (30+ min) is most valuable.

4. **Documentaries and profiles** — Film crews capture unguarded moments.

**What to pull:**

- Quotes where they describe their own psychology, fears, or motivations
- Quotes where they contradict their public image
- Quotes where they're clearly being honest rather than performing
- Quotes that are specific and vivid, not generic and polished

**Aim for 15-25 strong direct quotes with clear attribution** (source name, year if possible). The writing phase will use the best 10-15.

### 4. Testimony From People Around Them

What do the people who know them say? This is the most commonly missing material in weaker blogs.

- **Collaborators and co-workers**: Directors, producers, bandmates, business partners describing what it's like to work with them
- **Friends and family**: How do the people closest to them describe them privately?
- **Rivals and critics**: What do intelligent critics say? (Not trolls — substantive criticism that reveals something)
- **The surprise gap**: What consistently surprises people who meet them in person vs. their public reputation?

### 5. The Crisis That Revealed Character

Every person has at least one moment where things fell apart. That moment reveals more about personality than any amount of success.

- What was their lowest public point?
- How did they respond — did they withdraw, fight back, perform, build?
- What did they say about it afterward, especially years later when the response had time to become honest?
- Does their crisis response match their public image, or does it reveal something different?

### 6. Childhood → Adult Thread

The most psychologically satisfying analyses connect early experiences to present behavior. Not in a diagnostic way, but narratively.

- What was their family environment? (Not just "they grew up in X" — the emotional texture)
- What early experiences shaped the behavior the world sees now?
- What did love, approval, or safety feel like in their household?
- Is there a visible thread from a childhood moment to their adult patterns?

### 7. The Public/Private Gap

- What do they do when no one's watching?
- What do their close friends say about them that contradicts their public reputation?
- What do they admit in long-form interviews that contradicts their image?
- What surprises people who meet them in person?

## Research Quality Standards

- **Verify information** across multiple sources
- **Prioritize behavioral evidence** over speculation
- **Include direct quotes** with clear attribution (source, year)
- **Gather current information** (within last 5 years preferred, but formative material from any era)
- **Note contradictions** — these are features, not bugs. They're often the best material.

## Output Format

Present findings organized around the structure above. Include:

1. **Central contradiction** — stated in one phrase: "[X] vs. [Y]"
2. **Best signature details** — the 3-5 small moments that could anchor the analysis
3. **Strongest quotes** — 15-25 direct quotes with attribution
4. **Testimony from others** — what people around them say
5. **Crisis/failure moments** — how they responded when things fell apart
6. **Childhood thread** — the early experience that connects to now
7. **Public/private gap** — what the world misses
8. **Promising video/podcast list** — URLs for potential transcript gathering

**Flag anything that surprised you during research.** Surprises are often the best material.
