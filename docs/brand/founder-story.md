## The narrative

My wife and I were newlyweds and we were fighting. I kept trying to make the situation better with the tools I had, and the tools weren't enough. The way I handled conflict was not working. When things cooled down, she said, "DJ, you need to take a personality test." I was humbled, and I wanted to try anything, so I took the Myers-Briggs.

It blew my mind. Before that, my view of people was simple: there's right and wrong, and you should be a good person. If you disagree with me, either you don't know the truth, or I don't know the truth, or you're not being a good person. The Myers-Briggs cracked that open. People don't just hold different opinions. They see the world fundamentally differently.

Then my wife said, "You need to check out the Enneagram. It's deeper." She was right. The Enneagram goes after your core fears and desires. It identifies you in a way the Myers-Briggs never did. We were living in DC at the time, and our church had a small group reading Ian Cron's Enneagram book. I dove in. Every week we went chapter by chapter, and hearing how differently each person in that room saw the same material was the hook for everything that came after.

Here's what it actually fixed in my marriage. I'm an Enneagram 8. My wife is a 7. She feels fear and anxiety about things, and I was dismissing her: "Don't worry about that." I get angry, and she didn't know how to handle my anger, and sometimes she made it worse. I finally told her: let me be angry, please be patient with me, I'll come out of it. Trying to stop me just makes it worse. And I learned to make space for her fear instead of waving it off. I didn't fully understand her fear. She didn't fully understand my anger. The Enneagram gave us both a map. It was also the gateway to real skills: approaching conflict with curiosity, actually listening, mirroring people, making them feel safe.

If you want to know why the 8 fits, here's the resume. Pastor's kid. Held back in second grade, put in the special learning program. Homeschooled for two years that set me back so far that when I got to high school, kids I'd outscored were in the advanced classes and I was in the beginner ones. I didn't even make the JV soccer team. A buddy said, "Try out for wrestling, there's no cuts." So I wrestled. Hardest sport there is. My whole strategy was just don't quit. By senior year I was the team captain.

Then the Marine Corps, with a recon contract. I survived seven months of a holding platoon where they try to drown you in the pool and run you to death. I got dropped from the reconnaissance course over a knots test, a figure eight loop tied with one pigtail facing the wrong direction. The day before, I had flown around Camp Pendleton hanging from a helicopter on that exact knot. They kicked me out anyway. I talked my way into staying infantry, landed at 29 Palms, and tried out for the sniper platoon. I made it the way I make it through everything: I wasn't the top performer, but people kept quitting and I was one of the few who didn't. I passed sniper school. My dad had been diagnosed with cancer back when I was 19, my senior year of high school. Right after sniper school, around 2015 or 2016, he died. The highest high of my life and the biggest loss, at the same time. I don't think I ever fully processed it.

After the Marine Corps I went to a coding bootcamp that started six days after my contract ended. Tim Ferriss has this line that if you're not technical, you're at the mercy of the people who are. I wanted to build my own ideas without waiting on anyone. Bootcamp led to government contracting at the Census Bureau, and then I wanted a startup. The interview challenge was building a web scraper. I already had one, because I'd built a scraper to collect everything anyone on the internet was saying about the Enneagram. My obsession got me the job.

The whole time, I was tinkering with the idea that became 9takes. The first version was a personality Facebook. I killed it because it was a little dystopian. People don't like being labeled. They want to self-discover. I'd been on the type forums where self-appointed experts just tell you who you are, and arguing with someone who has decided what you are is miserable. I also knew the evangelist trap firsthand, because I fell into it: telling people their type before they were ready. It's like people who try mushrooms for the first time and decide everyone should do mushrooms. People have to be ready.

So instead of telling people what they are, I went with questions. If you ask the right questions, people discover on their own that other people see the world differently. Reddit almost does this, but Reddit has a power law: lurkers everywhere, early comments snowball, late answers die. Question and answering doesn't really happen on Reddit. If you want authentic answers, people have to give their own take before they see the crowd. That's the give-first mechanic, and it's the core of 9takes. One situation, nine takes. Some people get angry at the exact situation that makes other people fearful and other people insecure. That's the thing I wanted to unearth.

The famous-people analyses started as a growth hack. I was already typing everyone around me, so I went deep on public figures. Then something happened that changed what they were for: when I analyze someone I don't even like, I find things I genuinely like about them. The analyses generate empathy, in me first. You have to understand someone's lore, what they've actually been through, before you can say anything true about their emotions. PersonalityDB is people voting on a label. I write the mini autobiography.

On validity: I think the Enneagram is extremely powerful, and the Myers-Briggs is part of why people doubt it. Sixteen types built on binary dimensions you can flip between is wishy-washy, and it delegitimizes the whole category. The Enneagram's real weaknesses are its convoluted origin story and a community that overcomplicates it, leading with wings and instinctual subtypes before people have the basics. I teach three core emotions: anger, fear, shame, mapping to instinctual, intellectual, and emotional intelligence. Judge it on its merit, not its history.

What 9takes refuses to become: a place that shoves a type down your throat. Self-discovery or nothing. The analyses stay unbiased and empathy-generating. No ads. And we will never sell user data. This is deeply personal data, and I won't let it be used for nefarious purposes.

Winning in five years looks like a core of people steadily asking and answering questions, some takes going viral, and "get everyone's take on it" meaning "put it on 9takes." Plus Enneagram coaching, plus podcasts with the people I've written analyses of.
