# Research Brief: Ana de Armas as Paloma — _No Time to Die_ (2021)

For the 4w5 personality-analysis draft. Topic under-covered in current draft: her Bond cameo as Paloma.
Compiled 2026-06-28. Every factual claim is outlet-attributed; verbatim quotes flagged.

---

## 1. The character (shape of the role)

- Paloma is a Cuban CIA contact who appears in **one extended action sequence in Cuba**, roughly 10 minutes of screen time — "so brief it's almost a cameo," and she does not return later in the film. (AV Club; Screen Rant, "Why Ana de Armas Doesn't Return")
- Tonally she is the opposite of de Armas's "interior" roles: **bubbly, joyful, nervy, comedic, weightless**. AV Club describes her greeting Bond "not with sultry aloofness but with infectious enthusiasm, bubbling with a nervous energy," played "with a disarming affability, balancing bright-eyed eagerness with cool nonchalance."
- Running gag: Paloma keeps saying she's only had "three weeks training" yet fights brilliantly — a comedic beat, not a tragic interior. (SlashFilm; AV Club) This line was lifted directly from de Armas's own behavior on set (see §3).
- Character origin: the role **did not exist in the original script**. Director Cary Joji Fukunaga created/expanded it specifically for de Armas after _Knives Out_; Phoebe Waller-Bridge handled the comedic expansion. (Collider; The Playlist; NME — "role was originally even smaller")
- No tragic backstory, no excavation of a hidden self — by design a "bubbly, beautiful unexpected thing… strong and self-sufficient, a peer to Bond." (de Armas, via Variety)

## 2. Reception — the scene-stealer evidence

Strongest specific evidence that Paloma stole the movie:

- **AV Club (Cameron Scheetz)** — headline: _"With one thrilling sequence, Ana de Armas steals Bond's spotlight in No Time To Die."_ Body: "one need only watch the way she waltzes into the James Bond franchise and steals the spotlight from its hero in under 20 minutes"; calls her "a talented scene-stealer"; "her absence is sorely felt throughout the rest of the movie."
- **The Guardian (Peter Bradshaw)** — praised her "witty and unworldly turn." (reported; see flag below — paraphrase from search, not fetched verbatim)
- **Screen Rant** — multiple pieces frame audiences having "fallen so in love with her" that many wanted Paloma back for Bond 26.
- Recurring critical consensus: she is the "welcome spark" / standout of an otherwise somber, heavy film. (AV Club; SYFY Wire "Unsung Heroes")
- Audience proof point: the role is widely credited as a career-elevator — "even a tiny part in a James Bond film will make you known globally" (de Armas's own framing, Variety).

**Flag (weaker counter-signal):** Not every outlet treated it as scene-stealing. **Empire (John Nugent)** gave it only a passing line — "Ana De Armas in a tiny but winning repeat of the _Knives Out_ chemistry she shared with Craig" — grouping her with supporting players who "offer great assists." So "stole the movie" is strong but not universal; lead with AV Club, which is the cleanest verbatim.

## 3. What ANA HERSELF said (direct quotes)

Why she took it / star-visibility motive:

- "I loved working with Daniel Craig on 'Knives Out' and I wanted to work with Cary and even a tiny part in a James Bond film will make you known globally." (Variety, 2022)

Her hesitation — she did not want a generic "Bond girl":

- "Obviously I was jumping all over the place and very excited. But I needed to be sure it wouldn't jeopardize all the work I'd been putting in, that it wouldn't ruin everything. And the Bond women have always been, for me at least, unrelatable." (via Cinemablend)
- "My character feels like a real woman. But you know, we can evolve and grow and incorporate reality, but Bond is a fantasy." (via Cinemablend)
- "Times have changed and the women in Bond films need to be different." (paraphrased across Variety/Cinemablend — flag: wording varies by outlet)

On the role being built for her / the three-weeks-training gag:

- "This character, I think, didn't exist in the film, so it was just talking to Cary [Joji Fukunaga], that he really wanted me in the movie, and they just made it happen." (SlashFilm)
- "The funny thing is I also had only three weeks training. I think I just kept saying it so much that Cary [Joji Fukunaga] was like, 'Just say it. It's fine.'" (SlashFilm)

On fun / doing something new (KEY for thesis reconciliation):

- "It was magical, I had a wonderful experience, it was so much fun." (via CBR)
- "Paloma is one of my favorite characters from everything I've ever done. She turned out to be really special." (via CBR)
- About the action: "it was very satisfying to do something new, something that might surprise you" — and she'd do more action "as long as I can do the kicks and punches." (via Variety)

Director's read of her (useful color):

- Fukunaga conceived the role "specifically for Ana, adding a layer of humor to the character that I hadn't seen her do yet — which I thought might be fun." (Collider)
- On set, when she imported her quieter Marilyn register, Fukunaga's note was: "You're whispering." (Variety)

Production crunch context: she wrapped four months as Marilyn Monroe in _Blonde_ on a Friday and started Bond the **following Monday** — almost no prep runway. (Variety; SlashFilm; THR)

## 4. The spin-off question — who actually floated it

- **No producer, director, or de Armas quote publicly campaigning for a Paloma spin-off was found.** The spin-off talk is **critic- and fan-driven** (Screen Rant ran multiple "Bond should break its no-spinoff rule for Paloma" pieces; fan demand for Bond 26).
- **Barbara Broccoli (Bond producer) shut it down:** there are no spin-off plans — "We have not done [a spinoff] in the past, and I don't think we have any plans to do it in the future," and a Bond spin-off "would be like making Hamlet without Hamlet." (ComicBook.com; Screen Rant)
- de Armas herself, asked about female Bond futures, said she does **not** want to take over Bond: "There's no need for a female Bond. There shouldn't be any need to steal someone else's character. What I would like is that the female roles in the Bond films… are brought to life in a different way." (via search aggregation — flag: not fetched to primary source)
- Career note: Paloma is often cited as the proof-of-concept that led to de Armas leading action films (the _John Wick_ spin-off _Ballerina_). (MovieWeb; CBR)

## Thesis Assessment: does Paloma break the 4w5 "hidden interior" read?

**Honest answer: Paloma is the clearest counter-example to the draft's "she only picks roles for the invisible self underneath" thesis, and the brief should concede that openly rather than force it.** The role has no tragic interior, no Norma-Jeane-under-Marilyn, no Marta-style depth. It is a fun, comedic, weightless action cameo. By the draft's own logic, this is the role she "shouldn't" have chosen.

But it can be partially reconciled — and the reconciliation is more honest than a clean save:

1. **It was an instrumental visibility play, not an artistic excavation — and she says so.** "Even a tiny part in a James Bond film will make you known globally" (Variety). That is a strategic, career-architecture motive, not a 4w5 "I must inhabit the hidden self" motive. A 4w5 can be deliberate about leverage; this reads more as ambition/pragmatism than Individualist depth-seeking.

2. **Even here she resisted the generic.** Her hesitation ("the Bond women have always been… unrelatable"; "my character feels like a real woman") shows the same instinct to refuse the surface archetype — she didn't want to play the flat "Bond girl." That instinct is thesis-consistent even though the resulting character is light. So the _selection filter_ (no clichés, make it specific) survives; the "must have a hidden interior" specific claim does not.

3. **She frames it as relief/contrast — "do something new."** "It was very satisfying to do something new, something that might surprise you." This supports a softer thesis: the interior, withdrawn work is her center of gravity, and Paloma was a deliberate, joyful _departure_ from it — which actually fits a withdrawn type who occasionally bursts outward, but does NOT support "she only picks roles for the part nobody sees."

**Recommendation for the editor:** Do not claim Paloma proves the thesis. Use it as the draft's honest stress-test — "the role that looks like the exception" — then narrow the thesis from "she only picks hidden-interior roles" to the defensible version: "she refuses flat archetypes and treats even a 10-minute cameo as something to make specific and her own." Paloma supports _that_; it contradicts the stronger Norma-Jeane formulation. Forcing Paloma into "hidden interior" would be the weakest move; conceding and reframing is the strongest.
