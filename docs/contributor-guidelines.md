# 9takes Contributor Guidelines — Personality Analysis Blogs

> **What we do**: Deep-dive personality analyses of public figures through an Enneagram lens.
> **What we don't do**: Academic papers, listicles, or generic "10 things about Type 3" posts.

---

## What You're Writing

Each piece is a 2,000–3,000 word narrative analysis of a public figure's personality through the Enneagram system. The goal is to **humanize** the person — show the authentic human behind the public image — while making the Enneagram accessible and fascinating to readers who may have never heard of it.

Think: _longform magazine profile meets personality psychology_. Not a Wikipedia summary. Not a fan page. A psychological portrait that makes fans say "I never thought of them that way."

**Published examples** (read these before pitching):

- [9takes.com/personality-analysis](https://9takes.com/personality-analysis)

## Who We're Looking For

- **Enneagram practitioners or enthusiasts** who can type public figures with nuance (not just "they're ambitious so they're a 3")
- **Psychology-minded writers** who understand motivation, defense mechanisms, stress patterns, and growth arcs
- **Pop culture writers** who can research a person deeply and find the specific stories that reveal personality
- **Anyone** with strong Enneagram knowledge and a conversational writing voice

You don't need a credential. You need to understand the Enneagram beyond surface level and write like you're explaining it to a smart friend, not a classroom.

## Voice & Style

### The 9takes Voice

| Do This                                                                        | Not This                                                                    |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "Type 6s signal loyalty through questions — answer them, don't get defensive." | "Type 6s tend to exhibit loyalty behaviors through interrogative patterns." |
| "She's not 'being difficult' — Type 1s need things done right the first time." | "She might not be 'being difficult.'"                                       |
| Specific quotes, dates, incidents                                              | "Many people say..." or unsourced claims                                    |
| Show the psychology through behavior                                           | Declare the psychology abstractly                                           |

### Key Principles

1. **Conversational, not academic** — Write at a 7th–8th grade reading level. If you wouldn't say it to a friend, rewrite it.
2. **Show, don't tell** — Don't say "she's driven." Show the moment she flew to Nashville at 14 and knocked on record label doors.
3. **Tactically direct** — No hedging. No "might" or "tend to." Make the call.
4. **Humanizing, not hagiographic** — We're not writing fan tributes. We're revealing the real person. Include struggles and contradictions.
5. **Bursty sentence rhythm** — Mix short punchy sentences with longer descriptive ones. Never let 3 sentences in a row be the same length.

### Banned Words

These are AI-sounding clichés. Never use them:

- Enigmatic / enigma
- Delve / delving
- Unravel / unraveling
- Quintessential
- Unveiling
- Tapestry
- Landscape (as metaphor)
- "At the end of the day"

## Blog Structure

Every analysis follows this skeleton:

### 1. Opening (Quote + Hook)

Start with a compelling quote from or about the person. Follow with 2–3 sentences that hook the reader — target fans specifically and create curiosity about the psychological angle.

```
> "I have been doing this for 44 films and I am getting faster."
> — Tom Cruise

Most people see a movie star. The Enneagram sees something else entirely — a man who has turned the act of never stopping into an identity.
```

### 2. Personality Type Identification

**H2**: "What is [Person]'s personality type?"
**H3**: "[Person] is an Enneagram Type [X]"

Brief explanation of the type's core motivations, fears, and patterns. Then immediately connect to specific evidence from the person's life. Don't over-explain the Enneagram system — create curiosity about it.

### 3. Formative Background

Childhood, upbringing, key influences. How did their early life shape their personality? Use specific details — names, places, incidents. Include quotes from family or early collaborators when possible.

### 4. Rise to Fame / Career Arc

Their breakthrough and how their type drove their career choices. What Enneagram patterns show up in their professional decisions?

### 5. Personality Deep Dive (2–3 subsections)

Pick 2–3 specific quirks, habits, or patterns that reveal their type. Each gets its own H3. Use specific anecdotes and quotes as evidence.

### 6. Challenges & Growth

Controversies, struggles, public criticism. How did their type show up under stress? How have they grown? This is where the analysis gets real — don't shy away from the hard stuff, but handle it with respect.

### 7. Ending (NO Conclusion)

**Do NOT write a conclusion.** No summary. No CTA. No "what does this mean for you?" The blog ends on its sharpest analytical insight and then stops. Cut to black.

**The test**: Does the ending feel like a mic drop or like a flight attendant thanking you for flying? If it's the latter, cut everything after your strongest line.

**Good ending example**:

> "He has been doing this for 44 films and he is getting faster. At some point you have to wonder: is he running toward something, or has the running itself become the point?"

Then nothing. Done.

### Section Format: 1-3-1

Each section follows the 1-3-1 pattern:

1. **One punchy opening** — Strong statement or provocative question
2. **Three supporting details** — Examples, anecdotes, evidence
3. **One concluding insight** — Bridges to the next section

## Research Process

### Before You Write

1. **Watch/listen to 3+ long-form interviews** (30+ minutes each). Podcasts, YouTube, documentary clips. This is where the real personality comes through. Short press junkets are useless.
2. **Find 5 positive contributions** the person has made (concrete, specific, verifiable)
3. **Find 5 lesser-known facts** that most people don't know
4. **Identify their stressors, sources of pride, and habitual patterns**
5. **Collect 8–12 direct quotes** with attribution (these will be your evidence throughout)

### Sources to Prioritize

| Best Sources                                                                | Worst Sources                    |
| --------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Long-form podcast appearances (Rogan, Lex, Armchair Expert, Diary of a CEO) | Press junket sound bites         |
| Memoirs, essays, letters they've written                                    | Tabloid gossip                   |
| Documentary footage                                                         | Anonymous "sources say" articles |
| Collaborator testimonials                                                   | Wikipedia summaries              |
| Their own social media (candid posts, not PR)                               | Fan wikis                        |

## Formatting Requirements

- **Header hierarchy**: H2 → H3 → H4 (proper nesting)
- **Bold** the person's name and key terms
- **Paragraphs**: Under 700 characters each (mobile readability)
- **Bullet points** for scanning
- **Quotes**: Block-quoted with attribution
- **SEO title**: Under 60 characters, include person's name
- **Meta description**: Under 155 characters, include person's name + "Enneagram Type [X]"

## FAQ

**Do I need to be a certified Enneagram practitioner?**
No. You need to understand the system well enough to make a compelling case for someone's type using behavioral evidence. If you can explain why someone is a 6w5 vs a 5w6 using specific examples from their life, you're qualified.

**Can I write about someone not on your list?**
Yes. Pitch them. We love unexpected choices — the more interesting the personality, the better.

**How long does the process take?**
Most pieces take 1–2 weeks from pitch to publication. Research is the biggest time investment.

**Will you rewrite my draft?**
We'll edit for voice and brand consistency, but we won't rewrite your analysis. If the Enneagram reasoning is weak, we'll ask you to strengthen it. If the writing voice is off, we'll give specific notes.

**What if I disagree with a person's commonly-accepted type?**
Make your case. We love well-argued alternative typings. Just bring the evidence.
