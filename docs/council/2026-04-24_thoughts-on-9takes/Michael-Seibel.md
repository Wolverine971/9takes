# Michael Seibel — Council Response

## First Impressions

My first reaction is suspicion, and I want to be honest with you about that. You described the product for two paragraphs and then the third paragraph jumped to "pattern-recognition tool for human behavior" and "the emotional operating system that drives people." That is a vision pitch, not a product pitch, and I've read 17,000 applications where the vision paragraph was doing work the metrics couldn't do. The second thing I noticed: the give-first mechanic is actually interesting. I've seen maybe three consumer mechanics in the last decade that I thought were novel enough to matter, and forcing a contribution before a reveal is in that family (Whisper, BeReal, Reddit's old karma gate). So I'm split. The mechanic has something. The framing around it is fogging my view of whether the thing works.

## How I'd Reframe This

You asked me what I think of 9takes. That is not really the question. The question is: are you lying to yourself about whether this has product-market fit yet, and if the honest answer is no, what specific signal are you hunting for in the next 90 days that would tell you it does. The rest (Enneagram as a lens, the blog strategy, the coaching pillar) is noise until that core question gets answered. Because I've watched this exact shape of company a hundred times. Smart founder, interesting mechanic, genuinely thoughtful product theory, five surfaces (Q&A, blog, coaching, admin, email), and a year from now the founder is still building because building feels like progress and the PMF question never gets a clean answer. I'm not going to dodge your actual question with the reframe. But I want you to hear the reframe before I answer what you asked.

## The Face-Value Answer

Here is what I actually think of 9takes, on your terms.

The good: the give-first mechanic is the best thing you have, and I don't think you're selling it hard enough in your own description. It's buried in paragraph two. That mechanic is your wedge. It solves a real problem (lurker culture, one-hot-take-wins dynamics) with a mechanism that is cheap to implement and structurally defensible. I like it.

The risky: personality-system-as-platform is graveyard territory. MBTI made it work as a test-taking business, not a community. 16Personalities gets the traffic; nobody builds a durable social graph on top of a typology. Enneagram is a narrower, more niche, more contested framework than MBTI, which cuts both ways (smaller addressable audience, more engaged core). You are betting that the frame is a feature, not a wall.

The concerning: five surfaces for a solo or small-team founder is too many. Q&A + blog + coaching + admin + email sequences is the surface area of a Series A company. You are building like you already have product-market fit and are optimizing for monetization. I don't yet see evidence in your description that you do.

The honest bottom line: I think the mechanic is worth building around. I think the Enneagram frame is narrower than you believe it is and that's a problem for the "pattern-recognition tool for human behavior" vision. And I think you are building too many things at once. If you force-ranked the five surfaces and killed the bottom three for 90 days, I'd be more bullish on this conversation.

## My Framework Applied

The PMF lie detector. Let's run it.

The number one lie founders tell themselves is whether they have product-market fit. PMF is not "people use it." PMF is: if you shut it off tomorrow, a non-trivial cohort of users would be genuinely upset, would email you asking when it's back, and would come back the moment it's up. Sean Ellis phrased it as the "very disappointed" test. You can run that survey this week. You should run it this week. If less than 40% of active users say they'd be very disappointed if 9takes went away, you do not have PMF and the blog strategy and the coaching pillar are distractions from getting it.

Second framework: CEO cards. You have an extremely limited number. Each time you commit to a new surface (coaching, email sequences, admin tooling) you are spending one. You cannot get them back. Look at your five pillars and ask: which of these did I commit to because it was load-bearing for PMF, and which did I commit to because it felt like progress. The blog I can make a case for (SEO funnel into the core product). The coaching pillar I cannot make a case for at this stage. That's a CEO card spent badly.

Third framework, and this one is harder: the five lessons, specifically #5. It's rare you are forced to give up. It's more common that you convince yourself you've lost. The mirror of that lesson is also true: it's rare you are forced to succeed. It's more common that you convince yourself you already have. The Enneagram-as-platform vision is emotionally satisfying. It's the kind of vision a founder can live inside for three years without ever getting punched in the face by the market. Don't let the elegance of the idea protect you from the question of whether the market wants it.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

I'd sit with two questions, and I wouldn't let myself move until I had honest answers.

First: what is the single number that tells me if this is working, and what is that number right now. Not traffic. Not blog impressions. Not email signups. A number that reflects the core loop (give-first contribution, then read others' takes, then come back). Weekly returning contributors is probably it. If that number is flat or under 100, the rest of the surface area is theater.

Second: am I building a Q&A platform or am I building a content business dressed as a Q&A platform. Because those are different companies with different teams, different unit economics, and different endings. The description you wrote tilts toward platform. The five pillars tilt toward content business. One of those is lying and I'd want to know which one before I wrote another line of code.

The thought that would keep coming back: five surfaces is too many. Always too many. The founders I've seen succeed in consumer after year one have one surface, deeply loved, and they resisted every temptation to add a second until the first one was unkillable.

### What I'd feel

Honestly, I'd feel the pull of the vision. The "emotional operating system" framing is seductive and it would pull at me too if I were you. That's the thing to be careful of. When a vision feels this good to describe, it is often doing work that the product isn't yet doing. I'd feel suspicious of my own enthusiasm.

I'd also feel the defensiveness that comes up when someone questions the breadth. You'd want to tell me "no, the pillars feed each other, the blog drives Q&A, the coaching funnels from both." I've heard that sentence from a hundred founders. It is occasionally true and usually a post-hoc rationalization for having built too much. I'd notice the defensiveness and treat it as data.

### What I'd do

This week: run the "very disappointed" survey on your active users. Not signups. People who have contributed at least one take in the last 14 days. Post the number publicly to your own team (or to a trusted advisor) so you can't revise it later.

This week also: write down, in one sentence, what the core loop is. Then write down which of the five pillars directly strengthens that loop and which does not. Be honest. Coaching probably doesn't. Email sequences probably do (retention). Blog probably does (acquisition). Admin is infrastructure, not a pillar.

This month: pick two of the five surfaces to pause. Not kill, pause. Ninety days of zero investment. See what breaks and what doesn't. If coaching leads dry up and nothing else does, you learned coaching was load-bearing and you were wrong to pause it. If nothing breaks, you just got back a chunk of your CEO cards.

This month also: interview ten users who came back in the last 30 days. Not a survey. A call. Ask them what they'd do if 9takes went away tomorrow. Their answers (the exact phrases, the hesitation, the shrug) will tell you more than any analytics dashboard.

This year: if by month six you do not have a weekly returning contributors number that is growing month-over-month with no paid acquisition, I would seriously consider whether the Enneagram frame is a ceiling rather than a floor. That is a hard conversation to have with yourself. Have it anyway.

What I would not do: I would not start running ads, I would not hire, I would not add a sixth surface, and I would not let the blog-SEO success (if it comes) convince you that the platform is working. Content traffic is not platform PMF. Don't let one win paper over the question the other four pillars aren't answering.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

I am a systems reader. I read funnels, retention curves, PMF signals, and founder honesty. What I don't read well is the interior pull of a founder who is building something that is partly a product and partly a personal meaning-making project. 9takes sounds like it might be that kind of company for you. If the Enneagram is a thing you deeply love and the platform is, in part, your way of being in the world, then my "pause three pillars" advice is partially missing the point. I won't pretend to have authority on the question of when a founder should honor the shape of their own obsession versus when they should subordinate it to the market. That's a different lane.

I also don't know enough about the specific shape of Enneagram communities online (forums, Discords, Reddit) to tell you whether the existing audience is big enough, engaged enough, and underserved enough to support a platform, or whether it's a passionate-but-ceilinged niche. That's a consumer-behavior research question and you should answer it with actual data, not my intuition.

And the axis I am least qualified to weigh in on: whether the deep emotional framing of the product (the "pattern-recognition tool for human behavior") is actually the strongest positioning, or whether a sharper, narrower utility framing (for example, team dynamics, or relationship debugging, or hiring) would convert better. I default to function over meaning. If the answer here is actually about meaning, you need a different kind of read than mine.

One more thing I might be wrong about: I've been harder on the five-pillars sprawl than might be warranted if you have more infrastructure and leverage than a typical solo founder. If you have a real team, or if these surfaces are mostly automated, some of my CEO-cards concern softens. I'm reading from the description and projecting a standard early-stage shape onto it. Correct me if I'm off.

The care underneath all of this: I think the mechanic is good. I think you're smart. I want you to not spend the next two years in the comfortable middle zone where the thing is working a little and growing a little and never gets punched in the face by the market hard enough to force the pivot or the focus. That middle zone is where most founders die. Get the honest PMF answer. Then commit.
