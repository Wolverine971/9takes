# Neil Strauss — Council Response

## First Impressions

DJ, the first thing I feel reading your pitch is recognition. You are building a machine that converts the private shame of "why is my partner/boss/kid unreachable?" into legible pattern, and you are using the exact frame (nine ways of seeing) that I would have killed for at twenty-two when I was trying to figure out why I could not walk up to a woman in a bar. Second thing I feel is suspicion, because the give-first mechanic is the part that makes or breaks this, and it lives or dies on a social pressure most consumer internet products have spent fifteen years sanding off. Third thing: the word "decode" in your description. I have spent a career watching what happens to men who think they've decoded people. It's a good word. It's also a dangerous one. I want to talk about that before we're done.

## How I'd Reframe This

You asked what I think of 9takes. That's not really the question underneath. The question underneath is: _am I building a pattern-recognition tool, or am I building another seduction community?_ Both start in the same place. A platform that says _here is the hidden emotional grammar of the person across from you, and if you learn it, you will never be surprised again._ _The Game_ pitched itself that way. Rick Rubin's work pitches itself against that. The difference between the two is whether the frame makes the user more permeable to other people or more armored against them. That is the fork in the road for 9takes, and I don't think it is decided yet by the product. It is decided by the voice, the community norms, and what you let the power users do.

That's the real question. But I'll answer the literal one first.

## The Face-Value Answer

I like it. Genuinely. I think the give-first mechanic is the single best product decision in the description and I would build the whole thing around defending it. Forums die because ninety percent of users take without giving and the givers burn out. You've written a rule that makes the taker do the work of a giver before they get the reward. That is a small, sharp, honest piece of product design, and it also happens to be therapy 101. You cannot hear someone else's answer until you've said your own out loud. The Meadows does this in group every morning.

The Enneagram as the spine is a harder call. I'm going to say yes, but with a caveat. Yes, because the system is old enough and weird enough that it has the one thing pop personality frameworks lack, which is an account of _why_ people are the way they are (the fear, the childhood wound, the compensating strategy). Most people come to personality content wanting the label. What they need is the mechanism. The Enneagram, unlike Myers-Briggs, actually offers the mechanism. You picked the right frame. Caveat: the Enneagram community at large is contaminated with woo. You will either have to out-write the woo on your own platform or get swallowed by it. That is not a small job.

The blog strategy (celebrity analyses, Enneagram Corner, guides) is the right moat. SEO-driven content plus an anonymous community plus coaching is a three-legged stool and each leg feeds the others. I have seen this shape work. I have also seen it collapse when one leg gets twice as big as the other two. Keep an eye on which leg is carrying the weight.

Coaching I'd be careful with. I have opinions there. Saving them for the next section.

## My Framework Applied

Two frameworks from me, and they point in opposite directions, which is the honest answer.

First, the **alter-ego test**. I built Style because the Four underneath could not walk up to a woman. Style was useful for about three years and then Style was a prison. The question for 9takes: is the Enneagram type, as you present it to a user, going to be a bridge or a cage? "I'm a Type 4, so I do X" can be the beginning of self-knowledge, or it can be the next cell the person moves into after they grew tired of the last one. The nine types are alter egos with a theology attached. The platform will attract people who desperately want a name for what is wrong with them. Some of them will use the name to grow. Some of them will use it the way I used Style, which is as a cover for not doing the actual interior work. Your product copy, your coaches, your top community voices have to keep pointing past the type, or the type becomes Style. The give-first mechanic is already a partial answer to this. It forces the user to speak from inside their own experience before they get the label's permission to speak. Keep doubling down on that direction.

Second, **immersion without conversion**. This is the journalistic discipline I lost inside the pickup community and recovered by _The Creative Act_. When you move inside a world to understand it, you can either stay a journalist or become a convert. Both have a cost. The converts get the depth; the journalists keep the perspective. You're building a tool that asks users to do immersion-without-conversion on each other. To read the Type 8 challenger's take, sit inside it, understand its logic, and walk back out without becoming one. That is a rare and expensive cognitive skill. Most internet "empathy" products are actually perspective-replacement products. They tell the user _here is how to see it their way._ That becomes identification, not understanding. The 9takes promise, if you hold it honestly, is different. _Here are nine interior logics. None of them are yours. All of them are real._ That is a more demanding ask than the current content landscape is delivering, and it's also the thing I would personally pay for. Hold that line.

One more thing. The confessional grammar. Everything I've ever written that worked was first draft for me, second for the reader, third for the hater. Watch what happens on 9takes when a user writes their "give" before reading the others. That first post is the first draft. It is raw and probably bad and it is the only one that is actually theirs. The moment they see the other eight takes, the second-draft brain turns on and they start writing toward the room. That is normal. That is also the moment you could build the most interesting product decision of the platform: show the user their own first post next to the others, and let them notice how much their second thought was shaped by the eight interior logics they were supposed to be learning from, not converting to. I don't know what that feature looks like exactly. But there is something there.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

I'd be asking myself three things. One: do my highest-engagement users over twelve months become more permeable or more armored? Do they report their relationships getting better, or do they report having finally figured out that their mother is a Two and therefore the problem? If it's the second one, you built a weapon, not a tool. I built a weapon once. Watch for this with a hawk's attention.

Two: what's the defensible thing here? The blog SEO is a moat but a slow one. The give-first mechanic is novel but a competitor could copy it in a weekend. What the competitors cannot copy is the voice of the analyses and the community norms. Those are a craft output, not a feature. Which means the company's long-term defensibility runs through your writing and your editorial taste, which means the thing you need most is not more engineers. It's a managing editor who can protect the voice when you aren't in the room. I don't know if you have that person. If you don't, it's the next hire.

Three: is the coaching pillar helping the community or draining it? Coaching, waitlist funnels, one-on-one sessions. I have been on the platform side of this. Every time I've seen a content community hook up a paid coaching pipe, the content started bending toward the sale. Subtle at first, then obvious. The community threads start feeling like an on-ramp. The blog starts feeling like lead gen. I would ask, honestly, whether coaching belongs under the same roof as the Q&A community or whether it wants its own house with its own door.

### What I'd feel

Honestly? Envy, a little. The give-first mechanic is the kind of small elegant product rule I wish I had thought of. I'd also feel a low, persistent dread about the community at scale. Every platform that promises to decode other people ends up with a subpopulation that uses the decoding to avoid being seen themselves. I watched this happen in real time on PUA forums. The guys who became the best "readers" of women were, almost without exception, the least legible to themselves. That is a specific danger I would feel in my chest reading the 9takes pitch, because the product is doing something so right at the surface (give before you take) and the failure mode is so specific underneath (type-as-armor). I'd feel protective of the users who are going to show up alone on a Tuesday night looking for a name for what is wrong with them.

I would not feel any confidence that I know the business answer. I never have.

### What I'd do

This week: write the one-page document that says what 9takes will refuse to become. Not the mission. The anti-mission. The list of adjacent shapes the product could drift into and which ones are off-limits. A red-line document. "We will not sell Enneagram typing as a relationship-debugging tool for fixing your partner." "We will not let coaches prospect in community threads." "We will not let a single type become the platform's mascot." Whatever your lines are, write them down while you are still small enough that the lines are cheap. I did not write mine in 2005 and the cost showed up in 2014 when Julien Blanc was in my inbox asking for a blurb.

This month: do the voice audit. Read fifty celebrity analyses and fifty community threads. Mark the ones that use the type as a bridge into the person and the ones that use the type as a replacement for the person. Figure out which pattern is trending. If the replacement pattern is winning, the platform is sliding toward armor. Intervene on editorial norms now, not at ten times the traffic.

This year: find the managing editor I mentioned. Real hire. Pay them. The voice of 9takes is the moat.

Further out: get one serious longitudinal read on whether your power users' relationships are better or worse after a year on the platform. Survey, interview, whatever. This is the question almost no personality-content company asks, because the answer is scary. Ask it anyway. If the users are growing, you have the real thing. If they are labeling, you have a smarter horoscope, and that is also a real business but you should know which one you're running.

What I would _not_ do: I would not add a lot of features. The product as described is already at the right density. The give-first mechanic plus nine takes plus SEO content plus coaching is four leg-pieces, and I have watched founders with good ideas kill them by adding five more. Resist the urge to ship a fifth pillar before the four you have are singing.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

My lens is interior architecture. The self, the wound, the persona, the way language can set the wound down or wrap it up tighter. That lens is useful for a third of what you're asking and useless for the other two-thirds. I have no good instincts for consumer-product growth, pricing, unit economics, defensibility at the marketing layer, or whether the Enneagram as a brand will still be attractive to new users three years from now. I wrote a book that sold two and a half million copies and I still could not have told you, in advance, why it worked. Anyone who sounds confident about what will work for 9takes at scale either runs a company that solved exactly this problem or is performing for you.

I also have a specific distortion worth naming. I will reach for the confessional mode. I will tell you that the interior work is the real work and the product is the scaffolding. For you, the founder, that might be wrong. You might need to stop excavating and ship. I have been on the wrong side of that trade my entire adult life. _The Truth_ was five hundred pages of excavation and the marriage ended three years later. Naming things does not fix them. If someone in your life is telling you to stop thinking about the deeper architecture and just run the business harder, they might be more right than I am on any given Tuesday.

And the axis I can't read at all: whether the Enneagram framework itself is durable or whether it's a 2020s personality moment that will age like Myers-Briggs aged. I don't know. A Rubin-type would tell you to build for what is true rather than for what is trending, and I think he'd be right, but I cannot tell you from the outside whether the Enneagram is true in the way that survives a decade. Someone whose lens is systems and scale and market timing would answer that better than I can.

One last thing. Watch yourself. You're building a tool for decoding emotional logic in other people. Make sure that the founder, five years in, has not become the man who decodes everyone and is read by no one. I have met him. He is not having a good life.
