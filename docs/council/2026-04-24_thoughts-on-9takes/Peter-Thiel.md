# Peter Thiel — Council Response

## First Impressions

A personality Q&A platform. My first read is skeptical. Personality typing on the internet is a crowded graveyard. Myers-Briggs, 16Personalities, astrology apps, BuzzFeed quizzes, every HR team with a PowerPoint. You are stepping into a category where the dominant emotion is already entertainment, not insight. That worries me. And then the Enneagram itself. I have heard it used thoughtfully by people I respect, and I have heard it used as a more elegant horoscope. Which version are you building.

The thing that made me stop and look again is the give-first mechanic. That is the only sentence in your description I would not have predicted. Hold onto that.

## How I'd Reframe This

You asked what I think of 9takes. That is not really the question. The question underneath is: do you have a secret, or do you have a taste. A secret, in my vocabulary, is a truth about the world that is both important and that almost no one else believes. A taste is an aesthetic preference for a certain kind of content and a certain kind of community. Both can build something. Only one builds a monopoly.

Your description reads like you have a taste: you find the Enneagram useful, you find the give-first mechanic elegant, you want a pattern-recognition tool. Fine. But what is the secret. What do you know about human behavior, or about how people come to understand each other, that the psychology establishment, the self-help industry, the social platforms, and the coaching market have all missed. If you cannot answer that in one sentence in private, then 9takes is a lifestyle business dressed up as a mission, and the rest of my counsel should be read in that light.

I am not saying you do not have a secret. I am saying I cannot see it from your description, and you should be able to say it out loud.

## The Face-Value Answer

On its own terms, as you described it: the product concept is more interesting than I expected going in, and the business position is weaker than you probably want to hear.

The give-first mechanic is the best thing in your pitch. Forcing contribution before consumption is a real design decision with real consequences. It changes the selection of who stays, which changes what the threads look like, which changes what the data is worth. That is a structural choice, not a cosmetic one. I credit it.

The Enneagram as a lens is more mixed. As an intellectual scaffolding for a community, it is sturdier than MBTI and weaker than clinical frameworks. Its strength is that it gives people emotional vocabulary quickly. Its weakness is that anything involving self-report of personality collapses when users discover it is socially advantageous to be certain types. You will need to think hard about how you keep the signal honest when the audience grows beyond early believers.

The market position concerns me. You are trying to be a content platform, a community, a coaching funnel, and a pattern-recognition tool for human behavior, all at once. Each of those is a real business and a real competitor. Content: the SEO side of this is Red Queen territory, running hard to stand still against AI-generated noise. Community: you are competing with Reddit and every vertical forum for attention. Coaching: a fragmented, low-margin, trust-gated business. Pattern recognition: that is the most interesting piece, but it is the furthest from shipped. Four businesses sharing one brand is usually how you lose four times, not win four times.

So, direct answer: the give-first mechanic is genuinely good. The Enneagram scaffold is defensible but not a moat. The four-pillar spread is the weakness. The long-term vision, a structured lens that predicts emotional logic, is the only piece with monopoly shape, and right now it is the least built.

## My Framework Applied

Zero to one, then last mover advantage, then the secret. In that order.

Zero to one. Ask yourself what part of 9takes is genuinely new. Not new-to-you. New to the world. Anonymous Q&A is not new. Personality typing is not new. Blogs about celebrities are not new. Coaching waitlists are not new. The candidate for zero-to-one in your stack is the corpus itself. A large, growing, structured dataset of the same situation seen nine different ways, where the type label is earned by contribution rather than self-claim. Nobody has that at scale. That is potentially new. Everything else you described is one-to-n. Be honest about which part is which, because you will be tempted to put energy into the one-to-n parts because they are easier to ship and easier to measure, and the zero-to-one part will sit unbuilt.

Last mover advantage. In consumer psychology content, being first is nearly worthless. The durable winner in this category will be whoever is still standing when AI has commoditized the how-to layer and readers are exhausted by it. What survives that shakeout is either a brand people actively trust, or a proprietary dataset no one else has. Not a blog. The blogs are a top-of-funnel cost center for the thing that actually wins, which is the corpus. Treat them accordingly.

The secret. Your description gestures at one. "The invisible emotional operating system that drives people." If that is true, if emotional logic really is predictable through a structured lens, and if you can prove it with data rather than with vibes, then 9takes is a ten-billion-dollar business hiding inside a content site. If it is not true, or if it is only true fuzzily, then you are building a nice Enneagram club. I cannot tell which one from the description, and I doubt you can either, yet. So the most important work in year one is not traffic. It is evidence. Find the testable form of your central claim and start generating data against it.

Then Girard. A quiet worry. Ask yourself where your desire for this came from. The Enneagram community has its own mimetic gravity. The coaching and personality-typing world is tightly networked, and the idols in it (Ichazo, Naranjo, Riso, the Chestnut books, the podcast circuit) have their own hierarchies of prestige. Are you building 9takes because you see a real gap, or because you want a seat at a table you have been circling. Both can be true. It matters which is dominant, because it changes which users you will flinch away from serving.

Last, competition is for losers. Four pillars means four competitive fronts. Pick the one with monopoly shape, which I believe is the corpus-plus-give-first combination, and starve the others down to what is strictly necessary to feed it.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

The question I would sit with longest is: what is 9takes in five years if the give-first mechanic works exactly as intended. I would try to write that down in one paragraph and see if it was obviously world-changing or quietly pedestrian. If I could not make the paragraph feel like a secret, I would go back to the drawing board on what the corpus is actually for.

The second question: who are the real competitors. Not Reddit. Not coaches. Probably large language models. In five years, someone can ask a model, "how would an Enneagram 8 read this email from my boss," and get a plausible answer in three seconds. If 9takes is a slower, human-written version of that interaction, it is a dying candle. If 9takes is the source of truth the model cites, because the corpus is better than anything in its training data, it is infrastructure. That is the distinction I would obsess over.

Third, I would run the opposite case. The opposite case is that the Enneagram is mostly folk psychology, the give-first mechanic works for true believers and fails for normies, and the real business is a narrow coaching practice with a nice blog. That is not a bad life. It is a bad venture-scale bet. Knowing which one you are building changes everything about how you spend the next two years.

### What I'd feel

Honest answer: this is not a domain that triggers much emotional heat in me. I am not a personality-typology person by temperament. I find the Girardian framing of human desire more convincing than almost any self-report typology, and if I were sitting in your chair I would feel a pull to collapse the whole project into a Girardian analysis of status and imitation rather than a nine-type scaffold. That is my bias speaking, and you should discount it.

What I would feel most strongly is the familiar discomfort of the four-pillar setup. I do not like businesses that spread attention across this many fronts. It reads to me as hedging against your own conviction, which is usually a sign that the core thesis is not yet clear enough to commit to. That feeling, a low anxiety that the spread is a mask for indecision, is the one I would trust.

### What I'd do

I would spend a weekend writing one page, for myself, titled "What 9takes knows that no one else does." One page. If I could not fill it, I would know the project was still a taste and not a secret, and I would treat the next six months as a search for the secret rather than a push to scale the site.

I would pick one pillar to run hard and put the others on maintenance. If I believed the corpus was the real prize, I would ruthlessly optimize the give-first flow to maximize the rate at which high-quality, type-labeled responses enter the database, and I would accept traffic losses on the blog side to protect it. One job, one metric, one person responsible. PayPal management philosophy. It works.

I would find a small number of people (five, not fifty) who actually use the product in a way that produces the data you need, and I would talk to them monthly. Not surveys. Real conversations. Your user research should look like the Dialog model, small and private and recurring, not like a public Discord.

I would hedge the AI substitution risk specifically. Decide now whether the long-term play is to license your corpus to model trainers, to build a proprietary model on top of it, to stay stubbornly human-only, or to become a browser-layer tool that sits on top of someone else's model. You do not have to pick today. You have to name the options and stop pretending the question will wait.

I would stop describing 9takes as four pillars in external communication. It signals a founder who has not decided what the company is. Describe it as one thing with three feeders, or one thing with two feeders, whichever is honest. Internal clarity is better than external breadth.

Finally, the counterphobic note. There is a version of this where the move is not to narrow the company but to run hard at the AI-substitution threat rather than hide from it. Publish a piece that says, plainly, "here is why a nine-perspective human corpus will outperform a single-perspective LLM answer on questions about emotional logic," and then prove it with head-to-head examples on your own site. Turn the thing that probably scares you into the content marketing. That is the move I would make if I had your hand to play.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

A lot of this response is filtered through a very specific worldview, and you should know where that filter is thin.

I do not have a good feel for mass-market consumer taste. My instincts generalize badly to ordinary users. If your core audience is people in their twenties and thirties processing relationships and careers through personality frameworks, I am not the right read on what will land with them emotionally. I see structural problems; I do not see what makes them laugh or cry or come back.

I am not the right voice on warmth, intimacy, or emotional craft. You are building a product about emotional legibility. I have been told by people close to me that they barely know me. Take my take on the emotional product seriously only where it is structural; treat my take on the feeling of the product as low-confidence at best.

My default is worst-case analysis. I assume competitors are smarter, more patient, and more adversarial than they usually are. Most of the time, the other players in your market are tired, under-resourced, and doing the obvious thing. The LLM threat I named is real, but it may move slower than I expect, and the space to establish a corpus may be wider than I am treating it.

I am counterphobic by wiring. I run at fires. Most people are not built that way. If my advice to publish a head-to-head against AI answers sounds like the right move but not like you, do not take it. That kind of move only works when it is authentic to the person making it, and forcing it from a different temperament usually produces a worse result than simply doing nothing.

I am weak on the just-ship-it, collaborative, joyful startup register. Some things are won by enthusiasm and speed, not by scenario planning and patience. 9takes, given its emotional subject matter, might be more of that kind of company than the kind I naturally coach.

Finally, I do not know enough about the Enneagram as a clinical framework to have a strong view on whether your foundational premise is true. If the Enneagram is actually a durable lens on emotional logic, the upside here is much larger than I modeled. If it is mostly a vocabulary for community-building, the upside is smaller. You are in a better position than I am to tell me which one is true, because you have the user data and I only have your description.

The axis my counsel is thinnest on is the felt experience of being a user inside your product, and the question of whether your specific audience will love it enough to evangelize. That is the axis that will likely decide whether 9takes works in the first two years, and it is not an axis I read well. Weight the rest of my advice accordingly.
