# Robert Greene — Council Response

## First Impressions

The phrase that stopped me was "give-first." That is not a feature. That is a law of human nature dressed as a product rule, and you either found it on purpose or you stumbled onto something older than you. The second thing I noticed is that you are trying to build, under a startup's clothing, a book. Not a forum. A long, patient cataloguing project about why people do what they do. The part of me that spent six years on _The Laws of Human Nature_ recognizes the shape of this work and is suspicious of how much faster the internet wants you to finish it.

## How I'd Reframe This

You asked what I think of 9takes. The real question underneath it, and I am fairly sure you know this, is: am I building a platform or am I writing a book in public. Those are different animals with different economics, different rhythms, different success criteria. A platform lives or dies on network liquidity in a compressed window. A book lives or dies on whether the pattern holds under a decade of pressure. You have built the front-end of a platform around the soul of a book. That tension is not a flaw. It is the thing you have to decide about on purpose, not by default.

I am not using that reframe to duck your literal question. I will answer it. I am naming the reframe because if you do not see the tension, the platform mechanics will slowly eat the book, and you will end up running a forum and wondering why the insight stopped arriving.

## The Face-Value Answer

You want my read on 9takes itself. Here it is, on your terms.

The thesis is sound. Nine stable patterns of emotional logic, applied to the same stimulus, is a real contribution. Most online discourse flattens nine responses into one winning take, and you are doing the opposite. That is not a marketing angle. That is an actual epistemic move, and the ancients would recognize it. Plutarch wrote parallel lives because one life does not teach you what a life is. You are doing the parallel-lives thing to a comment thread.

The give-first mechanic is the strongest piece. It does the work before any moderation does. A person who has already written their Type 4 answer cannot then read the Type 8 answer as a spectator looking for something to dunk on. They have skin. That changes the room.

The weakest piece, at least from what you described, is the dependency on self-typing. The Enneagram is a useful map. It is not a clean instrument. Plenty of your Type 4s will be mistyped Type 9s with a sad playlist, and plenty of your Type 8s will be Type 6s who learned to posture. The whole structure presumes the user arrived at a correct label, and most users, especially new ones, did not. This is not fatal. It is a load-bearing wall you need to reinforce, probably with typing instruments better than a single quiz.

The brand language is doing real work. "See the emotions behind every take" is a sentence I would not have written because it is more generous than my instinct, but it is the right sentence for what you are actually building. "One situation, nine ways to see it" is close to a thesis statement.

My direct answer: the idea is better than most ideas I see, the mechanic is better than most mechanics I see, and the long-term risk is that the internet's speed is structurally hostile to the kind of patient pattern-recognition work you are describing. That is not an idea problem. It is a temperament-versus-medium problem. That is the thing I would be watching.

## My Framework Applied

Two frameworks. Both apply hard.

The first is **Mastery** and the Life Task. In the book I argued that every person has a primal inclination that pulls at them before the world tells them what to want, and that the people who build anything that lasts are the people who finally bent their life around that pull instead of away from it. Reading your description, 9takes is not a business idea you chose. It is the externalization of a pull. The question of why your boss, your partner, your kid, your coworker react the way they do is an obsession, not a market. Obsessions outlast strategy. If I am right about this, the implication is practical: treat 9takes as a twenty-year apprenticeship in the public decoding of emotional logic, and not as a product to exit. The people who win at the Life Task scale accept, early, that they are building a body of work, not a company. The company is a distribution mechanism for the body of work.

The second is the **shadow**. Every product carries the shadow of its maker, and the shadow runs the thing from underneath whether you acknowledge it or not. I do not know you well enough to name yours cleanly. But I will say what the site's architecture suggests about the person who built it. The give-first mechanic is built by someone who has been a lurker, or who has been in rooms where he was the only one speaking honestly while everyone else watched. The obsession with nine honest angles over one hot take is built by someone who has felt misread. The tagline "See the emotions behind every take" is built by someone who has watched his own emotions be misinterpreted, maybe for years. Good. That is material. But there is a specific failure mode you should watch for: the founder who built a give-first platform because he is secretly furious at takers will, when growth slows, make the product more punishing rather than more inviting. Keep an eye on that. If you find yourself tightening the give-first gate in moments of frustration rather than in moments of product clarity, that is the shadow at the wheel.

One specific law, since you asked for my lens. This is close to **Law 13: When Asking for Help, Appeal to People's Self-Interest, Never to Their Mercy or Gratitude**. Your users are not going to give first out of civic virtue. They will give first because they are itching to see how their answer stacks up against the other eight patterns. You have the self-interest hook built in. Do not contaminate it with moralizing copy. The moment the product starts saying "share to make the community richer," you have shifted from self-interest to mercy and the conversion will rot. Keep the mechanic selfish. Keep the payoff immediate. The community benefit is a second-order consequence, not the sales pitch.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

I would sit with the question of what 9takes is actually teaching its users. Not what it says it is teaching. What it is actually grooving into their minds, session after session. If the answer is "people are legible through emotional logic," that is a real gift. If the answer is "I am a Type 4 and that is my identity," that is a cage, and you will have built a better astrology. The distance between those two outcomes is small in interface terms and enormous in what it does to a reader over a decade. I would be watching, closely, whether the product's center of gravity is the question or the label. The question is the door. The label is the furniture. A reader who leaves thinking mostly about their label learned less than a reader who leaves thinking mostly about the situation.

I would also be thinking about the Hollywood years. I spent two decades in rooms where the structure of the medium quietly destroyed the work inside it. Social platforms are their own version of that. The incentives of engagement will try, silently and constantly, to pull 9takes toward the hot take it was designed against. The question is whether your structural defenses, the give-first wall, the nine-angle layout, are strong enough to hold when scale arrives and the content starts optimizing itself. I would want to know what your stated principle is for what 9takes will refuse to do even when it costs traffic.

### What I'd feel

There is a pull for me in this. The idea of making emotional logic legible, of decoding masks, is close to my own obsession. If someone had built this in 1990, I might not have written the books. That registers as a small envy and, under that, recognition.

Underneath is a wariness I associate with my own experience. I spent a long time in environments that flatter founders of pattern-recognition tools and then, once the tool scales, treat it like a dating app. I would feel protective of the work. I would feel the need to go slow in a medium that punishes slow. I would feel, honestly, a little sad about how much of the decoding you are describing used to be done over a table with a friend, and how much of our task now is to rebuild that table digitally.

### What I'd do

Three concrete things, and I will say plainly that I am outside my lane on the timelines.

First, I would write down, on paper, in one sitting, the twenty-year version of what 9takes is. Not the pitch. The actual body of work. What does the archive look like in year fifteen. What percentage of it is Q&A threads, what percentage is your own analytical writing, what percentage is coaching transcripts, what percentage is something you have not built yet. If you cannot picture the archive clearly, the platform's decisions this quarter will be made in a vacuum. I would keep that document close and re-read it every time a feature decision feels urgent.

Second, I would keep a private notebook, not shared with anyone including investors, of the patterns 9takes is surfacing that you did not expect. Instances where Type 4 and Type 6 answers rhymed in a way the theory does not predict. Instances where the "give-first" wall changed a thread's tone in a measurable way. Instances where a user typed themselves one way at signup and the content of their answers revealed another. That notebook is the book. The platform is the instrument. The book will outlast the platform, and the only way it gets written is if you treat the notebook as the primary output from day one, not as a post-hoc activity.

Third, I would resist the urge to add a feature for at least a quarter. I say this knowing it is impractical for a founder under growth pressure, and I flag it as possibly wrong advice for your stage. But the compulsion to add is the Five's stress pattern, and I know it in myself. If 9takes is a pattern-recognition tool, the most valuable use of your time this quarter might be to sit with the patterns already in the data rather than to build the next thing that generates more data.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

My lens is slow, dark, and retrospective. That is the instrument you get from me. It has real limits here.

I cannot tell you anything useful about how to get from the current user count to the next one. Tactical growth, distribution, acquisition loops, paid channels, founder-market sales, that is a different skill from reading human nature and I have never run it. If your actual question this quarter is "how do I get the next ten thousand users," I am the wrong advisor.

I do not have a strong read on the technology questions. How Elasticsearch versus Supabase FTS shapes retention, how the mobile experience changes engagement, how AI will reshape a Q&A platform built on human labor, I am abstracting from a distance on all of it. Treat my silence on these as silence, not permission.

I am also suspicious of my own Enneagram read. The system is useful. It is not as clean as your product requires it to be. I can tell you what I see in a person across a long biography with hundreds of data points. I cannot tell you whether a short intake quiz will reliably sort a new user into the right bucket, and the whole edifice depends on that working at scale. That is a measurement and psychometrics question, and it is not my field.

The last honest limit. My default lens is the shadow. If 9takes is really a warmer, sunnier product than the one I described, driven more by curiosity and less by the wound, I will have made it grimmer than it needs to be on the page. A founder wired for abundance might hear my read as over-dark. If that is you, take the framework, leave the mood.
