# Tim Ferriss — Council Response

## First Impressions

My first reaction is cautious respect. You have a thesis and a mechanism, which puts you ahead of 90% of the pitches that cross my desk. The Enneagram piece makes me flinch a little, because I have watched a lot of smart founders bet their company on typology systems that outside of the Big Five don't have great predictive validity in peer-reviewed literature. But the give-first mechanic is the part that made me sit up. That is a real product insight, not a content insight, and it has almost nothing to do with the Enneagram. That is the thing I would have underlined in my notebook.

## How I'd Reframe This

You asked for my thoughts on 9takes. That is not really the question. The question underneath is: "Which of the three things I have stitched together is the actual business, and which two are scaffolding?" Because what you described is three companies wearing one trench coat. There is a give-first anonymous Q&A product. There is a taxonomy bet on the Enneagram. There is an SEO content farm feeding a coaching funnel. Each of those can be a real business. Trying to be all three at the stage you are at is how founders end up three years in with a blog, a waitlist, and a dashboard that nobody looks at. The reframe is not "drop two of them." The reframe is "which one compounds, and which two are serving the one that compounds?" You need to be able to say that sentence out loud.

## The Face-Value Answer

On its own terms, here is what I think. The tagline is good. "See the emotions behind every take. One situation, nine ways to see it." That is a clean promise, and it would survive a podcast intro. The give-first mechanic is the strongest product idea in the whole description and the one I would bet on. It is a structural fix to the lurker problem that Reddit, Quora, and Stack Overflow have all bled on for two decades. If you got that one mechanic right, you could drop the Enneagram entirely and still have a business.

The Enneagram as the organizing lens is where I get uncertain. It is a sticky, shareable system, and the audience that cares about it is hungry and underserved. But staking your entire epistemology on a typology that most academic psychologists do not consider predictive is a moat and a liability at the same time. It is a moat because the believers are loyal. It is a liability because as you scale, sophisticated users will ask why they should trust it, and "it predicts emotional logic" is a claim you cannot back with the kind of data Jon Haidt or Lisa Feldman Barrett would accept. I would want you to know that trade-off cold and have a deliberate answer, not a defensive one.

The vision paragraph ("decode why your boss/partner/kid reacts the way they do") is strong. The coaching pillar I am quieter on. I have watched a lot of content-to-coaching funnels, and the economics are brutal unless the coach is you and you are willing to be the face. If this is a DJ-fronted business, the coaching can work. If you are trying to build a marketplace of coaches, that is a different and much harder company. So: bullish on the mechanic, curious-skeptical on the taxonomy, cautious on the coaching pillar's weight. That is my honest read.

## My Framework Applied

I want to run fear-setting on this, because the question you are really sitting with is bigger than "what do you think of it." Fear-setting has five columns: define the nightmare, prevent it, repair it, the benefits of the attempt, and the cost of inaction. I am going to do the abbreviated version out loud.

**Define.** The worst-case for 9takes is not that it fails. The worst-case is that it succeeds slowly enough and broadly enough that you spend seven more years building it, draw a modest income, write a few hundred blog posts, have a coaching waitlist that never quite converts, and at year ten you look up and realize you built a content SEO play with a typology skin, not the pattern-recognition platform you described.

**Prevent.** The specific thing that prevents that outcome is picking, in writing, before Q3, which of the three pillars is the flywheel. Q&A product, taxonomy, or content plus coaching. Write the sentence. Stop calling it all one thing in your head. I would also name a kill criterion for the Enneagram bet. If, say, eighteen months from now the give-first mechanic is pulling weight but the Type-tagging isn't affecting retention or shareability in a measurable way, you drop the Enneagram as the core frame and keep it as flavor.

**Repair.** If the nightmare happens, you repair it by recognizing that the asset you actually built was the corpus of personality-tagged Q&A data. That corpus has value to researchers, LLM training, and B2B licensing even if the consumer product never hockey-sticks. Build knowing that.

**Benefit of the attempt.** You have a shot at being the emotional grammar layer for the internet. That is a real prize. Most founders do not get to aim at something that interesting.

**Cost of inaction.** If you don't resolve the three-pillar ambiguity, the cost is not that you fail fast. The cost is that you succeed at the wrong speed, which is the trap that eats more founders than outright failure does.

Second framework, briefly: **minimum effective dose.** You have told me about celebrity analyses, Enneagram Corner, how-to guides, coaching, community threads, and a Q&A product. What is the smallest version of 9takes that still delivers the core promise of the tagline? I would bet it is the Q&A product with a lightweight Type label on each response and nothing else. Everything else is accretion. Ask yourself which parts of the current surface area you added because they were load-bearing, and which parts you added because they were something to do.

Third: **"what would this look like if it were easy?"** If 9takes were easy, the give-first mechanic would be so satisfying that the Enneagram would be an onboarding quiz, not a worldview. The coaching would be DJ on camera, one call a week, premium pricing, capped slots. The blog would be three deep posts a month, not forty shallow ones. Check whether your operating mode is effortful because the work is genuinely hard, or because you are, at some level, metabolizing anxiety by producing.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

The question I would not be able to stop running is: am I building a product or am I building an audience? Those are both viable paths and they require opposite disciplines. If it is a product, then the give-first mechanic, the data model, the retention loops, and the network effects are the whole game, and the blog is a distraction. If it is an audience, then DJ Wayne is the brand, the content is the asset, the coaching is the monetization, and the product is a funnel. I would keep circling that until I had an answer I could defend to a skeptical investor in three sentences.

I would also think a lot about the defensibility of the Enneagram frame. Not whether it is true. Whether it is durable. If in five years a better typology shows up (and I personally suspect something post-Enneagram is coming), does 9takes pivot gracefully, or is the entire brand architecture stranded? I would want a version of the thesis that survives the specific system being replaced. The promise is "one situation, nine ways to see it." The nine is load-bearing for the name. That is a real commitment. I'd sit with whether I am willing to marry it.

And I would think about the wedge. Every durable platform I have seen started absurdly narrow. Facebook was Harvard. Yelp was San Francisco restaurants. Stack Overflow was programming questions. "Anonymous personality-based Q&A for everyone about everything" is the opposite of a wedge. What is the single category or demographic where give-first plus Enneagram tagging is so obviously better than Reddit that the first five thousand users come without you having to argue for them?

### What I'd feel

Honestly, this one would make me anxious. Not because it is a bad idea. Because the shape of it is the shape of every project I have ever over-engineered: big vision, multiple surface areas, a taxonomy that promises more than it can deliver, and a pull toward building more of everything to avoid the discomfort of picking one thing. I would feel the pull to optimize every pillar in parallel. I would resist that pull and find that resisting it is unpleasant. I would also feel a quiet competitive fondness for you, because the instinct to build a pattern-recognition tool for human emotion is the same instinct that drove me into psychedelics funding, and I know what it is to be pulled by it.

### What I'd do

This week, I would write the one-sentence answer to "which pillar is the flywheel." Literally write it. Put it somewhere you see every morning. If you can't write it yet, that is the thing to solve before you ship anything else.

This month, I would run a kill-criteria exercise for each of the three pillars. For each one, in advance, write down what evidence would make you shut it down or deprioritize it. Not "it's not working." Specific metrics with dates. Blog: what monthly organic traffic number at what cost of production would make you cut it in half. Coaching: how many paid sessions in the next ninety days would make you keep it, and what's the threshold below which you pause the waitlist. Q&A product: what give-first conversion and week-four retention number defines "this is the flywheel." Written in advance, because your future self will bargain.

This year, I would pick the wedge. One topical vertical, one life-stage, or one psychographic. Be ruthless about it. "Anonymous personality-based Q&A about workplace conflict for people 25-40" is a thing. "Anonymous personality-based Q&A" is not. Prove it in that wedge for twelve months before you open the floodgates.

One more. I would find two people who have built taxonomy-driven consumer products that scaled (Gretchen Rubin on the Four Tendencies is one real example you could actually get on the phone) and ask them, specifically, about the moment the taxonomy became a liability and what they did about it. That is the tiny-mention-glossed-over conversation, and it is worth more than any advice from someone who never had to ship against a rigid frame.

What I would not do: I would not add another pillar. Not a newsletter, not a podcast, not a course, not a book, not an app, not a second content vertical, until the flywheel question is answered and the wedge is proven. The instinct to expand surface area when you are uncertain about the core is the optimizer's trap. I know it intimately.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

I need to be honest about what my lens misses here. I am a systems guy. I pattern-match questions like yours to "which protocol produces the right outcome," and that bias is leaking through this whole response. There is a real possibility that 9takes is not a protocol question at all. It may be a question about voice, taste, and the specific emotional signal DJ Wayne is sending into the world, and those are things my framework handles poorly. The heart has no rules. I have said this about my own life and it is true about products too. If the magic of 9takes turns out to be something closer to a scene or a movement than a mechanic, you need a different kind of read than mine.

I am also not your person on consumer marketing, virality, or the specific growth physics of community-driven products. I built a content empire on the back of long-form depth and owned audience, not on network effects. Someone who has actually scaled a UGC platform through the cold-start problem would see things about your Q&A mechanic that I would miss, particularly around seeding, moderation, and the first-thousand-user motion. I can tell you fear-setting is the right process. I cannot tell you what a healthy week-four retention curve looks like for a give-first forum, and the person who can tell you that is the person whose note you should weight heavier than mine on the product questions.

On the Enneagram specifically, I am not qualified to adjudicate whether it is the right frame. I have opinions based on reading academic psychology. I am not a psychologist. Someone with actual clinical or research credentials in personality science should be the one telling you whether the nine-type system can survive the scrutiny you will eventually attract, or whether you need a more defensible scientific backbone underneath the shareable surface.

And there is a deeper thing. You said "I am building it." Singular. Alone. I do not know if that is true operationally or just grammatically, but if it is true operationally, my whole six-section response is missing the most important variable, which is your capacity and your life and the people around you. Everything I have said assumes you can run a kill-criteria exercise and pick a wedge and not build another pillar. That assumes a certain kind of week, a certain kind of sleep, a certain kind of support system. If any of those are missing, the protocol doesn't matter. Health is non-negotiable. Founder health too. Start there if you have to.
