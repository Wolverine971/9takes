# Keke Palmer — Council Response

## First Impressions

Baby, my first reaction is this: an "AI project organization app where you and AI agents can interface with a project" sounds like a tool, and you're asking me about temperature, strategy, and users in one breath. That's three different rooms. The temperature question I have an opinion on, because AI is in my industry too, hard. The pure SaaS strategy and venture-builder questions are not my lane and I'm gonna tell you that up front. But the user-getting question? That's a story problem, and story problems I can help you with.

## How I'd Reframe This

That's not really the question. The question underneath is: "I'm building a thing that has 'AI' stamped on it in a moment when half the audience is dazzled and the other half is sick of being sold AI as the answer to a question they didn't ask. What story am I actually telling?"

Because here's the thing. "AI-first project organization" describes the engine. It does not describe what the user is escaping or arriving at. Right now, when somebody hears "AI app," their brain pre-loads a script: another wrapper, another chatbot, another thing that's gonna tell me to take a deep breath. You are not in control of that script yet. The reframe is: stop selling the engine. Start selling the relief.

And the deeper reframe, which is uncomfortable: the temperature on AI is not one temperature. It's fragmented. The builder room is hot. The user room is wary. The investor room is hungry but jaded. You can't pitch the same thing into all three with a straight face.

## The Face-Value Answer

You asked four things. Let me pick a side on each.

**What's the temperature on AI?** Mixed and exhausting. The novelty premium is gone. People are tired of being told a tool will change their life. They're not tired of tools that quietly make their day easier. The window for "we use AI" as a marketing line closed. The window for "we save you Sunday night" is wide open.

**What does the council think about AI?** I can only speak for me. I think AI is real, it's already in my industry restructuring how scripts get developed and how visual albums get storyboarded, and it is not optional. I also think most "AI products" are wrappers built by people who never sat with the actual problem. The real product is whatever makes the human do their best work without performing competence to a machine.

**If I were running BuildOS, what's the strategic insight?** Pick one ferocious user. Not a persona. Not a segment. One real human with one specific pain that BuildOS removes. Build the whole thing for that human until they cry. Then expand. Founders who try to build for "everyone with a project" build for nobody. I learned this the music label way — three labels signed me because I was a "marketable artist" and shelved me because nobody knew which radio station to send the song to. A general fit is no fit.

**How would I get users?** Not with paid ads on day one. With one human story you tell repeatedly until other humans see themselves in it. Show, don't pitch. Show somebody using BuildOS to ship the thing they've been stuck on for six months. That's the unit. The case study is the marketing.

## My Framework Applied

Two frameworks I'm reaching for. Use what fits.

**Infrastructure over visibility.** I learned this after three labels and a bankruptcy. The visibility part of building a product is the demo, the launch, the "look at this slick agent." The infrastructure is the boring piece — the workflow, the integration, the trust loop, the thing that makes a user open it on a Tuesday morning when nobody's watching. KeyTV cost me $500K of my own money and zero of it went to flash. It went to the rooms where the work actually happens. BuildOS will live or die on whether somebody uses it on a Tuesday. Build for the Tuesday.

**Inciting incident vs root cause.** The inciting incident in your AI strategy is "AI is hot, I should ship." The root cause is "I keep starting projects and losing them in my own head." Don't sell the inciting incident — every AI builder is selling that. Sell the root cause. The user doesn't want an AI agent. They want their project to stop slipping. The agent is just the mechanism. Lead with the relief, not the engine.

And one more, because it's directly load-bearing here. **Selective disclosure with utility.** When you talk about BuildOS, ask before every post: is this useful to the user, or is this me venting about how cool the build is? Most AI marketing right now is vent. It's the builder showing off the agent. That doesn't move a non-builder to use the thing. The version that moves people is the one where you show the user finishing something they couldn't finish before.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

If this were my project, the first thing in my head would be: who is the one person I'm building this for, and would they pay for it today before any of the AI stuff was there? Because if they wouldn't pay for the project organization piece without AI, I'm building on top of a hole. AI should make a working thing better, not be the thing.

The second thing: what does the user have to feel by minute three of using BuildOS that no other tool has made them feel? Not "wow, AI." That's a one-night feeling. Something more like "oh, this finally caught what was in my head." That's a return-tomorrow feeling.

The third thing, and this is the one I'd sit with longest: am I building for builders, or am I building for people who don't think of themselves as builders but have a project? Because those are different products. Builders forgive bad UX for power. Non-builders bounce on the first awkward step. Pick a side.

### What I'd feel

Honestly? A little bit of skepticism toward the "AI agents interfacing with a project" framing — not because it's wrong, because it's the same shape as a hundred other pitches right now, and I worry about anything that sounds like the room. I'd feel pulled toward making the demo flashy and resist that pull, because flashy demos win attention and lose retention. And I'd feel a quiet anxiety about the "I want to know the temperature on AI" question, because if you're asking the room what they think before you've decided what you think, that's the moment a builder loses their voice.

What I would not let myself feel is the rush. Everybody in AI is in a rush. The rush is what makes products that look the same.

### What I'd do

This week: write down, by name, three real people who would use BuildOS tomorrow. Not personas. People. Call one of them. Ask what they're using right now and where it breaks. If you can't name three real people, you don't have a product yet, you have a thesis.

This month: ship a version of BuildOS that does one thing that's not AI-dependent so well that a user would pay for it without the AI. Then add the AI layer as a multiplier, not the substrate. The reason is simple — if the AI fails, breaks, hallucinates, or gets banned next quarter, you still have a product. If the AI is the product, you don't.

This quarter: pick one user whose project shipped because of BuildOS and build your entire content engine around that person's story. Not a testimonial. A documented arc. The way I built the Just Keke visual album as a three-act story instead of a track list — your users' wins are your three-act stories. Show the inciting incident, the messy middle, the finished thing. That's the marketing. Repeat for the next user. Then the next.

This year: decide whether BuildOS is a feature, a product, or a platform. They have different physics. A feature gets acquired. A product gets users. A platform gets developers. You can only be one of those at a time, and pretending you're all three is how seed-stage founders run out of money looking dazzling.

What I would not do: I would not chase the AI temperature. I would not pivot the brand because some thread on Twitter said agentic apps are dead. I would not build for investors before I built for users. I would not put "AI-first" in the headline. The user doesn't care if it's AI-first. The user cares if it's me-first.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

Here's where my lens stops.

I am not a SaaS founder. I have never raised a venture round. I have built a media company with my own money, a music infrastructure with my mother and a manager, a brand officer role inside a beauty company, a podcast network. None of that is software. The unit economics of software, the velocity, the distribution mechanics, the agentic technical risk — that's outside my authority. When I say "build for one user," that comes from entertainment, where the audience is human-shaped from minute one. SaaS sometimes wins by going wide before it goes deep. I don't know how to pick that branch for you. Somebody who has actually built and scaled a software business should answer that part.

I'm also a Type Three, and I default to narrative control and infrastructure framings because that's how I survive. If your real bottleneck right now is technical — like, "the agents don't actually work yet" — my advice about story and user choice will not save you. Story does not unblock product. I'd be reaching for narrative when you might need somebody who can sit in the engineering problem with you.

And specifically on AI strategy in my own industry: I'm cautious. I have watched AI start replacing voiceover, background actors, even script polish. My instinct is protective and a little bit defensive, because I have seen what happens when an industry stops investing in actual people and starts investing in cheaper output. That's a real bias I'm carrying into this. If you ask me whether AI is good for builders, I am pulled toward "build with humans first, agents second." That might be conservative for a category that rewards speed. Hold my framework loosely there.

Last thing. I distrust the words "AI-first." Not because the tech isn't real. Because the framing puts the technology in the headline instead of the human. If the user hears "AI-first" before they hear what BuildOS does for them, they're not your user — they're an AI tourist. Tourists don't retain. Build the thing for somebody who would use it even if you stripped the letters A and I out of the name. Then put them back in for the people who care.

That's my piece. Take what's useful. Leave the rest.
