# Kristen Bell — Council Response

## First Impressions

Okay, full disclosure: pure startup strategy is not my lane, and I'm going to keep saying that because I don't want to fake authority I haven't earned. But the second you said "AI project organization app where you and AI agents can interface with a project," my body went into the same low-grade hum I get when someone hands me a five-tool wellness app for my anxiety. Tools without a nervous system regulating them are just more noise. So my gut is suspicious in a useful way. Not of you. Of the category.

## How I'd Reframe This

You asked what I think about AI and how I'd run BuildOS. The actual question I hear underneath is: what does this product do for a person at 11 p.m. when they're overwhelmed and staring at a project that's grown teeth? Productivity tools live or die in the panic moment, not in the demo. You're not selling project organization. You're selling the regulation that comes from finally externalizing the chaos in someone's head into a place an agent can grab and work on while they sleep. That's a remedy box for builders. If you frame it as productivity, you're competing with Notion and Linear and ten thousand AI wrappers. If you frame it as "the place your project lives so you can stop carrying it in your body," you're competing with anxiety. Different fight.

## The Face-Value Answer

On the temperature of AI: people are exhausted. Not skeptical, exhausted. The early thrill is over and the middle phase is here, where everyone has tried six tools and most of them feel like a smart intern who doesn't know what they don't know. There's a real opening for products that respect that fatigue and don't add to it. I'd build for the person who is one bad week away from quitting their solo project, not the person who is optimizing their already-functional team.

If I were running BuildOS, I'd do four things. One, I'd pick one user, one job, one panic moment, and refuse to expand until that one person says it changed how their week feels. Two, I'd make the agent's first interaction feel like a person who actually scanned the room before speaking. Are you thirsty? Are you hungry? In product terms: what's the smallest thing I can do for you right now without asking you to fill out fourteen fields? Three, I'd be ruthlessly transparent on mic, the way I am about marriage. Show the agent's reasoning. Show what it got wrong yesterday. Show the seams. Trust gets built in the seams. Four, I'd find ten people who already love it, not a hundred who tolerate it, and treat those ten like a village. Audit them. Invest in them. Let them write the next feature with you.

On getting users: I would not run paid acquisition until you have ten people who say "this changed my week" without you prompting them. The whole AI category right now is people paying to acquire users who churn in three weeks. That's a leaky food truck. Fix the bucket first.

## My Framework Applied

The remedy box. That's the lens.

A remedy box is a set of pre-decided tools, written down on cards, tested when the person was calm, so they're available when the person is not calm. The whole point is that working memory collapses under stress. You can't ask someone in a panic to remember their seventeen breathing techniques. You hand them the card.

BuildOS is a remedy box for projects. The user's working memory is collapsing. The project has too many tabs, too many threads, too many half-decisions. They open BuildOS and the agent says, here's what we're doing, here's the next move, here's what I'll handle while you go to bed. Pre-decided. Available in panic. That's the value, and that's the only value that matters at first.

So the design questions become specific. What's on the card? What does the agent know about the project before the user opens the app on a bad day? What's the equivalent of "find a song you love and sing out loud" — the smallest possible action that breaks the spiral? When my six-year-old asked for the toolbox, she didn't say "build me a system for managing my emotions." She said "wouldn't it be great if we had a real toolbox?" Your version of that question is the one to listen for. Not "what features do you want." But "what's the moment where you wish this existed and it didn't?"

The other framework I'd reach for is the village audit. You're going to be tempted to build for everyone. Don't. Pick the ten people you'd put in your evolutionary herd. The ones who'll actually tell you the agent is being weird, who'll text you at 7 a.m. with a bug and a hug. Build for them obsessively. Everyone else later.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

I'd be running two questions in parallel. First: who is the one person whose week I want to change? Not a persona. A real human with a name and a project I can picture. Second: where does this product fail in a panic moment? Because the demo will look great. The 11 p.m. test is the one that matters. I'd also be thinking, hard, about whether I'm building the thing because builders need it or because the AI category is hot. Those are different motives and one of them ages well and the other doesn't.

### What I'd feel

If I'm being honest about my own wiring, I'd feel the pull to make BuildOS too friendly, too accommodating, too willing to do whatever the user asked. That's the food truck reflex in product form. Building for love, sacrificing the spine of the thing. The product needs an opinion. The agent needs to push back. "I don't think we should do that this week" is more valuable than "sure, I'll do that." I'd feel the resistance to letting the product disappoint people on purpose, and I'd have to practice it the way I practiced not apologizing.

I'd also feel some grief about the AI hype cycle. There's a real thing here, and it's getting drowned in noise made by people who don't care about the user. That's worth naming so it doesn't poison your motivation.

### What I'd do

This week: write down, on actual cards, the five panic moments BuildOS is meant to solve. Not features. Moments. Then ask three real builders if those are their actual panic moments or your projection of them.

This month: pick the ten people. Get on a call with each one, separately, no group calls. Watch them open the app. Don't talk. The places where they hesitate are your roadmap. After every call, write down one thing the agent did that felt human and one thing it did that felt like a smart intern who didn't read the room. Fix the second list before you ship anything new.

This year: build a public artifact about how the agent reasons. Not marketing. Documentation that doubles as trust-building. Show what it got wrong, what it learned, where it's careful. The Armchair Expert version of product development, where you let people see the seams. People don't trust AI products that perform competence. They trust the ones that show their work.

What I would not do: chase a launch. Chase a user. One real user who tells someone else, unprompted, that BuildOS changed their week, is worth more than a thousand signups from a Product Hunt push.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

I am not a startup theorist. Capital allocation, market timing, technical architecture, agent design, the actual mechanics of competing in an AI-saturated category — that is not my lane and I'd be making it up if I tried to go deeper there. The lens I brought is a nervous-system lens, a Helper-products-for-anxious-builders lens. That lens makes me reach for protocols and remedy boxes faster than this question may need. Some founders need stillness, not another tool. Some products need a cold market analysis that I don't have the muscle to do.

A couple of biases worth naming on this one. My instinct is to systematize feelings, including builder feelings, and not every product decision has a feeling underneath it. Some of this is just market math. Also, my whole framework assumes a village and a structured nervous system at the base layer. I do not have a reliable read on the founder who runs solo and regenerates alone, and BuildOS may serve a lot of those people. Listen harder than I would to the ones who don't want a community feature, don't want the agent to be warm, just want a tool that gets out of their way. They're real, and my caretaker instinct will undercount them.

The strategic axis I can't help you with is the one about AI specifically — model choice, infrastructure, positioning against incumbents, the velocity of the category. You'll need a sharper, colder lens for that. What I can tell you is that if the panic moment is solved well and the ten people genuinely love it, the rest of the strategy gets easier. That's a Helper's bet, and I'll own that it's a bet.
