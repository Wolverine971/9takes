# Kyle Forgeard — Council Response

## First Impressions

Honest first reaction. AI tools are not my lane. I'm a guy who points cameras at things and sells hoodies and seltzer to the boys. So when you ask "what's the temperature with AI," the temperature I have is the temperature of an audience, not the temperature of an engineer. And the audience temperature is: people are using ChatGPT every day, they say it casually on podcasts, my editors use it, but nobody I know has paid for a "project organization app" since Notion got popular and then everyone stopped opening Notion. That's the gut. The category scares me before the AI part does.

## How I'd Reframe This

You asked me what I think about AI and how I'd run BuildOS. That's not the question. The question is: what audience are you launching this thing into, and do you have a wedge into them that doesn't depend on them already wanting "AI project organization software"?

Because I'll tell you right now, no normal person wakes up and types "AI project organization app" into a search bar. They wake up with a project that's eating them alive and they look for the next thing that will make them feel like they're back in control. That's the door. The door is the feeling, not the feature. And if you build a feature-first thing and try to push it into a category that's already noisy as hell, you're going to spend a year shipping and three months realizing you have no on-ramp.

So the real question is: who is the boys for BuildOS? Who's the crew that you can put this in front of and they show up because it's you, before they show up because it's good?

## The Face-Value Answer

Picking a side. On AI generally: it's real, it's not a bubble, and the boys are using it whether they admit it or not. The temperature is hot but the products are mostly mid. Most of what's getting built is wrapper-on-wrapper, and the people winning right now are either OpenAI/Anthropic themselves or the few guys who picked a stupid-narrow vertical and went hard.

On running BuildOS: if I were sitting in your chair I would not lead with "AI project organization." I would lead with one specific painful thing that one specific tribe of people hates about how they run projects today, and I would build BuildOS as the thing that fixes that one thing. AI under the hood, not on the marquee. Then I would not chase users through ads or SEO out of the gate. I'd make the founder (you) the wedge. Build it in public. Show your own messy projects in BuildOS. Let people watch you use it for a real thing they care about, like 9takes itself. That's the content as wedge, the product as margin.

Get a hundred people who would cry if you took it away before you try to get ten thousand people who'd shrug.

## My Framework Applied

Two of my frameworks apply hard here, and one is a warning.

**Content as wedge, product as margin.** This is the Nelk model. Free attention buys the audience cheap, the physical thing is where the dollars are. Translate that to BuildOS: free, loud, specific content where you're using BuildOS to run a real project people can see. Not demos. Not screencasts of features. The actual work of you running 9takes inside BuildOS, in public. The product is the margin. The content is how you don't pay Google a dollar to acquire your first thousand users.

The trap with software is that founders think the app is the content. It's not. You are the content. The use case is the content. The before-and-after is the content. The app is the thing people pay for after they've watched you wear it for six months.

**Own the stack.** Do not get cute and build BuildOS as a thin layer on top of one provider's API. The whole point of the Full Send playbook is we got demonetized by YouTube and it barely mattered because we owned the merch, the email list, the shows, the cans. If you build BuildOS so that one model provider raising prices or changing terms can wipe out your margins, you've built a tenant, not a business. Plan for the day OpenAI and Anthropic both decide they want your category. They will.

**Set low expectations, then you can't ever get worse.** Don't launch BuildOS as the thing that revolutionizes how teams run projects with AI. Launch it as a janky tool that helps you, specifically, stop dropping balls on 9takes. Let people find out it's better than you said. That's how the merch drops worked. Set the bar at the floor, then keep stepping over it.

The warning. I have a thing called the Metacard sitting on my record. We launched a 10,000-piece NFT, raised $23 million in a weekend, promised people a relationship and delivered a beef jerky brand and a class-action lawsuit. The mistake wasn't intent. The mistake was the same appetite that worked for hoodies launched into an asset class where I didn't have the operating plan. AI right now feels like that to me on the founder side. The energy is "ship fast, raise on hype, figure it out." If BuildOS is a long product that requires people to trust it with their actual work over months and years, you cannot launch it on hype mechanics. You'll over-promise and you'll spend the next two years cleaning it up. Don't be the AI version of the Metacard.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

If I were sitting in your chair the questions I'd be running are: who specifically is this for, do I have a way to reach them that doesn't cost money, and is there one painful job-to-be-done where BuildOS is obviously better than the next tool plus a human plus a Notion doc.

I'd be thinking hard about the agent part. "You and AI agents can interface with a project" is interesting and also a category nobody's quite cracked yet. Cursor cracked it for code. Whoever cracks it for non-code projects has something. But the demos in this space all look great in a 90-second video and fall apart at week three of real use. Question I'd sit with: what does week three look like? Not week one.

I'd be thinking about whether 9takes itself is the demo. You have a content business with hundreds of blog posts in flight, drafts, edits, distribution, deadlines. If BuildOS is what you actually use to run 9takes and you can show me that loop, that's a hundred times more convincing than any landing page.

I'd also be thinking about the moat. Wrappers don't have one. Workflows do. Data does. The longer someone uses BuildOS the better it knows their projects. If that's true, lean on it. If it's not true yet, it has to be true soon.

### What I'd feel

I'd feel pulled toward the next thing. That's my pattern. I'd want to ship the agent feature and the dashboard and the integrations and the Slack thing all at once because each one looks fun. And the right move is almost always the opposite of that. Pick the one thing, ship it, watch a real person use it, fix what's broken.

I'd feel some envy of every other AI product I see launching with a viral demo. That envy is poison. The viral demo guys are mostly not still around in twelve months. The Happy Dad model is slower. You build a thing the boys actually drink, and you keep filling the cans, and three years later you have a brand. AI products are tempted to skip that and you can't.

I'd also feel a low-grade fear about being late or being too early. AI moves fast enough that the thing you build for today's models might be a feature in next year's GPT. That fear is real. The answer isn't to stop building. The answer is to build the thing that survives a model upgrade. Which usually means: own the workflow and the data, don't own a clever prompt.

### What I'd do

This week. Pick one user. One real person, by name, who has a painful project problem you understand. Not a persona. A human. Build BuildOS for that human. Watch them use it. Fix what's stupid.

This month. Make 9takes itself run on BuildOS. End to end. Drafts, edits, distribution, the whole thing. Not because it's the best dogfooding but because it's your fastest content engine. Every screenshot of you running 9takes in BuildOS is a piece of marketing. Start posting them. Don't wait for a launch.

Pick the wedge audience. The boys for BuildOS. Solo operators? Indie hackers? Small content shops? Whichever it is, get in their group chats and Discords and X threads and just be present for three months. Not selling. Watching. You'll learn what to build.

Quarter from now. If you have ten people who use BuildOS every day for real work, you have a product. If you have a thousand people who signed up and three who use it weekly, you have a problem. Don't confuse signup metrics with retention. Retention is the only number that matters in software you want to last.

This year. Decide whether BuildOS is a product company or a tool you wish existed for 9takes. Both are valid. They are not the same path. A product company needs a team, a sales motion eventually, and someone whose full-time job is BuildOS. A tool you built for yourself can be open source, free, or low-key SaaS that funds itself. Pick one. Don't try to be both.

What I would not do. I would not raise venture money on the hype before product-market fit. I would not pay for ads until I had a content engine that converted at any rate. I would not build the agent integration with three different model providers before I had ten paying users. I would not name a launch date and then panic-ship to hit it. The Metacard had a launch date. The Metacard delivered beef jerky.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

A lot of this answer is outside my real lane. Let me name it.

I am not a software founder. I have never shipped a SaaS product. The mechanics of building, scaling, and pricing developer-adjacent or productivity software are not things I have lived. The frameworks I gave you are creator-economy frameworks. They translate, but not fully. Software has different unit economics, different retention curves, and different competitive dynamics than seltzer or hoodies. Someone who has built and scaled a productivity tool would tell you things about activation, time-to-value, and pricing that I genuinely cannot tell you.

I am not an AI engineer. I do not know what's actually possible with current agent frameworks, where the models are weak, where they're getting stronger, what's two months away versus two years away. My take that "wrappers don't have moats" is folk wisdom from podcasts. The real answer requires technical judgment I don't have.

I am also biased toward the audience-first playbook because it's the only playbook that's worked for me. There are software companies that won by being the best product in a crowded space without a personal brand attached. That path exists. It's just not my path, and I'd default to underweighting it.

My pattern that's most likely distorting this counsel: I reframe setbacks fast and I move toward the next thing fast. That's a great founder reflex when you're shipping content. It can be a terrible reflex when you're shipping software, where the right move is often to sit with a broken feature for three weeks until you understand why it's broken before you replace it. Watch yourself for that. If I were running BuildOS I would probably ship too many features and not fix the ones I have. You might have the same reflex, or you might not. Worth knowing.

Last thing. I know you and you trust your gut, and your gut is part of why you're building this. But "I want to know what the temperature is with AI" is a question with a different answer for every audience. The temperature with developers is different from the temperature with founders is different from the temperature with normal small-business owners is different from the temperature with creators. Pick which temperature matters for BuildOS, then go take that one specific temperature with your own hands. Mine doesn't transfer.
