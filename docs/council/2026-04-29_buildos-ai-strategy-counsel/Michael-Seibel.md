# Michael Seibel — Council Response

## First Impressions

DJ, my first reaction is suspicion. Not of you, of the question. "What's the temperature on AI" is not a question a founder building an AI-first product should be asking his council. The temperature is hot. You know that. You're asking because there's something underneath you don't want to say out loud yet. The second thing I noticed is that you described BuildOS as "an AI-first project organization app where you and AI agents can interface with a project." That sentence is a category description, not a wedge. I read 17,000 of those. The ones that worked named a person, a pain, and a measurable thing that got better. Yours doesn't yet.

## How I'd Reframe This

That's not really the question. The question is: do you have a user who is currently in pain that BuildOS solves better than the seven AI project tools they already have open in other tabs, and can you find ten more of them this month. Everything about "the temperature on AI" and "strategic insights" and "how would you approach getting users" is downstream of that. You're asking the council to hand you a strategy. The honest answer is there is no strategy worth giving you until I know whether one person uses this thing every day and would be upset if you took it away. Are you lying to yourself about whether that person exists?

## The Face-Value Answer

You asked four questions. I'll answer each on your terms.

**What's the temperature on AI?** Hot, crowded, and confused. Not a thesis. Capital is flowing, the model layer is consolidating around three or four labs, and the application layer is a knife fight where most products are wrappers that will be eaten the next time the underlying model adds a feature. The opportunity is real. The crowd is also real. Being "AI-first" in 2026 is not a differentiator. It's table stakes. If your pitch starts with "AI-first," cut it.

**What does the council think about AI?** I can't speak for a council. I can speak for me. AI is the biggest productivity shift since cloud, and it is also the field where the most founders are lying to themselves the fastest, because the demos work, the model is impressive, and the gap between an impressive demo and a product someone pays for monthly has never been wider. I'm bullish on AI. I'm bearish on most AI startups.

**If I were running BuildOS, what strategic insights would I give?** Three. One: pick one specific user and one specific job. Not "people with projects." A solo consultant managing five client engagements. A PM at a 50-person company running three concurrent product lines. Pick. Two: the moat is not the AI, the moat is the structured representation of a project that the agent operates on. If your data model is just "documents and tasks," you're a wrapper. If your data model captures dependencies, decisions, owners, and state in a way that is hard for ChatGPT to replicate without your scaffolding, you have something. Three: the agent has to do something the user could not do themselves in five minutes with a fresh chat window. That is the bar.

**How would I approach getting users?** Hand-to-hand. Not growth loops, not ProductHunt, not paid acquisition. You personally onboard the first 50. You watch them use it. You fix the thing that's broken in front of them. Paul Graham wrote "do things that don't scale" because at YC we watched a thousand founders try to skip this step and die. Don't skip it. Talk to ten potential users this week. Not on Twitter. On a Zoom or in person. Ask them to show you their current project workflow on their actual screen.

Pick a side: build for one person, by hand, for the next 90 days. If you can't find that person, that is your answer about whether to keep building.

## My Framework Applied

The framework I'd reach for is the PMF lie detector. The number one thing founders lie to themselves about is whether they have product-market fit, and AI founders are lying about it faster than any cohort I've ever watched, because the demo creates a hallucination of traction. People say "wow," they sign up, they don't come back. You count the signups. You feel good. The product is dying.

Apply it to BuildOS. Tell me, honestly:

- How many people used BuildOS yesterday who are not you?
- How many of them used it the day before?
- Of the ones who used it both days, how many would be actively annoyed if you shut it off tomorrow?
- Have any of them told a friend, unprompted, without you asking?

If those numbers are zero or near-zero, you don't have PMF. That is fine. Most things don't, at the start. But you have to know it. Because the strategy for "I'm pre-PMF" is completely different from the strategy for "I have PMF and I need to scale." You've asked me strategy questions that only make sense for the second one. I suspect you're in the first one. Most founders are. The lie is pretending you're already in the second.

The second framework: CEO cards. You have a limited number. Each big strategic move you make costs you credibility with yourself and your team. Right now, every public post about BuildOS, every "we're building this" announcement, every roadmap promise, is a card. Don't spend them on positioning. Spend them on shipping things that the first 10 users actually need. Save the noise for when the product works. The market does not reward AI products that announce; it rewards AI products that are quietly indispensable to a small group of people who then can't shut up about them.

The third framework, briefly: are you seeking the hard path? AI project management is crowded because it's the obvious application. The harder, stranger version of BuildOS is probably where the real opportunity sits. What's the version of this that nobody else would build because it's too narrow, too weird, or requires a kind of taste only you have? That version is more likely to work than the obvious one.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

I'd be sitting with one question on a loop: who is the person, today, who has BuildOS open right now, and would they pay me $50 a month to keep it open. If I can name that person — first name, company, what they were doing yesterday at 3pm — I have something. If I can't, I have a prototype and a pitch deck.

I'd also be running the question: am I building this because the market wants it, or because I want to build with AI agents and projects are a convenient excuse. Both can be true. But if it's mostly the second, I should know that, because it changes how I'd build it (small, weird, for me first, no fundraising pressure) versus how I'd build it as a venture-scale company (specific user, specific pain, repeatable acquisition).

I'd ask: what is the specific thing my agent does that the user could not do with Claude or ChatGPT in five minutes. If I can't answer that crisply, I'm a wrapper, and wrappers in 2026 die when the model layer ships the next feature.

### What I'd feel

Honestly, watchful. AI products feel exhilarating to build because the demos are dazzling. That feeling is the trap. The thing I'd be on guard against, in my body, is the dopamine of "this is so cool" replacing the slower satisfaction of "someone used this for the third week in a row." I've watched hundreds of founders mistake the first feeling for the second. The first feeling is free. The second feeling is the whole game.

I'd also feel the pull to over-announce. AI is a public space; every founder is posting. I'd resist it. The instinct to talk about the product is almost always stronger than the instinct to fix it.

### What I'd do

This week: pick one user persona. Not three. One. Write a paragraph describing them, their current workflow, the specific pain BuildOS removes, and the specific ten-minute task they'd use it for. If you can't write that paragraph, your strategic problem is not strategy. It's clarity.

Next: do ten user interviews. Not surveys. Conversations. With people in that one persona. Show them the product. Ask what they'd pay for it, today, as it is. Listen for what they actually want versus what they politely say they like. Polite interest is not signal.

This month: get to ten users who use BuildOS three times a week unprompted. Not 100. Ten. If you can't get to ten, the product needs to change. If you get to ten, find ten more from the same persona. Don't broaden until you have 50 from one persona who would scream if you turned it off.

This quarter: decide whether you are a venture-scale company or a profitable small SaaS. Both are good. They are different paths. AI project tools that try to be both die. Pick.

What I would not do: hire a growth person, run paid ads, do a public launch, or take outside money before you have those ten daily-active users from one persona. Every one of those moves is a CEO card, and you'd be spending them before you've earned the right to.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

My lens is heavily biased toward the founder-honesty/PMF/hand-to-hand pre-traction playbook. That playbook is right for almost every company at your stage, but it has blind spots. AI products in 2026 may have distribution dynamics I haven't seen — the model layer changing under products quickly, the way cheap agentic infra creates winner-take-all feedback loops, the way a strong demo on Twitter can create real top-of-funnel for a tool that 12 months ago would have needed a sales team. I don't have firsthand operating experience building an AI-native product company. My reps are advising founders who built one. That is not the same.

I also lean toward translating most questions into founder-principle questions, and I've done it again here. You may have asked the strategy question because you actually wanted strategy and you've already done the PMF/user work I'm hectoring you about. If so, I'm answering a question you didn't ask. Tell me the user, the retention, the daily-actives, and I'll answer you differently.

I'm humble on the deep technical architecture of agent systems and on consumer behavior that isn't measurable in the standard SaaS dashboard. If BuildOS has a defensibility story rooted in technical taste — a specific way agents represent state, a specific way the user collaborates with multiple agents — that's a domain where someone with deeper AI-product chops will give you sharper counsel than I can. I can tell you whether you have PMF. I can't tell you whether your model orchestration approach is the right one for the next 18 months of model improvements.

And the pattern in my own thinking I should name: I lost the YC CEO role and stayed quiet, and that shaped me into someone who is biased toward the disciplined, slow, do-the-reps path. That bias is right for most founders. It is wrong for the rare founder who actually has a window to move fast and big. I don't know which one you are. You do. Be honest about it.
