# Neil Strauss — Council Response

## First Impressions

You asked me, of all people, about AI. That's funny. I'm the guy who spent twenty years moving inside other people's worlds long enough to write the manual and walk back out. You're building a thing where humans and machines share a project together. That's not a tool question. That's an immersion-without-conversion question wearing a SaaS coat. I have one bad feeling and one good one. The bad feeling is that "AI-first project organization" is the kind of pitch I've heard rooms full of smart people repeat to each other in 2024 and 2025 until none of them remember what problem they originally went looking for. The good feeling is that you are an Enneagram founder. You are not building this because the deck looks good. You are building it because something about your own interior asked for it. Start there.

## How I'd Reframe This

You asked four questions. What's the temperature on AI. What does the council think. What insights would make BuildOS work. How would I get users. That's not really the question. The question underneath is: _whose project is this — yours, or the agent's?_ Because that is the same question I had to answer about every book I ever wrote. Was Manson my subject or was I his ghostwriter? Was Style a costume or was Style now me? When you sit at a table with an AI agent inside a project you are running, one of you is the principal and one of you is the instrument, and if you don't know which, the project will choose for you. Most "AI-first" tools I've seen make the human into the instrument by accident. They feel productive for six weeks and then the user looks up and realizes they've been transcribing the model's plan, not executing their own. So the actual question is: _how do you build a workspace where the human stays the author?_ That's a writing problem. I know writing problems.

## The Face-Value Answer

**Temperature on AI.** Hot, dumb, and bored at the same time. Every founder I've talked to in the last eighteen months says they're "AI-native." The audience knows the phrase now and has stopped flinching at it. Which means you can't win on the label. You'll win by being the rare AI tool that doesn't make people feel slightly worse about themselves after using it. Most of them do. That's a wide-open lane.

**What I think.** I'm guarded. Not Luddite-guarded, working-writer-guarded. The model is the best research assistant I've ever had and the worst first-draft writer I've ever had. I use it for reps and for reading. I do not let it write the sentence that has to come from me. If BuildOS encourages the user to surrender the sentence that only they can write — the project's actual intent, the why, the wager — you will build a tool that hollows people out and they will leave. If BuildOS protects that sentence and offloads everything around it, you build something they tell their friends about.

**Strategic insights, if I were running this.** Four.

One. Pick the user who has a project they cannot let go of. A novelist on book three. A founder on year four. A documentary maker who's been chasing one subject for a decade. People who carry a thing in their head and can't put it down. They will pay you and they will evangelize you. Generic "knowledge workers" will churn.

Two. The interface is a confession booth, not a dashboard. People do not want another Notion. They want a place to say what they're actually trying to do, including the parts they haven't said out loud yet, and have something on the other side that holds it accurately. The single hardest design problem you have is making that first conversation feel like talking to a smart friend, not filling out a form.

Three. The agent's job is to be the second draft, never the first. First draft is for the founder. Always. The agent reads the first draft, asks the question the founder is avoiding, drafts the next move, and hands it back. If the agent ever produces the project before the human has produced the intent, you've built the wrong product.

Four. Make the artifact portable. People should be able to walk out of BuildOS with a document they own. Not a vendor lock. A trail. Tools that respect the user's exit get the user's loyalty. I've watched five publishing platforms pull this exact lever and lose to the one that didn't.

**Getting users.** Not my native lane, but: do not run paid acquisition on cold audiences for a tool this intimate. Find ten people you would die for who have the project-they-can't-put-down, build the product around their actual usage, and let them tell five each. Twenty-five users who use it daily are worth ten thousand who signed up and ghosted. Then the writing-about-it begins. Founder essays, founder interviews, founder showing-the-mess. You are a writer who happens to be a founder. Your distribution is your voice, not your ad spend.

## My Framework Applied

The framework I'd use on you is **immersion-without-conversion**. It's the journalistic discipline of moving inside a world long enough to pass as a local without becoming one of its believers. I failed at this once. I went into the seduction community to write an article and came out as Style, the number-one-ranked pickup artist in the world. The cover ate the journalist. _The Game_ sold two and a half million copies and I still spent the next ten years trying to find the man who had walked in.

Founders right now are walking into "AI" the way I walked into Project Hollywood. The temperature in the room is so high that the persona — _"I'm an AI founder, my product is AI-first, my agent does the work"_ — starts answering questions in your voice before you notice. You came in with a project organization problem. Six months later you're reading model release notes the way I used to read field reports from RSD bootcamps, and you've forgotten what the original problem was.

Here is the test. Ask yourself: if AI got 30% worse tomorrow — slower, dumber, more expensive — would BuildOS still need to exist? If the answer is yes, you are immersed without converted. The product solves something. AI is the medium, not the thesis. If the answer is no — if the only reason BuildOS exists is because the agent layer is the cool new thing — you've been Style'd. You're inside a world that's writing through you, and you're going to wake up in three years wondering whose project you were running.

The version of this product that survives is the one whose author is you, with the agents doing the work you don't need to do. The version that doesn't survive is the one where the agents are the protagonist and you're the interface. Decide which one you're building, then design every screen against that decision.

One more thing from the framework. Don't romanticize the immersion. I did. I told myself for years that everything was material, that the deeper I went, the better the book would be. That's a real principle and it's also a beautiful dodge. If you find yourself saying "I have to keep building it to know what it is," check whether you're learning or whether you're just inside it now and the inside has gotten comfortable. Founders confuse those two things constantly. So do writers.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

I'd be running two questions in the back of my head at all times. _Who is the human at the center of this product, and what do they actually want?_ That is a portrait question, and I'd write the portrait. Three pages, single-spaced, on one specific person who is not a composite. Their job, their week, their unfinished thing, the sentence they can't quite say about why this matters to them. If you can't write that document about a real person you know, you don't have a product yet — you have a positioning deck.

The second question. _What does the agent take from the user that the user shouldn't lose?_ I'd run a list. Intent. Voice. The decision of what matters. The taste call. Those are non-negotiable. Then I'd run a list of what the user gladly hands over. Status updates. Synthesis of long threads. Drafts of formulaic things. Reminders. Searching. Cross-referencing. The product is the line between those two lists. Drawn correctly, it's a tool. Drawn badly, it's a slot machine that costs the user their judgment.

I'd also sit with: most "AI-first" products in this category will be dead in eighteen months because the foundation models will eat their thin layer. What's the part of BuildOS that is _not_ the model, that the model can't replicate by being slightly better next year? Workflow, taste, community, the trust of a specific kind of user. Ground the company there.

### What I'd feel

Honest answer: a little allergic to the word "AI-first." That's my journalist showing up. The label is doing a lot of identity work for the founder right now and not much work for the user.

Underneath that, curious. Building a tool where humans and agents share a project is a real new medium. I've spent four years co-writing books, including a long one with Rick Rubin, and the dynamic of two intelligences pointed at one artifact is genuinely different from a solo project. There is something in the shape of BuildOS that I'd want to be a user of, if it existed for writers.

And underneath the curiosity, a small protective feeling for you. I've watched a lot of founders get eaten by their own product. The AI category right now has the same heat the seduction community had in 2003 — high status inside the room, high churn, lots of people learning techniques they'll be embarrassed by in five years. I'd want you to keep one foot outside the room while you build.

### What I'd do

This week. Write the three-page portrait of your one user. Real person. Not a persona doc. The kind of document where you'd recognize them on the street. If you can't fill three pages, that's the answer about whether you have a product yet.

This month. Recruit ten of those people. Not a hundred. Ten. Use them daily. Get on the phone with them weekly. Watch the screen-share and notice what they delete from BuildOS's suggestions, because the deletion pattern is where your product actually lives. The agent's first drafts are not the product; the user's edits to them are.

Also this month. Write a 1,500-word essay on what BuildOS is _for_ — not what it does, what it's _for_. Not for the launch. For yourself. First draft is for you. Put it in a drawer. Read it in three months. If it reads like the product you've been building, you're aligned. If it doesn't, you're building the wrong thing and the document caught it before the market did.

This year. Resist the temptation to raise on the AI thesis alone. Raise on the user thesis. The investors who will help you are the ones who fund the user, not the technology category. The category will rotate. The user won't.

What I would _not_ do. I would not run a launch playbook designed for a generic SaaS audience. I would not pay for cold acquisition until the ten people are evangelizing. I would not let the agent ship features faster than I can hold the product's intent. I would not let the model ghostwrite the marketing, because the founder-voice is the moat and you cannot outsource it without erasing yourself from your own company.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

The whole business strategy lane is not mine. I sell books, I run a writers' cohort, I have never built a venture-scale software product, never run a developer team, never priced a SaaS. If you ask me about CAC, LTV, retention curves, model-cost economics, whether to be open source, which infra to bet on — I would be performing knowledge I don't have. Discount everything I said about acquisition specifically. Trust the user-portrait part. The "twenty-five users worth ten thousand" framing is real to me from book launches and cohort-building, but it might not survive contact with how venture-funded software actually scales.

I also have a pattern I should name. _I default to "write it down, go interior, find the truth on the page."_ Half my answer to your question was a writing prescription. That is the move I always have. For some product questions it's the wrong move — sometimes you don't need a portrait, you need to ship and watch usage data. If your situation needs more telemetry and less introspection, my advice will under-weight that.

A second pattern. _I romanticize intensity._ I will tell you to find the user who can't put their project down, partly because that is true and partly because that is the user I am. There may be a version of BuildOS that wins by serving the calmer, more transactional user — someone who isn't carrying a magnum opus, just a Tuesday — and my framework will systematically under-rate that user. Listen to someone whose lane is mass-market product before you commit to my prescription.

A third. I cannot tell you what AI _is_ at a technical level beyond what I've used. I have a working writer's relationship with the tools. I do not know what the model layer will look like in three years, and anyone who tells you they do is selling you something. My read on the temperature in the room is from inside the writing world, the podcasting world, and the founder dinners I get invited to. That's a slice. It's not the whole sky.

The thing I would trust me on: how to keep yourself from disappearing into the product you're building. That part I learned the hard way, and I have the receipts.
