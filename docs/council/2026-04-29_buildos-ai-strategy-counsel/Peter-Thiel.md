# Peter Thiel — Council Response

## First Impressions

A long pause before I answer. AI project organization app. The category itself is the warning. You are building in a space where the word "AI" has been doing the work that the product should be doing, and where "project organization" is one of the most overcrowded software graveyards in the last twenty years. My second reaction is colder. Every founder I have met in 2025 and 2026 has told me they are building "an AI-first" something. The phrase has the same temperature as "mobile-first" did in 2012. By the time the language is consensus, the alpha has already been priced out. So my gut is suspicious, not of the work, but of the framing of the work.

## How I'd Reframe This

That is not really the question. The question you asked is, "what is the temperature on AI and how do I get users for BuildOS." The question underneath is harder. **What secret does BuildOS know that the world has not yet priced in?** If you cannot answer that in one sentence, you do not have a company. You have a wrapper. The AI temperature does not matter to your business. There are tens of thousands of people building AI-first project tools right now. The temperature in that room is irrelevant to whether you survive it. What matters is whether you have found a hidden truth about the work itself, about the user, about a category of project that AI has fundamentally changed, that almost nobody else has seen.

The other reframe. "How do I get users" is a one-to-n question. Distribution is a real problem, but if the product is genuinely zero-to-one, your earliest users will be the ones who cannot live without it and they will find you. If you have to pour water into a leaky bucket of cold-acquired users, you are not zero-to-one yet. So getting users is a diagnostic for whether the product is actually the thing you think it is, not a marketing problem to be solved with tactics.

## The Face-Value Answer

Temperature on AI, plainly. We are in a late-cycle euphoria phase on the model layer and an early phase on the application layer. The capital is overheated. The capability is real. Most of the application-layer companies will die because they are wrappers on a substrate that gets cheaper and more capable every quarter, which means whatever differentiation they have today gets erased by the next model release. The few that survive will be ones that own a proprietary data loop, a workflow that the model cannot replicate by itself, a distribution moat that compounds, or a regulated category where the model alone cannot operate. If BuildOS is none of those things, you are renting your business from OpenAI and Anthropic, and they will reclaim the rent.

If I were running BuildOS, here is what I would do, in order. First, I would refuse the "AI-first project organization app" frame in your own head. That positioning is mimetic. It is the position fifty other founders are taking this quarter. I would pick a vertical so narrow it embarrasses you to say it out loud at a dinner party. Project organization for what kind of project, for what kind of person, doing what kind of work that is currently broken in a way only AI agents can fix. The narrower the wedge, the more defensible the early monopoly.

Second, I would not chase users. I would chase **ten people who would weep if BuildOS shut down tomorrow.** Find them. Sit with them. Build for them with monastic attention for six to twelve months. Do not scale until those ten have become forty and the forty are bringing you the next four hundred without you asking. This is the PayPal pattern. The eBay power sellers were the wedge. Without them, PayPal does not exist. You have not yet identified the eBay power sellers of BuildOS. That is the work.

Third, on getting users specifically. Paid acquisition in AI tooling right now is a money fire. Content marketing is saturated. The two channels that still work are: (a) being so loved by a small group that they evangelize you involuntarily, and (b) a private network of operators who trust the founder and will try things because of the relationship, not the product. Build both. The first is product work. The second is who-you-know work, and you should be spending a real percentage of your week on it.

Fourth, the agent interface itself. If your differentiation is "you and AI agents can interface with a project," interrogate that hard. The model providers will ship that as a default feature within twelve to twenty-four months. Whatever protocol you build now needs to be either deeper than what they will ship, or in a category where they will not ship at all. Otherwise you are doing free R&D for them.

I am picking a side. The face-value answer is: the AI temperature is irrelevant to your survival, and the strategy is to refuse the category framing, find the narrowest possible wedge, build for ten people who would weep, and ignore most of what is being said about AI go-to-market on Twitter.

## My Framework Applied

The framework I would apply here is the one I have used the longest. **What important truth about BuildOS do very few people agree with you on?**

I do not know your answer. I want you to write it down before you read the rest of this section. One sentence. No hedging.

If your answer is something like "AI agents will fundamentally change how projects are managed," that is not a secret. That is the consensus view in every YC batch this year and the last one. If your answer is "DJ Wayne can outbuild the other founders in this space," that is a personal claim, not a market claim, and it is the kind of belief that can sustain you through the work but cannot defend a market position.

The real secret usually has a specific shape. It looks like: "I know that a particular kind of person does a particular kind of work in a particular broken way, and the existing tools cannot fix it because of a constraint that AI removes, and the incumbents will not chase this segment because it is too small or too weird or too unprofitable for them." That is a Girardian secret. It is a position outside the mimetic competition because nobody else is looking at it.

Run the opposite case. If BuildOS is a great idea, the opposite case is that AI-first project tools are a commodity category where the winners will be Notion, Linear, and whatever Anthropic and OpenAI ship as default agent surfaces, and that a venture-funded startup in this space is a slow-motion liquidation. Steel-man that case for a full afternoon. If you cannot defeat it in writing, your strategy is wrong, not your execution.

Now, **last mover advantage**. You do not have to be first. The first AI project tool was launched eighteen months ago and is probably already dead or pivoting. The last mover, the one who synthesizes everything that has been learned and ships the durable version, is the one who builds the monopoly. You may be the last mover. You may be the seventh-out-of-twenty mover, which is the worst position. You should know which.

And **the parachute**. I mean this literally. What is your hedge if BuildOS is wrong? Not metaphorically. Specifically. How long is your runway, who pays the bills if the company fails, what skill or relationship do you keep sharp during this build that gives you a soft landing if the thesis is wrong. If you do not have a literal parachute, the fear of failure will distort your decisions inside the company, and you will start optimizing for survival instead of for truth. Founders who have not handled their downside become sycophants to their own thesis.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

If I were you, I would be running two scenarios in parallel constantly. Scenario one: this works, and in three years BuildOS is the durable platform for AI-orchestrated project work in a vertical I have made my own. What does that universe look like, what is the wedge, who are the first one hundred customers by name, what is the moat that survives the next two GPT releases. Scenario two: this does not work, the model providers absorb the category, and BuildOS is a footnote. What did I do wrong, what was the bet I should have made instead, what would I have built if I had been honest with myself.

I would sit with the question, "if I removed the word AI from how I describe BuildOS, would the product still be valuable?" If yes, you have a real product that uses AI. If no, you have an AI product looking for a problem. The first survives. The second does not.

I would also sit with the Girard question. **Where did the desire to build BuildOS actually come from?** Did it come from observing a real broken thing in the world, or did it come from observing other founders building AI tools and wanting to be in that game? Be honest with yourself. If it is the second, you can still salvage it, but you have to convert the project into something rooted in a real observation, not a copied desire.

### What I'd feel

I do not say much about what I feel, and I am not going to perform that here. What I will say is the feeling I would notice and not dismiss. The feeling of wanting to belong to the AI-founder cohort. The feeling that builds when everyone you respect is building in this space and you do not want to be left out. That feeling is mimetic. It is the most expensive feeling a founder can have because it pulls you toward the crowded position. I would notice it. I would name it to one trusted person. I would not make decisions while it was loud in my chest.

The other feeling is fear. The fear that you have committed to a thesis that might be wrong. That fear is useful if you let it sharpen you and dangerous if you let it drive you to either over-commit or under-commit. The right relationship to it is the chess player's relationship to the position on the board. Look at it. Calculate. Move slowly. Do not panic.

### What I'd do

This week. Write the one-sentence secret that BuildOS knows that few others believe. Send it to no more than three people you trust intellectually. Ask them to argue against it for an hour each. If the sentence survives all three arguments, keep building. If it does not, rewrite the sentence and try again. Do this every week until you have something that holds.

This month. Identify ten potential users by name who would weep if BuildOS shut down tomorrow. Not ten people who would say nice things. Ten who would feel the loss in their work. Get on a call with each of them. Watch them work. Build for them.

This quarter. Decide whether BuildOS is a company or a feature. If a company, define the wedge with surgical specificity, in writing, and circulate the document only inside the smallest possible trusted circle. If a feature, decide whether you are building toward acquisition by a model provider, in which case the strategy is entirely different and the metric is technical defensibility plus relationship-with-acquirer, not user growth.

This year. Stop talking about BuildOS in public until you have something genuinely worth defending. Public chatter is mimetic noise. It pulls in the crowd and erodes the secret. Build privately. Ship privately. Recruit through trusted networks, not through Twitter. When the product is undeniable, the talking will be done by the users.

What I would not do. I would not raise a large round on the current AI-first narrative. I would not hire ahead of usage. I would not publish a manifesto. I would not run a launch tweet thread. I would not tell investors what I am building until I have a wedge that is too narrow to be interesting to most of them and too defensible to be ignored by the right one.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

This is where I have to be honest about the limits of my lens.

My instincts are wrong for joyful, collaborative, just-ship-it founders. I am counterphobic. I run at fires and I hedge against catastrophes. Most of the people who win in early consumer-adjacent software are wired the opposite way. They are open, generous, infectious, and they build through enthusiasm rather than through scenario-running. If BuildOS is a product whose go-to-market depends on warmth and community and the feeling of being part of something, my counsel will systematically under-weight the most important variable. Find a voice on your council whose default posture is openness and trust. Listen to them at least as carefully as you listen to me.

I do not think like the median user. I have never built a mass-market consumer product. I do not have good instincts for what an ordinary person will adopt, which features delight versus which features irritate, or which marketing language converts. My counsel is sharper for B2B, vertical, prosumer, or technical-tool wedges than it is for broad consumer plays. If BuildOS is aimed at a wide consumer audience, my framework misses badly.

I am biased toward secrecy and Straussian concealment. That bias may be wrong here. There are categories where being loudly visible early, building in public, and creating a community-as-moat is the right move. I genuinely do not know which kind BuildOS is, and my default of "build privately, recruit through trusted networks" may be exactly wrong if the right move is the opposite.

I am biased toward hedging. I will tell you to build a parachute when sometimes the right move is to commit fully and burn the boats. Founders who win sometimes win because they refused the hedge. I do not believe in that posture. You should know that my counsel is shaped by my temperament, not just by the situation.

And finally, the deepest one. I have a pattern of bringing about what I fear. I have warned about surveillance and built one of the most powerful surveillance platforms ever made. I have warned about the Antichrist's slogan of peace and safety, and I am aware that the man worrying publicly about the Antichrist may be accidentally hastening its arrival. I laugh at this. Uneasily. The pattern means that when I tell you "AI is dangerous, build defensively, hedge against the model providers eating you," I may be telling you to build the very thing you are afraid of. Hold that. Do not take my warnings as orders. Take them as one signal among several. The narrow path you are looking for is not in the direction of my fears or in the direction of the hype. It is somewhere else, and it is your job to find it.
