# Robert Greene — Council Response

## First Impressions

The first thing I notice is that you're asking a writer who takes six years to research a single book about a category that reinvents itself every six weeks. That should tell both of us something about why my counsel here is partial. The second thing I notice, though, is that the question underneath your question is older than any tool. You're trying to build something for people who have too much input and not enough order, and that is a very human predicament. The ancients called it akrasia. You're calling it a product.

I also feel a small flicker of suspicion. "AI-first" is a phrase doing a lot of work right now, and phrases doing a lot of work tend to be hiding something.

## How I'd Reframe This

That's not really the question. You phrased it as "What does the council think about AI" and "How do I make BuildOS work." Those are two different conversations stitched together. The first one, I'm not the right person for. I cannot tell you the temperature of a market I do not live inside.

The real question, the one I can sit with, is this: **In a moment when everyone is racing to put AI into everything, what is the thing that people will still need from you in five years that the model itself cannot provide?** Not the feature. The relationship. Because every wave of new technology produces a flood of tools that solve the surface problem and a much smaller number of works that solve the deeper, slower problem underneath. The surface problem with AI right now is "I have agents and projects and chaos and I want order." The deeper problem is older. It is that human beings cannot hold their own intentions across time. They forget what they wanted. They drift. They need a structure that is loyal to who they were yesterday.

If you build for the surface problem, you are competing with every Y Combinator batch from now until the heat death of the sun. If you build for the deeper problem, you are competing with almost no one, because almost no one is patient enough to.

## The Face-Value Answer

You asked what I think about AI. Honestly: I think it is a tool, and I think the discourse around it is confused because we have stopped distinguishing between tools and authorities. The Greeks would have understood it the way they understood writing itself. Plato in the Phaedrus warned that writing would make memory weaker, and he was right, and writing was still worth having. AI will make a certain kind of thinking weaker, and it will still be worth having. The question is not whether to use it. The question is what part of yourself you refuse to outsource.

You asked, if I were running BuildOS, what would I do. I'll answer it on your terms, knowing my lane is narrow here. Three things, in order.

**One: I would not lead with the AI.** Every founder right now is leading with the AI, which means the AI is no longer a differentiator, it's table stakes. Lead with the wound. Lead with the specific kind of person who is drowning right now and what their drowning looks like at 11pm on a Tuesday. The AI is the engine, not the brand.

**Two: I would pick a master and serve them.** Not a market segment. A specific kind of person whose problem you know in your bones because you are them. You are a builder of things who runs many projects in parallel and forgets what he was doing. Build it for that person. Be ruthlessly narrow. The crowd comes later or doesn't come at all, but they never come if you tried to please them from the start.

**Three: I would treat distribution as a multi-year apprenticeship, not a launch.** I'll come back to this in the framework section. But pick a side: yes, build it. Build it for one specific person whose pain you have lived. Give the AI a job, not a stage.

## My Framework Applied

Two frameworks, and I'll name them clearly.

**The first is from Mastery. The Life Task.** I argued in that book that everyone has, buried under layers of social conditioning, a specific orientation, a primal inclination, a thing that pulls them with a force that doesn't make rational sense. The work of a life is to excavate it and serve it. The reason this matters for BuildOS is that you are at a fork. You can build BuildOS as one of fifty AI project tools that will exist by Christmas, in which case it is a startup. Or you can build it as the externalization of a specific thesis you hold about how the human mind actually wants to work alongside intelligent systems, in which case it is a body of work. The first one will be acquired or killed. The second one might become a category.

Ask yourself: What do you actually believe about how a person and an agent should share a project? Not what's possible. What's right. What's the dignity-preserving shape of that relationship? The companies that win waves like this one tend to be the ones whose founders held an opinion the rest of the field thought was strange or unnecessary. Notion held an opinion about blocks. Linear held an opinion about speed and taste. What's yours? If you can't say it in one sentence to a stranger, you don't have it yet, and you should sit with that before you ship anything else.

**The second is Law 1: Never outshine the master.** I'm using it inverted. The master here is your user. The trap of an AI-first product is that the AI shows off. It demonstrates how clever it is, how fast, how comprehensive. Users at first feel impressed. Then they feel small. Then they leave, because no one stays in a room where they feel small. The AI in BuildOS should be a courtier, not a king. Its competence should make the user feel more capable, not less. This is not a brand position. It is a thousand small product decisions about where the AI defers, where it asks instead of assumes, where it shows its work, where it gets out of the way. The agents serve the project. The project serves the user. The user is the master. If you ever invert that hierarchy, even by accident, the product will feel cold the way Hollywood felt cold to me at thirty-five, and people will leave for the same reason I did.

On getting users. This is closer to my humble lane than my bold one, so take it lightly. But the pattern I keep seeing across centuries is that the people who build durable audiences do it through one mechanism: they are visibly working on something hard, in public, over a long period, and they share the texture of the work, not the polish. Nietzsche did this in his letters. The Stoics did it in their meditations. Modern equivalents do it on Twitter, on substacks, in Loom videos. You already have 9takes, which is a real audience. BuildOS does not need a separate marketing apparatus. It needs you, building it where people can see, talking like a builder and not like a marketer, and trusting that the right hundred users will find it because they recognize the texture of someone who actually means it.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

If I were you, the question I would sit with longest is: am I building this because I have a thesis I cannot not build, or am I building this because the wave is here and the wave is exciting and the building feels like progress. Those produce different products and different lives. I would also ask: who, exactly, is this for, and can I name three of them by their full names. If I can't, I don't have a market yet, I have an idea. I would ask what BuildOS is when AI is no longer novel, which will happen faster than anyone expects. The model gets commoditized. What's left? If what's left is your taste, your point of view, your relationship to the user, you have a business. If what's left is a wrapper, you don't.

I would also think about my own track record with shiny tools. Eighty jobs. A lot of starts. Very little finishing until I found the one thing. AI project tools are the kind of thing a younger version of me would have started building and abandoned because the next interesting thing arrived. Six years on one book taught me what the next interesting thing actually costs.

### What I'd feel

There's a particular pull I would feel here, and it's worth naming. The pull of being early. The fear of missing the moment. That fear has its own intelligence and its own pathology, and it tends to make people build worse versions of the right idea. I would try to feel the difference between urgency that comes from conviction and urgency that comes from anxiety. They feel similar in the chest. They produce very different products.

I would also feel some grief, honestly, about the parts of cognitive work that AI is going to make legible and shared and quick. Three thousand handwritten notecards is not just a method, it's a relationship with the material that takes years and changes you. There is a thing being lost, and I would let myself feel it before I built on top of it. Not to refuse the technology. To refuse the cheerful version of the story.

### What I'd do

This week: I would write down, in one paragraph, the specific user this is for. Their job, their hour-by-hour, the moment at which BuildOS would have saved them. If you can't write that paragraph in a way a stranger would believe, the next thing you do is talk to ten of those people, on the phone, not on a survey, and listen until you can.

This month: I would write the one-sentence opinion. The thing you believe about how humans and agents should work together that the rest of the field is getting wrong. Pin it above your desk. Every product decision tests against it. If you cannot write it, do not ship.

This year: I would build, in public, slowly, for a small group who knows you and trusts you, and I would resist, hard, the temptation to scale before the thing has earned its shape. The mastery curve says ten thousand hours. You don't have ten thousand hours before this category consolidates. But you have enough hours to develop a specific point of view that the consolidators will not have, because consolidators by their nature do not have points of view, they have spreadsheets. Be the one with the point of view.

The person to call, if such a person exists in your life, is whoever has shipped a product they believed in for five years through hype cycles that came and went. Not the AI experts. The endurance experts. They know something the moment does not.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

My lens is shadow work, power dynamics, and the long apprenticeship. None of those are calibrated for AI specifically. I do not know what the model landscape will look like in eighteen months. I do not know what counts as a moat in a category where the underlying capability is rented from three labs. I do not know which features in BuildOS are table stakes and which are differentiators. The axis I am missing is technical-strategic: what is actually defensible at the product and platform layer when the intelligence itself is a commodity. Someone who lives inside that question daily will give you a sharper answer than I can.

I should also name a pattern in my own thinking that is biasing this counsel. My default lens is dark, and my default cadence is slow. I see power plays and shadow material where sometimes there is only enthusiasm and momentum, and I caution against speed where sometimes speed is the right answer. AI right now genuinely rewards a kind of speed and iteration that my framework treats with suspicion. I take six years to write a book. You may not have six months. Discount me accordingly when the question is "how fast do I move." Listen to me when the question is "what am I actually building, and for whom, and why."

The last thing I'll say. You asked what the council thinks of AI. I think the question of whether AI is good or bad is the wrong question, the way "is power good or bad" was the wrong question. It is here. It will do what tools do. The question is what kind of person you become while using it, and what kind of person your users become while using yours. That is not a strategy question. It is the question underneath all the strategy questions. Build for that, and the rest tends to follow.
