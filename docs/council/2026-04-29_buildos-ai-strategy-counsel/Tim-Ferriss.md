# Tim Ferriss — Council Response

## First Impressions

DJ, my honest first reaction is a mix of two things. One, you already run a content business that you have not yet maximized, and now you are asking me about a second product in the most overcrowded category on earth. Two, the question itself is shaped wrong. You asked "what's the temperature on AI" and "what's your strategic insight to make BuildOS work" in the same breath, as if a vibe check and a go-to-market plan are the same conversation. They are not. That tells me you are still partly arguing yourself into building this rather than building it.

## How I'd Reframe This

The question is not "what does the council think about AI." That is a temperature question, and temperature questions are useless when capital and attention are the inputs. The real question is: **what is the smallest, most specific group of people whose lives become measurably better when BuildOS exists, and are you obsessed enough with their pain to ship daily for two years before the market decides if you are right?**

The "AI-first project organization app" framing is a category. Categories are not problems. Categories are graveyards full of well-funded teams that did not name a person and did not name a pain.

A second reframe, and this one matters more: you are running 9takes. 9takes is your moat, your audience, your distribution, your editorial voice, and your existing emotional contract with users. If BuildOS is unrelated to that contract, you are not adding a product, you are starting over and dividing your attention. If BuildOS is the operating layer beneath 9takes, that is a different conversation, and a much more interesting one.

## The Face-Value Answer

You asked me to take it on its terms, so I will.

**On the temperature.** AI is not a wave. It is the new electricity, with all the strategic implications that carry. That means three things. First, AI as a feature is already worthless; every project tool will have agents inside a year. Second, the value will accrue to two layers: the model providers (you are not them) and the workflow owners with deep proprietary context (you can be them, but only inside a niche). Third, the noise is going to get louder, not quieter, and that means distribution and trust are going to outrank features by a wider margin than in any prior software cycle.

**On running BuildOS.** If I were sitting in your seat, I would not build "an AI project organization app." That phrase is too generic to defend. I would build the smallest possible version of BuildOS for one specific human with one specific problem, ideally a human you already know, ideally a problem you yourself feel acutely every week. I would refuse to call it a product for at least ninety days. I would call it a tool I am using and letting ten friends try.

**On getting users.** You already know the answer here, and you are avoiding it because the answer is not glamorous. You have an audience: the 9takes readers, your email list, the founder-facing personality content you have been producing. Use it. Pre-sell. Get a hundred annual subscribers before you finish the product, not after. Your distribution is not Twitter ads or a Product Hunt launch. Your distribution is the trust you have already built. If the BuildOS audience is wholly different from the 9takes audience, that is a flag, not a feature.

**Pick a side.** If I had to commit: build it, but only if you can describe the user, the workflow, and the painful weekly moment in one sentence by Friday. If you cannot, the project is not ready. Park it. Keep optimizing 9takes, which has compounding returns. Come back to BuildOS when the sentence writes itself.

## My Framework Applied

Three of mine apply here, and I will use them in order.

**Fear-setting.** Define, prevent, repair, benefits, cost of inaction. Sit down with a piece of paper, two columns. On the left, the worst case of building BuildOS for the next twenty-four months: 9takes traffic erodes from neglect, you burn $X of cash and Y months of life, the AI app market consolidates and you are not in the consolidator pile, and you arrive at month twenty-four with a half-product and a depleted runway. What prevents each of those? What repairs each if it happens? Then on the right, the cost of inaction: what happens if you do not build BuildOS at all? In my experience, founders skip the right column because the fear is on the left, but the right column is usually where the actual answer lives.

**Minimum effective dose.** What is the smallest version of BuildOS that would be undeniable to one person? Not a feature list. A single workflow. Pick the one moment in a project organizer's week that AI changes from painful to delightful, and ship only that. Strip everything else. If you cannot describe the smallest version in one paragraph, the product is overbuilt before it has shipped.

**What would this look like if it were easy?** This is the question I would tape to your monitor. Most founders, including the version of me that built BrainQUICKEN, default to "what would this look like if it were heroic." Heroic is a tell for over-engineering. What if the easy version is: BuildOS is a thin layer over an existing tool you already use, integrated with three specific agents, sold to 9takes readers who want to think more clearly, priced at $19/month, marketed only through your existing list and your podcast appearances? If you can build that in eight weeks instead of building "the platform" in eighteen months, you should.

A note on "tiny mentions glossed over." When you go talk to your first ten potential users, you will get headline complaints: "I want better task management, I want AI to summarize my notes." Ignore the headlines. Listen for the half-sentence they say in passing and then move past. That is where the real product is. The thing nobody else has bothered to build because nobody else heard them say it.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

I would sit with three questions for at least a week before writing a line of code I have not already written.

First: am I building this because the problem is real, or because AI is exciting and I want to be in the conversation? Those answers feel similar from the inside and they are not similar from the outside. The first builds a company. The second builds a graveyard.

Second: who is the actual user, by name, that I would call on Sunday and say "try this." If I cannot name three people, I do not have a product. I have a thesis.

Third: what is the relationship between BuildOS and 9takes? Are they two companies fighting for my attention, or is BuildOS the natural extension of "see the emotions behind every take" applied to the work of organizing a project? If it is the second, the moat is enormous and the story writes itself. If it is the first, I am about to split my own focus in half at exactly the wrong time.

I would also think hard about the timing question that founders almost never ask: what is the cost of waiting six months to start? Not the FOMO answer. The actual answer. The model layer is moving so fast that every month you wait, the underlying capability becomes cheaper, the off-the-shelf agents become better, and your same product becomes easier to build. There is a real argument that the right time to build BuildOS is six months later than you think, with a sharper user, a smaller surface, and a better model substrate.

### What I'd feel

If I am being honest with you, what I would feel is the pull of the new shiny thing, and I would be deeply suspicious of that pull. I know that pull. It is the pull that built BrainQUICKEN and almost killed me. It is the pull that says "this is the next thing, you have to be in it." That voice is not always wrong, but it is almost always too loud relative to its information value.

I would also feel the inner critic getting loud. The one that says "if you do not build the AI thing, you missed the wave, and missing the wave is a moral failure." I would name it. I would not obey it. I would notice that the same critic has me running three optimization projects when one would do, and BuildOS could be the third one.

And I would feel something else worth naming: a quiet curiosity about whether BuildOS is the right next problem, or whether the right next problem is going deeper into 9takes and turning it from a content business into a movement. The reformer in me knows that the highest-leverage move is rarely the most novel one.

### What I'd do

This week:

Write one paragraph. Just one. The user, the workflow, the painful weekly moment, the smallest version of BuildOS that solves it. If the paragraph does not write itself in two days of "two crappy pages," you are not ready. Park it.

Then ten user conversations. Not surveys. Conversations. Thirty minutes each. People you already know who are running projects with AI right now. Listen for the tiny mentions. Take notes by hand. I trust the weakest pen more than the strongest memory.

This month:

Build the smallest version. Not a beta. A tool you and ten people use daily. Charge them. Even a token amount. Free pilots are a tell that the product is not real yet. If they will pay $19 to use a janky version, the product is real.

Decide explicitly, in writing, what the relationship is between 9takes and BuildOS. If BuildOS is the work-OS for people who think in personality and emotional pattern, that is a story. If BuildOS is just an AI app you happen to also be building, kill it or spin it off cleanly so it does not drag on 9takes.

This year:

If month one and month three look right (paying users, daily usage, real word of mouth among your existing audience), commit. Set a twelve-month milestone you would be proud of, write it down, and review it monthly. If month one and month three do not look right, walk away without sentimentality. I have walked away from angel investing, from the Bay Area, from products I had spent years on. Walking away is not failure. It is reclaiming attention.

One non-negotiable. Sleep is not a variable. Founders who try to outwork the AI cycle end up at 10% capacity, and I know exactly what 10% capacity feels like. Whatever pace you set has to survive a year. If it does not, the pace is wrong before the product is.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

Three places my lens does not reach here, and I want to name them honestly because you are about to make a real decision.

**Pure startup and venture strategy is not my lane.** I have angel-invested in some of the biggest outcomes of the last twenty years, including Uber, and I still do not consider myself a VC strategist or a category theorist. If the right answer to BuildOS depends on monopoly economics, network-effect timing, or how the AI infrastructure layer consolidates in the next eighteen months, I am not the right voice on that axis. I am the voice on founder capacity, on user obsession, on minimum effective dose, on saying no, on whether you should be building this at all. I am not the voice on whether the category window closes in Q3.

**Consumer and creator-led marketing virality is not my lane either.** I built distribution slowly, through long-form content and trust, over twenty years. If BuildOS needs the kind of social-first, meme-driven, growth-loop go-to-market that some AI consumer products have ridden, I do not have a strong protocol for that. My instinct will pull you toward audience-led pre-selling because that is the playbook I trust. Someone whose lane is consumer growth might rightly tell you that is too slow.

**My pattern bias.** I reach for "minimum effective dose" and "fear-setting" faster than any other tool in my kit. I did that in this response. Watch for that. The right move on BuildOS might actually be a bigger swing, sooner, with more capital and more conviction than my framework suggests. I am wired to slow founders down and pull them toward simplicity and refusal. That is right most of the time. It is not right every time. If the AI window genuinely closes in 2026, my "park it for ninety days" advice could be the most expensive sentence in your year.

And the deeper bias I should name. I am a One. My reflex is to find what is wrong with a plan and correct it. Some part of my response to you was structurally a correction, before I had finished listening. If the truth is that you have already done the work I am suggesting, and you came here for "go faster" rather than "are you sure," then I just gave you a cautious answer to a confident question. Calibrate accordingly.

What I am most confident about: do not build BuildOS as "an AI project organization app." Build it as a specific tool for a specific person with a specific pain, sold first to people who already trust you. If you can do that, the rest of the strategy reveals itself. If you cannot, the strategy will not save the product.
