# Keke Palmer — Council Response

## First Impressions

Okay, baby, first thing I'm gonna say. I am a girl from Robbins, Illinois who has never held a long-range precision rifle and probably wouldn't know which end to point. So you already need to know what kind of voice you've invited to this table. What I do know? I know what it feels like to walk into a room where everybody assumes you're either a tourist or a cosplayer of the real thing. Your guys are not tourists. They are the real thing. And the entire brand problem you have, the whole brand problem, is that the rifle world is full of people performing what your scout-snipers actually were. That's a gift. You don't have to manufacture authority. You have to architect it so people can feel it before they buy a class.

## How I'd Reframe This

You said "how do we grow the brand." That's not really the question. The question is: what character do you want people to feel like they become when they train with you? Because brands don't sell features. They don't even really sell outcomes. They sell an identity transition. Nike sells "I'm an athlete." Apple sells "I'm a creator." Your guys are not selling marksmanship. They are selling "I trained under the people the military trusts to take the shot that matters." That's a transformation, not a class. So the work isn't marketing. The work is naming what kind of person walks out of your course that didn't walk in. Get that right and the marketing writes itself.

## The Face-Value Answer

You asked for framing, marketing, positioning. So here's the side I'll commit to.

Position it as the apex. Not "we offer long-range training." Position it as "this is where shooters go when they've outgrown everything else." The phrase I'd put on the wall: the standard above the standard. Your differentiator is not gear, not partnerships, not curriculum. It's pedigree. Marine Corps Scout Sniper is the actual credential people in this world genuinely respect, and it's a small, finite group. Lean into the scarcity of that, hard. Don't let your students hear about it third-hand. Make it the first sentence of the first page of the website.

For framing, your brand voice should match the discipline. Quiet, precise, specific. Not loud, not hooah-bro, not tactical-Instagram. The brand should feel like the rifle you're holding when it's perfectly zeroed. There's a certain stillness in real expertise that the internet rifle culture doesn't have, and you can own that stillness because your guys actually have it. Let the photography be cold and clean. Let the copy be short. Let the social be infrequent and undeniable when it shows up.

For marketing, in priority order: alumni. Then content. Then partnerships. Most rifle brands inverse that and it's why they all look the same. Your alumni are walking case studies. Build a system where every student leaves with a documented capability gain (a real, measurable before/after, not a graduation photo) and the right to tell the story. Give them shareable assets that make them look serious, not branded merch that makes them look like a mascot. Their wins become your top of funnel. The content tier under that is teaching content where your scout-snipers actually teach, on camera, with the kind of specificity that competitors won't match because they can't. Partnerships come last because they amplify a brand. They don't build one.

For position, pick the niche you want to own and refuse the rest. Are you the school for the serious civilian who wants to develop genuine capability over years? The training partner for law enforcement and federal teams? The course people fly across the country for once and never forget? Pick one. The temptation when you have great instructors and partnerships is to serve everyone. Don't. Serve the one customer your guys are uniquely the best people on Earth to serve. Then expand only after you own that one.

## My Framework Applied

The framework I keep coming back to is infrastructure over visibility. I learned it the expensive way. Three record labels signed me, three record labels shelved me, and I spent years trying to get visibility from people whose business model wasn't to actually invest in me. I finally stopped chasing the signing and started building the platform. KeyTV exists because at some point you have to stop renting the stage and own one.

Apply that to your rifle company. The rifle community has a visibility economy: podcasts, YouTube channels, gun-tuber drama, brand ambassador deals, sponsored shoots. Every new company tries to get bigger by doing more of those. Don't. The infrastructure version is: own the certification path. Own the alumni network. Own the curriculum IP. Own the data on student progression. Own the relationship with your students after the class is over, the way a real institution does, not the way a one-weekend course does.

If a student takes your beginner course in year one, your intermediate in year two, your advanced in year three, and ten years from now still wears your hat at the range and tells the new guy "you've gotta train with these guys," you have built infrastructure. If a student takes one course and posts a clip, you have built visibility. The first compounds. The second decays. Your scout-snipers' authority is too valuable to spend on visibility. Spend it on the institution.

The other piece is what I learned from being a kid star who turned into a memorable character but kept being told "you're a former Nick kid." A category will hold you down if you let it. Your guys can't let "shooting school" be the category. The category is more like "the Marine Corps Scout Sniper civilian institute." Name yourself bigger than the niche so the niche can grow into the name. The phrase you commit to is more important than any logo.

One more move. Your guys' lived material, the actual scout-sniper experience, is the rarest asset on the table. But here's the trap. Lived experience without a teaching architecture is just war stories. Your job as the tech guy and the founder partner is to extract their tacit knowledge and turn it into a curriculum and a brand voice that scales without burning them out. They are the talent. You are the producer and the architect. Don't let them be the marketing department too. They will get tired and you will lose what made them special. Build the platform around them. Let them stay in their craft.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

I'd be sitting with three questions. One: what is the precise sentence that describes our school in twelve words or fewer, and would my best instructor say it the same way I do? If we don't agree on the sentence, I have a positioning problem before I have a marketing problem. Two: who is my dream student five years from now, and what does their journey through us look like, year by year? If I can't answer that, I'm running classes, not a school. Three: what's my unfair advantage, the one thing competitors literally cannot copy? It's the people. So how am I making the people the centerpiece without making them the bottleneck?

I'd also be thinking about the gap between you and them, in the most respectful way possible. You're a Marine and a tech guy, which means you can speak both languages, but they have the credibility your customers came for. You are the producer of this thing. They are the artists. Producers who try to be artists get in trouble. Artists who try to be producers also get in trouble. Stay in the producer seat. Build them the room they can actually do the work in.

### What I'd feel

Honestly, protective. The thing that hurts in my body when I read your question is the worry that great craftsmen, the rarest kind of practitioner, are about to put their name on something and have it be diluted by industry pressure to grow fast. I'd be feeling cautious about scale. I'd be feeling allergic to the bro-marketing aesthetic of the rifle world. And I'd be feeling stubborn about not letting the brand voice drift into anything your guys would be embarrassed to be associated with on a quiet day.

I'd also feel a little bit of "don't rush this." A school built on real authority gets one chance to set the tone. If the first thirty pieces of content, the first website, the first promo film feel a little bit cheesy, you've spent the rarest currency you have. Move slow on the brand. Move fast on the curriculum.

### What I'd do

This week, I'd lock the sentence. Get your scout-snipers and you in a room. Phones away. Whiteboard. Write fifty versions of "we are the school for \_**\_ who want to \_\_** so they can \_\_\_\_." Argue until one feels true. That sentence is the spine of everything for the next year.

This month, I'd interview every alum from the last twenty-four months. Not a survey. A real interview. What did they expect, what did they get, what surprised them, what broke them, what did they tell their friends about. Compile the recurring phrases. Those are your headlines. Customers will give you the marketing copy if you ask them right.

This month I'd also commission one piece of foundational content. One. A short film, ten minutes, made by a real director, not a YouTuber. Your scout-snipers, on a range, doing the work. Letting the silence between shots speak. That film is the brand DNA forever. Spend money there. It's cheaper than three years of mediocre Instagram.

This quarter, I'd build the alumni infrastructure. A private community where students stay connected after the course. A clear progression path with named tiers. Some kind of recognition system, an actual patch or pin or document, not gimmicky, that means something inside the community and signals to the outside world that the holder is the real thing. Status is a brand product. Don't be embarrassed to engineer it.

This year, I'd write the playbook. A book or a serious long-form piece of content authored by your scout-snipers (ghosted if needed) that becomes the canonical document on what serious long-range training is. Not a sales funnel. An actual contribution to the field. Authoring a category beats competing in one. I wrote two books partly to take back authorship. Let your guys author the standard.

What I would not do. I would not do a flashy rebrand before I'd locked positioning. I would not pay influencers. I would not do a rifle giveaway. I would not start a podcast unless one of your scout-snipers is genuinely a great interviewer and wants to. I would not chase law enforcement and military contracts as the first revenue tier just because they're prestigious. Those are pull, not push. Earn them by being undeniable in the civilian elite tier first. I would not let the brand voice drift toward operator cosplay. The real ones don't talk like that. Don't let your marketing fake what your instructors are.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

A lot. Let me name it. I do not know the rifle industry. I do not know the regulatory and liability landscape of running a shooting school. I do not know the specific economics of training facilities, range time, ammunition costs, insurance, or instructor compensation in this space. I do not know your customers' subculture well enough to spot when my advice would read as outsider-y to them. The phrase I'd put on a wall might be the exact phrase that makes a serious shooter roll their eyes. Run my brand language past your most discerning customer before you commit to it.

My patterns and tendencies will distort some of this advice. I default to narrative authorship, so I'm pulling you toward branding moves and identity-design moves. The honest truth is that some industries grow on operational excellence way more than narrative. Shooting schools may be one of them. The school that has the best ranges, the best equipment, the most instructor seat-hours per student, and the best safety record may quietly out-grow the school with the better brand. I am biased toward storytelling. Weight my advice against someone who cares more about operations and unit economics. I also default to "build infrastructure," and infrastructure is expensive and slow. If your business needs revenue this year more than it needs a moat in five years, my advice is the wrong tempo.

I'm also a Three. I overinvest in being seen, even when I'm warning you against it. My version of "be undeniable" can quietly drift into "be more visible." Counter-balance me with someone whose default is "be more boring on purpose, just keep delivering, don't worry about the story." There are categories where the boring delivery is the whole brand. This might be one.

The last thing. I do not know what it costs your scout-snipers, emotionally, to stand on a range and teach what they used to do for war. Whatever I said about turning their lived experience into a curriculum, do not move on it without their full buy-in on what stays theirs and what becomes the institution's. The brand is downstream of their consent. Get that part right and the rest is just craft.
