# Kristen Bell — Council Response

## First Impressions

My honest first reaction is that I am not the obvious person to put in this room. I do not shoot. I do not know the difference between two rifle barrels. My nervous system reads "long-range precision rifle" and goes mildly sympathetic before it goes anywhere else, and I want to be transparent about that so you can weight what I say accordingly. What I do recognize, instantly, is the shape of the people you are describing. Marines. Snipers. Men who have done the work, who teach the work, and who are now trying to translate "we are exceptional at a thing" into "we are a brand." That's a translation I have watched go badly more times than I can count. So my gut is: be careful with the translation. The credibility is already there. Branding is what you wrap around it, not what you replace it with.

## How I'd Reframe This

I think you asked a marketing question. I don't think it is a marketing question yet. The actual question underneath is: what kind of company do you want this to be for the people who already know you, and who do you want to invite in next without diluting them? Long-range precision shooting has a small, deeply credentialed core, and a much larger, much greener perimeter of people who are buying the aesthetic faster than they are buying the skill. You will get pulled toward the perimeter by every growth instinct you have as a tech guy. The reframe is: pick the village first. Decide who you're for, who you're not, and what you refuse to do for either. The marketing will come out of that almost on its own.

## The Face-Value Answer

You asked how to frame, position, and market it. My side, picked: position around the instructors, not the gear. The category is awash in companies positioned around products and influencers. What you actually have, and what almost nobody else can replicate, is real Marine Corps Scout Sniper instructors teaching civilians to shoot at a level the civilian world does not have meaningful access to. That is the moat. Make the humans the brand. Long-form video of them teaching, written field notes from them, named curricula tied to specific instructors, students documented over a year of growth. The course is the product. Everything else is downstream of how good the course is and how clearly the world can see who teaches it.

On framing, I would not lead with "tactical." That word is tired and the perimeter crowd has made it cringe. Lead with precision, patience, discipline, and earned skill. The people you want will read that as respect for the craft. The people you don't want will not show up.

On partnerships, I would tighten. You said you have a bunch. A bunch is usually a sign that you said yes to almost everyone. Audit them. Keep the ones whose products your instructors would actually use on a personal rifle and would pay for if you were not partnered. Quietly let the rest go. Affiliation dilutes when it is not curated.

On marketing channel, the unsexy answer: long, well-produced video and writing on owned channels, with a real email list. Not because it is trendy. Because your audience is exactly the kind of audience that reads carefully, watches twenty-minute videos at full attention, and respects depth. Short-form has its place, but if you live there you will get matched with everyone else living there.

## My Framework Applied

The framework I keep reaching for here is the one I built into Hello Bello: I will not do business that does not have a service layer. Not because every company needs a charity arm, but because a service layer forces you to answer the question "who is this actually for, and what do we owe them?" That answer becomes the spine of the brand. Without it, you drift toward whatever is converting that quarter.

Applied to your company, the service layer is staring you in the face. Veterans. Specifically, combat veterans, scout snipers transitioning out, law enforcement marksmen, and the grown children and spouses of all of the above. Build something formal here. A scholarship seat in every class for a transitioning service member. A free or deeply subsidized advanced course for active scout snipers in the off-cycle. A real, named partnership with a vetted veteran mental health organization that your customers can see and donate to alongside you. Not a logo on a hat. A line item in your P&L.

Two things happen when you do this. First, your instructors get to keep doing the thing the Marine Corps formed them to do, which is to develop other shooters with intention. That keeps them sharp and keeps them whole. Second, your customers, the high-end civilian shooters who are buying premium rifles and chasing precision as a craft, are almost always people who want their hobby to mean something. You give them a way to belong to something bigger than their own group at the range. That is what turns a class buyer into a lifetime customer.

The other framework I would borrow, and this one is from my actual operating system, is what I call analyzing the village. Look at every relationship you currently have in this business — instructors, partners, returning students, the press contacts you have, the voices in your niche — and ask honestly which of them would still show up for you if you were not paying them. Invest deeply in those. Be polite and underweight to the rest. Brands at your stage live or die by the strength of the inner ring, not the size of the outer one.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

If I were you, I would be running a few questions in my head on repeat. What is the one thing we want our students to be able to truthfully say about us five years from now that nobody else in the category can say? Who are the three people in this niche whose respect we actually want, and what would earn it? What is the line between honoring the warrior craft and aestheticizing it for people who haven't done the work? That last one is the question I would not let go of. I think the brands in this space that go wrong, go wrong there.

I would also be asking, very specifically, what role you play. You said you are the tech guy and they are the operators. The brand needs the operators to be the face. It also needs you to be honest with yourself about whether you are building a media business that happens to teach shooting, or a teaching business that uses media to grow. Different shapes. Different staffing. Different metrics. Pick one and let the other be a side effect.

### What I'd feel

Honestly, I would feel some hesitation about the category, and I'd want to name that to myself before it leaked into the work. Firearms culture in the broader American conversation is not always the version of firearms culture you are actually building. The brand you make has to be confident enough that people in your world don't have to defend you, and grounded enough that people outside your world cannot easily caricature you. That is a real burden and I would not pretend otherwise.

I would also feel protective of the instructors. These are men who have been trained and shaped by something heavy. I would worry about a brand machine asking them to perform online in ways that are not native to who they are. The pull will be there, especially as growth signals start to come in. I would want a written agreement with myself, early, about what we will and will not ask them to do for content.

### What I'd do

This week, I would write down, in one document, the three sentences. Sentence one: who this company is for. Sentence two: who this company is not for. Sentence three: what we will refuse to do, even if it grows the business. Hand it to your instructor partners. If they push back on a word, that is the most important conversation you will have this quarter.

This month, I would build the service-layer commitment formally. Pick the veteran organization. Pick the scholarship structure. Put a number on it. Announce it once, on your own channels, without fanfare, in plain language, and then never lead with it again. Let students discover it.

I would also do an honest audit of every partnership and start having the conversations to tighten the list. This will be uncomfortable. It is supposed to be.

This year, I would invest in one signature piece of long-form content that the niche will pass around for the next three years. A documentary-style series on a single course, start to finish, with one student followed in detail. Real. Slow. Patient. The kind of thing that signals to your perimeter that you do not chase, and signals to your core that you understand the craft. Hire a real director. Pay them well. Do it once, do it right, and let it work for you for a long time.

Across all of it, I would resist the reflex to optimize for monthly social engagement metrics. In a niche this credentialed, depth compounds. Volume does not.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

A lot, here, and I want to be specific. I don't shoot. I don't know your customer. I have never sat through a precision rifle course, and I do not know the actual texture of the conversations between an instructor and a student at hour eight of day three. The advice I am giving is structural, and structural advice from outside the practice can miss the part that matters most.

I also don't know the economics of this category. I don't know whether scaling instruction is even desirable, or whether premium tightness at low volume is the better business. I have a strong instinct that it is the latter, but instinct is not a financial model. Someone with category-specific revenue experience would be sharper here than I am.

The pattern bias I should name: I default to a service-layer answer because that is how I built the one company I co-founded, and because helping is so deeply wired in me that I genuinely cannot see a business without one. That can be a real insight, and it can also be me projecting my operating system onto someone else's. If your instructors hear "scholarship seats" and feel like it makes the business about something other than the craft they came to teach, listen to them. The whole point of this answer is that they are the brand. My framework does not get to override their read.

The other place my lens does not reach is the political and cultural texture of firearms in America. I have no template for the parts of this that involve regulatory shifts, range politics, or the way the broader conversation can pull a brand into fights it did not start. You will need a lens for that, and it is not mine.
