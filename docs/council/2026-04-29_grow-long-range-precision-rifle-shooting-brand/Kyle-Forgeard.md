# Kyle Forgeard — Council Response

## First Impressions

Okay, I love this one. Long-range precision shooting with actual Marine scout snipers running it. That's not a brand problem, that's a goldmine that hasn't figured out it's a goldmine yet. My first thought is the boys are sitting on the most credible story in the entire firearms space and they're probably acting like a school. The minute I hear "they teach classes" my gut tightens, because classes don't scale, and classes aren't a brand, they're a service. But the people teaching the classes? That's a brand.

## How I'd Reframe This

That's not really the question. The question isn't "how do we grow the company." The question is: are you building a school, a media company, or a product company? Because they look the same on day one and they look completely different at $10M. Right now you've got partnerships with parts companies, you've got classes, you've got Marine sniper credibility. That's three businesses pretending to be one. Pick the wedge first.

My read: the wedge is media. The classes are the proof. The product is the margin. You don't grow this thing by booking more classes. You grow it by becoming the channel that the entire long-range community watches, and then the classes sell out and the product line writes itself.

## The Face-Value Answer

Here's how I'd actually do it. If me and the boys had Marine scout snipers and parts-company relationships, day one I'm putting a camera on the snipers. Not training videos. Not "10 tips for better grouping." A real show. Long-form YouTube, like 30 to 60 minutes per episode, where the snipers go to a ranch, take impossible shots, and you film it like a film. You hire a real director (or you act like one). Cinematic. Slow. The opposite of the loud red-white-blue rifle YouTube aesthetic that everybody else is doing.

The pitch to the audience is simple: "These are the guys the military trained. Watch them work." That's it. You don't need a slogan. You need a face. Pick one of the snipers, the one who's quietest and most watchable on camera. He's the brand. The other guys are the crew. Crews always read better than committees.

For positioning: be a step above by being a step quieter. The whole industry yells. Tactical bros, Operator content, MERICA fonts, blood-and-gunpowder edits. If you go restrained, technical, almost boring in how serious you are, you'll stand out so hard that the parts companies you partner with will start asking to be in your videos instead of the other way around.

Marketing-wise, I wouldn't run ads for a year. I'd post the show, drop merch tied to specific episodes, and let the classes sell themselves on the back of the content. Drops, not subscriptions. When you announce a class, it should feel like a Supreme drop. Cohort of 12, named after the location, sells out in 4 hours, gone. Then a behind-the-scenes mini-doc of that cohort drops two months later. The class becomes the content. The content becomes the class. The flywheel is the same one Full Send uses, just translated into a different audience.

For product, eventually you've got to have your own thing. A bag, a chassis component, a load development service, an apparel line, something. Don't license your name to one of the parts companies you're already partnered with. Co-develop. You bring the audience and the cred, they bring the manufacturing. That's the deal worth making. Licensing your name is renting. Co-development is owning.

## My Framework Applied

The model I'd run this through is **content as wedge, product as margin, classes as proof**. That's a remix of what we did with Nelk. Free attention pulls in the audience, a physical product captures the margin, and the classes are the credentialing event that proves you're not just another YouTube channel with a logo.

The other framework is **own the stack**. Don't run this on Instagram and TikTok alone. Email list from day one. Direct-to-consumer storefront from day one. Long-form on YouTube because that's where serious shooters actually live and where the audience has patience for 40-minute videos. Short clips on Instagram and YouTube Shorts as the funnel. If you build it on a platform that can demonetize you (and rifle content gets demonetized constantly), you're cooked. Email list and direct sales make you bulletproof. Same lesson YouTube taught us in 2018, just applied to a different vertical.

And the third one: **drop mechanics**. Limited cohorts on classes, limited runs on apparel, limited collabs with parts companies. Scarcity is what turns a transaction into a status moment. A class isn't a class anymore, it's a ticket. Merch isn't a hoodie, it's a membership card. The audience knows when something is limited and they show up differently for it. Don't restock. Don't expand the cohort. Let the FOMO do the work.

One more thing on positioning: lean hard into the Marine scout sniper credential. That is not generic veteran content. Scout sniper is a specific, hard-earned thing. Most of the rifle space is staffed by hobbyists who got good. Your guys did it under fire for the country. Don't be loud about it. Be specific about it. Mention it once per video and let the work speak. The minute you over-flag the military credentials, it reads as cosplay. The minute you under-flag and just film them being unbelievably skilled, it lands harder.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

I'd be running the same calculation I ran when we were figuring out what Nelk was past the prank channel. The questions I'd sit with: who's the face? Out of the scout sniper guys, which one does the camera love? Because the face is non-negotiable. A faceless brand in a personality-driven category dies. I'd also be thinking: what's the one product we're going to sell that we can ship in 90 days? Because content without a product is a podcast, and podcasts are a sport, not a business. And I'd be thinking about the parts-company partnerships you already have. Are those licensing deals or are they relationships? Because if they're licensing deals, you're getting underpaid. If they're relationships, you've got a head start most companies in this space dream about.

### What I'd feel

Honestly, jealous. This is the kind of brand that's allergic to the bro register I'm best at and would actually punish anybody trying to make it Full-Send-loud. The audience is patient, technical, and respects quiet competence over volume. That's a constraint I'd find fun to work inside. I'd also feel a little bit cautious, because the firearms space has tripwires Nelk doesn't have. Demonetization, ad-platform bans, payment processor risk. None of that scares me, but it means the stack-ownership move isn't optional, it's existential. If you don't own your distribution, somebody else will turn it off.

### What I'd do

This week, three things. One, sit with the snipers and pick the face. Whoever it is, get them comfortable on camera. Tell them they don't have to perform, they just have to work. Two, write a one-page pitch for the show: format, episode length, location, vibe. Hand it to a director, even a young hungry one, and shoot the pilot in 30 days. Three, audit the parts-company partnerships. Find out which two could become co-development deals, not licensing deals. Have those conversations.

This month, ship the pilot. Build the email list. Set up the storefront. Pick one product (apparel is easiest, a piece of gear is harder but better) and start the development process. Announce one cohort of one class as a drop. Sell it out. Film it.

This year, ten episodes minimum. One real product launched. Two co-development partnerships locked. A cohort calendar that's booked six months ahead. And by month nine, hire an operator. The Shahidi-brothers move. You and the snipers are creators and instructors. Somebody else has to run the company. If you try to do both, the wheels come off around month seven. Hire the adult before you need them, not after.

I would not raise outside money for at least 18 months. The cleaner the cap table, the better the long-term decisions. Bootstrapping is slower but the freedom is worth it, and this audience can fund itself.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

A bunch of places. First, I don't know rifles. I know audiences and I know creator-led product businesses, but I'm not a shooter. There are technical, regulatory, and cultural specifics in the long-range space I'd be guessing at. Somebody who actually lives in this world will catch things I'd miss, like which parts companies are actually respected versus which ones the audience sees through, or what certifications matter for instructors. Don't take my product instinct and skip the technical reality check.

Second, the firearms space has compliance and legal complexity that none of my businesses have. Payment processors that drop you, ad platforms that won't run your inventory, state-by-state regulation on what you can sell and ship. I've never had to think about that. A lawyer who knows the firearms vertical needs to be in the room early. My drop-mechanics playbook assumes you can actually fulfill orders. In this space that's not a given.

Third, my pattern is to launch what I don't fully understand. The Metacard is the case study. I'm telling you to launch a product line in 90 days because that's how I'd move, but the version of this advice from a more cautious operator (one who'd say "build the audience for a year before you sell anything") is also defensible and probably safer. My bias is to ship faster than is wise. Adjust accordingly.

Fourth, I don't know the long-range community well enough to know if there's an existing dominant brand that already owns the position I'm describing. If there is, the play changes. You don't take them head-on, you find the niche they're not serving. I'd want a real reconnaissance pass on who's already in the space before locking the strategy.

And finally, the political and cultural lane this brand sits in. The firearms world has internal politics I'm not the right person to navigate. I'm a connector and a marketer, not a culture interpreter for that audience specifically. Whoever you bring in to run marketing needs to actually be of this world. Outsiders get sniffed out fast in tactical communities. The face has to be a real one. The voice has to be a real one. I can give you the structure, but the texture has to come from inside.
