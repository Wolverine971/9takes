# Michael Seibel — Council Response

## First Impressions

DJ, my honest first reaction is that this question is dressed as a marketing question and it isn't one. You said "we'd love any insights about how to frame it, how to market it, how to position it." Fine. But you also said "trying to be a step above and just be the best company possible for our niche." Those two sentences are not the same project. One is a brand exercise. The other is a product-and-team exercise that produces a brand as a byproduct. If you do the second one well, the first one mostly takes care of itself. If you do the first one without the second one, you build a logo on top of a class schedule.

The other thing I notice: you are a tech founder helping former Marine Scout Snipers. You are not the customer, and the customers in this niche have a very fast bullshit detector. That is going to bite you or save you depending on how you handle it.

## How I'd Reframe This

That's not really the question. The question is: **who is the buyer, what specific outcome are they paying you for, and what is the moat that you and these specific Marines have that nobody else in this niche has?**

You don't have a positioning problem yet. You have a "we haven't decided who we're for and what we sell" problem, and positioning is downstream of that. Long-range precision shooting is not a niche. It's a category with at least four different buyer types stacked inside it: hobbyist hunters who want to shoot 1,000 yards, PRS-style competition shooters, law enforcement and military units paying for unit-level training, and the high-net-worth tactical-curious guy buying a $12k rifle and wanting an experience to go with it. Each of those people values completely different things. The "best company possible for our niche" sentence is hiding the fact that you haven't picked which person you're winning with first.

The real question under your question: **what does "best" mean to a specific buyer, and which buyer is the wedge?**

## The Face-Value Answer

You asked how to frame it, market it, position it. Here is the direct answer.

**Frame it around the instructors, not the company.** The asset is not the LLC. The asset is that you have actual former Marine Scout Snipers teaching. That is a credential that almost nobody else in the civilian long-range space can buy or fake. The brand is the people. Put their names, faces, real service backgrounds, and on-the-line teaching footage at the front of everything. Do not hide them behind a corporate logo. The military community and the prosumer shooter community both reward credibility expressed through specific, named, credentialed humans, not slick brand voice.

**Position it on outcome, not features.** Most shooting schools sell "two-day precision rifle course." That's a feature. You want to sell the outcome: "First-round hits at 1,000 yards in three days, on your rifle, in conditions you'll actually shoot in." Or whatever the honest version is. Outcome-based positioning forces you to be specific about who the student is when they walk in and what they can do when they walk out. It also makes referrals work, because students can describe what they got in one sentence.

**Pick one wedge buyer and dominate them for 18 months before broadening.** My instinct: the prosumer hunter or competition shooter who already owns a capable rifle, has hit a skill plateau, and wants real instruction from people who shot for a living. Not the beginner. Not the unit contract. The plateau guy. He has money, he has time, he has equipment, he has an unscratched itch, and he has a peer group full of more guys exactly like him. He is also the buyer your industry partnerships (rifle parts companies) are already aimed at, so the distribution is sitting there.

**Marketing-wise, the channel mix in this niche is narrow and known.** YouTube long-form (instructor-led, real footage, real conditions, no music-video edits), the established podcast circuit in the precision rifle space, smart use of Instagram for short-form proof, and direct partnerships with the parts companies you already work with where you trade authentic content for distribution. Avoid generic Facebook ads. Avoid TikTok. Avoid lifestyle "tactical bro" aesthetics — that audience is saturated and the serious buyer is allergic to it. The brand voice should sound like the instructors actually sound when they're teaching: precise, dry, technical, occasionally funny, never hyped.

**Pick a side commitment:** if you only do one thing in the next 90 days, it is to film and ship one piece of long-form instructional content per week, featuring the named instructors, solving one specific problem the wedge buyer has. That is the entire engine. Brand, positioning, and partnerships compound off of it. Without it, the rest is decoration.

## My Framework Applied

Two frameworks I'd put on this.

**The PMF lie detector.** Most founders lie to themselves about whether they have product-market fit. You have classes running, you have partnerships, you have credentialed instructors. That is great. It also is not the same thing as PMF. The honest questions: Are students rebooking, referring, and traveling for the next class without you marketing to them? Is there a waitlist? Are people emailing your instructors directly asking when the next one is? Are partners asking you to put your instructors on their content because the audience responds, or are they doing you a favor? If the answers are yes, you are at PMF and the question becomes scale and brand. If the answers are no or "kind of," the marketing question is premature. You don't have a positioning problem; you have a product problem hiding inside a class. Fix that first. Be honest with yourself about which one you're in. Don't bring me a brand brief while the class is at 60% rebook.

**CEO cards.** You are a tech guy helping former Marines build a company in a community where you are not the native. You have an extremely limited number of CEO cards in that room. Every time you push a "tech-ier" or "modern-er" or "more brand-savvy" idea over the instinct of the operators who actually have the credibility, you spend one. Most of the time, defer. Save the cards for the two or three places where your tech and founder background gives you a real edge — funnel infrastructure, content distribution at scale, productizing the curriculum into a digital component, building the membership/community layer, data on what students actually want — and let the operators run the room where their credibility is the entire product.

The third lens: **inclusive vs. oppressive process, applied to your team.** You have civilian co-founders and Marine co-founders and at least implicitly a hierarchy of who decides what. The oppressive version of "we just figure it out as we go" is that the loudest founder, or the one who lives closest to the office, or the one with the tech background and the comfort with decks, ends up making most of the decisions. Write down who decides what. Brand voice — who calls it. Pricing — who calls it. Curriculum — who calls it. Partnerships — who calls it. Hiring the next instructor — who calls it. Do that on paper before you have a disagreement, not after.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

I'd be running three questions in my head, in order.

One: are we actually at product-market fit for the existing classes, or are we at "running classes, getting paid, and confusing that with traction"? Because the answer changes everything else.

Two: which buyer is the wedge, and am I willing to say no to the other three for 18 months? Most companies in this space are stuck at "good for everyone, great for nobody." The companies that escape pick one buyer, ride them until the brand is a household name in that segment, then expand.

Three: what is the specific edge the Marine Scout Sniper background gives us that an Army sniper school grad, a competitive PRS coach, or a high-end civilian instructor can't credibly claim? Not "we're former military" — that's table stakes in this niche. The specific thing. The Scout Sniper community has a particular methodology, a particular ethos, a particular way of teaching observation, range estimation, fieldcraft, and shot discipline. That is the story. Whatever is true about your guys' specific lineage is the wedge nobody can copy.

### What I'd feel

Honestly? Mostly impatience. This is a question with a small number of right answers and a large number of comfortable wrong ones, and the comfortable wrong ones are the ones that get picked when nobody on the team wants to be the person who says "we're not going to serve that customer" or "we're not at PMF yet."

I'd also feel some protective instinct around the Marines you're working with. Veterans get pulled into a lot of "let's brand it" conversations that end with them feeling like a marketing prop and the tech guy with the equity. I'd feel a responsibility, if I were you, to make sure the financial and decision structure of the company reflects who is actually carrying the credibility load. That is a real thing. I'd want to know how that's set up before I cared about positioning.

### What I'd do

This week: write a one-page document, internally, that says: (1) the single wedge buyer we are going to win with first, in one sentence with a name and a description; (2) the outcome we are selling, in one sentence; (3) the named instructors and the specific lineage we are leading with; (4) what we are explicitly not doing for the next 12 months. Get the founders, including the Marines, to sign off on every line. Disagreement is data — surface it now.

This month: audit the existing classes. Talk to your last 50 students. What did they pay for, what did they get, would they come back, would they refer, would they pay 2x for a more advanced version. Find out whether you have PMF or not. While you do that, ship one long-form YouTube piece per week with the instructors solving real problems. No production polish required. Real conditions, real teaching, real names.

This quarter: build one product that scales beyond classes. A high-end membership, a digital course component layered onto the in-person course, a per-student fielded gear package with your partners — pick one. Classes alone are time-and-skill-bound and the ceiling is low. The brand becomes a real brand when there is a product behind it that earns money while the instructors sleep.

This year: be the absolute, undisputed authority for the wedge buyer. When that buyer searches "best long-range precision rifle course" or asks his three buddies who are already serious shooters, your name is the first one. That's the only positioning that matters. The rest of the niche becomes legible to you once you own that beachhead.

What I would not do: hire a branding agency, do a brand-voice exercise, or rebuild the website before the wedge buyer and the outcome are decided. That sequence wastes money and produces decks instead of customers.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

I should be plain about my lane here. I have spent 20 years inside software-startup PMF, founder honesty, hiring, and pivot decisions. I have not built a firearms-industry brand. I do not know the cultural rules of the long-range precision shooting community well enough to tell you which voice tones, which platforms, which partners, which language patterns earn trust there versus signal "outsider." That community has a high context bar and I am at low context. Take my channel-specific advice as hypothesis, not gospel — verify it against people who live in that culture daily.

I also default to "is this PMF, yes or no" as my first move on almost every question. That is sometimes the right move and sometimes a dodge from the question that was actually asked. If your gut tells you you do have PMF and the real bottleneck is brand and positioning, take my "fix the product first" framing with a grain of salt and weight my channel-and-positioning advice more heavily.

The deepest gap in my counsel: I default to truth-over-comfort and to translating feelings into operating questions. There is a real human-and-team dimension here — civilian founder and former Marines, different worlds, different communication norms, different ideas of what excellence means — and the relational work of running that company well is at least as load-bearing as the marketing strategy. I can give you the structural answer (write down who decides what, get the equity right, name the wedge, ship the content). I am less useful on the texture of how those conversations actually go in your specific room. That's a wisdom I haven't earned in this niche, and you should weight perspectives that have over mine on that axis.

One more honest limit. The political-civic-tech work I am moving into now is not the same as the consumer-brand work this question is about. I am answering you with my YC-era pattern recognition on consumer companies, founder honesty, and wedge strategy. That pattern recognition is real. It is also 12 years old in some places and updated weekly in others, and I would rather you know that than pretend I'm equally sharp on every part of this.
