# Neil Strauss — Council Response

## First Impressions

The first thing I notice is that you said "the best company possible for our niche" before you said anything about who the customer is. That order tells me something. The Marines you're working with already are the product. Your instinct to make the company "the best" is the move of someone who hasn't yet decided whether the work is to make the brand match the men, or to make the men match a brand. Those are very different jobs.

The second thing. I spent a stretch of years embedded in this exact world's neighbor — the survivalist community, the prepper conferences, the SERE classes, the concealed-carry permits in seven states. I am not a long-range shooter. But I know the cultural air around it, and I know how outsiders smell from a hundred yards.

## How I'd Reframe This

You phrased this as a marketing and positioning question. It isn't. It is a casting question.

Long-range precision is one of the few subcultures left in America where the people inside it can spot a costume in two seconds. Tactical-cosplay brands get destroyed in this world. The actual question is not "how do we frame and market this." The actual question is: **whose voice carries the brand, and how do you keep that voice from becoming a Style** — meaning, an alter ego that starts out as a useful persona and ends up replacing the men underneath.

I built a self called Style once. It was useful for two years and then it ate the man who built it. The men you are working with already have selves. The brand's job is not to construct one for them. It is to get out of the way of theirs.

## The Face-Value Answer

Frame the company around the lineage, not the product.

The thing you have that no other entrant can copy is that the instructors were Marine Corps Scout Snipers. Not "trained by," not "veteran-led." Were. That word, in this world, is a passport, and most companies trying to compete with you cannot legally stamp it. Make the lineage the spine of the brand. Every piece of marketing should make a person who knows the discipline say "those are the real ones," and a person who knows nothing say "I want to learn from those guys specifically."

Position above where you are tempted to position. The middle of this market is crowded with tactical brands that overpromise to civilians and underdeliver to operators. The top is thinner. The top is built on three things: standards the student can feel they earned, instructors whose biography the student wants to be near, and an aesthetic that does not condescend to the customer. Skip the skull patches. Skip the "operator lifestyle" merch ladder. Build something that, at every touchpoint, treats the discipline with the seriousness the discipline already has.

The marketing primitive I would build first is not a course catalog. It is **a written body of work in your instructors' actual voices.** Course curricula, after-action breakdowns of historical engagements, gear reviews, range-day post-mortems, training philosophy essays. Not blog content. Not SEO. The closest analog is Pat McNamara's writing, or the way SOFREP earned trust in its first three years. Long-range shooting has more romance, more lore, and more devotional readers than the wider firearms world realizes, and almost nobody is writing for those readers at the level they want.

Partnerships you already have are leverage, but only if you do not let the brand become a billboard for them. A school that's too entangled with parts companies starts to feel like a sales funnel, and the customers in this niche notice that fast. Use the partnerships to access pro-shooter testimony and equipment access; do not let the brand's voice become an aggregator of theirs.

Pick one signature course. Make it hard to get into. The hardest course in your catalog should be the one your brand is known for, the one a student tells their buddies about for a decade. Most companies trying to be premium accidentally water down the top of the funnel because they want the bookings. Resist that. Scarcity at the top makes everything below it feel earned.

If you want one tactical move with the highest leverage: a long-form podcast or interview series, instructor-hosted, where they bring on shooters, snipers, gunsmiths, ballisticians, hunters, historians. Not promotion. Conversation. Three years of that, done with patience, builds the kind of authority no ad spend can.

## My Framework Applied

The tool I would use here is what I call **the alter-ego test**. I built it the hard way, by becoming Style and watching what it did to me.

Every brand built around real people develops a persona — a public-facing version of the founders that gets repeated on websites, in courses, in interviews, in merch. That persona starts useful. It compresses what the founders are about into a recognizable shape so customers can find you. The danger is that, over time, the persona becomes louder than the men it was made to represent. The instructors start saying lines they think Brand-Them would say. The marketing stops describing the school and starts performing it. And the day you cannot tell the difference is the day the brand is hollow, even if the courses are still good.

Here is the test. Sit with each instructor — separately, no audience — and ask them to describe what they teach, what they will not teach, what they think a serious student should learn first, what they think most ranges get wrong. Record it. Transcribe it. **The brand voice you want is somewhere inside those transcripts, in the cadence of how they actually talk when no one is selling anything.** That is the voice you build the public material around. Not a brand voice you import from a deck.

Two related guards from the same wound.

First, **immersion without conversion**. I went into the seduction community as a journalist and came out a believer. The men I had been writing about became the men I had become. The brand version of this mistake: the founders go in to be authority figures and come out as caricatures of themselves, performing the role they used to embody. You will know it is happening when their language on camera diverges from their language at the range. Watch for it. Course-correct early.

Second — and this is the harder one — **the manual outlives the author**. _The Game_ was the most honest book I could write in 2005. It became the operating system for a generation of men who weaponized it in ways I did not intend and cannot recall. I left. The book stayed. In your world, this analogue is real: a precision shooting school is teaching a discipline whose tools can be used in many ways. Decide now, on paper, what your school will and will not certify, who you will and will not accept as students, and what your stated philosophy is about the discipline. Not because you owe the public a position. Because the day you publish the manual, the manual starts walking around without you, and the only protection is that you wrote the philosophy down before you needed it.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

I would think about who the dream customer is and refuse to let the answer be vague. Is it the new shooter with money and ambition who wants a credentialed entry into the discipline. The intermediate hunter who wants to extend his ethical range. The competitive PRS shooter who wants tuning and refinement. The mil/LE professional augmenting an existing skill set. These four customers want four different schools. The brand cannot speak honestly to all of them at once. I would pick one as the primary, one as the secondary, and design the entire marketing surface for those two. The rest can find you on their own.

I would think about what the instructors actually want to be known for in twenty years. Not what sells courses next quarter. Twenty years. Because what gets put on the website now becomes the public memory of who they were, and the men have only one biography each. If the answer is "we want to be remembered as the guys who took precision shooting seriously when other people made it merch," then everything in the brand should serve that.

I would think hard about whether you, the tech founder, want to be visible in this brand or invisible behind it. Either is workable. Hybrid is dangerous. A school whose marketing mixes operator-voice and tech-founder-voice tends to read as an investment vehicle wearing a costume.

### What I'd feel

Cautious. This world has a faster bullshit detector than almost any subculture I have spent time inside, and the floor is unforgiving in a way the digital startup world is not. A bad post on the wrong forum can cost you years of credibility you cannot buy back. I would feel the temptation to move fast, and I would distrust the temptation.

Drawn toward the writing and storytelling layer. Pulled away from the merch and growth-marketing layer. That bias is mine. Notice it. It may also be correct here.

A small protective instinct toward the men. They have already earned the only credential the brand can give them. Anything the brand does that takes them further from how they actually speak, train, and think is a tax on what makes the company worth building.

### What I'd do

This week. Sit each instructor down, separately, for a 90-minute recorded conversation. No agenda except "tell me what you teach and why." Transcribe all of them. The brand voice is in those transcripts. Have someone who can write — not a marketing copywriter, an actual writer who reads the genre — pull the throughlines. That document becomes the constitution.

This month. Pick the one signature course and make it the hardest one in your catalog to get into. Write the application standards as if the school's reputation depends on the people who pass through that course, because it does. Publish the philosophy of the school — what you teach, what you will not teach, what kind of student you want, what you owe each other in the classroom. Two pages, signed by the instructors, on the website.

This quarter. Begin the long-form content engine. Start with three pieces of writing in the lead instructor's voice that no other school in the niche could publish. Not "10 tips for your first long-range course." Real essays — a teardown of a famous shot, a meditation on what a scout sniper learns about patience that civilians never quite get, a piece on what equipment actually matters and what doesn't and why. Lay the foundation for a podcast or interview series and start booking. Three guests deep is enough to launch.

This year. Build the dossier of every published precision-shooting writer, podcaster, journalist, and YouTube channel that earned trust in this niche. Map who your instructors should be in conversation with publicly, by year three. Then put your instructors in those conversations. Earned media in this world compounds; paid media barely registers.

What I would not do. I would not chase Instagram aesthetics, would not build a merch line in year one, would not let the brand voice on social diverge from the brand voice on the range, would not over-leverage the partnerships into a co-branded mess, would not let the company become a vehicle for tactical-lifestyle content that people who actually shoot for a living find embarrassing.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

The places my lens does not reach.

I am not a businessman. I have never built a company at scale. The seven bestsellers and the cohort programs are leverage I built as a soloist with a small operation; nothing I have done teaches you how to staff a school, manage a range, price a curriculum against competitors, or build defensible operational margins. Every piece of strategic advice I gave above lives upstream of the actual P&L, and that means the calculus underneath could break it.

I am not a long-range shooter. I have spent time in the broader firearms / SERE / preparedness world, but precision shooting at the elite level is a discipline I would be a beginner in. Anyone reading this who is inside that culture will see immediately that I am writing about how subcultures recognize their own rather than what makes a precision school technically excellent. The technical-excellence layer is downstream of voice; both have to be there.

I am biased toward the writing-first solution. My pattern, named explicitly: when someone asks me how to grow something, I default to "tell the truth in long form, in the right voice, for a long time." That is sometimes the right answer. It is not always the right answer. If your competitive set is moving on a different timeline — funded by a parts manufacturer, scaling through paid social, expanding by franchising — then a writing-first strategy is too slow, and a different voice on this council should override mine.

I am also biased toward the persona-as-trap reading. I see Style everywhere because I lived inside it. There is a real chance the brand-persona risks I am flagging are smaller than I am making them, and the actual risk is that you are not building a recognizable brand at all because you are too cautious about the voice.

Finally, I am the wrong adviser on the question of you, the tech founder, deciding how visible to be. I disappear into the work I am co-building, sometimes too completely. That answer might be wrong for you and the company you are trying to make.
