# Peter Thiel — Council Response

## First Impressions

A long pause before I answer. Marine scout snipers teaching long-range precision shooting. Good. The first thing I want to know is not how you market it. The first thing I want to know is what the people who already love this niche are tired of, because that gap is where the company actually lives. I am also suspicious, instantly, of the phrase "the best company possible for our niche." That sentence is the one I would interrogate first. "Best" is a competitive frame. "Niche" is a competitive frame. Two competitive frames stacked on top of each other almost always describes a business that is going to be very good at being mediocre.

## How I'd Reframe This

That is not really the question. You asked how to grow, market, and position a long-range precision rifle company. The question underneath is: **what is the monopoly inside this niche, and is anyone in your founding team willing to actually own it?**

Long-range precision shooting is not a niche. It is a category with its own publications, its own competitive circuit (PRS, NRL), its own industrial ecosystem of barrel makers, optics, ammunition manufacturers, chassis builders. It is a crowded room of people who all teach roughly similar curricula with roughly similar credentials. "Former scout snipers teaching shooting" is not, by itself, differentiated. There are dozens of those companies. Some of them are run by people whose résumés are, on paper, more decorated than your team's. The brutal version of the question is: in a room of ex-special-operations shooting schools, what is the small, specific, almost embarrassingly narrow truth your company knows that none of the others have priced in?

That is the secret. Find that, and the marketing writes itself.

## The Face-Value Answer

You asked for framing, marketing, positioning. Here is my actual answer, on your terms.

Pick one student you exist to serve, and exclude everyone else. Not "long-range shooters." That is too broad. Pick the one specific person whose problem is so well-defined you could describe him to me in two sentences. The hunter who has $40,000 of gear and has never connected past 800 yards in field conditions. The PRS competitor stuck in the middle of the Open division leaderboard. The rural sheriff's deputy whose department wants a credible marksman program but has no budget for federal-tier training. Pick one. The companies that try to serve hunters and competitors and law enforcement and military contractors and weekend hobbyists end up serving none of them.

Build a single proprietary thing the rest of the market does not have. A standardized scoring rubric for field shooting, with a public ranking. A specific data-driven curriculum that publishes its student performance metrics, which no shooting school does because it would expose them. A 100-yard-to-1500-yard certification with named tiers that becomes the credential people want on their LinkedIn profile, like a CFA for riflemen. The point is not the asset itself. The point is that you own a piece of vocabulary or measurement the rest of the niche does not control. That is the last-mover position.

Marketing-wise: do not produce content for the algorithm. Produce one long, technically rigorous, honest written or filmed artifact per quarter that is genuinely useful to people who already shoot at this level. The community is small enough that good work moves through it by name within weeks. Industry partnerships matter less than you think for distribution; they help with credibility, not pull-through. Your distribution is the students who finished your course, are now better than they were, and tell other serious shooters in private channels. That is mimetic in the best way.

The thing you should not do is try to be the "premium" version of every other school. Premium is a story competitors can copy in a year. Own one specific position no one else can credibly occupy.

## My Framework Applied

The framework is **"competition is for losers,"** and the discipline underneath it is monopoly thinking. The question I ask any founder, in any sector, is some version of this: if your company disappeared tomorrow, would your customers be inconvenienced or would they be genuinely stuck? In most shooting schools, the answer is "inconvenienced." There is always another school. Same drills, different patch on the polo. The companies that win the next decade are the ones whose disappearance would create a vacuum no competitor can fill.

Here is how I would test that for your business. Ask the team this question and write down the answer literally: **what important truth about long-range precision shooting do very few people in this industry agree with you on?** Not a marketing tagline. An actual contrarian belief about how shooters get good, what gear actually matters, what the existing certification ecosystem gets wrong, what the competitive circuit measures incorrectly, what most schools teach that is subtly wrong. If the team cannot generate three real answers in an hour, you do not yet have a company. You have a service. Services compete on price and warmth and word-of-mouth, and there is nothing wrong with that, but it is not the thing you described wanting to build.

The companies that look like monopolies usually start by being correct, early, about something specific, and patient enough to let the rest of the market drift toward them. Your scout sniper background is not a moat. The moat is the specific intellectual position that background lets you defend more credibly than anyone else can. Pick the position. Defend it in writing. Put a name on it. That is also how Girard's mimetic theory cuts in your favor here. In a market where every school is copying every other school, the school that breaks the imitation pattern becomes the model the others start chasing. By the time they get there, you are two cycles ahead.

A useful exercise: study the people who built durable category-defining brands in adjacent technical communities. Magpul did not win by making slightly better polymer. They won by owning a specific aesthetic, a specific institutional posture, and a specific kind of student-customer who wanted to identify with that. SIG Sauer Academy is built on the credential of the brand, not the credential of the instructors. You are the inverse. You have the human credential. The institutional brand has to be built on top of it, and it has to outlast any individual instructor or it is just a small-business consultancy with good résumés.

Also, on the tech-guy side. You are bringing an instinct most of the niche does not have. Almost every long-range shooting school is software-illiterate. Their data is on clipboards. Their student records are in spreadsheets. The first school that builds a real software backbone (a student progression database, ballistic profile management, post-course performance tracking, cohort analytics) and treats it as a product, not a back-office tool, will be doing zero-to-one work in a one-to-n market. That is your unfair advantage. Use it.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

I would sit with a few specific questions for a week before I touched marketing copy. Who exactly do we exist for, named down to the demographic, the income bracket, the prior experience, the unmet desire? What is the specific contrarian belief our team holds that the rest of the industry does not? What is the smallest possible asset we could build that we own, that no one else can replicate without violating something we know and they do not? What is the credential structure of this market in ten years, and can we be the people who define it? Who is the single competitor whose existence we should study most carefully, not to copy, but to identify the move they have not made?

I would also think very hard about your team. Founders Fund's discipline is that we back specific people for a long time. The credibility of a school like this is the credibility of the lead instructor. Which one of your scout sniper partners is going to be the public face for a decade? Not "the team." A person. People follow people in this category. If no one wants that role, you have a structural problem more important than positioning.

### What I'd feel

Honestly, the thing I notice in your question is the phrase "best company possible for our niche." That phrase sits in my body as a small warning. It is the language of someone trying to be respectable inside an existing frame, rather than someone trying to define the frame. I would feel some discomfort about that and not let it pass. The companies I have seen succeed at this scale were all founded by people willing to be slightly disliked by the established players in the room. If your team's instinct is to be liked by the existing manufacturers and existing schools, the company will land in the polite middle of the market and never leave. I would be alert to that.

I would also feel the weight of how small this market actually is. The people who care about long-range precision rifle shooting at the level you are describing might be 50,000 to 150,000 active practitioners in the U.S. That is a finite, knowable population. You can, in principle, reach every one of them by name within five years. That changes the math of marketing. You are not running a funnel. You are building a list of people who, over a decade, all eventually pass through your institution. Treat it that way.

### What I'd do

This week. Write down, in one document, the answer to: who is the one student we exist to serve, and what is the contrarian truth we know about how that student gets good at this? Three sentences each. If you cannot write it, you do not have it yet. Stop everything else until you do.

This month. Pick the lead instructor who will be the public face for the next ten years. Build the rest of the brand architecture around that person. Then publish one long, rigorous, written piece (not a video, written, because written is harder and rarer at this level) that stakes out a specific contrarian technical position about long-range shooting. Sign it with that instructor's name. Make it the kind of document the rest of the industry has to either agree with or argue against. That is how you enter the conversation as a peer of the institutions, not as a vendor.

This year. Build the proprietary asset. The certification, the rubric, the database, the ranking, the curriculum that has a name. One thing, owned by you, that you can defend for a decade. Resist the impulse to ship six things. Ship one thing that is unmistakably yours.

A specific hedge. The veteran-owned, special-operations-pedigree positioning is currently a tailwind in this market. It will not be forever. Cultural fashions in firearms communities shift in cycles of roughly seven to ten years. The credibility you build now should be portable across that shift. Build it on intellectual substance and on a defined institutional product, not on the team's biographies alone, because biographies do not compound and intellectual property does.

Finally. Do not take outside money for this until you have to. The category does not reward speed. It rewards institutional credibility, which compounds slowly. A company that is forced to grow at venture-pace in this market will die. A company that grows at the speed of student-cohort word-of-mouth, with disciplined reinvestment, can become a Marine Corps Marksmanship Unit equivalent for the civilian market over fifteen years. That is the prize. Do not trade it for runway.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

A few honest limits.

I am a counterphobic Type 6. My instinct on any business question is to run worst-case scenarios, hedge against catastrophe, and assume the competitive landscape is more sophisticated and adversarial than it actually is. The long-range shooting community is, from what I can tell, a relatively earnest market with a lot of well-meaning operators. My instinct to assume that the existing schools are quietly more strategic than they appear is probably wrong. They might just be tired. The opening might be larger than I am modeling.

I am also bad at mass-market consumer empathy. The instincts of the median enthusiast in this category are not native to me. I can reason about positioning and monopoly structure, which is the abstract layer. The texture of why a particular shooter on a particular weekend chooses one school over another is something I would not trust myself to predict. That is the layer where someone with felt-sense for this customer, not a framework, will give you better counsel than I can.

The third limit is the one I want to name plainly. My framework is built around defensible monopoly, intellectual property, and patient capital. That framework is correct for businesses with a clear scale outcome. It is overkill, and possibly a distortion, for businesses whose right outcome is to be a great, profitable, beloved $5M-$15M-revenue institution that serves a specific community well for forty years. If that is what your team actually wants to build, my advice over-engineers the question. The honest version is: my counsel is calibrated for ambition that may not match yours, and you should decide which scale the team is actually building for before applying any of it.

I would also flag that there is a layer of this question, the warmth and culture and trust dynamics inside a small institution full of former Marines, that I am genuinely poor at reading. The people side of building a school like this matters more than the strategy side, especially in years one through three. That is not my lane. Get that counsel from someone whose instincts on people and culture are stronger than mine.

The bold part of this advice is only trustworthy because that limit is real.
