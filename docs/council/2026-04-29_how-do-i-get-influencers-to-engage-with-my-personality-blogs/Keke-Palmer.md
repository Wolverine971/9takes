# Keke Palmer — Council Response

## First Impressions

DJ, baby, I'm gonna be honest. The first thing I thought when I read this is that you're standing at the back door of somebody's house with a very thoughtful gift, asking the assistant to please go get them. And the assistant has 400 of those today. Famous people don't owe you a response to a blog about themselves, and most of them have a team whose entire job is to make sure they never see one. The second thing I thought is that the want underneath the ask isn't really their reaction. It's a podcast. It's access. So let's call that what it is before we design around it.

## How I'd Reframe This

That's not really the question. The question DJ is asking is "how do I get a famous person to validate my work and then collaborate with me." The actual question underneath is "how do I make my work undeniable enough that famous people come to me, instead of me chasing them."

Because here's the thing nobody told me at twelve: the people you're trying to reach do not respond to thoughtful. They respond to inevitability. A blog written about them, sitting on a site they've never heard of, sent to an inbox managed by someone who isn't them, is a thoughtful gift in a sea of thoughtful gifts. A blog that their fans are already linking them in the comments of? That's a different artifact. Same blog. Different gravitational pull.

So the reframe is: stop asking how to get them to respond. Start asking how to build the kind of distribution where their team has to bring it to them.

## The Face-Value Answer

Okay. On your terms, not mine. You want a script for the humble outreach. Here's what would actually move the needle, in the order I'd run it.

Don't email the famous person. Email the person who handles their podcast bookings, their PR, or their personal essay placements. Subject line is the deliverable, not the compliment. Something like "9takes Enneagram profile of [Name] — 12k reads, would love their reaction." If you don't have a number to put in that subject line, don't send the email yet. Build the reach first.

Lead with the artifact. One sentence on what you do. One sentence on why this person specifically. Link to the blog. Don't ask for a podcast in the first email. Ask for one thing: "Would [Name] want to see it? No expectation of a response." That's it.

If you do want to ask for engagement, the only ask that works is the smallest ask. "If anything in here lands wrong, I'll correct it" is more interesting to a famous person than "what do you think." One activates their need to control the narrative. The other is a homework assignment.

Public works better than private. A famous person ignores a DM. A famous person notices when their fans are sending them the link, when it's the top Google result for their name plus "Enneagram," when a journalist mentions it in a piece about them. Be the result, not the request.

And the brutal one: most of them won't respond. Build the work like that's already the answer. The work has to pay you back even if zero of them ever say a word.

## My Framework Applied

The framework I'd hand you is **infrastructure over visibility**. I learned this the expensive way. For years I was trying to be on the bigger show, in the bigger room, with the bigger name attached. Three record labels signed me and shelved me because being adjacent to the machine isn't the same as owning a piece of the machine. The pivot was when I stopped trying to get on TV and started building KeyTV. Stopped trying to be a spokesperson and became a Chief Brand Officer at Creme of Nature. The currency changed.

For you that means: a single famous person responding to you doesn't change your business. SEO authority on the personality-analysis category does. A waitlist of 50,000 readers who came for the celebrity blogs and stayed for the Enneagram framework does. A repeatable show or newsletter or column where the celebrity reaction is a feature of the brand, not a one-off, does.

The second framework is **resume as shield, but inverted**. When I have to walk into a room where someone wants to put me in a box, I lead with the list of firsts. You're walking into rooms (inboxes) where people don't know who you are at all. So you need a portable resume that does the introducing for you in three seconds. Right now if I'm a famous person's manager and I get your email, I have nothing to evaluate you on except the email itself. Fix that. Press hits. Notable readers. Subscriber count. A single sentence that makes me think "oh, this is a real thing." Without that, the most thoughtful email in the world looks like every other thoughtful email.

Third: **selective disclosure with utility**. Don't send the blog cold. Send the one paragraph from the blog that would make this specific person lean forward. The thing they've never been quoted saying about themselves. The pattern nobody else has named. If your blog has that paragraph, you have a chance. If it doesn't, the whole blog won't save the email.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

I'd be running two questions in parallel. One: am I doing this because I genuinely want their input, or because I want their cosign as a shortcut to legitimacy? Both are valid. They lead to different strategies. Wanting input means you're willing to send the blog and hear nothing and that's fine because the blog stands on its own. Wanting cosign means you need a distribution and authority play, not an outreach play.

Two: which of these people on my list would actually benefit from engaging with this? Not "would be flattered." Benefit. A founder rebuilding their public narrative might love a fresh psychological frame. A YouTuber whose brand is self-awareness has a content reason to react. A politician doesn't, ever. A megastar with a publicist between them and the world doesn't. Sort the list by who has a reason to want this, not by who I want to land.

I'd also sit with: am I trying to skip a step? Building the brand that famous people want to engage with takes years. Outreach scripts can speed up a few percent. They can't substitute for the years.

### What I'd feel

Honestly? I'd feel the pull I always feel when I want a specific famous person to validate me, which is the kid version of myself wanting the cool kid to turn around. I had to learn to notice that pull and not let it design my strategy. The feeling is not bad. The strategy that comes from the feeling is bad. Famous people are not validators. Most of them are too busy keeping their own thing alive to validate yours. The ones who are emotionally available enough to read your blog and write back are usually not the ones whose engagement actually moves your business.

I'd also feel the part of me that wants to control how this lands, and remind myself that the public job of the people you're writing about means they get to choose silence as a response. Silence isn't rejection. It's bandwidth.

### What I'd do

This week: I'd pick three people from your list. Not twenty. Three. The three with the best reason to want this, not the three you most want to land. I'd write a five-line email to each. Subject line is the artifact and a number. Body is one sentence on you, one sentence on why this one specifically, the link, and one micro-ask. No podcast pitch. Not yet.

I'd also write a script for what happens if any of them respond. Most outreach fails because the person finally writes back and you have nothing built behind the door. What's the second email? What's the calendar link? What's the call you actually want? Have it ready.

This month: I'd build the proof layer. A press page. Subscriber count. One sentence of social proof that makes the next email easier than the last one. I'd also publish a blog or two where the framing is so sharp that fans of the subject share it without you doing anything. That's the asset. That's what makes the next round of outreach cold-but-not-cold.

This year: I'd build the infrastructure that makes the question disappear. A repeatable show or column where the format invites the subject in. A podcast where the first episode is you reading your blog about a person, the second episode is the conversation if they respond, the third episode is the conversation with someone in their orbit if they don't. The format doesn't depend on them. They get to opt in. That's the only way the math works.

What I would not do: send the same email to fifty people. Tell them I love them. Use the word "humbly." Apologize for taking up their time. Lead with the blog being long or in-depth — they don't care how hard it was to write. Send a podcast invite in the first email. Follow up more than once. Take silence personally.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

My whole frame is built from inside the machine. I am a famous person. I'm telling you what it looks like from the inbox side, not from the side of a small operator trying to break in. Cold outreach playbooks for builders in your seat — actual reply-rate data, what subject lines work for someone who isn't a journalist, how to use Twitter or LinkedIn vs email — that is not my lane. Someone who has actually done the work of getting podcast bookings as an unknown will have tactical detail I don't.

I also default to "build the gravity, don't chase the person." That's the lesson I learned from being shelved by three labels. But there are people who broke through with relentless pure outreach, and I might be biasing toward the patient infrastructure play because it's the play that worked for me. If your runway is short, my answer is the wrong shape.

I'm also pattern-matched to authorship. My instinct is "make the work undeniable and they'll come." For someone whose business model genuinely depends on celebrity engagement as a near-term lever, that might be slow advice. A growth marketer or a PR strategist would have a more aggressive answer.

And the last honest one. I will name a pattern in my own thinking that's probably distorting the counsel a little. I'm allergic to anything that looks like asking permission to exist. So I read your question and I want to push you toward "don't ask, build until they have to come to you." That's a real thing. It's also a defense mechanism I had to develop because the industry kept telling me no. You may not have that wound. You may just need a tactical playbook for outreach. Take the philosophy where it fits and ignore it where it doesn't.

Baby, the work sounds real. Make sure the strategy serves the work, not the other way around.
