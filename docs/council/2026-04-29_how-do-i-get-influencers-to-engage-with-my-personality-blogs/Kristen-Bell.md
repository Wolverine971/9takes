# Kristen Bell — Council Response

## First Impressions

Okay. My first reaction is a flinch, because the way you've framed it ("I want them to respond, I'd love a podcast") is the food truck reflex with the brake off. The person you're writing about hasn't asked for anything. You're walking up to a stranger holding a portrait of them and asking if they'd like to do a project together. That's a lot to put in a first message. My second reaction is softer: the actual work, those psychological profiles, is genuinely interesting and that's a real asset. So let's not throw it away by mailing it with the wrong envelope.

## How I'd Reframe This

That's not really the question. The question you asked is "how do I intrigue them enough to respond." The question underneath is "how do I send something to a famous stranger that doesn't read like the eight hundred other things in their inbox that want a piece of their attention." Those are different problems. The first one assumes the lever is the writing. The second one assumes the lever is whether you can land in their nervous system as a safe, low-cost, generous interaction instead of an ask. Almost everything I've ever responded to from a stranger fell in that second category. Almost everything I've ignored failed it.

I'd also reframe the goal. "I want them to engage" is too dependent on someone else's behavior. The goal you can actually own is: send something that respects their time and asks for nothing the first time. The response, if it comes, comes.

## The Face-Value Answer

Send it. But strip the ask. The first message gets zero asks. Not "what do you think," not "I'd love your input," not "would you ever come on a podcast." Those are all small drains on the person, and small drains add up across an inbox.

Here is what I would actually send, on the assumption you can find an email or a DM that reaches a human, not a wall:

A short note. Three to five sentences. Tell them you wrote a long piece about them, link it, name one specific thing in the piece that you got from a source they probably forgot they said (a podcast moment, a line from an old interview). Tell them you're not asking for anything. If something in the piece is wrong or sits weird with them, your inbox is open. Then sign off.

That's it. No podcast pitch. No "would love to chat." No 9takes manifesto. The piece is the work. The piece does the asking on your behalf, and only if the piece earns it.

If they respond, you have a relationship to build. If they respond positively, then the second message can name an interest in a longer conversation, podcast or otherwise. If they don't respond, you've still planted a thing that exists in their world, and in a year when their team is doing a vibe check on you, the work is what they'll find.

The thing you don't want to do is make the first contact a transaction. The most famous people get transactional first messages constantly. They are exhausted by them. The thing that cuts through is being the one person that week who didn't want anything.

## My Framework Applied

I want to use my "are they thirsty? are they hungry?" framework here, because it's exactly the diagnostic move that makes the difference. When I walk into a room my first question is what does this person need. Not what do I need from them. If you applied that to a famous person, the answer is almost always: less. Less ask, less performance, less context, less of a paragraph about who you are and why your platform matters. They get plenty of "more" all day.

The other framework I'd hand you is the remedy box version of this. Pre-decide your tools when you're calm, so you can grab them when you're not. Right now, when one of these blogs lands well and you're excited, your reflex is to want a response from the subject and that's when you write the email. Don't write it then. Write the template now, while you're regulated. Three to five sentences. No ask. One specific reference to something they said years ago that they'd be flattered you remembered. A link. A door left open. Then when the next blog goes up, you fill in the variables and send. The pre-decided template prevents the version of the email that has too much of you in it.

And the third one is the fierce moral inventory move, but pointed at yourself before you hit send. Read the email back and ask: am I asking for something I haven't earned? If yes, cut it. The piece you wrote is what you've earned. Anything beyond "here it is, no strings" is borrowing against trust you haven't built yet.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

I'd think about the asymmetry. You spent days, maybe weeks, on this person. They have spent zero seconds on you. Any framing that doesn't account for that asymmetry will feel needy on the other end, even when it isn't. I'd think about who actually opens the message. Most famous people don't read their own DMs. Their assistant or manager or publicist does. So the message also has to pass the gatekeeper test, which means it has to look like nothing that's a problem to forward. "I wrote a long psychological analysis of them, no ask, here's the link" is forwardable. "I'd love them on my podcast" routes to a different inbox and dies. I'd also think about what the long game is. If your real goal is podcast appearances, the path isn't through cold outreach. It's through the third person you've already built a relationship with introducing you to the fourth. Cold outreach builds the very first node of that graph. It's allowed to be slow.

### What I'd feel

Honestly, I'd feel afraid that the subject would hate the piece. That's the Helper fear talking. I'd want to control their reaction, and I'd write extra sentences trying to soften the read in advance. Don't do that. The piece is what it is. If it's honest and not cruel, the people who matter will respect it even when they disagree. The ones who are upset are allowed to be upset. That's not your kid, that's not your blog to apologize for. I'd also feel a pull to over-explain who I am and why I have the right to write about them. Strip that out. The work explains you.

### What I'd do

This week. Pick the three to five subjects you most want to actually talk to someday and have already published on. For each one, find the most likely real human inbox. That's almost always a manager, agent, or publicist listed on IMDbPro, Muck Rack, or the person's website footer. Sometimes it's a verified personal email if they've published one. Sometimes it's a DM on the platform where they're most active personally (some of them do read those, you can usually tell from how they post).

Write one template. Three to five sentences. No ask. One specific recall of something they said in a real source. The link. A line that says you don't expect a reply, you just wanted them to know it existed. Send. Don't follow up. Don't double-tap the DM. If you didn't hear back in two weeks, move on.

This month. Build a quiet list of subjects who acknowledged the piece in any form (a like, a forward, a polite no-thanks from an assistant). That list is your warm pool. The next blog you write on someone in that pool, send it again, same template. You're building a memory in their orbit, not closing a deal.

This year. The podcast pitch only goes to people who have already responded to a no-ask message at least once. You earn the right to ask by demonstrating you don't need to. The first ten podcast guests you land probably won't be the most famous people on your list. They'll be the ones who liked the work enough to take a meeting, and they're often more interesting anyway.

The thing I would not do is run the entire effort through a single email blast. Don't batch this. Each one is its own quiet, specific, hand-built note. Volume is the wrong instinct here. Specificity is the lever.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

My whole frame is built around a Helper who has spent years training herself not to ask for things from people who haven't offered them. That's a useful corrective if you tend toward over-asking, but it might be too cautious if your real bottleneck is that you're already too restrained and not putting enough chips on the table. I don't know which side of that line you're actually on. If you're closer to the under-asking end, my counsel slows you down when you should be more direct.

I also don't know the cold-outreach mechanics here in any real way. I am the person being reached out to, not the person doing the reaching. My read on what a famous person wants in their inbox is real, but it's one person's nervous system. Other people are wired differently, run their own inboxes, love a direct pitch, find the no-ask version too coy. The numbers game and the deliverability craft of cold outreach is genuinely outside my lane. Someone who has actually built a creator-relations or PR pipeline at scale will give you better tactical instruction on subject lines, send timing, follow-up cadence, and which platforms convert.

And honestly, my Helper instinct is probably distorting the advice toward "make it easier on the recipient" when the actual answer might be "be more interesting and less polite." I know that about myself. I default to lowering demand on the other person, which is great for trust and bad for momentum. Run my answer through a sharper lens before you commit.
