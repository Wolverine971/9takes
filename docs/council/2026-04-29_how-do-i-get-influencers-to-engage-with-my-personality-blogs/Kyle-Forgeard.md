# Kyle Forgeard — Council Response

## First Impressions

Honestly? My gut said "good luck." Then I sat with it for a second. If somebody sent me a thoughtful blog about me, the first question I'd ask my team is "what does this person want from me." So your opening problem isn't the writing. It's that "what do you think?" reads like a setup for an ask, and the boys around these people are paid to assume it is. You're not getting ignored because the work is bad. You're getting ignored because the inbox is loud and the ask is foggy.

## How I'd Reframe This

That's not really the question. The question isn't "how do I get them to respond." It's "what's in it for them in the first ninety seconds." A famous person doesn't open a DM to evaluate your craft. They open it to figure out whether responding helps or hurts them. So the real question is: what specific, low-effort, high-upside thing are you putting in front of them that makes the reply the path of least resistance? You haven't designed that yet. You've designed a flattering artifact and a vague invitation. Two different things.

## The Face-Value Answer

Pick one person at a time and treat each outreach like a custom drop, not a mass campaign. Here is what works in my world.

Don't lead with the blog. Lead with one piece of cultural proof you actually understand them. One line, in their voice. Then tell them you wrote a long psychological profile, link it, and give them a specific reason to read it: a claim about them they have never seen anywhere else. Not a summary. A take. Something they will either nod at or want to fight you about. That's the thing that gets opened.

Then the ask. Not "what do you think." That's a homework assignment. Try one of these instead, and pick the one that fits the person:

1. "I'm not asking for anything. If any of it's wrong, I'd love a one-line correction so I can update it." This gets people who hate inaccurate things written about them.
2. "If this lands, I'd love to send you a printed version" or some tangible artifact. A physical thing in the mail moves people.
3. "I'm building a series. Would you give me twenty minutes by phone to fact-check my next one?" Lower commitment than a podcast. Gets you in the room. The podcast comes later.

Don't ask for the podcast on the first touch. You're asking the boss out on the third date in the first DM. Slow it down.

The last thing: stop trying to reach the person directly first. Reach the person who reads their DMs. Manager, agent, head of content, the friend who runs their socials. Different message, same artifact. Cleaner path.

## My Framework Applied

The framework I'd use is **drop mechanics, applied to outreach**. Every Full Send drop works because it's scarce, surprising, and respects the audience's time. Most influencer outreach fails the opposite of those three. It's mass, it's predictable, and it asks for a lot of attention up front.

So design the outreach like a drop. **Scarce**: you only do five of these a month, and you tell them that. "I write one of these every two weeks. You're the next one." That changes the energy from spam to invitation. **Surprising**: lead with a specific claim or piece of evidence that nobody else has put in front of them. The Enneagram angle is your edge here, but only if you use it to reveal a pattern they've never had named, not to label them with a number. **Respect their time**: a 30-second message, one link, one sentence on the ask. If they have to scroll, you lost.

Second framework: **content as wedge, product as margin.** Your blog is the wedge. The relationship is the margin. So the blog has to be free, fast, and irresistible to read. The ask comes later, smaller than they expect. The first goal is the open. The second goal is a one-line reply. The third goal, weeks later, is the podcast. Don't compress those into one DM.

Third thing: **public deadline + real proof + leverage.** This is how I built credibility for the ultramarathon. If you're going to keep doing these, build a thing they can see from outside that signals you're serious. A page that lists everyone you've profiled, with the depth of the work visible. Make it look like a real publication, not a personal blog. When their team Googles you, that page is what closes the deal. Right now I'd bet you have a great body of work and a presentation layer that doesn't match it. Fix the layer.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

I'd think: who in my actual orbit do I have a path to right now, and how do I get one yes that gives me a case study. The first one is the hardest. The second is easier. The fifth one you put in your DM signature: "previously profiled X, Y, Z." Now the famous person has social proof you exist before they read a word.

I'd also think about what kind of person actually responds to this. It's not the A-list. The A-list won't read it. It's the second tier who's still hungry, the comedian on the way up, the politician with a tough race coming, the founder who hasn't done a press tour yet. They have time, they have something to gain from coverage, and a long psychological profile flatters them in a way they don't get from regular press. Win there first. The A-list comes after the case studies stack.

And I'd think about the obvious thing: are these blogs published with the person's name visible to them? Are they showing up in Google when their team searches them? Because if they're indexed and ranked, the team finds it on their own and you don't have to send a DM at all. Then you DM after they've already seen it. Different conversation entirely.

### What I'd feel

Honestly, a little impatient with you on this one. You're asking how to get them to respond before you've decided what you actually want from them. The podcast want is real. The "input" want is fluffy. Pick. If you want a podcast, build toward the podcast. If you want the relationship, build toward the relationship. If you want the SEO and the credibility, you don't need them to respond at all and you can stop chasing.

Also a little protective. People in their position get pitched a hundred times a week. The thing that actually works is when you make them feel less alone in something specific. That's harder than flattery. But it's the only thing that cuts through.

### What I'd do

This week. Pick three names. Not thirty. Three. Tier them: one A-list dream, one realistic mid-tier, one second-tier you have a warm path to. Write a different message for each. Go.

For each one, find the human in their world before you go to the principal. LinkedIn the manager. Find the agency. DM the podcast booker. The principal is the wrong door.

Print the blog. Physical copy. Hand-deliver or mail with a one-page handwritten note for the realistic one. The dream one gets digital, the mid-tier gets digital with a follow-up package if you get a one-line yes, the warm-path one gets the physical artifact. Test the channel.

Build the index page. One URL, all your profiles, designed like a real publication. This is the thing you should have built before you sent a single DM. Probably this week.

Write a sample sentence per profile that is the boldest claim in the piece. Lead with that in outreach. Not the title. The claim.

This month. Do five outreaches a week, max. Track responses by tier and channel. After 20, you'll know what works. Stop iterating on the writing. Start iterating on the package and the path.

This year. If you can land three real conversations and one podcast in the next six months, the model works and you scale it. If you can't, the artifact isn't the problem, the publication isn't legible enough yet, and you build the brand around the work first. Famous people respond to publications. They ignore personal blogs. So the question becomes whether you're building 9takes into something that looks like a real outlet from the outside.

The one thing I would not do: send a generic message to fifty people. That's the version of this that fails. You'll feel productive and get nothing.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

My whole game is direct-to-audience. I get attention by being loud and selling product. I have never once cold-pitched a serious person on writing about them. The people I've gotten in the chair came through warm intros, mutual UFC people, Dana, the Shahidis, mutual managers. I don't know your world.

What I'm extrapolating from is drop mechanics and outreach to brand partners, which is a related but different muscle. The thing my framework misses: there is probably a craft to the actual sentence-by-sentence writing of an outreach DM that someone who has spent years getting yeses from celebrities would know cold, and I would not. The voice of the message, the cadence, the specific words that move a manager versus a principal. I'm guessing at that. Someone who's lived in the access-and-pitch world from the publicist or booker side will have sharper tactical answers than mine.

Second, I'm biased toward "make a thing, make it loud, build the audience first, the people come later." That's because that's what worked for me. If you're playing a slower long game where the relationships ARE the product, my "build the loud thing first" advice might be too aggressive. You might want a quieter, more relationship-led path. I have not built that.

Third, my pattern is to reframe slow situations into action quickly. I'm doing it again here. If the real answer is that you should send fewer DMs and let the work compound for another year before you start chasing, I will under-rate that move. My instinct will always be "go now, more, harder." That's not always right.

And honestly, my read on what makes a famous person respond is filtered through my own appetite. I respond to people who treat me like a peer and don't waste my time. Other personality types respond to different things. A Type 2 wants warmth, a Type 5 wants intellectual respect, a Type 8 wants a real exchange of value. You write Enneagram blogs. You probably know this better than I do. Use it.
