# Michael Seibel — Council Response

## First Impressions

Cold open: a polite "what do you think?" email is a layup the famous person dunks straight into the trash. They get fifty of those a week. The good news is the work itself is uncommon and probably better than 95% of what's pitched at them. The bad news is you're asking the wrong question. You're asking how to get them to respond. The real game is about whether the artifact you're handing them is something they want to be associated with on a bad day, not a good one.

## How I'd Reframe This

That's not really the question. The question is: **what would have to be true about the blog post for the subject to have no choice but to engage?**

Because right now you're optimizing for outreach mechanics — the email script, the humble framing, the soft ask. That's a marginal lever. The real lever is the artifact. A celebrity engages with a piece about themselves for one of two reasons: it shows them something they didn't know about themselves and got it right, or it threatens their narrative in a way they can't ignore. Polite analyses get read by the comms team and discarded. Pieces that crack the person open or call them out get answered.

The second reframe: **you're confusing the goal.** A response is not the goal. A relationship is. A podcast is. A long-tail thing where they reference your work without prompting — that's the goal. Optimize for the artifact and the relationship, not the reply.

## The Face-Value Answer

Here's what to do, on your terms.

**One.** Stop sending "what do you think?" emails. That ask gives them no shape to respond to. It's homework. People with audiences do not do homework for strangers.

**Two.** Pick five subjects, not fifty. The ones where (a) you have the strongest piece, (b) the person is genuinely reachable (a real DM or email exists, not a publicist gate), and (c) they have a known appetite for being analyzed (some do, most don't). Spray-and-pray dilutes your reputation and trains you to write outreach instead of write blogs.

**Three.** Send the piece with a specific, falsifiable claim attached. Not "I'd love your input." Try: "I argued you're a Type 8 because of [specific behavior]. The thing I couldn't reconcile is [specific contradiction]. If I got it wrong, I'd publish a correction with your reasoning." That's a chess move. You've handed them a hook to either correct you (which means engaging with your frame) or validate you (which is content for them).

**Four.** Do not lead with the podcast ask. The podcast ask in cold outreach to a famous person from a small platform is dead on arrival. Earn the second email. Get them to reply to the first one with anything — a correction, a thanks, a forward to their team. Then the podcast ask has a thread to live in.

**Five.** The replies you'll get most often will come from the second-tier influencers, not the A-list. That is not a failure. Build the case studies — "Here's the analysis. Here's what the subject said. Here's the conversation that came out of it." — and the A-list eventually responds because someone in their orbit told them you're worth replying to. Reputation flows downhill from peers, not upward from fans.

Pick a side, you asked. The side: **the asset wins, not the email**. Spend 80% of the effort on the artifact, 20% on the outreach. Most people invert it.

## My Framework Applied

I'll use two frameworks. **The PMF lie detector** and **CEO cards**.

**PMF first.** You're asking how to get influencers to engage. Before you optimize the outreach, ask the harder question: do you actually have product-market fit on the artifact for this audience? The PMF question for a blog about a famous person isn't "do readers like it?" It's "would the subject themselves recognize the piece as the most insightful thing written about them this year?" That's a high bar. Most personality analysis fails it because it reads like horoscope-with-citations. If your blog clears that bar, you don't have to chase the subject. The piece will reach them through their network, because someone they trust will send it to them. If your blog does not clear that bar, no email script saves you.

So the first audit is honest: pick three of your strongest pieces and ask, hand on heart, would the subject read this and think "this person actually saw me"? If yes, you have artifact-market fit and the outreach is downstream. If you're flinching at that question, the work is to sharpen the artifact, not the email.

**CEO cards.** You have a limited number of them. Every cold email to a famous person is a card. You burn one when you send a generic ask. You can't reach back out in six months and expect a different response — they remember (or their inbox does). So budget them. Don't email Drake until the Drake piece is the best Drake piece on the internet. Don't email Bill Gates until you've got something he hasn't seen written about himself a thousand times. Each outreach is a credibility withdrawal from a finite account. Spend them on the pieces and the people where you'd bet on a response.

The combined move: sharpen artifact to the point you'd defend it to the subject's face, then spend cards sparingly on the targets where the artifact is sharpest.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

I'd think about why I want the response in the first place. Is it validation? Distribution? Real relationship? Each one has a different correct strategy. Validation is the worst reason — you'll perform for them and the work will get worse. Distribution is honest — say so to yourself and structure the ask around what's in it for them (a tweet quote, a podcast clip, a co-authored response). Relationship is the long game and requires patience most people don't have.

I'd ask: what's the lying I'm doing to myself here? The lie I'd suspect is that the outreach is the bottleneck. The outreach is rarely the bottleneck. The artifact and the audience size are the bottleneck. I'd want to know: how many monthly readers do my Drake piece, my Elon piece, my Bill Gates piece have? If the answer is small, that's the constraint. Famous people respond when there's social proof or stakes. Pieces with no readers carry no stakes.

I'd also think about the reverse case — who has reached out to me cold over the years and gotten a response? Almost always: people who'd done specific homework, made a specific claim, asked a specific question that was answerable in two sentences, and were obviously not asking for anything other than the two sentences. The "would love to chat" emails go to the trash 100% of the time. The "I think you're wrong about X for Y reason, am I missing something?" emails get answered.

### What I'd feel

Honestly, I default to principle before feeling, so I'm not the strongest at this part. But if I sit with it: there's probably some yearning here. Wanting the subjects to validate the work is a real pull, and it can corrupt the writing if you're not careful. The work has to be defensible whether or not Drake ever reads it. If a "no response" from Drake makes you doubt the piece, the piece was already shaky. The emotional discipline is to write each one as if it's the only one that matters and then let go of who reads it.

The feeling I'd resist: the urge to soften pieces in anticipation of the subject reading them. The moment you write to be liked by the subject, you stop writing the piece a real reader needs. Don't do that. The bluntness, controlled and fair, is the reason any of this is worth reading.

### What I'd do

This week: pick five pieces. Not five subjects to email — five pieces that are already in your top tier. Read them with a colored pen and ask: "Would the subject recognize themselves in this?" Cut everything that wouldn't survive that test.

This week, also: write three different outreach templates. The first one is a correction-bait email — "I argued X, here's the line I couldn't reconcile, did I get it wrong?" The second is a peer-introduction ask through a mutual connection (LinkedIn, mutual followers, anyone in the second-degree network). The third is a "saw you mentioned [thing], here's a piece I wrote that touches it" — only sent when there's a current, specific hook in their public feed. Don't send the same email to fifty people.

This month: send to ten subjects, max. Track open and reply rates. The data will tell you whether the constraint is the artifact, the outreach, or the audience size — and you can stop guessing.

This year: build the case-study flywheel. Even one famous response on the record — a quote, a tweet, a podcast appearance — becomes the social proof for the next ten asks. The first one is the hardest by an order of magnitude. Don't underprice the value of landing one well-known fish, even a small one, and using it forever.

What I would not do: pay for distribution to influencers, hire a PR person, or run a coordinated launch campaign to "make a splash." That's noise, and famous people are immune to noise. The path through is the artifact and the patient compounding of one real reply at a time.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

My lens is founders, accelerators, and institutional sorting. The thing I am genuinely good at is reading whether a pitch is honest and telling someone they're lying to themselves. The influencer-outreach game is closer to entertainment-industry relationship-building than it is to YC office hours, and that is not my native field. I have never run a publicist outreach campaign. I have never landed a famous guest on a podcast through cold email. I am inferring from adjacent principles, not from direct reps.

Two specific limits to flag.

**One: I default to "the artifact wins" because that's how PMF works in software.** In media and entertainment, sometimes the relationship really is the artifact, and the right move is to spend a year cultivating one mutual connection before you ever send the email. I'm undervaluing that path because it's slow and unmeasurable, and I prefer measurable. If your gut says the relationship route is the way for a specific subject, trust it more than my framework.

**Two: I have a bias toward "stop optimizing outreach, fix the work."** That's almost always right at the level of strategy. But there's a tactical layer — the actual subject line, the time of day, the platform (DM vs. email), the threading — that matters more than I want to admit. I'd find someone who has actually landed cold-outreach replies from famous people and learn the tactical layer from them. My framework gets you the strategy. The execution craft I can't teach.

**Third, naming a pattern in my own counsel:** I translated your question about emotional engagement (intrigue, response, relationship) into a founder-principle question about PMF and credibility budgeting. That is sometimes the right move and sometimes a dodge. You asked how to make someone feel curious enough to reply. I gave you a framework for whether the work merits a reply. Those are not the same question. The axis I'm under-serving is the human, social, persuasion-craft axis — how famous people actually decide what to engage with on a given Tuesday morning. Someone who lives inside the influence economy would answer that better than I can.
