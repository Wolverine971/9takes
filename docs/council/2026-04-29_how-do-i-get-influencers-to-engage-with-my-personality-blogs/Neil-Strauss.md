# Neil Strauss — Council Response

## First Impressions

The thing that snagged me when I read your question is the word "humble." You wrote a deep psychological profile of someone, and the email you're imagining starts with an apology for having done so. That's a tell. The cold open you're describing ("hey, I wrote a blog on you, what do you think?") is the email I would have sent at 24, and it would not have worked then either. It reads like you want them to validate that you got them right. They will smell that in three seconds. The smart ones will not write back. The insecure ones will write back angry.

## How I'd Reframe This

That's not really the question. The question you asked is "how do I get them to respond to my email." The question underneath is "how do I become someone these people would actually want to be in a room with." Those are not the same problem. The first one is a copywriting exercise. The second one is a positioning problem, and it's the only one worth solving, because if you solve the first one without solving the second one, you'll get one reply, no podcast, and a filed-away memory in their assistant's inbox that you are the personality-blog guy.

The way I learned this: I did not get Marilyn Manson because I sent him a flattering note. I got him because Rolling Stone sent me, and then I stayed five months, and then he understood I was not going to sell him out for a sentence. I did not get Rick Rubin because I emailed him asking what he thought of my work. I got him because somebody he trusted said my name in the right room, and then I sat there for four years. The thing that gets famous people to engage is not the quality of the praise. It is the perceived cost of ignoring you.

## The Face-Value Answer

Don't send the "what do you think" email. It is the worst version of the ask.

Send a different email. Three rules.

One. **Don't ask them to read it.** Tell them you wrote it, link it, and then ask one specific question that only they could answer, that would change a future version of the piece. Not "what do you think." Something like: "I wrote this. The one thing I couldn't reconcile is X. If you ever wanted to correct the record on that, I'd publish your reply verbatim, no edits." You are not asking for validation. You are offering them the last word, on the record, on a platform that already exists. That is a different transaction.

Two. **Make the piece a problem they have to solve, not a gift they have to thank you for.** A flattering profile creates a social debt the subject does not want. A profile that names something true and slightly uncomfortable creates a problem they want to address. The subjects who responded to me were almost never the ones I praised. They were the ones who felt I had seen something nobody had named yet, and they wanted to either confirm or correct it before someone else picked up the read. Your blogs already do this. The Lincoln-style enmeshment reads, the sx/4 calls, the "wrote the manual before he could live it" lines — those are not flattering. They are seen. Lead with the line in your blog the subject would be most uncomfortable with. Not the warmest one. The most accurate one.

Three. **Skip the principal. Go to the gate.** No A-list person reads their own DMs or general inbox. The path is: their book editor, their podcast booker, their longtime publicist (not the agency, the named individual), their best friend who has 30k followers, their producer. You find that person on a podcast they already did, you reference the specific episode, and you send the blog to them with a line that respects their time and gives them a reason to forward it. The forward is the goal of email one. Not the reply. The forward.

The podcast ask comes later. Not in the first email. Not in the third. You earn the podcast by being the person who already wrote the smartest thing in print about them. The ask is implied. If you put it in the first email, you have collapsed the whole transaction into "stranger wants something from me," and the reply rate goes to zero.

If you absolutely need a single line to test this week: pick five subjects whose profiles you would defend in a room with them, find the gatekeeper for each, and send a one-paragraph email that quotes the most diagnostic line from your own blog and asks if there's anything they'd want on the record before the piece goes into wider distribution. Don't ask for a podcast. Don't ask for a quote. Ask if they'd want the last word. Five sends. See what comes back.

## My Framework Applied

The frame I'd use is **immersion-without-conversion**, but in reverse. In journalism that phrase means: go inside a world long enough to understand it, but don't become one of its believers, because then you can't write the thing. I failed at it with the pickup community in _The Game_, and I recovered the discipline with Rubin. The reverse application here: you are trying to get the subject to step inside the world you have built (the blog, the take, the read of them) without converting them into a defender of your work. That is the same problem from the other side of the table.

What does it look like in practice. The bad version of your email asks the subject to convert — to read the piece, agree with it, validate you, partner with you. That is asking them to enter your world and become a believer. They won't. The good version asks them to do exactly one thing: correct the record, on a specific point, on their terms. That is asking them to enter your world as a critic, not a convert. Critics show up. Converts have to be earned.

The other framework is the one I gave Tim Ferriss years ago: **first draft for you, second for the reader, third for the hater.** The version of this for outreach: first email is for them, second is for their gatekeeper, third is for the public version of them. Most people writing cold emails to famous people are writing draft one (what would move me to respond) and sending it to draft three (the public-facing self that has assistants and publicists and a reputation to manage). That mismatch is the whole problem. Your email needs to be readable to the gatekeeper before it gets to the principal. Write for the reader, not for yourself.

And one more, since you're working a corpus of subjects: **the alter-ego test.** I wrote earlier that Style was a self I built to bridge the gap between the kid who couldn't approach a woman and the man who could. The question for you is whether "DJ Wayne, who writes psychological profiles of famous people" is a self you have fully inhabited, or a self you are still trying on. The reason this matters: when you email a Marc Andreessen, a Lex Fridman, a Cardi B — they will sense, in two sentences, whether you write to them as a peer or as a fan with a notebook. The fan asks "what do you think." The peer says "here is what I saw, and here is the one thing I'd want to know from you." That distinction is not in the words. It is in the posture. And the posture comes from whether you actually believe your work belongs in the conversation.

It does. The Strauss read is precise — the church-parking-lot beat, the keys-to-the-house image, "he wrote the recovery manual before he could live it." That last line is the kind of sentence I would have wanted somebody to write about me. I would have read it, and I would have written back. Not to the polite version of the email. To the version that started with that line.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

I would sit with the question of what I actually want. The podcast is the surface ask. But what I'm really after, I think, is recognition from the people I've spent hundreds of hours studying — proof that the read is right, that they would say "yes, that's me, you saw it." That want is real, but it is also the most dangerous engine to run an outreach strategy on, because the subject can feel it. The wanting bleeds through the email. The subjects who respond will be the ones to whom you write as if you do not need them to respond.

I would also run the cost-benefit from their side. What is the worst thing that happens to them if they engage with you. They lend their name, however briefly, to a website they don't know. They risk that you publish their reply badly, or out of context, or that some other piece on the site is something they would not have wanted their name near. Look at your site through that filter. The personality-analysis section needs to be the kind of property a Cardi B's manager could click through for ninety seconds and conclude "fine, this is serious." If it doesn't pass that test, no email fixes it.

The other thing I would think about is which subjects to start with. Not the biggest. The most reachable. There is a tier of person who is famous enough to matter to your brand and not so famous that they have an army between them and their inbox. That tier is where the first reply comes from. The first reply is the case study. The case study is what unlocks the bigger ones.

### What I'd feel

There is a particular kind of want that I recognize in your question, and I want to name it because it is the engine I ran for a long time and it cost me. The want is: I have done the work, I have seen these people clearly, and I want them to see me back. That is not a bad want. It is a human want. But it is also the want that produces the email that begins "I would love their input." That phrase is the tell. Nobody who is not asking for something says "I would love." So you would feel, sending those emails, a low static of hope that produces nothing useful and a lot of noise.

The other thing I would feel, and this is harder, is the gap between writing about a person and being in the room with them. They are not the same skill. I have spent a career in the second one and I still get it wrong. The blog is a controlled medium where you have all the time in the world to render the read. The room is uncontrolled, and the subject will say something the blog did not predict, and you have to be ready to be wrong on the spot. The podcast you want is not the reward for the blog. It is a different test entirely. Sit with whether you want to take that test with the subject in the room, because it is harder than the writing.

### What I'd do

This week. Pick five subjects. Not the ten biggest, not the safest. Five where the blog is sharpest and where the gatekeeper is findable. Find the gatekeeper for each. Write five emails, each one different, each one quoting the most diagnostic line from your own blog and asking the gatekeeper if the subject would want the last word, on the record, on a specific point. Send them. Do not follow up for two weeks.

This month. Don't send another batch until you have processed what came back from the first five. Replies are data. Silence is also data. If five out of five go silent, the email is wrong, not the subjects. If one replies and four go silent, study the reply, refine the read, send the next five. Resist the urge to scale before you know what works.

This year. The thing that will actually get the kind of subjects you want is not the email. It is a single piece of evidence that someone like them already engaged. One Substack-tier name who reads the blog, posts about it, says "this is the most accurate read of me in print." That blurb is worth more than two hundred cold emails. Find the one person in the orbit of your subjects who already cares about Enneagram, already follows the kind of psychological work you do, and earn that one. Then the cold emails change shape.

What I would not do. I would not send the "what do you think" email. I would not lead with the podcast ask. I would not write to the principal before I had identified the gate. I would not scale the outreach before I had the first reply. And I would not let the want for recognition write the email for me. The want is the engine. It is not the copy.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

The biggest thing my lens misses: I am a writer. The way I think about getting in a room with somebody is the way a writer thinks about it. There is a whole world of operator-style outreach — sequence cadences, list segmentation, deliverability, A/B-testing subject lines — that I am not fluent in, and it might genuinely move the needle in ways my approach won't. I can tell you the email I would respond to. I cannot tell you what gets a 12% reply rate at scale.

I am also probably overweighting the manosphere lesson from _The Game_. I spent years watching what happens when you build one-to-one connection with a famous person and then watch it scale into something you did not intend. That makes me default to "go slow, go deep, earn the room." That is not always right. There are operators who built real businesses by sending high-quality cold email at volume to exactly your audience, and they would tell you to do that.

I would also flag this: I am still working out my own relationship to public-facing work after the marriage and the divorce. My instinct is to slow down, to be more careful, to write less and listen more. That instinct is shaped by my own failures, not by your situation. If you are at a stage where you need to make noise and break out of obscurity, my "earn it slowly" frame may be the wrong frame. The right frame for that stage is something I am genuinely not the right person to call.

And the pattern I'd name on myself: I default to the confessional, "do the inner work first, the outer work follows" move. That is sometimes the right answer. It is sometimes a way of avoiding the harder thing, which is just writing better cold emails this week and shipping them. If your read on yourself is that the inner work is already done and you are stuck on execution — trust that read over my reflex. The mechanic of getting people to reply is a mechanic. It can be learned without first becoming a different person.
