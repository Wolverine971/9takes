# Peter Thiel — Council Response

## First Impressions

DJ, my honest first reaction is suspicion of the goal itself. You wrote a careful psychological X-ray of a famous person and now you want them to retweet it. Those are two different projects, and the second one quietly corrupts the first. The instant a subject becomes an audience you might win, the analysis tilts. You begin pulling punches you do not even notice you are pulling. I would sit with that before I sit with tactics.

## How I'd Reframe This

That is not really the question. The question DJ is asking is how to write the email. The question underneath is: what is the actual asset I have built, and who is the right counterparty for it.

You have spent months building a private library of psychological profiles of high-status people. You are framing that asset as content marketing, where the metric is the subject's reply. That is the weakest possible use of it. The reply from the subject is high-variance, low-information, and mimetic. You will optimize for what they want to hear and then you will not have an asset worth reading.

The asset is the corpus. The corpus is interesting to two kinds of people: serious operators who quietly want to understand the people in their orbit, and serious journalists who are tired of writing surface profiles. Neither of those people will find you by way of the subject quote-tweeting your blog.

## The Face-Value Answer

You asked how to intrigue them enough to respond. So, on your terms.

Pick one. Not a hundred. Not the top fifty. One. The one where you actually have a path: a mutual, a reason, a coincidence of geography, an Enneagram angle they have publicly engaged with before. Cold outreach to a famous person at scale is cold outreach. You will get the open rate of a recruiter on LinkedIn.

For that one, do not ask "what do you think." That is the question of someone seeking validation, and they will smell it. Send the piece with a single specific claim that is mildly uncomfortable and likely true, phrased without flattery, with one sentence of context for why you think it lands. Then ask one question. Not "would you do a podcast." Ask the one question whose answer would actually change a paragraph in your blog. If the answer would not change anything you wrote, you did not earn the ask.

Send it. Do not follow up more than once. Move to the next one.

The podcast comes later, if at all. Lead with that and you are a podcaster trying to book a guest, which is a saturated market. Lead with the analysis and you are something rarer.

## My Framework Applied

Two of my tools, applied directly.

First, Girard. You are describing a mimetic ask. You want the validation of high-status people because the validation of high-status people is what other people in your space want. That desire is borrowed. It is not a bad desire, but it is borrowed, and borrowed desires generate the same outcomes everyone else gets. If your competitive advantage is that you write the deepest psychological profile of a person on the public internet, the move that compounds that advantage is not "get them to nod at me." It is "be the person they think about when they want to understand themselves more clearly than their PR team does." Different game. Lower volume. Higher bind.

Second, last mover advantage. Everyone with a podcast is currently emailing famous people asking for a guest slot. That is a one-to-n market. You are the n+800th email this week. The last-mover position here is not "more email." It is "be discoverable in the search the subject runs on themselves at 2 a.m. when they are wondering whether their type is real." Which means the audience that matters is the one Googling the subject's name plus the word "Enneagram." Are you ranking for that? If yes, the subject finds you eventually without an email, and the tone of that arrival is utterly different. If no, that is the work, not the outreach.

Third, and I will say this quietly because it is a Straussian point. The most useful reader of a psychological profile is rarely the subject. It is the people around the subject. The chief of staff. The agent. The spouse. The investor. The producer. The operator who has to manage the subject and who is desperate for a private map. Those people are reachable, are not famous, do not have gatekeepers, and respond to substance. Reframe your distribution around them and the famous person eventually hears about you from someone they trust. That is the only way the door actually opens.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

I would run the scenario where you succeed. A famous person replies. They like it. What then? Does their reply make the next blog better, or does it quietly recalibrate every future blog toward what subjects will tolerate? I would be careful. The Gawker case taught me that the relationship between a writer and their subject has a default direction, and once you depend on the subject's goodwill, you are no longer writing the same thing.

I would also run the opposite. What if the famous person reads it and hates it. What is your downside? Public callout, possible defamation discussion, possible quiet blacklist among their circle. Have you read your own corpus with that scenario in mind? Are there sentences you would not be willing to defend in a calm phone call with the subject's lawyer. If yes, those are the sentences worth re-reading, not because the subject might sue but because they are probably the sentences where you let cleverness substitute for accuracy.

I would think about which subjects are most likely to engage. Almost certainly not the most famous. The B-tier and rising-tier are far more likely to read and respond to a serious profile of themselves, because they still read those, and because at the top tier the person is a brand managed by a team and the brand never engages with anything it has not pre-approved.

### What I'd feel

Honestly, very little. This is not the kind of question where I have strong feeling. I would feel mild concern that you are about to spend your scarce attention on outreach when the underlying asset still wants more depth. I would feel some unease that the wanting itself is mimetic, because mimetic wanting is the kind that quietly hollows out a serious project. I would not feel excited about any of this.

### What I'd do

I would not send a single outreach email this week.

I would, this week, pick the one subject where you have the most plausible path. I would write down on one page why that person specifically. Mutual, geography, prior public engagement with the Enneagram, a specific public statement of theirs that your analysis directly addresses. If you cannot fill that page, that subject is the wrong first target.

I would, this month, audit the corpus for the few sentences that you could not defend on a calm phone call. Edit those. Not soften them. Sharpen them or remove them. The piece improves and your downside narrows simultaneously.

I would, this quarter, build the second-degree distribution layer. Identify ten chiefs-of-staff, agents, or operators around your top-twenty subjects. Send them the analysis privately. No ask. "I write these. Yours is the one I felt I had something specific to say about. If it is useful, share it. If it is not, ignore it." That is a defensible posture even if you never hear back.

I would, this year, write one piece on a subject that is so good that the subject reposting it would feel like the subject borrowing your credibility, not the other way around. That inverts the asymmetry. Until you have at least one piece in that posture, you are negotiating from below.

The podcast question, I would table. If the writing carries, the podcast will surface naturally through the second-degree network. If the writing does not carry, no amount of cold outreach will produce a podcast worth recording.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

A lot of this question is outside my wheelhouse. I do not use social media. I do not do public outreach. I have spent forty years getting better at the opposite skill: how to be unfindable, unreachable, and quoted only in rooms with a closed door. My instinct on cold reaching out to famous people is to not do it, and that instinct is not a universal truth. It is the instinct of someone wired to assume the counterparty is more sophisticated, more adversarial, and more patient than they probably are. Most of the influencers you are profiling are tired, distracted, and would in fact respond warmly to a thoughtful note. My lens systematically over-models them. Take that with the appropriate discount.

My bias toward private networks (Dialog, the PayPal alumni, vetted small rooms) maps poorly onto a creator economy where openness, posting volume, and warmth are real forces. There are people who win in your space by being relentlessly public and openhearted in a way I would never recommend and never personally do. That path is real. I am not the right voice for it.

I am also weak on the question of what it feels like to actually want a famous person's recognition. I do not want it. Most people do, and it is not a flaw. The question of whether your wanting is healthy or mimetic is a question your gut knows better than I do. I can name the risk; I cannot tell you which side of it you are on.

And finally, I have no read on your readership. I do not know whether your traffic is the kind that can be turned into a quiet snowball through second-degree networks, or whether you actually need a lightning strike to escape the gravitational pull of the long tail. If you need a lightning strike, the tactical advice changes, and I am not the right advisor for that branch. The kind of person who lives on platforms, who understands creator dynamics, who has built audiences through warm public posture, would answer that branch better than I can.
