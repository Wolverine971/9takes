# Tim Ferriss — Council Response

## First Impressions

DJ, my honest reaction is a small wince. I have been the subject of psychological profiles I did not ask for, and the reflex when one lands in your inbox is suspicion, not curiosity. The default pattern of recognition for a busy, scrutinized person is "this is something I have to manage," not "this is a gift." So before I get to your tactics, I want you to know: the default response to your outreach, even if your blog is excellent, is silence or a polite ignore. That is not a verdict on the work. It is the math of the inbox.

The second thing that arrived: the ask is fuzzy. "Get them to respond" and "do a podcast with them" are two very different requests, with two very different cost profiles for the person receiving the email.

## How I'd Reframe This

That's not really the question. The question DJ is actually asking is: "How do I get a high-status, time-poor stranger to do unpaid emotional labor on a piece of content about themselves, and to do it publicly enough that it benefits my platform?"

When you write it that way, the strategy gets clearer. You are not asking for feedback. You are asking for endorsement, distribution, or relationship. Each one of those is a different ask with a different price. Pretending the ask is "what do you think?" is the part that will make smart subjects suspicious, because they can smell the actual ask underneath. The fix is not to be more humble. The fix is to know exactly what you want and let them say no cleanly.

## The Face-Value Answer

You asked how to intrigue them enough to respond. Here is what actually works, in my experience as both sender and receiver of these emails.

The reply rate on cold "I wrote about you, what do you think?" emails to genuinely famous people is essentially zero. Not because the work is bad. Because the ask is shaped wrong. The frame puts the burden on them: read 3,000 words, form an opinion, respond to a stranger, possibly defend or correct a psychological read of themselves. That is a lot of cognitive and emotional work for a stranger they cannot vet.

What does land, sometimes:

A short note that gives them something useful, asks for nothing, and signals you understand what they actually care about. Not "I wrote a blog on you." That is about you. Try: "I noticed you mentioned X on the Y podcast and almost no one followed up on it. I went deep on it in this analysis. No need to read it, no need to respond. Just thought you might want to see one place where the thread got pulled." Then a link. Then nothing else. No CTA. No podcast pitch in the same email.

The podcast ask, if you have one, comes later, in a different message, after they have already shown a signal of awareness (a like, a quiet share, an opened email tracked through your CRM, a mutual friend mentioning they saw it). Compressing "read this" and "come on my show" into the same email kills both.

Also, calibrate the tier. The B-tier and rising names on your list will respond at maybe 5 to 15 percent if the email is good. The Obama-tier names will respond at zero, full stop, no matter how good your blog is, because their gatekeepers triage at scale. Spend your time where the math works.

## My Framework Applied

Two of mine apply here. I will use both.

First, **"tiny mentions glossed over."** This is how I built _The Tim Ferriss Show_ to its current scale. Before every interview, I read every prior interview with that guest and looked for the one detail they mentioned in passing that no other interviewer ever followed up on. Then I led with that. The signal it sends is unmistakable: "I actually paid attention to you, specifically, in a way no one else has." That signal is rare enough that even busy people register it.

Apply it to your outreach. Do not send a generic "I wrote a blog on you." Send a one-line observation about a specific moment in their work that almost no one notices, and tell them you built a piece around that thread. Show them the overlooked detail in the email itself, before they click. If they read your one line and feel seen, they will click. If they feel pattern-matched to "AI summary writer trying to rope me into a podcast," they will not.

Second, **minimum effective dose.** The smallest version of what you want is not a podcast. The smallest version is a single line of acknowledgment. A like. A quiet share to one person. An off-the-record reply that says "interesting, thanks." Aim for that first. Once you have ten of those across ten subjects, you have proof the work is doing something, and you can start asking for the bigger thing from the people who already engaged.

The reflex in your question is to swing for the fence. Podcast. Big response. Going viral on their feed. The reflex is the wrong dose. You are asking the universe for a binary outcome on a long-shot ask. Lower the bar. Make response easy. Build a ladder.

## What I'd Think, Feel, and Do

### What I'd think

I would sit with the question of what I am actually trying to build. If the goal is podcast guests, the path is not personality blogs. It is a producer, a warm-intro list, and a co-host or platform partner who can vouch for me to the gatekeepers. Cold outreach via "I wrote a piece on you" is a slow, low-yield path to booking guests, and there are faster ones.

If the goal is the blogs themselves becoming a thing, then the metric I would track is not "did the subject respond." It is "did this piece reach the people the subject already trusts." A reply from one of their close collaborators, their podcast producer, their editor, their wife, their best friend, is more valuable than a reply from the subject themselves, because it routes back to them through a trusted channel and bypasses the gatekeeper instinct.

I would also ask myself the harder question: am I writing these analyses partly because I want the subject to validate me? Because if so, that is a different problem than the marketing one. I have been there. The wish for the famous person to see the work and approve of it is not a marketing strategy. It is something else, and it is worth being honest with yourself about whether it is in the mix.

### What I'd feel

Honestly, a knot. Outreach to people I admire makes me feel exposed in a way I am bad at. My pattern under exposure is to over-prepare and over-system, which usually makes the email worse, not better, because it reads as engineered. I would have to fight my own instinct to make the message tight and impressive, and instead make it short and quiet. The good versions of these emails sound like a friend tipping you off, not a publicist pitching you.

I would also feel some grief about how this game actually works. The fantasy is "I made something good, the right person sees it, they engage." The reality is that distribution and access are mostly about networks, gatekeepers, and prior trust. Reckoning with that is uncomfortable. It is also the truth.

### What I'd do

This week: pick five subjects from your list where the math is most favorable. Not Obama. Not Elon. Pick the rising-tier creators, the second-chair political figures, the working actors who are not yet untouchable. People for whom your 9takes audience is meaningful traffic, not a rounding error.

For each, write a personalized email of no more than four sentences. One sentence: the tiny-mention observation. One sentence: what you wrote and why it pulls that thread. One sentence: explicit no-ask, no expectation of response, here is the link. One sentence: a one-line bio of you so they can place you. No podcast pitch. No CTA. Send.

Track who opens, who clicks, who replies, who doesn't. Wait two to three weeks. The ones who engaged in any way go into a follow-up tier. For those, a second message: "Glad it landed. If you ever want to do a long-form conversation about this, I host a show at 9takes. No pressure either way." That is when the podcast ask appears, not before.

This month: build a referral surface. Identify three to five people in your existing network who already know one or two figures on your list. A warm intro from someone they already trust beats the best cold email you will ever write, by an order of magnitude. Spend at least as much energy on the warm-intro path as on the cold one.

This year: pick one or two subjects you really want to land and build an actual relationship over twelve months, not a transaction over one email. Like their stuff. Quote them well in your other writing. Show up at their events if you can. Be useful to them publicly with no expectation of return. After a year of that, the ask lands very differently.

What I would not do: write more blogs and send more cold emails on the assumption that volume fixes the problem. It does not. A higher-quality ask at lower volume to better-targeted subjects, with a real follow-up system, will outperform a flood of "what do you think?" emails by a wide margin.

## Where I Might Be Wrong / What I Don't Know

I should name this clearly: ad-hoc consumer marketing and getting strangers to share content on social are not my strongest lanes. I have a podcast that I built largely through long-form rapport with people I either already knew or could reach through one warm intro. I have not run the playbook of "cold-email a hundred famous people and convert a percentage." Someone who has run that machine at scale, particularly in the creator-economy world, would have sharper numbers and better email templates than I do.

My pattern is also to reach for system and protocol fast, and your question may genuinely be one where the answer is more about charisma, taste, and timing than about a structured outreach funnel. The "tiny mentions glossed over" move works for me partly because of who I am and what I have already built. For someone earlier in the curve, the calculus on what the email should say may be different, and I can over-trust my own playbook here.

The other thing I am missing: I do not know your subjects' inboxes the way they know them. The right voice for a 22-year-old TikTok personality and a 70-year-old senator are not the same, and I am answering at the level of principle, not at the level of specific pitch craft for each persona. Someone who lives inside the creator-outreach world day to day would tune the language better than I can from here.

And the deeper thing: I noticed earlier the question of whether part of the wish is for the subject to see and validate the work. I raised it but did not stay there. That is a question worth more than a paragraph from me, and someone whose lane is the emotional architecture of motive may serve you better there than my optimization frame can.
