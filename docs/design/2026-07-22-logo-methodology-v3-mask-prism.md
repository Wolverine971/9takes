# 9takes Logo Methodology V3 — The Nine-Facet Mask

## Decision

The next logo exploration will fuse three ideas already native to 9takes:

- **The mask:** a visible role, temperament, or way of seeing.
- **Nine facets:** nine distinct perspectives within one human subject.
- **Dimensional construction:** the intellectual appeal of the original cube, without its collapsing hairline side.

The primary thesis is:

> **Nine facets. One face.**

The mark should feel like an artifact and a system at the same time: old enough to belong in a Greek theater, precise enough to belong to a modern platform.

## Why the previous direction failed

“Ancient voice, modern voice, same fire” was a good verbal idea but a weak icon construction. At app-icon scale, the two profiles lost their intended ages and cultural eras. The philosopher read as a religious figure; the younger listener read as a hooded follower. The mark depended on portrait detail and explanation, while showing neither nine perspectives nor a robust diagram.

This version removes people, clothing, and narrative illustration. The lineage lives in the form of the theatrical mask; the contemporary idea lives in its geometric construction.

## Research basis

Ancient Greek theatrical masks did more than conceal an actor. They made a character or role legible, and allowed one actor to embody different roles. Mask types varied through visible features such as hair, eyebrows, noses, and wrinkles—including distinctions of age and character.

The Acropolis Museum’s relief plaque of theatrical masks is especially relevant: it shows six related masks arranged in a two-by-three field, likely representing members of a chorus, with controlled differences between them. That is a historical precedent for “related voices, visibly distinct” without illustrating a literal conversation.

Greek theater was also communal. Actors, chorus, musicians, and audience participated in a civic event; the chorus supplied collective commentary from the orchestra. That makes the mask a credible bridge between Greek lineage and a modern meeting of perspectives.

## Meaning hierarchy

The logo should communicate in this order:

1. **A face or mask** — a human point of view.
2. **Nine distinct parts** — the nine takes.
3. **One coherent whole** — disagreement without fragmentation.
4. **Dimensionality** — perspective changes what is visible.
5. **Greek lineage** — conveyed through proportion and mask archetype, not costume or statues.

The mark does not need to communicate every idea at 24 pixels. At that size it must first be a memorable face-like silhouette. The nine-part construction should become apparent at 48–128 pixels.

## Construction routes

### Route A — Nine-Facet Prosopon

One calm, frontal theatrical mask constructed from exactly nine large contiguous planes. Minimal negative-space eye and mouth openings make it a face rather than a gemstone. This is the cleanest expression of “nine perspectives, one person.”

Method: metaphor fusion + construction geometry.

### Route B — Persona Prism

A three-quarter or isometric mask built from nine broad planes. Its depth must occupy at least 25–30% of the mark’s width so it remains intentional at small sizes. This route retains the cube’s dimensional appeal while making “perspective” human.

Method: metaphor fusion + negative space.

### Route C — Mask Cube

A substantial isometric cube whose primary three-by-three plane resolves into a mask through negative space. Side and lower planes must be broad, solid masses—not rows of hairline outlines. This is the closest descendant of the original 9takes cube.

Method: product metaphor + construction geometry.

### Route D — Chorus Plaque

A unified tablet or field holding nine related mask glyphs. This has the most literal connection to chorus and plurality, but it risks looking like an emoji grid or being illegible as an app icon. It is a secondary test, not the preferred route.

## Formal constraints

- Primary artwork is black and white only.
- Exactly nine planes or units when nine is asserted; no approximate “many facets.”
- One unmistakable outer silhouette.
- No strokes thinner than 1/16 of the mark width.
- On dimensional versions, visible depth is at least 25% of overall width.
- Eye and mouth openings remain open at 32 pixels.
- The logo must survive 16, 32, 64, 128, and 512-pixel tests.
- It must work as both black-on-white and white-on-black.
- Color and material are a separate phase after symbol approval.

## Resemblance exclusions

The mark must not resemble:

- comedy/tragedy twin masks;
- an Anonymous/Guy Fawkes mask;
- a skull, robot, superhero, or armored helmet;
- a Venetian carnival, tiki, “tribal,” or culturally generic ceremonial mask;
- a cult seal, occult sigil, or copied Enneagram diagram;
- a camera aperture, gemstone, shield, AI sparkle, or therapy logo;
- a literal Greek statue portrait;
- a Rubik’s Cube color scheme or branded puzzle.

## Evaluation scorecard

Each candidate receives 0–5 points on:

1. **Immediate read:** is it recognizably face/mask-like?
2. **Nine logic:** are nine perspectives meaningfully built into it?
3. **Ownability:** does it avoid generic mask and low-poly tropes?
4. **Small-size strength:** does it hold at 32 pixels?
5. **Dimensional intelligence:** does depth feel intentional rather than decorative?
6. **Cultural fit:** does it suggest Greek intellectual lineage without costume or cliché?
7. **Emotional tone:** thoughtful, curious, equal—not ominous or theatrical.

Any concept scoring below 4 on immediate read or small-size strength is rejected, regardless of total.

## Production sequence

1. Generate divergent monochrome construction sheets for Routes A–C.
2. Reject anything that needs explanatory copy to look like a mask.
3. Refine only the strongest construction into a clean geometric prototype.
4. Run small-size, inverse, and silhouette tests.
5. After symbol approval, explore stone/ink/night-fire palettes and the wordmark.
6. Only after final approval, replace app icons and favicon assets.

## Exploration outcome — Round 1

### Route A: Nine-Facet Prosopon — rejected

The frontal nine-plane masks became armored, robotic, or superhero-like. Symmetry plus hard seam lines overwhelmed the Greek-theater reference. They failed the emotional-tone and resemblance-exclusion tests.

### Route B: Persona Prism — held, not selected

Three-quarter volume made the mask more human and solved the disappearing-depth problem. However, the result remained a generic low-poly face and did not make the meeting or equality of perspectives visible.

### Route C: Mask Cube — rejected

The broad side plane fixed the original cube’s hairline failure, but the mask appeared pasted onto a box. It combined two symbols without producing a new idea.

### Route D: Chorus Circle — selected for refinement

The strongest construction is now named **Nine-Voice Agora**:

- nine identical voice/mask units;
- rotated in exact 40-degree intervals;
- all oriented toward one open shared center;
- no leader or privileged perspective;
- one stable circular silhouette;
- no thin depth plane that can collapse on iOS.

The vector is deterministic rather than AI-counted: it contains exactly nine instances of one base unit. At 32 pixels, the primary read is a nine-part meeting ring. At 64–128 pixels, the human/profile cuts become visible. This deliberate progressive reveal is acceptable; tiny app icons do not need to communicate the entire story at once.
