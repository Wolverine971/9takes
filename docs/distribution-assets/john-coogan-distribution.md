## Reddit Post — r/Enneagram

**Title:** Type 3 Case Study: John Coogan (TBPN, Soylent, Lucy) — The Achiever Who Can't Stop Leveling Up

**Body:**

I've been writing personality analyses of public figures through the Enneagram, and John Coogan is a fascinating Type 3 case.

Quick context: Coogan co-founded Soylent ($133M raised, though it later sold for ~$29M), then Lucy (nicotine products), built a 450K YouTube channel, and now co-hosts TBPN — the daily tech show NYT called "Silicon Valley's newest obsession." Guests include Zuckerberg, Altman, Mark Cuban, Satya Nadella.

**Why Type 3:**

**The trajectory speaks for itself.** Failed startup → Soylent → Lucy → YouTube → TBPN. Each chapter explicitly bigger than the last. For achiever types, lateral moves feel like regression. Everything must escalate.

**The adaptive performance.** He went from behind-the-scenes CTO at Soylent to on-camera daily live host — three hours a day, five days a week, no script. "Your mood doesn't matter. How you feel doesn't matter. We have an obligation to go live." That's a philosophy that subordinates internal experience to external output.

**The emotional distance.** In hundreds of pages of podcast transcripts, Coogan almost never discusses his emotional state. When asked if running a profitable business is more fun than a money-losing one: "I don't know if 'fun' is even a KPI I'd be worried about."

**The humor hedge.** Every venture starts with comedy — Soylent's cannibalism joke, TBPN's fake yacht ads, the "Temu Succession" trailer. Humor gives permission to fail. You can't lose if it was never serious. Only later does it become serious.

**Where it gets interesting.** Asked what he wants to be known for in 30 years: "I don't care. I don't want to be known for anything." But: "I want to KNOW things." The goal is knowing, predicting, understanding. Not feeling. Not being remembered.

## Reddit Post — r/startups

**Title:** The psychology behind serial entrepreneurship — what drives founders who keep building

**Body:**

[Requesting mod approval for blog link]

I've been researching what personality patterns drive serial entrepreneurs. Not the hustle-culture surface stuff — the actual psychological wiring.

I did a deep dive on John Coogan (co-founder of Soylent, Lucy, now TBPN) and found some patterns that I think a lot of founders would recognize:

1. **Each chapter has to be bigger.** Failed startup → Soylent ($133M raised) → Lucy → 450K YouTube → TBPN with Zuckerberg and Altman as guests. Lateral moves feel like failure. Everything must escalate.

2. **The near-death crucible.** When VC dried up in 2022, Lucy had 3 months of runway. Coogan called his board: "Anyone who thinks we're raising is delusional." Cut everything. Six months later: first profitable quarter in a decade of entrepreneurship. His framing: "a great crucible and very fun to be on the other side."

3. **Mood doesn't matter.** TBPN goes live every weekday, 3 hours, no matter what. "Your mood doesn't matter. How you feel doesn't matter." Compare that to startups where you constantly negotiate with yourself about whether to make that sales call.

4. **Humor as a launch strategy.** Every Coogan venture starts as a joke — the cannibalism branding at Soylent, fake yacht ads at TBPN. "Humor gives you permission to do something low status." It's also an emotional hedge: you can't fail if it was never serious.

5. **Partnership over solo.** He's been building with the same co-founder (David Renteln) since preschool. Their structure: "you don't want every decision turning into a committee vote."

I wrote a longer personality analysis if anyone's interested: [LINK]

What patterns do you recognize in your own founder psychology?
