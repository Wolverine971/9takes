## Strategy Snapshot (Cheat Sheet Alignment)

- **No-waste opener:** "Finding £13 in a chicken shop made him happier than becoming a millionaire."
- **Entertaining angle:** A man worth nine figures says the best financial moment of his life was digging coins out of a vinyl seat at 18.
- **Educational angle:** Type 3 (The Achiever) builds identity on output — so the "win" never lands, because the gap it was supposed to close was never about money.
- **Expressive angle:** 9takes POV — his drive was never ambition. By his own words it was insecurity, and the empire is the scar tissue.
- **Falsifiable claim:** A true Type 3 keeps achieving even after the achievement stops feeling good. If Bartlett had walked away after the millionaire anti-climax, the read would weaken.
- **Counter-signal:** If his motivation were pleasure/variety-seeking (Type 7), he'd chase novelty and avoid the grind. Instead he color-codes a calendar and wears the same outfit daily to remove friction from work. That's image-and-output, not escape.
- **9takes-only claim:** "Most takes call him a hustle-culture guru. We map the fear underneath the hustle — and he confirms it on the record."
- **One Mississippi result:** Passes — "£13 beat a million" is instantly legible.
- **Competitor-signability check:** A generic motivation account would post "Steven's mindset is incredible." Only 9takes pairs the chicken-shop scene with the Type 3 fear-of-worthlessness mechanism and the 22-hour Bali flight as the integration move.

## Abstract -> Concrete Rewrites (Required)

- **Rewrite 1:** "He's image-obsessed." -> "He wears the same all-black outfit every single day — board meetings, gym, stage — and films in a studio engineered to look like a therapist's living room."
- **Rewrite 2:** "He struggles with intimacy." -> "Time with his fiancée goes into a color-coded calendar as a scheduled event, because if it isn't blocked, it loses to work."
- **Rewrite 3:** "Success felt empty for him." -> "He's said becoming a multimillionaire was 'anti-climax' while finding £13.40 in a chicken-shop seat at 18 was 'euphoria.'"
