## Content Strategy

### The Core Play

**Don't create new questions. Find high-traffic existing questions and answer them.**

Quora's algorithm surfaces strong answers on popular existing questions far more than new ones. The sweet spot: questions with 30–150 existing answers (enough traffic to matter, not so many that you're buried).

### Answer Formula

```
1. Direct answer in sentence 1 — never bury the lede
2. "Here's the pattern I keep seeing..."
3. Break into 2-3 personality types that explain the behavior
4. Concrete action step (what to actually do)
5. Soft close: reference 9takes naturally if it fits
```

### Formatting Rules (mobile-first)

- 2-3 sentence paragraphs max — most readers are on mobile
- **Bold** the first sentence of each section
- Use bullet points for type breakdowns
- One blank line between every paragraph
- Links go 2/3 down the answer — never at the top
- Link to 9takes blog posts, not the homepage directly

### Link Strategy (avoid spam filters)

- Never link in the first third of an answer
- Link to a specific blog post, not a homepage or signup page
- Only add a link when it genuinely adds context — don't force it
- One link per answer max
- Never shorten URLs

## Sample Answers

### Answer #1: "Why do some people always play the victim?"

> They're not faking it — they've wired their brain to interpret threat everywhere.
>
> Two patterns look identical from the outside but run on completely different engines:
>
> **Type 6 (Loyalist):** Scans for worst-case scenarios constantly. Not manipulating — genuinely anxious. The "victim" framing is fear talking, not a strategy.
>
> **Type 4 (Individualist):** Identity is built around emotional depth and struggle. Victimhood can become a source of meaning, not just a complaint. They need to feel _understood_, not fixed.
>
> **The tell:** Type 6 wants reassurance. Type 4 wants depth of acknowledgment.
>
> Push back on a 6 and they spiral. Push back on a 4 and they feel dismissed and double down.
>
> Read the pattern first. Respond to what's actually happening, not the surface behavior.

---

### Answer #2: "Why does my partner shut down during arguments?"

> Because their nervous system hits a wall before their words do.
>
> Shutting down during conflict isn't weakness or manipulation — it's usually one of two things:
>
> **Type 9 (Peacemaker):** Conflict feels existentially destabilizing. They're not stonewalling you — they've gone numb trying to keep the peace. Pushing harder makes it worse. Giving them time and then asking a specific, low-stakes question is what reopens the door.
>
> **Type 5 (Investigator):** Needs to process internally before responding. An argument demands emotional real-time that they can't give in the moment. They'll disengage to think, and come back with something considered — but they need space to do it.
>
> The mistake most people make: treating silence as hostility. It's almost never that.
>
> What actually works: name what you're observing, not what you're accusing. "You seem far away right now" lands differently than "you always shut me out."

---

### Answer #3: "How do I deal with a coworker who always needs to be right?"

> Depends on _why_ they need to be right — the approach is completely different based on the engine driving it.
>
> **Type 1 (Reformer):** Needs things done correctly, not just their way. They'll push back hard on what they perceive as errors. The move here: acknowledge where they're right first, then introduce your angle. They're not ego-driven — they're standards-driven.
>
> **Type 3 (Achiever):** Being right is tied to status and image. Losing an argument feels like losing ground. Give them a way to look good while accepting your idea — frame your suggestion as something that makes them look sharp, not as a correction.
>
> **Type 8 (Challenger):** Tests ideas through confrontation. If you fold immediately, they lose respect for you. If you hold your position with confidence and evidence, they actually come around faster than most types. Don't appease — engage.
>
> Same surface behavior, three completely different responses needed. Treating them all the same is why most "dealing with difficult coworkers" advice doesn't work.

## Posting Schedule

### Frequency

- **Minimum:** 3 answers per week
- **Growth target:** 1 answer per day
- **Reality check:** Quality over volume — one great answer outperforms five mediocre ones

### Best Timing

- **Best days:** Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday
- **Peak day:** Wednesday (highest views + shares)
- **Time window:** Post between 8 AM – 10 AM EST for early upvotes (algorithm boosts answers that get upvoted quickly after posting)

### Consistency > Perfection

Authority on Quora compounds. The first 30 answers feel invisible. Around answer 50+, the algorithm starts recognizing you as a consistent voice in the niche and distributes your content more aggressively.

## Quora Spaces — Worth It?

**Not yet.** Launch a Space after 50+ answers when you have an established presence. A Space with no existing followers dies fast. The sequence:

1. Build answer history first (50+ answers)
2. Develop a following in the niche
3. Launch a Space as a content hub (e.g., "The Enneagram Explained")
4. Cross-promote on Twitter/Instagram

**Space idea when ready:** "Decode Your Personality" — curated Enneagram breakdowns, type-specific relationship tips, and questions about why people do what they do.

## What Not to Do

- Don't create questions to answer them — spam flag, possible suspension
- Don't put links at the top of answers — instant spam signal
- Don't use shortened URLs
- Don't be vague in credentials ("personality enthusiast" = invisible)
- Don't write academic-toned answers — Quora rewards personal, direct, experience-based voice
- Don't answer outside your actual depth — Quora readers in this niche can smell recycled content
