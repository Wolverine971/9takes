# Personality Analysis Series — North Star

**Status:** Permanent. This is the governing principle for every post in the @9takesdotcom personality-analysis series. All lineup docs, caption drafts, slide designs, and Reel adaptations defer to this doc.
**Owner:** DJ Wayne
**Adopted:** 2026-05-19

---

## The Goal

**Build curiosity about what makes this person tick.**

Each post stacks specific, insider, lore-rich facts — and lets the reader assemble the psychology themselves. The series wins when the reader walks away thinking _"I want to know more about this person."_ not _"That was a clever post."_

That's the whole star.

---

## The Three Words

If a post fails any one of these, it's not a personality-analysis-series post:

1. **Lore.** Every fact contributes to the portrait of who this person actually is. Not a hot take. Not a moral verdict. Not a meme. Lore — the kind of specific, verifiable detail that compounds into a character.
2. **Insider.** Things people probably don't know. If 80% of their fans could recite the fact from their Wikipedia opener, it doesn't go in.
3. **Curiosity.** The post must leave the reader with a _question_, not a conclusion. The blog is the answer. The post is the question.

---

## What Earns a Tidbit Slot

Run every candidate fact through these five tests. Five-for-five or cut it.

1. **Insider** — A casual fan would not already know this. If it's the headline of their Wikipedia page, cut.
2. **Engine-revealing** — It hints at _how_ they became this person. Childhood incidents, defining family details, recurring habits, decisions that show the pattern.
3. **Specific** — Real name, real date, real number, real quote. _"5,127 prototypes"_ beats _"thousands of prototypes."_ _"$7 in his bank account"_ beats _"he was broke."_
4. **Scroll-stopping on its own** — Read in isolation, a casual viewer pauses on it. If it needs setup to land, it's not a tidbit — it's a paragraph.
5. **Opens a question** — The fact raises a _why_ in the reader. It doesn't resolve a question; it creates one.

If a fact fails any of the five, it's research notes — not slide-2 material.

---

## Length & Rhythm (slide-2 design rule)

**Hard cap: 115 characters per tidbit.** Past that, the eye breaks. The slide isn't a paragraph; it's a stack of cards.

**Vary the rhythm across the 5–7 tidbits on every slide:**

- **Short shockers** (~50–80 chars) — punchy, one-line, surprise. _"He had less than $7 in his bank account before Game of Thrones."_
- **Deep serious** (~90–115 chars) — heavier, more weight, the lore beat that does the most psychological work. _"Pinochet's secret police drove his parents into the Venezuelan embassy when he was a baby."_

Aim for 2–3 of each on every slide. A slide of all-shockers reads flippant; all-serious reads heavy. The mix is the rhythm.

**Trim rules:**

- Cut attribution unless the quote IS the line (_"he told Bella Ramsey: 'my anxiety is right here'"_ — keep; generic _"he said in an interview"_ — cut).
- Cut hedge words (_roughly, approximately, around_) unless the number is the point.
- Round long numbers if the digits aren't the punchline ($600,000+ → $600K+).
- Spell out only what the reader needs. The blog has the footnotes.

## What the Caption Is Doing

The caption is **not** explaining the person. It's stacking lore until a real question forms in the reader's head, then pointing them to the answer.

**Recommended structure (variants welcome, the goal isn't):**

1. **Stack** — 2–3 lore beats from the slide-2 pool, flat delivery, no commentary
2. **The question** — A genuine question the lore raises about who this person is or how they became this way
3. **CTA** — "Full personality breakdown on 9takes — link in bio."
4. **Engagement bait** — Vary across the run: _"Which fact stayed with you?"_ / _"Which one wants the most context?"_ / _"Tag someone who needs this read."_

Avoid:

- Cheap reveal templates (_"Everyone thinks X / but actually Y"_) — they age fast and force binary framing onto people whose lore isn't binary
- Therapy-speak (_compassion, healing, vulnerable, journey_) — flattens the lore into self-help
- Conclusion lines that tell the reader what to think — robs them of the assembly work
- Adjectives that pre-judge — let the lore land naked

---

## What the Post Is NOT Doing

- **Not** proving a contradiction. The surface doesn't have to be wrong. The lore just has to be richer than the headline.
- **Not** explaining. We accumulate. We don't conclude.
- **Not** moralizing. _Good_ or _bad_ is irrelevant. _Interesting_ is everything.
- **Not** competing with stan accounts. We're not faster, hotter, or more current. We're deeper.

---

## Pre-Publish Test

Before any post goes up, every box gets ticked:

- [ ] Slide 2 gives the reader 5–7 things they probably didn't know
- [ ] Each tidbit passes the five-test filter (insider, engine-revealing, specific, scroll-stopping, opens a question)
- [ ] The caption stacks lore without telling the reader what to feel
- [ ] The post ends on a real question, not a conclusion
- [ ] The curiosity drives the reader to the blog, not just the comments
- [ ] No therapy-speak, no moral verdict, no cheap-reveal template

If a post can't pass all six, it's not ready.

## How This Governs Everything Downstream

- **The master tidbits doc** (`docs/instagram/post-ideas/2026-04-29_top-tier-people-tidbits.md`) is raw material. The five tests above turn it into slide-2 content.
- **Lineup docs** (per arc) defer to this doc. They schedule and assign; they don't redefine the goal.
- **Legacy posts** that don't match the goal (e.g., the 10-slide Peter Thiel lead-in carousel) stay legacy. Don't reverse-engineer them to fit — just don't repeat that format.
- **Reel adaptations** carry the same goal at a different tempo: one lore beat → the question it raises → "full read on 9takes."
- **The series can outlive specific arcs, themes, sub-formats, and seasons.** The north star doesn't move.
