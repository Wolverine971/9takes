## 1. Method note

I sorted the data pull's outliers (posts that hit a high multiple of an account's typical post) and asked, for each: **what was happening culturally when this dropped, and is there a personality / psychology angle that 9takes hasn't covered?**

I cross-referenced the bigger ones via web search to verify the cultural context (sources at bottom). Some of the highest-multiplier posts are old (2024 or earlier) — they survived in the recent grid because nothing has touched them. Old outliers tell us what the format / topic ceiling is for that account; recent outliers tell us what's hitting _now_.

## 3. Format / structural patterns I noticed

These are tactical — for our own posting, not just identifying topics:

1. **Carousels are king.** Almost every outlier in the pull is a carousel. Single image rare, video rare. Multi-slide story format with a strong-hook first slide is the format we should be writing for IG.
2. **Hook = "obscure-but-instantly-comprehensible fact."** Examples: Hugh Laurie's real limp, Cats share 95.6% DNA with tigers, John McAfee, Bourdain's secret Reddit. The pattern: take a known person/thing and reveal a hidden detail.
3. **Identification mechanics drive comments, not just likes.** Power Rangers ("which one are you?") had 1,727 comments. _Comments mean shareability mean reach._ For 9takes specifically, "which type are you?" is our native version of this and we should use it constantly.
4. **Long-form essay carousels work** if the writer has authority (relewans, flawdofficial). They probably wouldn't work for an account just starting out. We'd need to pilot to see.
5. **Lyric cards with identifiable artist** — works for music-curation accounts (lost.in.pieces). Not really our format unless we made personality-quote cards: "If this lyric speaks to you, you're probably a Type 4."
