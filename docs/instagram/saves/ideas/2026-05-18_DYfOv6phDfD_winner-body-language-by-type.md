# Content Idea: 5 "winner" signals, 9 type performances

## Core Reframe

Vanessa Van Edwards' 5 "winner" body-language signals — steady eye contact, open posture, slower movement, genuine smile, controlled gestures — are real and learnable. But they look like five different people depending on which Enneagram type is running them. 9takes adds the type-overlay: 3w2s perform all five natively (calibrated to the room); 5s, 6s, and 1s can _learn_ them but break under stress; 8s break the slow-movement rule by default; 4s undercut the smile with intensity. Falsifiable claim: if the signals were type-blind, two people running them perfectly should read identically. They don't. Counter-signal: cover the face in any video of a "trained" Type 5 vs a native Type 3 — the body still reads different because the cadence is type-sourced, not signal-sourced.

## Hook Options

- Hook A: Same 5 signals. 9 completely different versions of "winner." Yours might be backfiring.
- Hook B: Vanessa's 5 winner signals work. They also expose your type within 10 seconds.
- Hook C: You can learn body language. You can't learn the cadence — that's type-sourced.

## Evidence Needed

- Two paired examples: a public Type 3 (natively calibrated) vs a public Type 5 or 6 (trained but visibly rehearsed) running the same 5 signals. Need named figures whose type read has 9takes archive support.
- One example of an 8 breaking the slow-movement rule on camera and still reading as a "winner" — the signal-set is descriptive, not prescriptive.
- Counter-evidence to address: "trained body language clearly closes the gap for non-natives." True for low-stakes situations; under genuine stress the cadence reverts. That's the falsifier and the actionable insight.
- Link to vvanedwards' framing as the setup — extend, do not dismiss. She's a 1.1M-follower peer/anchor.
