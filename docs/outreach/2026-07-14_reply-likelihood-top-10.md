# Who Is Most Likely to Reply? — Top 10 Outreach Plan

**Created:** 2026-07-14  
**Scope:** The 382 published/profile-linked people in `src/lib/components/molecules/famousTypes.ts`, narrowed by reachability, current relevance, article strength, channel quality, and evidence of actual interaction.  
**Goal:** Earn a genuine correction, reaction, or short exchange—not a share, meeting, endorsement, or favor.

## The answer in one sentence

The best next move is not another campaign aimed at the twelve largest podcast stars. It is a small, evidence-led sequence: continue the already-warm Shaan Puri thread, then contact people who explicitly publish a direct route and whose 9takes article contains a question only they can answer.

## Evidence audit: what has actually happened

I searched the Gmail account for direct name matches, sent messages containing 9takes personality-analysis links, and inbox replies mentioning 9takes. The result changes the old campaign plan:

- **Shaan Puri replied.** His exact response was: “an entertaining read, about my favorite subject, me.” This is the strongest possible evidence that the basic device—an unusually specific, playful profile about the recipient—can work. A follow-up from DJ did not receive another answer.
- **Sam Parr was copied on the Shaan message and did not reply.** Do not send him a fresh cold version of the same pitch. A future attempt should only happen through a warm Shaan forward or after there is genuinely new work.
- **Alex Hormozi was emailed on 2026-05-18** through Acquisition.com support and did not reply.
- **Chris Williamson was emailed on 2026-05-15** and did not reply.
- **Andrew Schulz was emailed through Jamiel Hibbert on 2026-06-30** and did not reply.
- **Steven Bartlett was emailed at his press inbox on 2026-07-14.** The response was automated and explicitly said the inbox is handled by PR, receives high volume, and is not monitored by Steven. Wait; do not resend through another address.
- A Gmail message purporting to be from “Garry Tan” used a suspicious lookalike address and is **not** counted as relationship evidence.

The useful empirical lesson is not merely “Shaan likes stories about himself.” The successful note had four qualities the unanswered sends often lacked: it sounded like DJ, it proved real familiarity, it made the analysis entertaining before asking for anything, and it reached a personal inbox rather than a press queue.

## How the ranking works

This is a prioritization score, **not a literal reply probability**.

| Factor              | Weight | What earns points                                                               |
| ------------------- | -----: | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Warm/reply evidence |     35 | Prior human response, real relationship, or credible mutual path                |
| Direct channel      |     20 | Personal/owned inbox, explicit contact invitation, or active DM route           |
| Attention tax       |     15 | Fewer layers of staff and less celebrity-level inbound volume                   |
| Article leverage    |     15 | A vivid, defensible hook and a question only the subject can answer             |
| Timeliness          |     10 | The person/project is active now and the article connects to current work       |
| Personality fit     |      5 | The message can offer the kind of response the inferred type is likely to enjoy |

The Enneagram contribution is intentionally small. It is useful for choosing the _shape of the invitation_—play, precision, correction, recognition, challenge—not for pretending that a public figure’s type is known with certainty.

The inventory screen happened in two passes. First, I removed profiles whose subject is deceased or historical, people already contacted, heads of state and celebrity-scale accounts with no credible direct route, and unpublished (`link: false`) profiles. Second, I compared the reachable remainder on official contact evidence, current activity, audience saturation, and the strength of the article’s signature question.

## What DJ’s limited social presence changes

DJ’s strongest verifiable public surface is [LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/in/djwayne): 774 followers and 500+ connections at the time of research, with a credible story—USMC veteran, engineer, and builder. That is enough to look real when a recipient checks, but not enough to make “tag them and hope” an outreach strategy.

Use social as **identity verification and context**, not audience leverage:

1. Make the LinkedIn headline and Featured section immediately explain 9takes and feature one excellent profile.
2. For John, Jordi, Michael, and Alex, leave one substantive comment on a genuinely relevant new post. Do not mention the profile in that comment unless it directly contributes to the discussion.
3. Send the direct note one to three days later. The comment makes the name faintly familiar; it is not a manufactured “relationship.”
4. Do not mass-tag the top ten in a thread, cold-send the same image to all of them, or announce that they have been “typed.” Those moves make a limited account look promotional rather than observant.

## Recommended sequence

Do not send ten messages in one week. Send two per week, learn from the replies, and revise the next pair.

| Week | Action                                                     | Why                                                                           |

| 1    | Shaan thread; Neil Strauss                                 | One proven relationship plus the strongest new direct-inbox case              |

One follow-up at day 7 is enough. It must add something new—a correction made, a sharper single question, or a native card they requested. If there is still no answer, stop. The goal is relationship quality, not extracting a response.

## Operating rules for every draft

- The **scene**, not the Enneagram number, goes in the subject.
- Use one profile link and one question.
- Treat disagreement or correction as a successful reply.
- Never claim certainty about the person’s type.
- Never ask for a call, share, repost, quote, or testimonial in the first exchange.
- Do not send the same message through two channels simultaneously.
- Do not attach the square card to a cold email.
- Log every send and human reply. Automated replies do not count.
- Stop after one value-adding follow-up.
