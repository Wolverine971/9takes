## What counts as success

Rank outcomes this way:

1. A factual or interpretive correction
2. A short answer to the tailored question
3. A staff acknowledgment that confirms the message reached the right queue
4. A reaction or like from the principal
5. No response

A disagreement is a better result than a generic compliment. Do not steer a critical reply toward praise.

## Universal reply handling

### They correct the article

Reply within one business day:

> Thank you—this is exactly the kind of correction I was hoping for. I’m going back to the source and fixing it. I’ll send you the corrected passage once, then get out of your inbox.

Make the correction before asking anything else. Do not immediately request a quote, share, or endorsement.

### They say the read is accurate

Reply:

> That’s useful to know. Which line earned the “accurate”—the part I should preserve if I tighten the piece later?

If that question would turn a one-line acknowledgment into work, simply thank them instead.

### They ask what DJ wants

Reply:

> A correction or one-line reaction is the whole ask. I’m not trying to book you or turn this into a promotion.

### They share the article without replying

Thank them publicly in one sentence. Do not add another ask in the public thread. A private thank-you is optional but should be equally short.

### They do not respond

Follow the person-specific rule. Shaan gets no further follow-up. Neil’s form should not be resubmitted. John and Michael can receive one value-adding follow-up. Jordi should not be chased across platforms if he ignores the connection request.
