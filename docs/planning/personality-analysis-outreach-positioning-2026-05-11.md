## Core Pitch

9takes helps people understand themselves and others by decoding the emotional patterns behind public behavior.

The personality profiles are not gossip, diagnosis, or cheap provocation. They are public personality analysis pieces built from interviews, writing, decisions, public behavior, and the Enneagram.

The outreach pitch should communicate:

> I wrote a serious personality analysis of [Person]. The goal is to be sharp but fair. I would value your reaction because you know this person, this world, or this pattern better than I do.

## Positioning Pillars

### 1. Understanding Over Judgment

9takes should explain people without shaming them.

Use language like:

- "I am trying to understand the pattern, not dunk on the person."
- "The goal is to make public figures more understandable."
- "I want the piece to be fair, useful, and grounded."

Avoid:

- "I exposed..."
- "I diagnosed..."
- "I figured out what is wrong with..."
- "This proves they are..."

### 2. Public Pattern, Not Private Diagnosis

The analysis is based on public material. Say this directly when needed.

Use language like:

- "This is based on public interviews, writing, decisions, and behavior."
- "It is an interpretation, not a clinical diagnosis."
- "I use the Enneagram as a lens, not a verdict."

Avoid:

- "This is their true type."
- "I know exactly why they do this."
- "Their core wound is..."

### 3. Sharp But Fair

The work should have enough edge to be interesting, but enough humility to invite correction.

Use language like:

- "I tried to make it sharp without making it cheap."
- "If I overstated something, I want to know."
- "If I missed context, I am open to updating it."

Avoid:

- "This is a roast."
- "I dare you to prove me wrong."
- "This is brutally honest."

The Dave Portnoy path can be more direct, but the default brand should still be fairness first.

### 4. Better Source Context

The recipient should feel like they can improve the piece.

Use language like:

- "What did I miss?"
- "Is there a better example that explains this pattern?"
- "Is anything factually wrong, overstated, or unfairly framed?"

This turns the email from promotion into collaborative fact-checking.

### 5. The 9takes Meta-Angle

The public figure is the doorway. The deeper reason is that readers see themselves through the analysis.

Use language like:

- "People read these because they want to understand public figures, but they often end up recognizing patterns in themselves."
- "A good profile helps readers see the emotional logic behind behavior."
- "The point is not celebrity gossip. The point is pattern recognition."

This ties directly to the brand positioning: "See the emotions behind every take."

## Strategic Lesson From Existing Docs

The current 9takes strategy says distribution is about trust routing and entity authority, not just posting links.

For outreach, that means:

- The goal is not one email blast.
- The goal is to create credible third-party conversations around 9takes.
- A reply, quote, comment, public correction, social mention, or podcast mention can all become authority signals.
- Each profile can create a small network campaign around a person and their world.

The Breaking Points strategy adds another important lesson:

- Sometimes the profile is not the opener.
- Sometimes the profile is the follow-up relationship builder.
- If the recipient already cares about the subject, lead with the analysis.
- If the recipient cares about a broader issue, lead with that issue and use the profile as proof of methodology.

## Persuasion Framework

Before choosing the first target, score the person across these angles.

| Angle                | Question                                                                              | Why It Matters                                            |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- |
| Self-curiosity       | Do they already talk about psychology, behavior, identity, growth, or self-knowledge? | They are more likely to read a profile about themselves.  |
| Audience fit         | Would their audience enjoy personality analysis?                                      | Even if the subject ignores it, the audience can engage.  |
| Reachability         | Do they see DMs, emails, replies, comments, or tags?                                  | Great fit does not matter if there is no path in.         |
| Ego safety           | Will the profile feel interesting instead of invasive?                                | Lower defensiveness increases reply odds.                 |
| Amplification upside | Would a reply, comment, or share matter?                                              | Some responses can create meaningful exposure.            |
| Content quality      | Is the 9takes profile strong enough to send?                                          | Do not distribute a piece that would embarrass the brand. |
| Network map          | Are there accessible people around them?                                              | A network campaign beats a single cold email.             |

Best first-test profile is the one with the strongest combined score, not necessarily the biggest audience.

## Custom Email Recipe

Each customized email should be built from 7 blocks.

### Block 1: Specific Recipient Hook

Mention why this person is getting the email.

Examples:

- "Since you have interviewed [Person]..."
- "Since you write about the creator economy..."
- "Since you are close to the TBPN/startup media world..."
- "Since your audience already thinks about personality and self-knowledge..."

### Block 2: Founder Context

Keep it short.

> I am DJ, founder of 9takes. I write personality analysis essays using the Enneagram as a lens.

Optional expansion:

> The goal is to help people understand public figures more clearly, and through them, themselves and others.

### Block 3: Profile Link

Simple.

> I wrote one on [Person]: [link]

### Block 4: Why This Profile Matters

Tie the profile to the recipient's world.

Examples:

- "It maps the psychology behind his interview style and self-optimization drive."
- "It looks at the operating pattern behind how he builds and explains companies."
- "It breaks down why her interview persona works so well."
- "It tries to explain why his bluntness creates both trust and backlash."

### Block 5: Fairness Frame

This is the credibility block.

> The goal is to be sharp but fair. I am not trying to diagnose, flatter, or do a hit piece.

### Block 6: Main Ask

Use one ask. Do not stack five asks in the first email.

Best default:

> Does it feel fair? What is overstated, understated, wrong, or missing?

For the subject:

> What did I get right, and where am I off?

For a network insider:

> Is there context I am missing from the outside?

### Block 7: Adjacent Links

Use 1-2 links max.

> For context, I have also written about [Adjacent Person] and [Adjacent Person], which may be relevant to your world: [links]

Do not include too many links. The main profile should stay the focus.

## Subject Line Rules

Good subject lines:

- Specific
- Low pressure
- Curiosity-driven
- Honest

Examples:

- "Is this fair?"
- "Quick question about my [Name] analysis"
- "[Name], curious what I missed"
- "Would value your read on this"
- "I analyzed your public operating system"

Avoid:

- "You need to see this"
- "This will blow your mind"
- "I exposed your personality"
- "Please share this"
- "Collaboration opportunity"

## Response Ladder

If they reply positively:

> This is helpful. Are you okay with me quoting part of this, or would you prefer I treat it as private feedback?

If they correct something:

> Thank you. I will review and update that. If you have a cleaner way to frame it, I am open to using your language.

If they disagree with the typing:

> That is useful. I do not want the type label to flatten the person. What pattern do you think explains them better?

If they ask why you write these:

> Public figures give people a shared reference point. When readers understand why a recognizable person acts the way they do, they often start recognizing the same patterns in themselves and the people around them. That is the real point.

If they ask you to take it down:

> I understand why personality analysis can feel personal. I do not want the piece to be unfair or factually wrong, and I am open to corrections, clarifications, or adding your perspective. That said, I generally do not take down good-faith analysis of public figures based on public information solely because someone dislikes the interpretation. If there is anything inaccurate, private, or unfairly framed, I will address it directly.

## Pre-Send Checklist

Before sending a profile:

- The blog is published and strong enough to represent 9takes.
- The profile does not contain avoidable cheap shots.
- The strongest claim is clear.
- The most sensitive claim has evidence.
- The email has one primary ask.
- The recipient hook is specific.
- The adjacent links are relevant.
- The tone matches the recipient.
- The email does not ask for a share first.
- The email includes an opt-out path if used as cold commercial email.

## Working Principle

Lead with the reason they would care.

For self-knowledge people, the reason is introspection.
For operators, the reason is decision patterns.
For creators, the reason is public persona.
For insiders, the reason is accuracy.
For communities, the reason is debate.

The same blog can be pitched 5 different ways. The link stays the same. The frame changes.
