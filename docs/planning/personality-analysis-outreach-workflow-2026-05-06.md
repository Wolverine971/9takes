# Personality Analysis Outreach Workflow

Created: May 6, 2026

## Purpose

Use 9takes personality analysis blogs to start conversations with public figures and people in their network.

The goal is not to provoke for its own sake. The goal is to help people understand themselves and others better through public personality analysis, using the Enneagram as the main lens.

The desired outcomes are:

- Direct replies from the person or their network
- Corrections, clarifications, and missing context
- Public comments on the blog
- Quotes or source comments incorporated into the blog
- Social shares
- Introductions to people closer to the subject
- Better, fairer, more accurate profiles over time

## Brand Voice

Default voice: curious analyst with a cultural edge.

The tone should sit between:

- Curious analyst: thoughtful, fair, open to correction
- Sharp cultural commentator: specific, interesting, willing to say something real

The voice should flex based on the person.

Use more direct, casual language for blunt media personalities and creators. Use more thoughtful, intellectual language for founders, writers, academics, and operators.

The constant values:

- Respectful
- Clear
- Specific
- Open to being corrected
- Willing to touch sensitive topics carefully
- Focused on understanding, not dunking

Avoid sounding like:

- A fan begging for attention
- A therapist diagnosing someone
- A spammer promoting links
- A gossip writer trying to expose someone
- A manipulator using personality language to corner someone

## Positioning

9takes writes public personality analysis from public material.

Suggested positioning:

> I write personality analysis essays using the Enneagram as a lens. They are based on public interviews, writing, decisions, and behavior. The goal is to help people better understand public figures, and through them, themselves and others.

Important framing:

- These are interpretations, not clinical diagnoses.
- The work is based on public information.
- The goal is fairness and understanding.
- Feedback can improve the profile.
- Direct source comments can be added to the blog if the person is open to that.

## Campaign Unit

Each outreach campaign starts with one profile.

Example campaigns:

- John Coogan profile
- Dave Portnoy profile
- Alex Cooper profile
- Joe Rogan profile

For each campaign, define:

- Main profile URL
- Subject's likely communication style
- Strongest claim in the analysis
- Most sensitive claim in the analysis
- Likely objection
- Best ask
- 1-2 adjacent 9takes links the recipient may care about
- Target list of people in and around the subject's network

## Target Tiers

### Tier 1: The Subject

Email the person the analysis is about when a reasonable public/business contact path exists.

Primary ask:

> Is this fair?

Secondary asks:

- What did I get wrong?
- What did I miss?
- Is anything overstated or understated?
- Would you be open to leaving a comment?
- Could I quote your reaction in the piece?

### Tier 2: Direct Network

People who know, work with, collaborate with, interview, invest in, compete with, or regularly discuss the subject.

Primary ask:

> Since you are closer to this world than I am, does this read feel fair?

Secondary asks:

- Is the analysis missing important context?
- Is there a better example that explains the pattern?
- Is there someone else I should ask?

### Tier 3: Adjacent Network

People who may not know the subject directly but understand the world around them.

Examples:

- Creators in the same category
- Investors in the same scene
- Podcast hosts
- Journalists
- Industry commentators
- Fan account operators
- Community moderators

Primary ask:

> Curious if this matches how you understand this person's public persona.

### Tier 4: Audience / Community

Use carefully. Be upfront that the post is from 9takes.

Primary ask:

> I wrote this personality breakdown of [Person]. Curious whether fans think it is fair or off.

Do not pretend to be a random fan.

## Pre-Send Review

Before emailing anyone, review the profile and answer:

1. What is the clearest insight in this piece?
2. What sentence could make the subject defensive?
3. What factual claims should be checked again?
4. Is there anything private, speculative, or unnecessarily harsh?
5. What would the person likely respect about the piece?
6. What would they likely reject?
7. What is the simplest ask for this recipient?

If the profile has avoidable harshness, clean it up before outreach.

## CTA Ladder

Start with the smallest ask.

1. Reaction: "Is this fair?"
2. Correction: "What did I get wrong?"
3. Context: "What did I miss?"
4. Comment: "Would you leave a comment on the post?"
5. Quote: "Can I quote part of your reaction?"
6. Intro: "Is there anyone closer to this I should ask?"
7. Share: "If you think it is fair, I would appreciate you sharing it."

Do not lead with "please share this." Let sharing be an earned ask after they react positively.

## Comment Handling

Comments can be used in three ways:

- Added at the bottom of the blog as a comment
- Incorporated into the body of the blog as source context
- Quoted in an update note if the person gives permission

Default response when someone gives useful feedback:

> This is helpful. Are you okay with me using this as source context in the piece, or would you prefer it stay as private feedback?

## Optional Right Of Response

This is undecided.

Possible approach:

- Add a short "Corrections and source comments" section to selected profiles.
- Use it only when someone close to the subject provides meaningful feedback.
- Avoid making every profile look like a legal dispute or takedown negotiation.

Potential language:

> This profile is based on public information and may be updated with corrections, clarifications, or direct source comments.

## Handling A Takedown Request

Be calm, respectful, and clear.

Suggested response:

> I understand why personality analysis can feel personal. I do not want the piece to be unfair or factually wrong, and I am open to corrections, clarifications, or adding your perspective.
>
> That said, I generally do not take down good-faith analysis of public figures based on public information solely because someone dislikes the interpretation. If there is anything inaccurate, private, or unfairly framed, I will address it directly.

If they identify a factual error:

> Thank you. I will review and correct that.

If they say the interpretation is wrong:

> That is useful context. If you are open to it, I can include your response or adjust the framing so readers understand where you disagree.

If they are angry:

> I hear you. I am going to review the piece again with your objection in mind. My goal is understanding, not making people feel misrepresented.

## Outreach Guardrails

- Use public or reasonable business contact paths.
- Do not use deceptive subject lines.
- Do not imply the person gave access or approval if they did not.
- Do not present Enneagram typing as certainty.
- Do not use private information.
- Do not lead with a request to share.
- Do not mass-send the same copy without tailoring.
- Keep a suppression/opt-out list.
- Honor requests not to be contacted again.

For US commercial email basics, review the FTC CAN-SPAM guide:

https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/can-spam-act-compliance-guide-business

## First Test

Run this manually with one profile.

Recommended first campaign:

- John Coogan if the goal is thoughtful replies and useful corrections
- Dave Portnoy if the goal is testing public reaction and sharper hooks

For the first campaign:

1. Pick 10 recipients.
2. Write one subject-specific email to the person.
3. Write 3-4 tailored network emails.
4. Write 3-5 adjacent creator/commentator emails.
5. Track every send and reply.
6. Update the blog if feedback improves it.
7. Save the best-performing language for the next campaign.
