# Neil Strauss — Poll Response

"You're so honest." That one. Every time.

It lands as praise. It reads to me as a diagnosis. Because the honesty people mean when they say that is almost always the performed kind. The page-ready kind. The kind where I have already metabolized the thing, rehearsed it, and rendered it into a sentence clean enough for a stranger to nod at. If a reader finishes my book and their takeaway is that I was brave, what they are actually telling me is that I am good at the final draft. They are not praising the truth. They are praising the packaging.

The version I am not saying out loud is worse. "So honest" means I was willing to put the shame on the page. It does not mean I was willing to sit with it in the room. I wrote five hundred pages about love addiction and then lost the marriage three years after the book came out. That is not the profile of a man whose honesty saved him. That is the profile of a man who can describe a locked door beautifully from the hallway.

There is a version of "honest" that is a dodge. The confession becomes the costume. The reader feels let in. I feel, for a minute, like I exist. And then the feeling wears off and I go looking for the next thing to be honest about, because the honesty was never the point. The rendering was.

So when someone calls me honest, I hear a compliment that is really a mirror. They are telling me I am good at showing the wound. They cannot tell, from the outside, whether I have actually touched it. Most days I cannot tell either.

The praise I would actually trust is quieter. "You seem settled." Nobody says that to me. And I understand why.
