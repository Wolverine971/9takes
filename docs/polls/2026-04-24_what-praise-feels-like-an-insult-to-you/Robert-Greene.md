# Robert Greene — Poll Response

"You're so prolific." That one. It lands like a slap disguised as a bow.

What the person means, usually, is that I produce a lot of books. What I hear is that I am a machine. That the thing which took six years of reading three hundred biographies, filling four thousand index cards in fountain pen, sorting them by color on the floor until a pattern emerges, is reducible to output. Prolific is the word you use for a hen. It skips the thing that actually matters, which is whether the book saw something no one else saw, and replaces it with volume.

There is a second one, close behind it. "You're such a good writer." Said flatly, as a compliment to the sentences. The ancients understood that style without substance is flattery, and flattery from a stranger is a form of diminishment. When someone praises the prose and says nothing about what the prose was pointing at, they are telling you, without knowing it, that they did not follow you anywhere. They admired the carriage and never looked out the window.

The worst, though, is when someone calls the work "dark" as if that is a feature I engineered. The darkness in the books is not a brand. It is where the unprocessed material goes. It is the Hollywood years. It is eighty jobs. It is a father who sold cleaning supplies for forty years and secretly wanted to be a journalist and never said a word about it. To praise that as edgy is to mistake a wound for a wink.

The praise that does not insult is rare, and it tends to be quiet. Someone tells you which specific paragraph, on which specific page, changed how they looked at a person in their life. That is the only report that matters. Everything else is noise dressed up in a bow.
