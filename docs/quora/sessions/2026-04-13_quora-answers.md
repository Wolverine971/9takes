### Answer 1: Why do people ghost instead of verbally breaking up?

**Question URL:** https://www.quora.com/Why-do-people-ghost-instead-of-verbally-breaking-up
**Views:** Est. 50K+
**Answer Count:** 67
**Enneagram Angle:** Types 9, 5, 6
**Blog Link Used:** https://9takes.com/enneagram-corner/enneagram-types-being-ghosted
**Link Placement:** Sentence ~28 (6th paragraph of 7)
**Status:** Drafted

---

Ghosting isn't cowardice. It's what happens when a breakup conversation feels more threatening than disappearing does.

There's a pattern here. The behavior looks the same from the outside, but what's driving it is different for every person.

I like to break things down by the Enneagram.

Enneagram Type 9 (Peacemaker): A 9 doesn't go cold suddenly, they fade. Responses get slower. They're "so busy." By the time the messages stop entirely, they've already said goodbye a dozen times in their own head. A breakup conversation means sitting with someone's pain while being the cause of it, managing their reaction, possibly weathering their anger, all while defending a decision that's already been made. That's not discomfort for a 9, that's existential. Disappearing doesn't register to them as abandonment. It registers as the only way to stop everything from collapsing at once.

Enneagram Type 5 (Investigator): They've already processed the end internally, alone, over time. They've explained it to you in their head and played out how you'd respond and none of those responses sound like something they have energy for. So they skip it. Cut straight to the result and bypass all the emotional back and forth. The ghost isn't coldness. It's depletion.

Enneagram Type 6 (Loyalist): A 6 has probably set themselves a mental deadline. "I'll tell them this weekend." That weekend came and something felt off. "Next week." Then next week something else. Meanwhile they've imagined the conversation so many times the gap between how it should go and how it might actually go has grown huge. At some point enough time has passed that now they'd have to explain the delay too. So they don't. They ghost to avoid a catastrophe they've already imagined in detail, and a second one they accidentally created by waiting.

If you want closure from someone who ghosted you: don't frame your message as a confrontation. One low-pressure note that gives them an out, "you don't owe me a long explanation," works better than anything that sounds like a demand.

9takes has a breakdown of how each of the 9 types processes being ghosted and why some types carry it harder than others: https://9takes.com/enneagram-corner/enneagram-types-being-ghosted

That context matters when deciding whether to reach out at all.

### Answer 2: What is the definition of an extreme "people-pleaser"?

**Question URL:** https://www.quora.com/What-is-the-definition-of-an-extreme-people-pleaser-What-causes-this-personality-type-How-can-someone-overcome-it-Is-it-possible-to-become-less-of-a-people-pleaser-over-time
**Views:** Est. 30K+
**Answer Count:** 32
**Enneagram Angle:** Types 2, 9
**Blog Link Used:** https://9takes.com/how-to-guides/dating-dynamics-by-enneagram-type
**Link Placement:** Sentence ~27 (6th paragraph of 7)
**Status:** Drafted

---

An extreme people-pleaser is someone who's built their entire identity around not being a burden, and helping others is how they buy their right to exist.

That's not hyperbole. Here's what's actually happening:

**Type 2 (Helper):** Pleasing isn't a habit, it's a strategy. They remember what you mentioned six weeks ago and ask how it's going. They offer help before you've thought to need it, sometimes help you didn't want. Anticipating your needs is how they stay close, how they make themselves necessary. The cost: after years of tuning into everyone else, they've stopped tuning into themselves. Ask a Type 2 what they actually want and watch them pause. They'll often give you what they think you want to hear they want. Resentment builds invisibly and tends to come out sideways, months later, about something unrelated.

**Type 9 (Peacemaker):** Says yes because saying no feels like lighting a match near a powder keg. They'll agree to something they dread, build low-grade resentment about it for weeks, then cancel last minute or not show up at all. Then the conflict they were avoiding arrives anyway, just uglier. They're not spineless. They just experience conflict as existential disruption, and compliance is cheaper than the internal alarm that fires when they say no.

**The difference:** Type 2 pleases because they need to feel _needed_. Type 9 pleases because they need things to stay _calm_.

To actually stop: practice tolerating the discomfort of a single no before you try to sustain it as a habit. Not "learn to set limits in theory." Say no to one low-stakes thing today, then sit with the feeling that follows. The catastrophe they're bracing for almost never comes.

The romantic version of this pattern, where it shows up hardest, is here: https://9takes.com/how-to-guides/dating-dynamics-by-enneagram-type

Worth reading if you recognize this in yourself or someone you're close to.

### Answer 3: What is the psychology behind people being attracted to emotionally unavailable partners?

**Question URL:** https://www.quora.com/What-is-the-psychology-behind-people-being-attracted-to-those-who-are-emotionally-unavailable-in-relationships-and-why-do-they-continue-to-pursue-them-despite-knowing-it-will-not-lead-to-a-successful-relationship
**Views:** Est. 30K+
**Answer Count:** 36
**Enneagram Angle:** Types 2, 4, 6
**Blog Link Used:** https://9takes.com/how-to-guides/how-to-navigate-early-relationship-stages
**Link Placement:** Sentence ~26 (6th paragraph of 7)
**Status:** Drafted

---

They keep pursuing because the unavailability itself is doing something specific for them. It's not bad judgment or low self-esteem alone.

Here's the breakdown by type:

**Type 2 (Helper):** An emotionally unavailable person becomes a project with the highest possible stakes. He texted back. He told me something personal. He let me in a little. Each small opening is proof the strategy is working and proof she's worth the effort. If she can finally get someone withholding to open up and love her back, that's not just a win, that's proof she's worth loving. The problem: the threshold keeps moving. There's always one more level to earn.

**Type 4 (Individualist):** The unavailability itself is the draw. When he finally texts back after three days, the relief isn't just relief, it's electric. That's not a red flag to a 4, that's a signal the connection is real. A partner who's consistently available and easy can feel flat by comparison, even shallow. Type 4s are drawn to loves that cost something, and they'll sometimes mistake predictability for a lack of depth.

**Type 6 (Loyalist):** This is the counterintuitive one. Full emotional availability is actually more threatening to a 6 than distance. When someone is all-in, there's something real to lose, and a 6's brain immediately starts calculating when and how that happens. An emotionally unavailable partner keeps the stakes at a level the anxiety can manage. They never fully have them, so they can never really lose them.

The actual move: identify what the unavailability is giving you. Validation-seeking, intensity, or controlled distance. The answer is different for each type, and so is the fix.

The early stages of a relationship are when this pattern locks in and it's much easier to interrupt then than later: https://9takes.com/how-to-guides/how-to-navigate-early-relationship-stages

Catching it early changes the trajectory entirely.

### Answer 4: Why do people self-sabotage, and how can they stop?

**Question URL:** https://www.quora.com/Why-do-people-self-sabotage-and-how-can-they-stop
**Views:** Est. 40K+
**Answer Count:** 48
**Enneagram Angle:** Types 4, 6, 1
**Blog Link Used:** https://9takes.com/how-to-guides/using-the-enneagram-for-self-development
**Link Placement:** Sentence ~27 (6th paragraph of 7)
**Status:** Drafted

---

Self-sabotage isn't irrational. It's your personality protecting you from something that feels more threatening than failure.

Here's the breakdown:

**Type 4 (Individualist):** Success creates an identity problem. The story of the hard road, the struggle, the "I'm different from people who have it easy," only holds if things don't go too well. They'll sometimes get real close to finishing something and then just stop, no clear reason. Or they'll hit the goal and feel empty instead of good. "I thought I'd feel different." That feeling of deflation confirms a deeper fear that they aren't actually special, that they're ordinary like everyone else. It's not low self-esteem exactly. It's a reluctance to release a familiar self.

**Type 6 (Loyalist):** A promotion means more to lose. A 6 who gets one often starts running worst-case scenarios about how it could go wrong, overworks to stay safe, burns out, and the performance slips. They've engineered the outcome they feared. But there's also relief in it, because now the threat is known and manageable. The uncertainty before was worse.

**Type 1 (Reformer):** They do a lot of prep. Outlines, research, planning documents, notes, everything except the actual thing. Because the actual thing is when the gap between the standard they've set and what they can currently produce becomes visible and undeniable. "I'll start when I feel more ready" sounds reasonable. Except ready is a bar they keep raising. Procrastination is the most common version, and it looks like laziness from the outside.

The fix is different depending on which engine is running. Type 4 needs to decouple their identity from struggle. Type 6 needs to practice tolerating things going well, incrementally. Type 1 needs to practice shipping imperfect work on purpose.

The Enneagram is actually useful here because "self-sabotage" isn't one thing: https://9takes.com/how-to-guides/using-the-enneagram-for-self-development

The method that helps changes once you know the source.

### Answer 5: What is the psychology behind people who always need to be right?

**Question URL:** https://www.quora.com/What-is-the-psychology-behind-people-who-always-need-to-be-right-How-can-we-deal-with-such-people
**Views:** Est. 60K+
**Answer Count:** 69
**Enneagram Angle:** Types 1, 3, 8
**Blog Link Used:** https://9takes.com/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-8
**Link Placement:** Sentence ~28 (5th paragraph of 6)
**Status:** Drafted

---

The psychology behind needing to be right is different for every person, and the approach that works for one type completely fails for another.

Three engines, three different responses:

**Type 1 (Reformer):** They're not arguing to win. They genuinely believe there's a correct answer and that getting it right matters. They hold themselves to the same standard they're applying to you. Prove them wrong with actual evidence and they'll update on the spot. They might even come back to the conversation days later to correct something they said. That part surprises people.

**Type 3 (Achiever):** Being wrong is an image problem. Losing an argument means losing status, credibility, the version of themselves that knows what they're doing. They're not defending the idea, they're defending their position. Watch for the mid-argument pivot: "Well, that's not actually what I was saying." The move: give them a way to absorb your point while still looking sharp. "So you're saying..." and frame their agreement as their insight. They'll take it.

**Type 8 (Challenger):** Uses confrontation to test conviction. Fold immediately and they lose respect for you, fast. Hold your position with confidence and actual evidence and something shifts. An 8 who gets challenged well often comes around faster than any other type. What looks like aggression is frequently an audition. Pass it and you've earned a real conversation. Fail it and every future interaction plays out on their terms.

The pattern that changes everything: read what's actually driving the need to be right before you respond. Treating all three the same is exactly why most "dealing with difficult people" advice doesn't work.

The Type 8 dynamic specifically is worth understanding on its own, it reframes the whole confrontation pattern: https://9takes.com/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-8

Read it before your next conversation with someone like this.

### Answer 6: What is the best reaction to being stonewalled by a partner?

**Question URL:** https://www.quora.com/What-is-the-best-reaction-to-being-stonewalled-by-a-partner-significant-other
**Views:** Est. 20K+
**Answer Count:** 42
**Enneagram Angle:** Types 9, 5
**Blog Link Used:** https://9takes.com/how-to-guides/definitive-guide-to-relationship-conflict-part-1
**Link Placement:** Sentence ~26 (5th paragraph of 6)
**Status:** Drafted

---

Stop pushing. Not because they need to be accommodated, because pushing harder guarantees nothing useful comes back.

Stonewalling almost never comes from one place, and your response needs to match the actual cause:

**Type 9 (Peacemaker):** Their face doesn't go angry. It goes blank. Like a light behind the eyes turned off. They're not sitting with the argument, they've left it mentally and they're somewhere quiet waiting for it to stop. Pushing them to engage while they're in this state locks them down further. It confirms exactly why they went quiet in the first place. Give it time. Come back later with one low-stakes question that has nothing to do with the argument. Get them talking about anything. That's the door back in.

**Type 5 (Investigator):** The silence is processing, not punishment. They've hit the limit of what they can handle in real-time. You'll see it happen: they look away mid-sentence, maybe start fiddling with something. Not being dismissive, their brain genuinely can't process and respond simultaneously. Give them a concrete out: "Let's pick this up after dinner." Not "whenever you're ready," that stays open and they'll postpone it forever. A specific time, a clear ending point. They'll come back and they'll have something actually useful to say.

The mistake most people make: interpret silence as hostility, then escalate in response to that interpretation. That spiral solves nothing and confirms their reason for going quiet.

The framing that changed how I approach this across all types is here: https://9takes.com/how-to-guides/definitive-guide-to-relationship-conflict-part-1

What you say before you say anything is the whole game.
