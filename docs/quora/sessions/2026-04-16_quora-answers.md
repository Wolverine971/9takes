### Answer 1: Why do some people hate confrontation while others look for it?

**Question URL:** https://www.quora.com/Why-do-some-people-hate-confrontation-while-others-look-for-it
**Views:** Est. 25K+
**Answer Count:** Est. 40–70
**Enneagram Angle:** Types 9, 8, 1
**Blog Link Used:** https://9takes.com/how-to-guides/definitive-guide-to-relationship-conflict-part-1
**Link Placement:** Paragraph 7 of 8
**Status:** Drafted

---

Conflict isn't neutral. It's either a threat to shut down or a tool to figure out where you stand with someone. Which one you default to came from your wiring, not your choice.

Same behavior on the outside, completely different internal code. The cleanest way I've found to read it is through the Enneagram.

**Type 9 (Peacemaker):** Conflict feels destabilizing at a core level. It's not that they dislike arguments. Arguments make them go numb in real time. They'll agree with positions they don't actually hold, fade out of the conversation, or say "it's fine" just to stop the internal alarm. That's not cowardice. That's survival wiring.

**Type 8 (Challenger):** The opposite program runs here. A confrontation is how an 8 finds out what's real. If you fold under pressure, they can't trust you. If you push back and hold your ground, now they know where you actually stand, and they can relax. They're not picking fights for drama. They're picking them for information.

**Type 1 (Reformer):** The third engine, and the sneakiest one. A 1 will avoid confrontation for months until a principle gets violated. Then they can't stop themselves. The escalation isn't emotional for them. It's moral. Which is why the blowup can feel like it came out of nowhere to everyone watching.

So the person who avoids confrontation isn't weak, and the person who seeks it isn't difficult. They're solving different problems. Before you respond next time, ask yourself which problem this person is actually running: preserve peace, extract truth, or protect a principle. The answer changes what you do next.

The deeper playbook for reading conflict styles by type is here: https://9takes.com/how-to-guides/definitive-guide-to-relationship-conflict-part-1

Worth reading if you've ever walked out of an argument wondering what actually just happened.

### Answer 2: Why do some people feel the need to control everything?

**Question URL:** https://www.quora.com/Why-do-some-people-feel-the-need-to-control-everything-1
**Views:** Est. 40K+
**Answer Count:** Est. 50–100
**Enneagram Angle:** Types 6, 1, 8
**Blog Link Used:** https://9takes.com/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-6
**Link Placement:** Paragraph 7 of 8
**Status:** Drafted

---

"Control freak" is a label that hides three completely different fear systems. They look identical from the outside. They need completely different responses.

The simplest way to tell them apart is through the Enneagram.

**Type 6 (Loyalist):** Controls to manage threat. A 6's brain is constantly scanning for what could go wrong, and controlling variables is how they bring the anxiety down to a level their nervous system can live with. From the outside it looks like paranoid micromanagement. From the inside, it feels like trying to stay upright in a world that keeps shifting under their feet.

**Type 1 (Reformer):** Controls for correctness. Not "my way." The right way. Mistakes feel genuinely painful to a 1, like walking around with a rock in their shoe they can't remove. Handing work off means tolerating that rock while watching someone else do the thing wrong. That's not ego. That's a standards problem their nervous system treats as urgent.

**Type 8 (Challenger):** Controls to stay safe. For an 8, handing someone else the wheel is the same as handing them a weapon. Being in control means no one can blindside them, corner them, or use something against them later. The controlling behavior isn't about dominance. It's a refusal to be vulnerable with people who haven't earned it yet.

Same behavior, three different engines: Type 6 runs on anxiety, Type 1 runs on standards, Type 8 runs on self-protection. Try the same response on all three and you'll make two of them worse.

If you're dealing with someone like this, don't argue the behavior. Ask them what they think would happen if they let go of this one thing. The answer tells you which engine you're actually dealing with, and what would actually help.

The anxious, threat-scanning version is the one most people mean when they say "controlling" and don't understand why it runs so deep. The full breakdown of that pattern is here: https://9takes.com/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-6

That's the engine that almost never shows up in the top answers on this topic.

### Answer 3: What is the psychology behind people pleasing, and why do so many of us do it?

**Question URL:** https://www.quora.com/What-is-the-psychology-behind-people-pleasing-and-why-do-so-many-of-us-do-it
**Views:** Est. 30K+
**Answer Count:** Est. 30–80
**Enneagram Angle:** Types 2, 9
**Blog Link Used:** https://9takes.com/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-2
**Link Placement:** Paragraph 7 of 8
**Status:** Drafted

---

People-pleasing isn't one thing. It's two completely different fears wearing the same costume: one about being needed, and one about keeping the room from breaking apart.

The cleanest way I've found to separate them is the Enneagram.

**Type 2 (Helper):** The pleasing here is relational. Helping is how a 2 buys their place in your life. They remember the dentist appointment you mentioned six weeks ago. They send the care package before you've thought to ask. They anticipate needs you didn't know you had. Underneath all of it is a quiet worry: if I stop being useful, they won't stay. The cost shows up later. After years of tracking everyone else, they've stopped tracking themselves. Ask a 2 what they actually want for dinner and watch them pause.

**Type 9 (Peacemaker):** The pleasing here is conflict avoidance wearing a smile. A 9 says yes because saying no sets off an internal alarm they can't tolerate. They'll agree to something they dread, build low-grade resentment for weeks, then cancel at the last minute or quietly not show up. That isn't flakiness. It's what it costs to live in a nervous system that treats disagreement like a house fire starting.

The tell between the two: push back on a 2's help and they feel rejected. They gave you love and you handed it back. Push back on a 9 and they feel destabilized. The peace they were guarding started cracking, and now they're in the exact situation they were trying to prevent.

If you're the pleaser, stop trying to "set better boundaries in general." Pick one low-stakes thing today and say no to it. Sit with the feeling that follows. The catastrophe you're bracing for almost never arrives, and that single data point is what starts to unwind the pattern.

The Type 2 version is the one most people underestimate because it looks generous on the surface. Full breakdown of that engine is here: https://9takes.com/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-2

The fix isn't to stop giving. It's to stop giving to earn something that was never on the table in the first place.

### Answer 4: What is the psychology behind FOMO? And what type of people are most sensitive to it?

**Question URL:** https://www.quora.com/What-is-the-psychology-behind-FOMO-Why-is-it-so-powerful-And-what-type-of-people-are-the-most-sensitive-to-it
**Views:** Est. 20K+
**Answer Count:** Est. 20–50
**Enneagram Angle:** Types 7, 3, 6
**Blog Link Used:** https://9takes.com/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-7
**Link Placement:** Paragraph 7 of 8
**Status:** Drafted

---

FOMO is powerful because for some people, it isn't a quirk. It's the actual operating system their brain runs every day.

And the type most sensitive to it isn't the anxious overthinker. It's the one who looks happiest in the room.

The Enneagram splits what people call "FOMO" into three different engines:

**Type 7 (Enthusiast):** For a 7, FOMO isn't a side effect. It's the program. Their deepest fear is being stuck with pain they can't escape, so options feel like oxygen. They scan constantly for the next experience, plan, idea, or conversation. Not because they're shallow. Because closing any one door feels like containment. "Fear of missing out" is just the polite name for a much deeper fear of getting trapped.

**Type 3 (Achiever):** Status-based FOMO. If the important people are at the dinner, the conference, the event, being absent means falling behind. Missing the room isn't really about fun for a 3. It's about position. Not being there is not being in the running.

**Type 6 (Loyalist):** Anxiety-based FOMO. A 6's brain runs risk calculations in the background. What if the thing I skipped turns out to matter? What if the one I said no to was the safe choice? Their FOMO isn't pleasure-seeking. It's the background worry that missing something will cost them later.

If you want to quiet your own FOMO, don't try to "accept you can't do everything." That doesn't work on any of the three. A 7 needs to practice staying in one chosen experience until it's actually complete. A 3 needs to interrogate whether the thing they're chasing matters to them or to an audience. A 6 needs to ask, on repeat, "what if the thing I skipped turns out to be fine."

The Type 7 version is the one most people confuse with "fun-loving." The deeper pattern and what it actually costs is written up here: https://9takes.com/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-7

The question asks what type is most sensitive. The honest answer: 7s aren't the most sensitive to FOMO. They're the most formed by it.

### Answer 5: Why do some people struggle with talking about their feelings?

**Question URL:** https://www.quora.com/Why-do-some-people-struggle-with-talking-about-their-feelings-What-are-the-consequences-of-being-unable-to-express-oneself-emotionally
**Views:** Est. 10K+
**Answer Count:** Est. 15–35 (verify on Quora before posting — borderline threshold)
**Enneagram Angle:** Types 5, 8, 1
**Blog Link Used:** https://9takes.com/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-5
**Link Placement:** Paragraph 7 of 8
**Status:** Drafted

---

Because "talking about feelings" is asking three different things of three different kinds of people, and the cost of that ask is very different for each of them.

The cleanest way to see it is through the Enneagram.

**Type 5 (Investigator):** For a 5, feelings are data they haven't processed yet. Ask them how they feel in real time and you'll get silence, deflection, or a short answer. Not because they're closed off. Because they need to step back and observe their own state before they can describe it. A 5 will often share the real answer in writing, days later, after they've thought it through. Push for emotional transparency in the moment and the whole system shuts down.

**Type 8 (Challenger):** For an 8, feelings are exposure. Showing vulnerability is handing someone a weapon and trusting them not to use it. Most people haven't earned that trust yet. So an 8 projects strength, locks the soft stuff down, and lets almost no one in. When an 8 actually tells you something tender, pay attention. It cost them more than you know.

**Type 1 (Reformer):** For a 1, the inner critic runs 24/7. Sharing a feeling means exposing it to the same judgment they use on themselves. They'll especially hesitate on anger, because they've already decided their own anger is "wrong." So they swallow it, restate it as "disappointment," or explain it into a shape they can tolerate presenting to someone else.

What makes this worse: "you never open up" lands as an accusation for all three types and makes the wall taller. Lower the cost of sharing instead. Text a 5 when you ask how they're doing. Ask an 8 what they think about something before you ask how they feel. Ask a 1 what's bothering them with a clear signal that you're not going to judge the answer. Different doors, same house.

The consequence of never getting to share at all is that the feeling doesn't disappear. It leaks out sideways: through somatic stuff, passive aggression, sudden withdrawals, or a relationship that quietly loses weight over time.

The Type 5 pattern is the one people most often mistake for coldness. The full breakdown of how 5s actually process is here: https://9takes.com/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-5

People who don't talk about feelings aren't withholding. They're running a translation that costs them more than you're asking about.
