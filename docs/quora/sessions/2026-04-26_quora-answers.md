### Answer 1: What makes Rick Rubin special?

**Question URL:** https://www.quora.com/What-makes-Rick-Rubin-special
**Views:** Unverified (fetch blocked)
**Answer Count:** Unverified
**Enneagram Angle:** Type 4 (4w5)
**Blog Link Used:** https://9takes.com/personality-analysis/rick-rubin
**Link Placement:** Paragraph 4 of 5 (~80% down)
**Status:** Drafted

---

Most producers reach for the console. Rubin lies on a couch in Malibu with his eyes closed and tells the band the song isn't about what they think it's about.

The "I have no technical ability, I know nothing about music" line isn't humility. It's protection. Knowing too much would interfere with what he actually does, which is feel whether the song is doing what it's supposed to be doing. The refusal of craft is the craft.

That's why artists in transformation keep flying to him. Cash at the end. Petty mid-divorce. Adele between identities. Kanye in free-fall. Rubin doesn't fix the song. He listens for the version of you that's underneath the version you walked in with, and he says "that one." Anthony Kiedis once described it as Rubin walking away from the room and somehow already knowing the drum pattern.

In Enneagram terms it's a Type 4 pattern. The Four's whole project is hunting for what's emotionally true under what's surface-true. Most Fours never turn it into a career. Rubin built a multi-genre empire on nothing else. I went deep on this, including why he keeps getting misread as a Type 5: https://9takes.com/personality-analysis/rick-rubin

Short version: he's special because he's the only person in the room whose job is the song's interior. Everyone else is fixing the recording.

### Answer 2: What is Rick Rubin like in person?

**Question URL:** https://www.quora.com/What-is-Rick-Rubin-like-in-person
**Views:** Unverified
**Answer Count:** Unverified
**Enneagram Angle:** Type 4 with 5 wing
**Blog Link Used:** https://9takes.com/personality-analysis/rick-rubin
**Link Placement:** Paragraph 4 of 5 (~80% down)
**Status:** Drafted

---

He's slow on purpose. The bare feet, the beard, the long pauses, the way he doesn't fill space with small talk. None of it is a vibe he picked up. It's a working method.

People who've actually been in a room with him say the same thing in different ways. Anthony Kiedis described him as walking away from the room and somehow already knowing the drum pattern. Tony Iommi joked the actual production technique was "lying on the couch with a mic in my hand and saying 'Next.'" Kirk Hammett said working with him meant Rick was almost never around.

What that adds up to: Rubin treats his own attention like an instrument that gets contaminated easily. If he chats, he's not listening. If he's clever, he's not feeling. So he protects the channel, even when it makes him look weird or vacant. That's not Buddhism. It's identity defense.

Type 4 with a 5 wing is the cleanest read on what you're seeing. The bare feet and the silence look like a monk's aesthetic, but the actual function is the Four's hunt for what's emotionally true under what's surface-true. He removes external noise so the internal channel stays open. Full breakdown of how this shows up across four decades of his work: https://9takes.com/personality-analysis/rick-rubin

So when you meet him, the "what is he like" answer is: nothing's happening on the surface because everything is happening underneath. The slowness is the work.

### Answer 3: Why would people buy "The Game" from Neil Strauss when the author got divorced...

**Question URL:** https://www.quora.com/Why-would-people-buy-books-like-The-Game-from-Neil-Strauss-when-the-author-got-divorced-with-all-his-game-Do-people-forget-that-a-successful-marriage-is-more-about-marrying-a-faithful-person-instead-of-using-a-game
**Views:** Unverified
**Answer Count:** Unverified
**Enneagram Angle:** Type 4 (sexual subtype)
**Blog Link Used:** https://9takes.com/personality-analysis/neil-strauss
**Link Placement:** Paragraph 4 of 5 (~80% down)
**Status:** Drafted

---

Strauss didn't fail at marriage because his game stopped working. He failed at marriage for the same reason he wrote _The Game_ in the first place. He was looking for a self.

Most readers came to that book wanting tactics. The actual story underneath was a guy with no stable identity moving into a world full of personas and trying one on. "Style," the seducer character he played, wasn't a journalist's costume. It was a real attempt to become someone who could be chosen.

Then he got chosen, married the woman he was supposed to want, and discovered the missing-self problem hadn't gone anywhere. So he went to sex rehab and wrote a 500-page confession called _The Truth_. Then that marriage ended in 2018.

Look at his career as a whole. Pickup artist. Rock journalist. Survivalist. Love addict. Recovered love addict. Ghostwriter for Marilyn Manson, Mötley Crüe, Jenna Jameson, Kevin Hart. What you're watching is a person who keeps moving into a world, trying to become someone in it, and walking back out with the manual. That's an Enneagram Type 4 pattern, sexual subtype specifically, which burns the life down chasing the person who might finally complete them. Full breakdown of why every Strauss book is a different attempted self: https://9takes.com/personality-analysis/neil-strauss

So the question answers itself. People still buy _The Game_ because they're chasing the same thing he was. The divorce doesn't disprove the book. It shows what happens when you find what you were chasing and it isn't enough.

### Answer 4: What is Marc Andreessen like in person?

**Question URL:** https://www.quora.com/What-is-Marc-Andreessen-like-in-person
**Views:** Unverified
**Answer Count:** Unverified
**Enneagram Angle:** Type 5 (intellectual voracity → downstream warmth)
**Blog Link Used:** https://9takes.com/personality-analysis/marc-andreessen
**Link Placement:** Paragraph 5 of 6 (~83% down)
**Status:** Drafted (v2 — leads with curiosity, not critique of "down to earth" framing)

---

Marc is one of the most intellectually voracious people you'll ever meet. Almost everything else you notice about him in person, the warmth, the memory, the way he makes you feel he's actually listening, is downstream of that.

He's not "approachable" in the small-talk sense. He's approachable because he's genuinely curious about whatever you do. If you build something, he wants to know how it works. If you have a model of an industry, he wants to compare it to his. He'll remember a conversation you had three years ago because the conversation was a data point and he doesn't lose data points.

The voracity has a clean fingerprint. He reads constantly, "barbells" his reading between real-time tech news and timeless texts, and calendars every second of his day, including sleep, to protect time for the input. He famously courted his wife Laura with "a twenty-five-minute monologue on why we should go steady, with a full intellectual decision tree in anticipation of my own decision tree," then sent her seventeen follow-up emails the next day. That's not dating. That's a Five working through a problem with the highest-stakes data set he's ever encountered.

The same voracity is why he can co-author "Software is eating the world" in 2011, write the Techno-Optimist Manifesto twelve years later, and start visiting Mar-a-Lago without flinching. He doesn't experience updates as identity rupture. He experiences them as the system getting better. "Strong opinions, weakly held" isn't a leadership tactic for him. It's how the machine actually runs.

In Enneagram terms it's Type 5 all the way down. The Five's core orientation is gathering models that explain the world and protecting the time and energy required to build them. The warmth and approachability people remark on aren't humility, they're the side effect of someone who treats every conversation as potential input. Full read on the Five's "open the world, close the self" architecture: https://9takes.com/personality-analysis/marc-andreessen

So what's he like in person? He's the smartest person in the room who actually wants to know what you think, because what you think might update the model. The warmth is real. It's also a downstream effect of the engine underneath.

### Answer 5: Why did the quality of Rick Rubin's work decline so much?

**Question URL:** https://www.quora.com/Why-did-the-quality-of-Rick-Rubins-work-decline-so-much
**Views:** Unverified
**Answer Count:** Unverified
**Enneagram Angle:** Type 4 (consistency reframe)
**Blog Link Used:** https://9takes.com/personality-analysis/rick-rubin
**Link Placement:** Paragraph 4 of 5 (~80% down)
**Status:** Drafted

---

Calling it a decline assumes the early Rubin was technique-driven and lost his touch. He was never technique-driven. The early work just had more raw input to subtract from.

Reign in Blood. Blood Sugar Sex Magik. American Recordings. Wildflowers. The move is always the same. Strip until what's underneath shows up. Cash at the end of his life. Petty mid-divorce. The Chili Peppers off heroin. There was a lot in the room to subtract from in the 1990s.

Late Rubin is doing the same move with less input. An artist who isn't in transformation gives him less to listen for. So you get records that feel weightless, because the engine was always "find the emotional truth and remove everything else." When there's no emotional truth to find, removal looks like absence.

What you're watching is a Type 4 consistency problem. The Four doesn't change methods. The Four runs the same emotional-truth scan on every project, and when the project doesn't have a deep enough well, the result is thin. That's not a producer in decline. That's a producer whose whole career rides on the artist showing up with something real to find. Full read on why his approach hasn't actually changed: https://9takes.com/personality-analysis/rick-rubin

Short answer: he didn't decline. The artists stopped arriving with something to be transformed out of. Same listener, weaker signal.

### Answer 6: What do you think of recent PUAs like Neil Strauss and Mystery? Does their technique work?

**Question URL:** https://www.quora.com/What-do-you-think-of-recent-PickUp-Artists-PUA-like-Neil-Strauss-and-Mystery-Does-their-technique-work

**Blog Link Used:** https://9takes.com/personality-analysis/neil-strauss
**Link Placement:** Paragraph 4 of 5 (~80% down)

---

If you mean "approach a stranger at a bar without freezing," the techniques do something. If you mean "fix the thing that made you study them in the first place," they don't.

The clearest evidence is what happened to the two most famous figures in the scene. Mystery doubled down. He's still teaching variants of the same system twenty years later. Strauss wrote _The Game_, then went to sex rehab, then wrote _The Truth_, a 500-page confession about how the system had broken him. Then that marriage ended in 2018.

Same scene. Same techniques. Two completely different inner machines.

Mystery reads as image-driven and persona-tracking. The system _is_ the identity for him, so doubling down is the only move that makes sense. Strauss is the opposite. He's an identity-searcher who tried "Style" on like a costume and discovered the costume didn't fix the missing-self underneath. In Enneagram terms that's the Type 3 versus Type 4 distinction. Threes optimize the persona. Fours hunt for what the persona is failing to give them. Full breakdown of why Strauss eventually exited the scene and Mystery never did: https://9takes.com/personality-analysis/neil-strauss

So does it work? It works at the level it's pitched at. The deeper question, which Strauss eventually asked and Mystery never has, is whether that's the level your actual problem lives on.

### Answer 7: What is it like to work with Ben Horowitz?

**Question URL:** https://quorasessionwithgregsands.quora.com/What-is-it-like-to-work-with-Ben-Horowitz
**Views:** Unverified
**Answer Count:** Unverified — Quora Session format; verify comments are still open before posting
**Enneagram Angle:** Type 6 counter-phobic
**Blog Link Used:** https://9takes.com/personality-analysis/ben-horowitz
**Link Placement:** Paragraph 5 of 6 (~83% down)
**Status:** Drafted

---

The thing nobody warns you about is that Ben isn't a confident leader by temperament. He's a worried leader who's done it scared so many times that you can't tell the difference anymore.

The Andy Grove "imagine you've been fired and a new CEO walks in tomorrow" exercise was a one-time mental rehearsal for most operators. Ben never stopped doing it. He runs every meeting like the floor might drop out, prepares for the layoff before he believes it's coming, and writes the manual on the worst day of the company so other founders won't be alone when their worst day arrives.

What that's like to work with: you're never the most pessimistic person in the room. He already mapped the failure mode you were going to bring up. He already drafted the speech for if it happens. He's already worried about the second-order failure underneath that one. And then he goes home and does it all again at 2 a.m.

Pair that with extreme loyalty. Thirty-plus years with Marc Andreessen. Thirty-seven with his wife. A firm built around the principle that you do not replace founder-CEOs. People who get inside the trust circle stay inside the trust circle. People who don't, don't get a second look.

This is a Type 6 counter-phobic pattern in its purest form. Phobic Sixes manage anxiety by avoiding the threat. Counter-phobic Sixes run toward it because sitting with the dread feels worse than the fight. Ben built a $40+ billion firm doing the second one in public. Full read on why "wartime CEO" is really a Type 6 self-portrait: https://9takes.com/personality-analysis/ben-horowitz

Short version: working with him means you've signed onto someone whose default state is preparing for the next disaster. The upside is he's almost always a little bit right.

### Answer 8: What has made Marc Andreessen so successful?

**Question URL:** https://www.quora.com/What-has-made-Marc-Andreessen-so-successful
**Views:** Unverified
**Answer Count:** Unverified
**Enneagram Angle:** Type 5
**Blog Link Used:** https://9takes.com/personality-analysis/marc-andreessen
**Link Placement:** Paragraph 5 of 6 (~83% down)
**Status:** Drafted

---

"Strong opinions, weakly held" gets quoted as a leadership philosophy. It's actually a description of his personality.

Andreessen's edge has been the same engine since he was eight years old, teaching himself BASIC out of a library book in a Wisconsin town with no stoplight. He stockpiles models. He runs new evidence against them constantly. He updates the model the second the evidence shifts, without much sentimental attachment to the old version.

That's why he can co-author "Software is eating the world" in 2011, write the Techno-Optimist Manifesto twelve years later, and start visiting Mar-a-Lago, all without seeming to feel the contradiction. Other people experience an ideological pivot as identity rupture. He experiences it as a model update.

The other half of the engine is energy management. He calendars every second of his day, including sleep. He runs his body like equipment serving the mind. He once joked about wanting a "no hugging, no touching" T-shirt, and the joke wasn't really a joke. Anything that drains cognitive bandwidth gets routed around.

In Enneagram terms it's a Type 5 pattern. The Five's core fear is being depleted by the world, so the whole life gets organized around conserving energy and stockpiling models that explain things. Andreessen built a multi-decade career on doing that more relentlessly than anyone else in tech. Full read on the temperament that scales when "strong opinions, weakly held" becomes your operating system: https://9takes.com/personality-analysis/marc-andreessen

Short version: he's successful because the same temperament that made him weird in seventh grade scales beautifully when you're managing tens of billions of dollars and need to update your worldview faster than the market does.
