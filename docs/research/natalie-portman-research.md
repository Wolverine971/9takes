## 1. CHILDHOOD, FAMILY, AND FORMATIVE EXPERIENCES

### Birth and Roots

- Born Nathalie Hershlag on June 9, 1981, at Hadassah Medical Center on Mount Scopus, Jerusalem, Israel.
- Only child of Shelley Stevens (American-born artist from Ohio) and Avner Hershlag (Israeli-born gynecologist/fertility specialist).
- Hebrew was her first language.
- Family moved to the US when she was 3; lived in Washington D.C. (father's medical residency), then Connecticut (1988), then settled in Syosset, Long Island.

### Holocaust Heritage

- Paternal grandparents emigrated from Poland to Israel in the late 1930s. Her grandfather had led a Jewish youth movement in Poland and went to Israel expecting to send for his family later -- but they were killed in Auschwitz.
- Paternal grandmother, originally from Romania, worked as a spy for the British during WWII.
- Maternal grandparents were Jewish immigrants from Russia and Austria who settled in the US.

### Childhood Identity

- Attended Charles E. Smith Jewish Day School (D.C.) and Solomon Schechter Day School of Nassau County (Long Island).
- "I don't even remember who my friends were before I was nine." -- She has remarked on lacking roots due to frequent moves; family was her only constant.
- Self-described: "I was different from the other kids. I was more ambitious. I knew what I liked and what I wanted, and I worked very hard. I was a very serious kid."

### Adolescent Crisis After Leon: The Professional

- After filming The Professional at age 12, returned to school in 7th grade and was shunned: "I cried every single day when I came back from shooting. My friends were not my friends and were saying things like 'She thinks she's so hot now.'"
- During adolescence: "I was locking myself in the bathroom, threatening to kill myself -- being so mean to my mom and fighting with her."

## 2. BEING SEXUALIZED AS A CHILD STAR

### The Professional and Its Aftermath (Age 12-13)

- Made her film debut at 12 in Leon: The Professional (1994), playing a girl with an ambiguous relationship with Jean Reno's adult hitman.
- In deleted scenes, her character tries to kiss Leon at dinner; in another scene, offers her virginity.
- Current view: "It's a movie that's still beloved, and people come up to me about it more than almost anything I've ever made, and it gave me my career, but it is definitely, when you watch it now, it definitely has some cringey, to say the least, aspects to it." (The Hollywood Reporter)

### The "Sexual Terrorism" She Experienced (2018 Women's March Speech)

- "I was really sexualized, which I think happens to a lot of young girls who are onscreen. I felt very scared by it."
- Her first-ever piece of fan mail was a rape fantasy written by a man.
- Her local radio station created a countdown to her 18th birthday -- "euphemistically, the date that I would be legal to sleep with."
- Movie reviewers wrote about her "budding breasts" in reviews.
- "I understood very quickly, even as a 13-year-old, that if I were to express myself sexually I would feel unsafe and that men would feel entitled to discuss and objectify my body to my great discomfort."

### The Protective Persona She Built

- "I rejected any role that even had a kissing scene, and talked about that choice deliberately in interviews. I emphasized about how bookish I was, and how serious I was."
- "I'm so serious. I'm so studious. I'm smart, and that's not the kind of girl you attack."
- She told Jenna Ortega (Interview Magazine, Spring 2025) that she endured a "long Lolita phase" and learned at a young age to project a tough exterior on film sets to avoid sexualization.
- "I wanted it to be inside of me, not directed towards me."
- "I was like, if I create this image of myself, I'll be left alone."
- **KEY TENSION**: "Being sexualized as a child took away from my own sexuality because it made me afraid."

### The Public/Private Disconnect

- "There's a public understanding of me that's different from who I am."
- "I'm stupid and silly in real life" (contrasting her bookish public image).
- "I'm not a particularly private person in real life -- I'll tell you anything. But in public, it was so clear early on that if you tell people how private you are, your privacy gets respected a lot more."

## 3. HARVARD -- IMPOSTER SYNDROME AND IDENTITY CRISIS

### Why She Went

- Enrolled at Harvard in 1999, just after the release of Star Wars: Episode I, graduating in 2003 with a degree in psychology.
- Feared people would assume her admission was fame-based, not merit-based.

### The Imposter Syndrome

- "I felt like there had been some mistake, that I wasn't smart enough to be in this company, and that every time I opened my mouth I would have to prove that I wasn't just a dumb actress." (2015 Class Day Speech)
- Took only very difficult courses (neurobiology, advanced Hebrew literature) to prove she wasn't "just a dumb actress" -- despite never having written a 10-page paper before.
- Eventual realization: "Seriousness for seriousness' sake was its own kind of trophy, and a dubious one, a pose I sought to counter some half-imagined argument about who I was."

### Depression and Struggle

- Admitted to struggling with depression during sophomore year, blaming it on "a combination of heartache, birth-control pills, and spending too much time missing sunlight."
- "I'm still insecure about my own worthiness, overwhelmed with what I was supposed to pull off, when I could barely get myself out of bed in the morning." (Still felt this even 12 years after graduating)

### What She Learned

- "Achievement is wonderful when you know why you're doing it. And when you don't know, it can be a terrible trap."
- By graduation: "After four years of trying to be something else, I admitted to myself that I couldn't wait to go back and make more films."
- "If my reasons are your own, your path, even if it's a strange and clumsy path, will be wholly yours."
- Attributed her ability to have a normal college experience to attending "before the age of camera phones, online gossip, and Twitter." Said: "I was completely left alone for four years. I really was just like a regular student there and everyone treated me like that."

### People-Pleasing Pattern

- "I was who everyone else -- my parents, my friends, society -- wanted me to be. I was a pleaser, someone who wanted to make everyone happy, to not let anyone down."
- Later evolution: "As a kid, I was really interested in pleasing other people. And now, I'm very much into setting new challenges for myself." (Interview Magazine, 2025)

## 4. BLACK SWAN -- PERFECTIONISM MADE PHYSICAL

### The Preparation

- Trained for a full year before filming with former New York City Ballet dancer Mary Helen Bowers.
- Daily regimen: swimming a mile (front crawl and breaststroke), 2 hours of ballet exercises and resistance work, plus 3 hours of ballet training -- approximately 5-8 hours daily.
- Lost 20 pounds off an already small frame.
- Diet reportedly consisted mainly of carrots and almonds.
- "I was given instructions to lose as much weight as I could without getting sick."

### Physical Toll

- Dislocated a rib: "I had a dislocated rib, which basically we just dealt with by not... I didn't get deep breaths for six weeks."
- Toenails fell off. Blisters and calluses.
- "When I really started dieting and lost a serious amount of weight, all of a sudden I started getting compliments from everyone. But it was very much like what that world is."
- Became so skeletal, "there were nights she thought she was going to die."

### Psychological Dimension

- "For this part, you know, just reading the script and immediately having insight into obsessive-compulsive behavior and ritualistic, almost religious practices."
- "The physical discipline of it really helped for the emotional side of the character -- I was able to get the sense of the monastic lifestyle of only working out, that is a ballet dancer's life."
- Could not shut off the character: "It was really difficult on this one, probably because I had so much training outside of work to do. I had to go to the gym after work, and then had to wake up at 5am before work and work out beforehand. It was constant throughout the day, so there wasn't really any time to relax and be myself."

### The Final Line

- Portman worked with Aronofsky to change Nina's last line to "It was perfect" -- because her character is only artistically successful "when she finds perfection and pleasure for herself, not when she's trying to be perfect in the eyes of others."

### Connection to Aronofsky

- "We're very similar in our extremism. We're both very, very disciplined and very focused, and extreme. I think we both throw ourselves into things in a similar way."
- "My experience with Darren also felt almost telepathic. I never had that with any director before."
- "It was amazing to have Darren in the war with me."
- Aronofsky on their collaboration: "It was clear early on that I was working with Natalie. The script was constantly evolving and Natalie was a big part of that."
- Aronofsky tried to stoke a rivalry between Portman and Mila Kunis on set: "Mila and Natalie both realised very quickly what I was doing and made fun of me, so it quickly became a joke that we all understood."

## 5. MENTORSHIP, MALE DIRECTORS, AND POWER DYNAMICS

### Mike Nichols -- The Exception

- Nichols was the only older male mentor "without there ever being a creepy element in it."
- "I think he was a genuine feminist. There was nothing, nothing, nothing there except him seeing you as a creative, interesting, talented human. It is the rarest, finest quality, and not many directors of his generation had it."
- "He was the one who had the best words, the right hug for hard times, the funny comment to diffuse your pain. He saved me again and again. He was the best way to be a person and an artist."
- Nichols wrote a letter to Anthony Minghella: "Put her in Cold Mountain, I vouch for her" -- saving her career after the Star Wars films.
- Portman was 19 when Nichols cast her in The Seagull alongside Meryl Streep, Philip Seymour Hoffman, and Christopher Walken.

### The Implication About Other Directors

- By singling out Nichols as the ONLY non-creepy male mentor, Portman implicitly indicted every other older male director/mentor she worked with.
- "Everyone's so nice to you when you're an actor and it's sometimes hard to see the signs that someone's not a great person."
- "A lot of people just turn into yes-people, which is not a good way to be a person in the world."

### Standing Up

- "I've stood up to producers before, and even a director when I saw them being abusive," recognizing that many people on set are scared to speak up when not treated right.
- On a director dismissing her input while listening to male co-stars saying the same things: He told her "You're exhausting." Her response: "I'm exhausting for telling you my opinion about my job?"

## 6. METOO, TIME'S UP, AND HARASSMENT

### Her "100 Stories" Realization

- When Weinstein allegations broke: "wow, I'm so lucky that I haven't had this."
- On reflection: "I've had discrimination or harassment on almost everything I've ever worked on in some way."
- "I went from thinking I don't have a story to thinking, Oh wait, I have 100 stories."
- "A lot of people are having these reckonings with themselves, of things that we just took for granted as like, this is part of the process."

### The Private Plane Incident

- A producer invited her on a private plane: "I showed up and it was just the two of us, and one bed was made on the plane."
- "Nothing happened, I was not assaulted. I said: 'This doesn't make me feel comfortable,' and that was respected. But that was super not OK, you know? That was really unacceptable and manipulative and could have been -- I was scared, you know?"

### On Beautiful Girls (1996) Set

- Was 13 on a set "where all the women around me were being really, really hurt" (by Weinstein). "I was lucky to be oblivious to that, but it was such a big wakeup call."

### Golden Globes 2018

- Presenting best director, she said: "And here are the all-male nominees." The moment was pre-planned; she had consulted with other women in the industry.

### Polanski Recanting

- Has said her earlier defense of Roman Polanski "was a mistake."

## 8. DIRECTORIAL DEBUT -- A TALE OF LOVE AND DARKNESS

### The Passion Project

- Based on Amos Oz's 2002 memoir about growing up in Jerusalem.
- Eight-to-ten years in the making; Portman started thinking about it at age 26.
- Wrote the screenplay, directed, and starred as Oz's mother Fania.
- Entire film shot in Hebrew (subtitled).
- "Israel just captures my imagination in a way, that's where I wanted to tell my first filmic story."

### Self-Doubt as Director

- "I was constantly second-guessing myself."
- Initially thought Fania should be played by a more mature woman, but realized donors would only fund the project if she also acted in it.

### Complex Relationship with Israel

- "It's like family -- you love it more than anything else in the world, and you are also more critical of it than anything else in the world."
- 2018 Genesis Prize controversy: Declined to attend the ceremony in Jerusalem, writing: "I chose not to attend because I did not want to appear as endorsing Benjamin Netanyahu, who was to be giving a speech at the ceremony."
- Insisted: "I'm not part of the BDS movement and do not endorse it. Like many Israelis and Jews around the world, I can be critical of the leadership in Israel without wanting to boycott the entire nation."

## 9. VEGANISM AND MORAL COMMITMENTS

### Timeline

- Vegetarian since age 9.
- Became vegan in 2011 after reading Jonathan Safran Foer's "Eating Animals."

### Key Quotes

- "When I read Eating Animals, that was what made me become a vegan. I think until then, I had thought, 'Oh, with eggs and dairy, you're not killing animals. It's just their natural byproducts.' But when I started learning about the conditions -- and the environmental effect of all these animals and the impact on humans of having large groups of sick animals together, it really made me want to change immediately."

## 10. PERFECTIONISM, CONTROL, AND SELF-AWARENESS

### Self-Criticism

- "I'm extremely self-critical" and "never really happy with what I do."
- Even 12 years after Harvard graduation, with awards and family: still felt "pangs of insecurity."

### The People-Pleaser to Challenger Arc

- Past self: "I was a pleaser, someone who wanted to make everyone happy, to not let anyone down."
- Current self: "As a kid, I was really interested in pleasing other people. And now, I'm very much into setting new challenges for myself."

### Risk-Taking Through Ignorance

- "I was so oblivious to my own limits that I did things I was woefully unprepared to do. So the very inexperience that in college had made me insecure... now was making me actually take risks I didn't even realize were risks."
- "If I had known my own limitations, I never would have taken the risk, and the risk led to one of my greatest artistic and personal experiences."
- "Accept your lack of knowledge, and use it as your asset."

### Motherhood as Release from Control

- "Being a mother has taught me to let go of control."
- "Motherhood has made me much calmer under stress, because there's that weird parent thing you develop, that when things get really bad, your voice gets calm and your blood pressure slows, and you can make everything okay again."
- "Give yourself a break. We all hold ourselves to such a high standard and it's a constant balancing act."

### Ethical Boundaries

- "I always ask myself, would I want someone to do something that wasn't comfortable for them just to please me? And the answer is no."

## 11. ACTING PHILOSOPHY AND MASTERCLASS

### Empathy as Core Method

- MasterClass (2019): Portman teaches that empathy is "at the core of every great performance."
- "I think that everyone around you is a potential teacher."
- Self-taught actor who developed personal techniques for character work.
- Teaches students to "be playful, not worry about making mistakes, and have fun above all else."

### Approach to Roles -- Choosing Fear

- On Jackie (2016): "I was terrified and thought it was probably not a wise choice to take on because it's very easy to fail, I think, but I like things that scare me and usually end up doing the things that scare me the most."
- On Jackie preparation: Studied Kennedy by watching videos, reading books, listening to audiotapes. Mastered her distinctive finishing-school accent mixing posh, New York, and British elements.
- Pablo Larrain on directing her: imagined Jackie "trying on all her dresses, drinking and smoking, walking around by herself" -- exploring "the identity crisis she's left with when she's no longer her husband's wife and the First Lady."

### Learning From Collaborators

- "Ninety per cent of how you learn is watching great people. When you are surrounded by good actors it lifts your performance."

## 12. ANGEL CITY FC AND WOMEN'S SPORTS

### Founding

- Co-founded Angel City FC in 2020 with Kara Nortman and Julie Uhrman. The club became the largest female-led ownership group in professional sports.
- Inspiration came partly from watching her son's enthusiasm for women's World Cup soccer.

### Philosophy

- "The more all kids see women virtuoso athletes in the same light as they see male virtuoso athletes, the more everyone benefits."
- "Women's sports have not had the same investment, have not had the same broadcasts, have not had the same sponsorship dollars. So they've been at a disadvantage -- and then that is used as evidence that it's not possible to be successful. We wanted to show that with a different belief and ability to garner sponsorship dollars and supporters, it is possible to be successful."
- "In Hollywood, we are making 30 cents to the dollar" (compared to men's salaries).

### Results

- Angel City players receive a share of ticket sales (groundbreaking in women's sports).
- Team achieved $250 million valuation -- most valuable women's sports franchise in the world.

## 13. PARENTING PHILOSOPHY

### Core Approach

- Values weekend rituals: laundry, family time "usually somewhere in nature, often with friends who have children."
- "Pay attention and hold them close because before you know it, they're moving out of the house."
- Lives a "very quiet and private life, behind cameras, without public social media accounts and constant photos of their child."
- Does not parade children around red carpets; keeps them away from paparazzi.
- "I set up a little bit of a barrier to be like, 'I'm not going to do photo shoots with my kids.'"

### Feminist Parenting

- Authored "Natalie Portman's Fables" to provide relatable books with strong female characters for children.
- Emphasizes importance of female athletic role models.

### Seeking Wisdom

- Asked Cate Blanchett for parenting advice. Blanchett said: "You just do. Stressing about it doesn't help."
- "Parenting is a totally different experience for every single person. There are no universal rules about what constitutes good motherhood."

## 17. KEY PERSONALITY TENSIONS AND CONTRADICTIONS

### The Intellectual vs. The Silly

- Public image: bookish, serious, cerebral. "There's a public understanding of me that's different from who I am."
- Private self: "I'm stupid and silly in real life."

### The Controlled vs. The Vulnerable

- Projects extreme discipline (Black Swan training, Harvard rigor, veganism).
- Privately: depression, imposter syndrome, self-described as "never really happy with what I do."

### The Private Person Who Tells Everything

- "I'm not a particularly private person in real life -- I'll tell you anything."
- Yet strategically built a private persona: "In public, it was so clear early on that if you tell people how private you are, your privacy gets respected a lot more."

### The Moral Crusader Who Admits Mistakes

- Called out all-male Golden Globes nominees. Spoke at Women's March. Time's Up leader.
- Also admitted her defense of Polanski "was a mistake."
- On Israel: Loves it "like family" but declined its highest honor over political disagreement.

### The People-Pleaser Who Stands Up

- Self-described former people-pleaser who now has confronted producers and directors over abusive behavior.
- "I'm exhausting for telling you my opinion about my job?"

### The Child Star Who Chose Armor

- Was sexualized at 13 and built a protective intellectual persona that defined her public image for decades.
- Only recently (Interview Magazine 2025) began openly discussing how this shaped her: "Being sexualized as a child took away from my own sexuality because it made me afraid."

## 18. ENNEAGRAM TYPING EVIDENCE

### Evidence for Type 1 (The Reformer/Perfectionist)

- **Inner Critic**: "I'm extremely self-critical" and "never really happy with what I do." "I was constantly second-guessing myself" (directing debut).
- **Moral Conviction**: Veganism since childhood, Time's Up leadership, standing up to abusive directors, Angel City FC founding, Genesis Prize stance.
- **Perfectionism**: Black Swan training (5-8 hours/day, starving herself), Harvard course-load to prove worthiness, decade-long development of directorial debut.
- **Self-Control as Defense**: Built an identity around being "serious" and "studious" to protect herself from sexualization.
- **People-Pleasing (unhealthy 1)**: "I was a pleaser, someone who wanted to make everyone happy, to not let anyone down."
- **Growth**: "Being a mother has taught me to let go of control." Evolution from pleaser to self-challenger.

### Evidence for Type 1w9 Specifically

- Withdrawal tendency: prefers quiet life, privacy, nature weekends with family.
- Intellectual depth: Harvard psychology degree, Hebrew literature, love of books.
- Avoidance of conflict contrasted with principled stands when pushed.

### Counter-Evidence / Alternative Types to Consider

- **Type 3 (Achiever)**: Harvard + Oscar + activism + sports team ownership = achievement-oriented. But she's more motivated by moral rightness than image/success.
- **Type 6 (Loyalist)**: Fear-driven protective strategies, hypervigilance about sexualization, building armor. But her moral certainty feels more 1 than 6.
- **Type 4 (Individualist)**: Artistic depth, identity crisis, emotional processing. But she leans more toward duty/correctness than uniqueness/authenticity as primary driver.

### Strongest Typing: Enneagram 1w9

- The armor she built (intellectual, serious, controlled) is classic Type 1 defense.
- Her inner critic is constant and self-reported.
- Her activism is driven by moral conviction, not image management.
- Her growth edge is learning to release control and embrace imperfection -- textbook Type 1 growth path.
