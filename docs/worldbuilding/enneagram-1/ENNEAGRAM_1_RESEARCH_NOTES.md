### Applied internal Type 1 docs

- `docs/content-research/overthinking-enneagram-research.md`
  - Type 1 loop: moral perfectionism. "Did I do the right thing? Could I have done it better? I should have..."
  - Triggers: any right/wrong decision, feedback (even positive - they find the flaw), their own imperfect behavior, witnessing others' mistakes.
  - Why they cannot stop: the inner critic believes self-criticism prevents future mistakes. Stopping feels irresponsible.
  - Interventions: "the decision has been made" mantra, time-boxing self-reflection, self-compassion as the type-specific lever.

- `docs/content-research/self-sabotage-research.md`
  - Pattern: "Nothing is ever good enough to ship." Endless revision, refusing to start until conditions are perfect, criticizing own work before others can, giving up because it cannot be done right.
  - Cycle: impossible standard, inevitable failure, shame and anger at self, give up or double down, never complete.
  - Growth frame: practice intentional imperfection in small doses.

- `docs/content-research/manipulation-enneagram-outline.md`
  - Type 1 manipulation style: moral superiority. "I'm not controlling you - I'm just showing you the right way."
  - Tactics: criticism as "help," conditional approval, black-and-white framing, silent disapproval (sighs, looks).
  - Recognition: you feel constantly evaluated; you can never quite meet the standard; their help feels like criticism.
  - Response: "I hear your concern. I'm choosing to do it this way." Do not justify, argue, defend, explain.

- `docs/content-research/red-flags-dating-outline.md`
  - What draws people in: integrity, reliability, moral compass, high standards read as "they'd never let you down."
  - Red flags: constant criticism disguised as improvement, rigid rules about the right way, unable to relax without finding something wrong, silent resentment that explodes, "never quite good enough" atmosphere.
  - Useful partner language: "When you point out what's wrong, I feel criticized instead of helped. Can we talk about what's going well first?"

## External Follow-on Research

### Enneagram Institute (Riso-Hudson) - Type 1

Source: https://www.enneagraminstitute.com/type-1/

Useful findings:

- Type name: "The Reformer," framed as "The Rational, Idealistic Type: Principled, Purposeful, Self-Controlled, and Perfectionistic."
- Basic fear: of being corrupt/evil, defective.
- Basic desire: to be good, to have integrity, to be balanced.
- Key motivations: to be right, to strive higher and improve everything, to be consistent with their ideals, to justify themselves, **to be beyond criticism so as not to be condemned by anyone**.
- Stress arrow to Four: methodical Ones suddenly become moody and irrational. Growth arrow to Seven: angry, critical Ones become more spontaneous and joyful.
- Superego language: Ones rely heavily on their superego, "a learned voice from their childhood"; growth involves separating from it and evaluating its genuine strengths and limitations - not obeying it, not destroying it.
- Levels sketch: healthy Ones become wise, discerning, "transcendentally realistic"; average Ones are high-minded idealists simultaneously afraid of making a mistake; unhealthy Ones become dogmatic, self-righteous, intolerant, obsessed with imperfection.
- Growth recommendations: release perfectionism, patience with others' pace, manage superego attacks, develop emotional awareness, address self-righteous anger before it alienates.

### Integrative9 - Type 1

Source: https://www.integrative9.com/enneagram/introduction/type-1/

Useful findings:

- Type name: "The Strict Perfectionist." Motivational need: to be good and right.
- Self-talk summary: the world is imperfect and I must work toward improving it; I must be good, right, perfect and avoid mistakes.
- Gifts: principled, objective, conscientious, structured, quality-minded.
- Action/gut center, but control aims mostly inward: discipline, principle, self-control. May delay action on ambiguous issues.
- Anger handling: rarely expressed outwardly; surfaces as irritation and resentment; when anger builds, Ones become **physically rigid and extremely polite**.
- Subtypes:
  - Self-preservation 1 - "Worry": inner-focused perfectionist, anticipates problems, anxious, detail-oriented.
  - Social 1 - "Non-Adaptable": sets an example of integrity; attention to what is good, right, and appropriate.
  - One-to-one/sexual 1 - "Zeal": the countertype; intense reformer who expresses anger more directly when others resist reform.

### CP Enneagram Academy (Chestnut-Paes) - Type 1

Source: https://cpenneagram.com/enneagram-type-one

Useful findings:

- Focus of attention: noticing error (deviations from an internally generated ideal), discerning right and wrong, reliance on rules and structure.
- Emotional pattern: repressed anger. Expressing anger conflicts with being virtuous, so it leaks out as resentment, annoyance, criticism, and self-righteousness.
- Passion: anger - appearing in repressed form as resentment that seeks resolution through pursuing perfection and virtue.
- Virtue: serenity - totally peaceful within while fully accepting the way people and things are.
- Blind spots: their own anger and its effects, the impact of their criticism, their feelings and impulses generally, the downsides of pursuing perfection, their rigidity, their need for relaxation.
- Subtypes:
  - Self-preservation One ("Worry"): the true perfectionist; anger most repressed; expressed through striving for personal perfection.
  - Social One ("Non-Adaptability"): perfect model of the right way; anger cooled into intellectual control; teacher/exemplar energy.
  - Sexual One ("Zeal"): the countertype - more impulsive and outwardly angry, focused on reforming others rather than themselves.

### The Narrative Enneagram - Type 1

Source: https://www.narrativeenneagram.org/enneagram-type/type-1-the-improver/

Useful findings:

- Type name: "The Improver." Core belief: you must be good and right to be worthy.
- Focus of attention: what is right, wrong, correct, incorrect.
- Defense mechanism: **reaction formation** - feeling one thing and expressing the opposite, used to avoid direct anger and maintain a self-image of being right.
- Life lesson: change what can be changed, accept what cannot, develop the wisdom to know the difference.
- Inner critic, first person: a powerful inner critic "monitors my thoughts, words and deeds"; focus on being good while repressing impulses and desires for pleasure.
- Speaking style: precise, clear, direct, honest, detail-oriented; can land as judgmental, sermonizing, closed-minded.
- Somatic pattern: rigidity and tension in jaw, neck, shoulders, diaphragm, and pelvic floor; impeded breathing from diaphragm tension; abundant energy held under intense self-control.
- Relating tips: take their concerns seriously, respect their integrity, honor agreements, admit mistakes, recognize their sensitivity to criticism.

### Psychology Bridge: Perfectionism And Self-Discrepancy Research

Use as supporting psychology analogies, not Enneagram doctrine. High scorers on these measures are not "Ones."

- **Hewitt & Flett multidimensional perfectionism (1991).** Three dimensions: self-oriented (demanding perfection of self), other-oriented (demanding perfection of others), socially prescribed (perceiving that others demand perfection of you). Thirty-year review: Flett & Hewitt, "Multidimensional Perfectionism Turns 30" (https://www.researchgate.net/publication/349254312). Loose mapping for editorial use only: self-oriented rhymes with the self-perfecting SP1, other-oriented with the other-reforming SX1.
- **Frost's concern-over-mistakes dimension (Frost et al. 1990).** Modern factor models split perfectionism into "perfectionistic strivings" (high standards per se) and "perfectionistic concerns" (concern over mistakes, doubts about actions, socially prescribed pressure). The concerns factor carries most of the maladaptive payload.
- **Perfectionism and depression.** Smith et al. 2021, Clinical Psychology Review meta-analysis of 67 longitudinal studies, N=20,583 (https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33556805/). Smith et al. 2016, European Journal of Personality meta-analysis: socially prescribed perfectionism, concern over mistakes, doubts about actions, and self-criticism predicted increases in depressive symptoms even after controlling for baseline depression and neuroticism (https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1002/per.2053).
- **Perfectionism and burnout.** Hill & Curran 2016, Personality and Social Psychology Review meta-analysis of 43 studies, N=9,838: perfectionistic concerns showed medium-to-large positive relationships with burnout; perfectionistic strivings showed small negative or non-significant relationships (https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1088868315596286).
- **Higgins' self-discrepancy theory (1987).** Actual-vs-ought discrepancy theorized to produce agitation emotions (anxiety, guilt, tension). Cleanest analogy for the One's is/ought tension, but do not overstate: Mason et al. 2019 (Psychological Bulletin, 70 studies) found self-discrepancy robustly associated with psychopathology overall, with effects actually larger for actual:ideal than actual:ought - contrary to the theory's specificity claim.
- **Practical copy implication (the Type 1 parallel to the Type 8 reactance bullet):** the research lever is not "lower your standards." Strivings are not reliably harmful; concern over mistakes and self-criticism are. Type 1 content should therefore attack the self-punishment layer and leave the standards standing. "Keep the bar. Drop the trial."

## Evidence And Interpretation Rules

1. **Enneagram doctrine:** fear/desire, passion, virtue, defense mechanism, arrows, wings, and subtypes come from Enneagram teaching sources. Attribute disagreements rather than presenting one school as settled psychology.
2. **9takes synthesis:** the Inner Courtroom, the Gap, the Compression, the Resentment Ledger, the Recess, and "grade the work, not the soul" are editorial concepts built from those sources. Useful metaphors, not validated constructs.
3. **Psychology bridges:** perfectionism and self-discrepancy research describe general processes. They rhyme with Type 1 doctrine but do not validate the type.
4. **Biography:** public-person typings are hypotheses. A credible profile needs repeated evidence of motivation across contexts, and behavior alone (tidiness, criticism, discipline, activism) is insufficient.
5. **Origin stories:** many Ones recognize a childhood story shape (early responsibility, costly mistakes, praise for being good). It illustrates the pattern when self-reported. It is not required, it is not causal proof, and the type must never be used to infer how someone was raised. This rule exists because the pre-2026 Type 1 blog violated it.
6. **Clinical separation:** Type 1 is not OCD, not OCPD, not anxiety. Never use disorder names as shorthand for the pattern.

## School Disagreements To Flag

- **Countertype: no disagreement.** Integrative9 and Chestnut-Paes both name the sexual/one-to-one One ("Zeal") as the countertype. Contrast with Type 8, where the countertype is the social 8.
- **Passion language diverges.** Chestnut-Paes: anger (repressed into resentment). Palmer/Narrative lineage: often "resentment" directly. Riso-Hudson leads with fear/desire. Attribute rather than harmonize.
- **Type name diverges:** Reformer (Riso-Hudson), Strict Perfectionist (Integrative9), Improver (Narrative), Perfectionist (Palmer/Chestnut lineage). 9takes canon: "The Reformer" as archetype label (already in `enneagramTypeProfiles.ts`), "Perfectionist" as the common-search alias.
- **Arrows are doctrine.** The 1-to-4 stress and 1-to-7 growth movements are Riso-Hudson teaching; the 2021 systematic review found little empirical support for intertype movement. Keep the arrow hedge in public copy.

## Model Limitations

Source: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33332604/

- Hook et al. (2021), systematic review across 104 independent samples: mixed evidence for Enneagram reliability and validity; factor analyses typically yield fewer than nine factors; no clustering studies validating the nine-type structure; little support for secondary features such as wings and intertype movement; several studies report personal/spiritual growth benefits.
- Editorial implication: present Type 1 as an interpretive self-reflection framework, not settled clinical science. Applies with extra force to the 1-to-4 and 1-to-7 arrows.

## Synthesis

The shared pattern across internal and external sources:

1. Type 1 does not start from pickiness. It starts from the conviction that being wrong and being bad are the same thing.
2. Attention organizes around the gap between how things are and how they should be, and the gap registers as personal assignment: mine to close.
3. The critic was built as protection - catch the mistake before anyone else can, be beyond criticism so no one can condemn you.
4. Anger is the type's engine emotion but is morally forbidden, so it compresses: renamed as frustration, disappointment, concern; leaked as criticism, rigidity, resentment, self-righteousness; held in the jaw, shoulders, and diaphragm. "Physically rigid and extremely polite" is the tell.
5. Resentment has a bookkeeping structure: the One does more than asked, silently, and bills later in a currency nobody agreed to.
6. The standard moves. Perfection functions as an admission price for rest, joy, and worth, and the price is never fully paid.
7. The One's greatest relational problem is that correction is their dialect of care, and the people they love hear it as rejection.
8. Growth is not lowering the bar. The research and the doctrine agree from different directions: the harm lives in the self-punishment layer, not the standards layer. The courtroom can adjourn while the craft remains.
9. The Type 1 worldbuilding image should be a courtroom that finally takes a recess, not a loosened slob, a burned rulebook, or a "just relax" poster.
