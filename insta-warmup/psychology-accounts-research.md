# Instagram Accounts: Psychology, Therapy, Mental Health & Self-Awareness

Research compiled for 9takes Instagram warmup engagement strategy. Each account is evaluated for how personality/Enneagram pattern insights could add value in their comments section.

---

## TIER 1: HIGH-FOLLOWER ACCOUNTS (500K+)

### 1. @the.holistic.psychologist (Dr. Nicole LePera)

- **Followers:** ~9M
- **Content focus:** Self-healing, childhood conditioning patterns, ego vs. truth, relationship dynamics, self-awareness tools
- **Format:** Text on solid backgrounds, infographics, carousels, Reels
- **Engagement quality:** Extremely high. Comments are flooded but the audience craves self-understanding frameworks. Posts often get thousands of comments.
- **Why 9takes should engage:** Nicole constantly talks about patterns shaped by childhood, emotional reactivity, and self-sabotage. Her audience is primed to think about personality as a lens for understanding behavior. Enneagram insights about WHY people default to certain coping mechanisms (e.g., "This pattern maps closely to how Type 2s lose themselves in others" or "Type 8s build walls precisely because vulnerability felt unsafe") would stand out in her sea of "this is so me" comments.

### 2. @millennial.therapist (Dr. Sara Kuburic)

- **Followers:** ~2M
- **Content focus:** Existential therapy, self-identity, boundaries, relationships, personal growth, authenticity
- **Format:** Text-on-blue-background lists, growth reminders, self-reflection prompts, carousels
- **Engagement quality:** Very high. Audience is reflective and engaged. Comments trend toward personal sharing.
- **Why 9takes should engage:** Sara's work on "losing yourself" and identity directly maps to Enneagram core fears. Her audience is already doing the existential work and would be receptive to personality-type framing. Comments like "The Enneagram calls this the 'passion' of each type -- the emotional pattern that keeps us stuck" would resonate.

### 3. @nedratawwab (Nedra Glover Tawwab)

- **Followers:** ~2M
- **Content focus:** Boundaries, healthy relationships, empowerment, codependency, communication
- **Format:** Text and lists on white backgrounds with yellow accents, Q&A sessions, videos, resources
- **Engagement quality:** High. Her audience actively asks questions. Weekly Q&As drive engagement.
- **Why 9takes should engage:** Boundary struggles differ dramatically by Enneagram type. Type 2s over-give, Type 9s merge, Type 1s feel guilty saying no. Adding this layer to boundary conversations shows WHY the same advice doesn't work for everyone -- the emotional pattern underneath the people-pleasing varies.

### 4. @thesecurerelationship (Julie Menanno)

- **Followers:** ~1.2M
- **Content focus:** Attachment theory, couples therapy, secure love, relationship scripts and communication tools
- **Format:** Carousels, educational text posts, Reels, scripts for difficult conversations
- **Engagement quality:** Very high. Audience is deeply invested in attachment work. Lots of tagging partners.
- **Why 9takes should engage:** Attachment style + Enneagram type creates a much richer picture than either alone. A fearful-avoidant Type 4 and a fearful-avoidant Type 5 look completely different. Comments that bridge these frameworks would be genuinely valuable and novel.

### 5. @gottmaninstitute (The Gottman Institute)

- **Followers:** ~1M
- **Content focus:** Evidence-based relationship research, the "Four Horsemen," conflict management, intimacy, trust
- **Format:** Infographics, Q&A, research summaries, articles, carousels
- **Engagement quality:** High. Audience is research-minded and values data-driven insights.
- **Why 9takes should engage:** The Gottman method is about patterns in relationships. 9takes can add: "Research shows these patterns, and Enneagram mapping reveals WHY specific people default to criticism vs. stonewalling. Type 1s criticize because they see what should be better; Type 5s stonewall because they're emotionally overwhelmed."

### 6. @matthiasjbarker (Matthias Barker, LMHC)

- **Followers:** ~1M
- **Content focus:** Trauma healing, romantic relationships, family estrangement, positive psychology
- **Format:** Extended video content (Reels), educational deep-dives, practical challenges
- **Engagement quality:** Very high. His video content drives extensive discussion. Audience is earnest about healing.
- **Why 9takes should engage:** Matthias talks about trauma patterns and how they show up in relationships. Enneagram adds the "operating system" layer -- same trauma, different personality type = different manifestation. His audience appreciates nuance and depth.

### 7. @mindfulmft (Vienna Pharaon)

- **Followers:** ~724K
- **Content focus:** Breaking relationship patterns, origin wounds, family dynamics, self-awareness
- **Format:** Text on black/white backgrounds, myth-busting, Q&A sessions, podcasts
- **Engagement quality:** High. Her "What's Your Origin Wound?" quiz shows her audience loves self-discovery frameworks.
- **Why 9takes should engage:** Vienna literally built a framework around "origin wounds" that shape relationship patterns. This parallels the Enneagram's core wounds. Her audience is already quiz-takers and framework-seekers -- they want to understand their patterns through structured lenses.

### 8. @werenotreallystrangers (WNRS)

- **Followers:** ~5M
- **Content focus:** Vulnerability, self-reflection, human connection, mental health awareness, self-worth
- **Format:** Reflective text posts/quotes, interactive content, card game prompts
- **Engagement quality:** Massive. Posts get tens of thousands of comments. Audience tags friends constantly.
- **Why 9takes should engage:** WNRS posts open-ended reflection prompts. Adding personality-type perspective to "why do you push people away?" or "what are you afraid to say?" creates a deeper, more specific conversation. Their audience craves meaningful connection -- exactly what personality insights facilitate.

### 9. @therapyforblackgirls (Dr. Joy Harden Bradford)

- **Followers:** ~526K
- **Content focus:** Destigmatizing mental health for Black women, therapy access, community support
- **Format:** Community Q&As, expert deep dives, podcast clips, educational content, therapist directory
- **Engagement quality:** High. Strong community feel. Audience actively discusses and shares resources.
- **Why 9takes should engage:** The intersection of cultural experience and personality type is underexplored. Enneagram patterns manifest differently across cultural contexts, and adding this layer to conversations about mental health shows personality insight isn't one-size-fits-all.

## ENGAGEMENT STRATEGY NOTES

### Comment Approach by Account Type

**For therapist/educator accounts (Nicole LePera, Sara Kuburic, Nedra Tawwab, etc.):**

- Add depth, not correction. Frame personality insights as "another layer" to what they're already teaching.
- Use language like: "This maps to..." / "Personality research shows..." / "The Enneagram framework calls this..."
- Be specific: name the type number and the pattern. Generic comments get lost.

**For attachment-focused accounts (Julie Menanno, Silvy Khoucasian, Jessica Da Silva):**

- Bridge attachment + personality type. This is genuinely underexplored territory.
- Example: "Anxious attachment in a Type 6 looks like loyalty-testing, while in a Type 2 it looks like over-giving. Same insecurity, different expression."

**For community/reflection accounts (WNRS, Lisa Olivera, Notes From Your Therapist):**

- Answer their open-ended prompts through a personality lens.
- When they ask "what's your biggest fear in relationships?" -- reply with a type-specific answer that invites others to see themselves.

**For shadow work/inner child accounts (Nate Postlethwait, theuntamedself):**

- Connect the Enneagram's formation theory (type develops as childhood coping mechanism) to their healing frameworks.
- This is the most natural overlap -- Enneagram literally IS a map of childhood survival strategies.

### High-Value Comment Templates

1. **Pattern-naming:** "This is exactly what happens when [Type X] is under stress -- they [specific behavior] because their core fear is [specific fear]."
2. **Bridge-building:** "Attachment theory shows the pattern, and personality type explains why the same attachment style looks so different in different people."
3. **Specificity add:** "For people who struggle with [topic of post], the path forward depends a lot on whether you're avoiding pain (Types 7, 3, 1) or avoiding disconnection (Types 2, 6, 9) or avoiding loss of self (Types 4, 5, 8)."
4. **Question-asking:** "I'm curious -- do you find that clients with different personality structures need different approaches to [topic]? On 9takes we see 9 completely different emotional responses to the same situation."
