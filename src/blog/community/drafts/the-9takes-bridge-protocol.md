<p class="firstLetter">If your goal is to change a mind, your first job is to lower threat, not raise pressure.</p>

Most people try to win too early. They rush to claims, counterclaims, and receipts before they establish safety.

This protocol is built for one purpose: move a hard conversation from defensive to curious.

## The Protocol at a Glance

1. Reset the goal
2. Build an accuracy check
3. Name values, not labels
4. Run a "how" question ladder
5. Exchange update conditions
6. Close with next-step curiosity

## Step 1: Reset the Goal

Say this before your argument:

- "I don't want a dunk. I want a real understanding of how you see this."
- "If we still disagree at the end, that's fine. I just want us to understand each other better."

Why it works: it lowers perceived attack intent.

## Step 2: Build an Accuracy Check

Before pushing your view, reflect theirs.

Template:

- "Let me try to steelman your position: you think ** because **, and your biggest concern is \_\_. Did I get that right?"

Do not move on until they say yes.

Why it works: people soften when they feel represented accurately.

## Step 3: Name Values, Not Labels

Shift from tribe labels to human values.

Examples:

- "It sounds like your core value here is safety."
- "It sounds like fairness is the key concern."
- "It sounds like freedom/autonomy is what you're protecting."

Why it works: values are easier to discuss than identities.

## Step 4: Run a "How" Question Ladder

Use this sequence in order:

1. "How did you first come to this view?"
2. "How confident are you right now, 0-10?"
3. "How do you decide which sources to trust on this?"
4. "How would you explain your view to someone you care about who disagrees?"

Avoid "why are you wrong" framing.

Why it works: "how" questions make reasoning visible without immediate shame.

## Step 5: Exchange Update Conditions

Ask for falsifiability on both sides.

- "What would make you update this view, even by 10 percent?"
- "Here's what would make me update mine."

Why it works: mutual vulnerability removes one-sided interrogation dynamics.

## Step 6: Close with Next-Step Curiosity

Never force a conversion moment.

Use one of these exits:

- "I learned something important about how you see this."
- "I still disagree, but I understand your logic more clearly."
- "Let's keep this open and revisit after we both think on it."

Why it works: mind change is often delayed, not immediate.

## Fast De-Escalation Lines (Use Mid-Argument)

- "I think we switched from ideas to identity. Can we reset?"
- "I hear this matters deeply to you. I want to understand before I respond."
- "I might be missing context. Can you walk me through your process?"
- "Can I try to summarize your point before I reply?"

## Type-Aware Wording Tweaks

### If they are Type 8/1 (respect + principle driven)

- be direct
- avoid hedging games
- show fairness and consistency

Line:

- "I respect how principled you are on this. I want to challenge one piece directly and cleanly."

### If they are Type 2/4 (relational + emotional signal sensitive)

- acknowledge emotional stakes
- avoid cold cross-exam tone

Line:

- "I can hear this is personal and meaningful. I want to stay connected while we unpack it."

### If they are Type 5/6 (analysis + trust risk scanning)

- define terms
- make assumptions explicit
- state uncertainty honestly

Line:

- "Let me define what I mean so we don't talk past each other. If my assumptions are weak, call them out."

### If they are Type 7/9/3 (friction-avoidant or speed-oriented)

- keep structure tight
- avoid sprawling conflict loops
- focus on one point at a time

Line:

- "Can we isolate one disagreement first, solve that, then decide if we keep going?"

## What Not to Do

- don't diagnose personality during conflict
- don't front-load 20 facts in one burst
- don't ask rhetorical trap questions
- don't demand instant concession
- don't confuse silence with agreement

## 9takes-Compatible Question Prompts

Use these on `/questions` to generate higher-quality threads:

- "What belief did you change recently, and what changed it?"
- "What's one issue where you disagree with your own side?"
- "What value are you protecting in this argument?"
- "What evidence would move you by even 10 percent?"
- "When have you felt understood by someone who still disagreed?"

## Success Metric

A successful conversation is not "I won."

It is:

- both sides can summarize each other accurately
- emotional temperature drops
- both sides can name update conditions
- the relationship is intact

That is the bridge.

That is where real mind change starts.
