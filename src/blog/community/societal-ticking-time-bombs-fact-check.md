## Misleading / needs tightening (biggest risks)

### 1) **Antidepressant withdrawal prevalence (“56%… nearly half severe”)**

This is the **shakiest** and most likely to get attacked.

- The newer large meta-analysis coverage in 2024–2025 has been widely reported as **~15%** experiencing withdrawal symptoms _caused by discontinuation_ and **~3% severe** (depending on definitions/method). ([BMJ][19])
- Your linked JAMA Psychiatry item is not what most people cite for “56%” in a clean way, and across the literature the _rate depends heavily on how you define/attribute symptoms_ and whether you include nonspecific symptoms. (This is exactly where critics will hit you.)

**Suggested edit:**
Replace the hard number with something that reflects disagreement:

> “Estimates vary widely depending on definitions and study design. Recent meta-analytic work suggests **a minority** experience discontinuation symptoms directly attributable to stopping, with **a smaller minority** experiencing severe symptoms—but many patients do report distressing experiences, especially with abrupt cessation.”

…and then cite the 2024/2025 coverage noting ~15% / ~3%. ([BMJ][19])

### 2) **“Long-term antidepressant use may lower serotonin levels”**

Moncrieff’s review strongly challenges the serotonin hypothesis, but **“long-term use lowers serotonin”** reads like a settled causal claim. The umbrella review doesn’t establish that as a definitive clinical outcome; it’s more complicated (adaptations in serotonergic systems, receptor changes, etc.) and not cleanly measurable as “serotonin levels” in a way lay readers assume. ([PMC][6])

**Suggested edit:**
Soften to: “Long-term use may produce compensatory changes in serotonergic signaling,” and avoid sounding like “SSRIs create the imbalance.”

### 3) **“34 million Americans on antidepressants right now”**

This might be in the right ballpark, but you should either:

- cite a solid national estimate, or
- convert from CDC prevalence carefully.

CDC reports **11.4% of adults took prescription medication for depression in 2023**. If you translate that into a raw count, you must specify “adults” and the population base. ([CDC][20])

**Suggested edit:**

> “In 2023, **11.4% of U.S. adults** reported taking prescription medication for depression…”

…and drop the “34 million” unless you compute + cite the computation source.

### 4) **Therapy vs meds relapse numbers (31% / 76% / 47%)**

Directionally, your argument is supported (enduring effect of CBT; relapse risk after stopping meds), but the **exact trio of percentages** is a precision target. Since the PubMed record doesn’t display those numbers, a critic can say you’re cherry-picking or misquoting. ([PubMed][9])

**Suggested edit:**
Either (a) quote the paper exactly with the specific relapse figures from the full text, or (b) restate more generally:

> “Follow-up studies show cognitive therapy has an enduring effect and can reduce relapse risk compared with discontinuation…”

### 5) **Gambling stats that look like universal truths**

- **“96% of sports bettors lose money”** — This is plausible as a _survey-based_ claim (and you cite a consumer finance article), but it’s not an audited industry statistic. Keep it, but label it as survey/self-report and avoid implying it’s a law of nature.
- **“10% of men 18–30 meet criteria for gambling addiction” / “45% men under 30 reported at least one gambling problem behavior”** — These are the kind of stats that vary massively by instrument and sample. You should treat them as “in some surveys” and keep the sample context prominent (e.g., “a poll found…”).
- **“Problem gamblers have the highest suicide rate of any addiction disorder”** — The relationship between gambling disorder and suicidality is strong, but “highest of any addiction disorder” is an over-broad superlative that’s hard to prove cleanly across disorders. Better to say “very high” and cite systematic review findings (ideation/attempt rates). ([PMC][21])

### 6) **Microplastics section**

You cite a Nature Medicine / UNM-hosted PDF about microplastics in decedent brains. The “increased 47%” claim should be **tied explicitly to that paper’s measurement comparison** (2016 vs 2024 tissue samples) and you should avoid overclaiming about BBB timing (“within 2 hours”) unless you have a direct human/animal kinetic study handy (that part is easy to contest). ([UNM Health Sciences Center][22])

### 7) **NAEP “34% below basic”**

NAEP highlights page shows **32%** nationally (public) “below NAEP Basic” for 8th grade in 2022. You wrote **34%** and “worst in 32-year history.” The “34% / worst in 32 years” framing appears in commentary/secondary summaries, while NAEP’s own highlight puts the national public figure at 32%. ([Nations Report Card][23])

**Suggested edit:**
Use NAEP’s number for the cleanest defense:

> “In 2022, **32% of 8th graders in public schools** performed below NAEP Basic in reading…”

### 8) “40% read zero books in 2025”

Your link to NerdWallet is weak, but the **claim itself is supportable** if you cite the YouGov 2025 reading survey or its writeup. ([YouGov][24])

**Suggested edit:**
Swap the link to YouGov (or the YouGov PDF) and define whether audiobooks count.

## A few “wording landmines” to defuse

These are not “false,” but they are **rhetorically satisfying and fact-checker bait**:

- **“The chemical imbalance was a lie.”**
  Safer: “oversimplified,” “unsupported as a general explanation,” “not established.”

- **“Doctors misdiagnose withdrawal as relapse” (as a general claim).**
  Better: “can be mistaken for relapse” unless you have prevalence data.

- **“Pharma executives aren’t on SSRIs / gambling investors aren’t betting.”**
  That’s essentially unverifiable and easy to dunk on. If you keep it, frame as conjecture or drop.
