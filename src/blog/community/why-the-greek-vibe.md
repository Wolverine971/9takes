<p class="firstLetter">The Greeks didn't invent social media. They invented something better: structured disagreement.</p>

Plato didn't walk into the agora to post hot takes. He asked questions—hard ones—and expected people to think before responding. The agora was Athens' open marketplace where people congregated and ideas got stress-tested against competing perspectives. Not liked into oblivion. Not ratio'd into silence. Actually examined.

That practice—open questions, multiple viewpoints, thinking before reacting—barely exists online. Twitter rewards speed. Reddit rewards consensus. Neither rewards the kind of slow, multi-perspective inquiry that Socrates literally died defending.

**9takes seeks to be the modern day agora where fundamental questions can be discussed openly.**

The ancient Greeks didn't just invent democracy—they invented the infrastructure for productive disagreement. They understood something we've forgotten: **you can't have good answers without good questions.**

The Socratic method wasn't about winning arguments. It was about:

- **Asking questions** rather than making statements
- **Exposing assumptions** that people didn't know they held
- **Following logic** wherever it led, even to uncomfortable conclusions
- **Recognizing multiple valid perspectives** on complex issues

This is the [deeper question about human nature](/community/consensus-on-human-nature) that 9takes is built around. And it's exactly what's missing from modern online discourse.

### Why Modern Social Media Fails at Discourse

Twitter, Reddit, and Facebook were designed for engagement, not understanding. They optimize for:

| Social Media Optimizes For | Greeks Optimized For |
| -------------------------- | -------------------- |
| Quick reactions            | Deep reflection      |
| Winning arguments          | Seeking truth        |
| Tribal identity            | Individual reasoning |
| Content consumption        | Idea exploration     |
| Algorithmic curation       | Personal inquiry     |

The result? We're more connected than ever but understand each other less than ever.

## The 9takes Philosophy: Built on Greek Principles

9takes draws directly from Greek philosophical principles, building on the same [philosophical traditions](/enneagram-corner/philosophy-psychology-and-the-enneagram) that shaped personality psychology:

### 1. Questions Before Answers

Like Socrates, we believe the quality of your questions determines the quality of your understanding. Every conversation on 9takes starts with a question, not a statement or opinion.

### 2. Multiple Perspectives by Design

The Greeks understood that truth often lies at the intersection of competing viewpoints. The "9 takes" in our name refers to the nine Enneagram personality types—each offering a distinct lens on any question. You don't just see one take, you see how different types of minds approach the same issue.

### 3. Think Before Reacting

The Greek academy required students to listen and consider before responding. On 9takes, you must submit your own answer before seeing what others have said. This prevents groupthink and ensures every voice is genuinely independent.

### 4. Anonymous Discourse

In the agora, ideas were debated on their merits, not based on who said them. 9takes allows anonymous participation, letting your thoughts stand independently of your identity, status, or follower count.

## What Socrates Would Have Built

If Socrates were designing a platform, three things would have been non-negotiable:

1. **Questions that reveal character**: The Greeks believed character determined destiny. On 9takes, questions surface how different personality types approach the same situation—not just what they think, but why they think it.

2. **No rushing to consensus**: Socrates was famously unpopular because he wouldn't let conversations end prematurely. 9takes doesn't have upvotes or popularity metrics that force conformity. There's no mechanism to pile on.

3. **Emotional honesty**: The Greeks studied pathos (emotion) alongside logos (logic). 9takes treats emotional responses to questions as valid data, not noise to be eliminated. How you feel about a question reveals as much as how you reason through it.

## Why This Matters Now

We're living through a crisis of discourse. Social media has balkanized us into filter bubbles. Political polarization makes understanding "the other side" feel impossible. Nuance has become a liability.

The Greeks faced similar challenges. Athens was politically divided, foreign ideas threatened tradition, and populist demagogues gained power. Their solution? Institutions designed to slow down thinking and expose people to different perspectives.

9takes is that kind of institution for the digital age—a space specifically designed for the kind of thinking that other platforms accidentally discourage.

**The Greek aesthetic isn't just decoration. It's a reminder of what we're building toward.**

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why does 9takes use Greek statues in its imagery?

Greek statues represent idealized human form—not because the Greeks ignored human flaws, but because they believed in human potential. The statues remind us that we're capable of deep thought, meaningful discourse, and understanding each other across differences. They're aspirational, pointing toward what conversation could be, not what it typically is on modern social media.

### What is the connection between Greek philosophy and personality types?

The Greeks pioneered systematic thinking about human temperament. Hippocrates' four humors (sanguine, choleric, melancholic, phlegmatic) were an early personality typology. The Enneagram, while not Greek in origin, continues this tradition of recognizing that humans have different fundamental orientations. 9takes uses the Enneagram to add a Greek-style systematic understanding of how different minds approach the same questions.

### How does 9takes bring back Socratic dialogue?

Three key ways: First, the platform is question-centered, not statement-centered—you start with inquiry, not assertion. Second, you must contribute your own answer before seeing others, preventing the groupthink that Socrates fought against. Third, seeing 9 different personality perspectives mirrors the Socratic practice of considering multiple viewpoints rather than accepting the first plausible answer.

### What would Socrates think of modern social media?

He'd probably be horrified—but also recognize the potential. Socrates was killed for "corrupting the youth" by teaching them to question authority. Social media has democratized information but not democratized wisdom. Socrates would likely appreciate platforms that slow down reaction and encourage reflection. He'd probably spend his time asking uncomfortable questions that got him banned from Twitter.

### Is 9takes trying to replace other social media?

No. 9takes serves a different purpose than Twitter, Instagram, or TikTok. Those platforms excel at entertainment, news distribution, and casual connection. 9takes is specifically designed for exploratory conversation—the kind where you're genuinely curious what other people think, not just looking to share your own take or consume content. Think of it as a complement, not a replacement.
