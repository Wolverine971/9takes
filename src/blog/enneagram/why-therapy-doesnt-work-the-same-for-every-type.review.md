# Review notes: Why Therapy Does Not Work the Same for Every Type

Extracted from `src/blog/enneagram/why-therapy-doesnt-work-the-same-for-every-type.md` page source on 2026-07-15, then expanded during the evidence rebuild on 2026-07-15.

## Reader feedback status from 2026-04-01

The April pass trimmed the redundant intro, consolidated the Freud references, sourced the old 50 percent claim, expanded therapist matching, integrated alexithymia, added access and culture, acknowledged integrative therapy, qualified the FAQ timeline, and trimmed repeated ending material.

The July evidence audit found that several revised claims still did not hold up. The article therefore received a full rebuild rather than another surface edit.

## Resolution log from the July rebuild

| Review issue                                                                           | Resolution                                                                                                                                                                |
| -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Educational and clinical boundary was too weak                                         | Replaced with an explicit boundary stating that the Enneagram is neither diagnostic nor a validated treatment-matching system. Added crisis and safe-transition guidance. |
| Psychotherapy was reduced to a single “talking cure”                                   | Replaced with a description of modern behavioral, skills-based, relational, exposure-based, and trauma-focused work.                                                      |
| Universal verbal, cognitive, and somatic processor categories                          | Removed. No adequate evidence was found for three fixed processor groups or distinct type-linked neural pathways.                                                         |
| Generic 40 to 60 percent response rate                                                 | Removed. Psychotherapy response varies by condition, treatment, comparator, outcome, severity, and follow-up.                                                             |
| Enneagram centers presented as clinical mechanisms                                     | Reframed as reflection prompts. Added the Hook et al. systematic review and its mixed reliability and validity finding.                                                   |
| Claims that certain Enneagram types are overrepresented or underrepresented in therapy | Removed. No credible prevalence evidence was found.                                                                                                                       |
| MBTI Thinking versus Feeling claim used to recommend CBT                               | Removed. It did not establish Enneagram-based treatment selection.                                                                                                        |
| “Body-stored anger” and speech-center trauma claims                                    | Removed. The article now uses disorder-specific clinical guidelines when discussing trauma treatment.                                                                     |
| Personality traits presented as likely misdiagnoses                                    | Removed. The old list risked minimizing clinical assessment and conflating type with diagnosis.                                                                           |
| Rapport framed as comfort that can block growth                                        | Rebuilt around the evidence-based working alliance: bond plus agreement on goals and tasks. Productive disagreement is separated from coercion or unsafe pressure.        |
| Modality prescriptions by center or type                                               | Removed. The new article tells readers to select treatments by clinical need, evidence, therapist competence, preference, previous response, and access.                  |
| Alexithymia described as proof that talk therapy fails                                 | Corrected with 2024 and 2026 systematic reviews showing that psychological interventions can reduce alexithymia. Added concrete communication adaptations.                |
| Fixed 50-minute format dismissed as unscientific                                       | Removed. The new article focuses on goals, monitoring, pacing, format, and access without making an unsupported origin claim.                                             |
| Therapist Enneagram pairings presented as predictable                                  | Removed. Replaced with alliance evidence, a measurement-based therapist-matching RCT, and consultation questions grounded in the client's concern.                        |
| Access and culture were treated as a footnote                                          | Integrated into the fit model. Added SAMHSA cost resources and research on culturally adapted care.                                                                       |
| Type advice remained too prescriptive                                                  | Rebuilt as a nine-row set of optional behavioral hypotheses and first-person therapist scripts. Readers are told to keep only the row that fits observable behavior.      |
| Therapy timeline implied a universal threshold                                         | Replaced with condition-specific review guidance, observable outcome tracking, and an explicit statement that no universal deadline exists.                               |
| FAQ and structured data repeated unsupported claims                                    | Rewritten and synchronized around clinical boundaries, fit, timelines, alexithymia, and translating type labels into observable behavior.                                 |
