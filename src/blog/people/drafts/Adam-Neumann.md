> "He took the divorce very hard. It was a terrible time. I took him to see a psychiatrist — he didn't yet speak English, so I had to translate." — Avivit Neumann-Orbach, on her son Adam, age 7

<p class="firstLetter">At ten, Adam Neumann handed his mother $150 and said, "I earned $300 from my paper route. This is my share of the rent." She was a single mother on an $1,800-a-month salary, raising two kids alone in Indianapolis. Her son was already making sure he wasn't a burden.</p>

By twenty-two, he had lived in thirteen different homes.

By thirty-one, he had convinced a Japanese billionaire to write him a $4.4 billion check after a twelve-minute office tour.

By forty, he was barefoot in conference rooms, demanding $140 tequila shots in interviews, and telling people — out loud, on the record — that he wanted to be the world's first trillionaire, the prime minister of Israel, and "president of the world."

The contradiction is the man. The boy who couldn't stand to owe his mother fifty bucks grew up to lose other people's billions and reframe it as a learning experience.

## What is Adam Neumann's personality type?

### Adam Neumann is an Enneagram Type 7w8

The clean version: he is a [Seven](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-7) with a strong Eight [wing](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-wings-complete-guide), sometimes written 7w8 or "the Realist."

Sevens are the type the Enneagram tradition associates with avoiding pain by chasing options. Their core fear is being trapped — in a feeling, in a consequence, in a small life. Their core strategy is to keep moving so the bad thing can't catch up. That description fits Neumann so cleanly it almost feels lazy. Thirteen homes by twenty-two. A company built around the promise of belonging that he ran like a touring band. A trillion-dollar fantasy life, on the record, in a Wall Street Journal profile, while his actual company was hemorrhaging cash.

The Eight wing is the part that often gets misread as the whole story. It is the brashness, the height (he is 6'5"), the willingness to dominate a room, the demand that the tequila tray come around again. Sevens with a strong 8 wing aren't peacocks; they are bulldozers with vision boards. They can charm a billionaire into a $4.4 billion check in twelve minutes; they can also stage a layoff like a music festival, which we'll get to.

What the Enneagram does for Neumann that no other lens does is resolve the question that hangs over every WeWork postmortem: was he a fraud, or did he believe it? Sevens are almost never frauds in the way we usually mean it. They believe everything they say in the moment they say it. The lie isn't to the audience. The lie is to themselves about what they will still believe tomorrow.

## Adam Neumann's childhood: 13 homes by 22, and a psychiatrist who needed a translator

The defining fact of Adam Neumann's childhood is not the kibbutz. It is the moves.

He was born in Beersheba, Israel, in 1979. His parents — both doctors, both Ben-Gurion medical-school graduates — divorced when he was seven. His mother, Avivit Neumann-Orbach, took him and his younger sister Adi to Indianapolis for her medical training. He spoke no English. He has dyslexia, which meant he couldn't read or write fluently in any language until third grade. The psychiatrist scene from the epigraph — his mother translating his sentences into a language she didn't fully speak yet either — is from a 2020 [interview with CTech](https://www.calcalistech.com/ctech/articles/0,7340,L-3802511,00.html).

By the time he was twenty-two, he had lived in thirteen different homes. His mother's salary was $1,800 a month. The paper-route scene from the opening is the one she keeps coming back to in interviews — a ten-year-old calculating half of his mother's rent and handing it to her like a roommate.

Read that scene as a Seven and it stops being charming. It is a child trying to transact his way out of debt to the only stable adult in the room — paying for the right to belong to a household that had already proven it could disappear.

The instinct that produces "I earned $300, this is my share of the rent" at age ten is the same instinct that, thirty years later, produces a 350-page IPO filing built around the phrase "elevate the world's consciousness." If you can't trust the room to be there tomorrow, you don't sit in it. You sell it.

## How Adam Neumann turned a kibbutz childhood into WeWork

The kibbutz part is the part everyone tells. It is also slightly misleading.

In his teens, Neumann's mother sent him from Indianapolis to live on Kibbutz Nir Am, three kilometers from the Gaza border. He has called it both _"the most unbelievable place to grow up"_ and, more often, _"a failed social experiment."_ The thing the kibbutz gave him — friends from morning till night, shared meals, shared rooms, shared school — became the marketing material for WeWork. The thing he resented about it — that the laundry worker and the doctor were paid the same — became WeWork's anti-marketing material.

He has said, on multiple occasions, that WeWork was meant to be _"a Capitalist kibbutz."_ The phrase is more revealing than he probably intended. A kibbutz is a place you live with people who can't be fired. A Capitalist kibbutz is a place where the togetherness is a product and you can leave the lease at any time. He didn't want the kibbutz. He wanted the feeling of the kibbutz, on a 30-day cancellation clause.

This is a Seven move and it works for almost any Seven you can name. Take the part of the experience that fed you. Strip out the part that constrained you. Sell it back as a service. The carbon-credit blockchain startup he launched in 2022 — Flowcarbon — is the same move with rainforests. The residential real-estate company that came after — Flow — is the same move with apartments. He keeps remixing the kibbutz.

There is a quieter detail buried in the WeWork story that Reeves Wiedeman flagged in [_Billion Dollar Loser_](https://www.amazon.com/Billion-Dollar-Loser-Spectacular-Neumann-ebook/dp/B08CV6GB22): in the early years, before the SoftBank money, Adam and Rebekah Neumann would talk publicly about the "asset-light lifestyle" of the sharing economy while owning five homes. That isn't hypocrisy in a Seven. That is a Seven not noticing the gap, because the story they're telling is true to them in the moment they're telling it.

## How Adam Neumann sold Masayoshi Son on $4.4 billion in 12 minutes

In 2017, Masayoshi Son — the head of SoftBank, the largest tech investor on earth — visited WeWork's headquarters. The tour was scheduled for two hours. It lasted twelve minutes.

Halfway through the walk-around, [Son pulled out his phone](https://www.fastcompany.com/90426446/wefail-how-the-doomed-masa-son-adam-neumann-relationship-set-wework-on-the-road-to-disaster), invited Neumann into the back of his SUV, and sketched out the outline of a $4.4 billion investment on the way to Trump Tower. WeWork's valuation went from a few billion dollars to twenty billion dollars by the time the SUV stopped moving.

The Vision Fund had run months of due diligence in the background. But the human moment — the part Adam Neumann lived inside — was twelve minutes. Twelve minutes is exactly long enough for a Seven with an 8 wing to do what they do best: tell a vision so loud and so certain that the listener forgets to ask how the math works.

There is a Bruce Dunlevie quote about WeWork that captures what was happening in those rooms. Dunlevie was the Benchmark Capital partner who put in some of the earliest serious money. He has [described the bet](https://www.bookforum.com/print/2704/how-low-quality-visionary-adam-neumann-gets-it-done-24269) as resting on _"an indefinable feeling that you couldn't quite put your finger on."_ That is what a Seven feels like across a table. The numbers don't move you. The man does.

The signature move in this section isn't the size of the check. It is the rhythm. Twelve minutes of total exposure to Adam Neumann was enough for the most prolific tech investor of the decade to commit billions. By the time anyone slowed down enough to read the prospectus, the company had already grown into the valuation. That is the Seven's trade — get the room to commit before anyone has time to think.

## Adam Neumann's tequila-and-barefoot leadership style

The barefoot stories are real. So are the tequila stories. So is the marijuana-in-a-cereal-box-on-the-private-jet-to-Israel story, which somehow keeps getting buried under the others.

Former employees, [interviewed at length](https://forward.com/fast-forward/431698/we-work-adam-neumann/) after his 2019 ouster, kept circling back to three specific images: Adam barefoot in the office, Adam barefoot on the conference table, Adam yelling at someone from the conference table he was barefoot on. One employee, recalled to a reporter, _"He would jump on his desk, or jump on the conference table barefoot and yell at people."_ The tequila stories are even more documented: $140-a-bottle Don Julio passed around in interviews, in staff meetings, and at a 2016 layoff announcement where seven percent of the company was being told they were out, and a member of Run DMC was performing live, and the trays kept coming.

There is a temptation to read the bare feet and the tequila as a costume — a tech founder cosplaying as a guru. That is the wrong read.

Sevens at a high state are genuine adventurers; the energy is real. Sevens [under stress](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-stress-number) borrow the next-most-intense thing in the room because their own system can't generate enough novelty to outrun the dread. Bare feet in conference rooms is not a costume. It is a Seven who has run out of reasonable inputs and started reaching for unreasonable ones. The tequila isn't a celebration. It is a sedative for a person who cannot tolerate the sound of a quiet office.

Watch the layoff scene through that lens. Run DMC is on stage. The tequila is moving. He is announcing the firings personally. A Seven cannot sit in a room where they have to deliver bad news without staging a distraction. The 8 wing makes him willing to deliver the news. The 7 core makes him insist that the room be loud enough that nobody — including him — can sit with what is being said.

## What the WeWork collapse revealed about Adam Neumann

In August 2019, [WeWork filed its S-1](https://www.cnbc.com/2019/08/14/wework-ipo-filing-sells-a-romantic-vision-alongside-losses.html). The world saw the numbers for the first time. A $1.9 billion loss on $1.8 billion in revenue. The mission statement — _"Our mission is to elevate the world's consciousness"_ — sat in the opening sentences of the prospectus. The corporate-governance section made multiple lawyers laugh on the record. The valuation sat at $47 billion.

Six weeks later, the IPO was withdrawn. The valuation cratered toward $8 billion. Neumann was forced out by his own board. He took a personal exit package reportedly worth around $1.7 billion. SoftBank ate the loss.

Two years later, Neumann sat down with Andrew Ross Sorkin at the [DealBook Summit](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/10/25/cnbc-transcript-wework-co-founder-former-ceo-and-flow-founder-adam-neumann-speaks-with-cnbcs-andrew-ross-sorkin-on-squawk-box-today.html) — his first real public interview since the collapse — and said, _"I have had a lot of time to think, and there have been multiple lessons, multiple regrets."_ Sorkin pressed him on whether he had anything to say to the thousands of WeWork employees who had lost their jobs and watched their stock options evaporate. Neumann did not apologize.

The line that mattered most was this one:

<div class="pull-quote">"The valuation made us feel like we were right, which made me feel that whatever style I was leading at was a correct style at the time." — Adam Neumann, DealBook Summit, November 2021</div>

It is the most honest thing he has ever said publicly, and it is also the perfect Type 7 sentence. The way Sevens process pain is by relocating the cause. The valuation went to my head. The market gave me permission. The number told me I was right. The structure of the sentence is a Seven's coping mechanism in real time: the responsibility is real, but it has been gently handed to an external object — the valuation, the market, the moment — so the speaker can carry it without sitting in it.

When Sorkin pushed for an apology to the employees, the apology never came. Sevens cannot apologize for a previous self they no longer feel like. They have already moved.

## Adam Neumann's comeback: Flow, and the boy who can't stop moving

In August 2022, [Andreessen Horowitz announced Flow](https://a16z.com/announcement/flow/). It was a16z's largest single check ever — $350 million — for a residential-real-estate company that had not yet started operating. The valuation was over $1 billion. The pitch deck was, depending on which reporter you ask, either thinner than the Flowcarbon deck that came months earlier or somehow thinner than that. Marc Andreessen called Neumann a _"visionary leader"_ who would _"revolutionize"_ residential housing.

The structural pattern is the kibbutz again. Take the part of the experience that fed you (community, belonging, rent that doesn't kill you). Strip out the part that constrained you (price-fixed wages, immobility, having to stay). Sell it back as a service. By 2023, Flow controlled over 3,000 apartment units across Miami, Fort Lauderdale, Atlanta, and Nashville.

Masayoshi Son, asked about Neumann after the WeWork collapse, said: _"a smart guy"_ with _"a lot of capabilities... I'm a big believer that he will be successful, and that he has learned a lot from his prior life."_ Even the man who lost the most money on the bet refused to write him off.

That is the most Seven thing about Adam Neumann's career: he has never once allowed himself to be the person things ended for. The IPO collapsed; he reframed it as a chapter break. The board fired him; he took $1.7 billion and bought 3,000 apartments. The press called him a cult leader; he became a wellness landlord. There is no version of his story in his own telling where the ending is final. There is always a next door.

This is where the man and the boy meet. The seven-year-old who needed his mother to translate at the psychiatrist. The ten-year-old who handed her cash so he wouldn't owe anyone. The twenty-two-year-old who had lived in thirteen homes. The forty-year-old who built the world's most expensive belonging machine and then could not bring himself to be inside it. They are all the same person, running the same play. The play is: do not be in the room when the room ends.

He is barefoot because his feet are still moving.

He is selling togetherness because togetherness is the one thing he has never been able to keep.
