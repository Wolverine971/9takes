> "He took the divorce very hard. It was a terrible time. I took him to see a psychiatrist — he didn't yet speak English, so I had to translate." — Avivit Neumann-Orbach, on her son Adam, age 7

<p class="firstLetter">At ten, Adam Neumann handed his mother $150 and said, "I earned $300 from my paper route. This is my share of the rent." She was a single mother on an $1,800-a-month salary, raising two kids alone in Indianapolis. Her son was already making sure he wasn't a burden.</p>

By twenty-two, he had lived in thirteen different homes.

By thirty-one, he had convinced a Japanese billionaire to write him a $4.4 billion check after a twelve-minute office tour.

By forty, he was barefoot in conference rooms, demanding $140 tequila shots in interviews, and telling people — out loud, on the record — that he wanted to be the world's first trillionaire, the prime minister of Israel, and "president of the world."

The contradiction is the man. The boy who couldn't bear to owe his mother fifty bucks grew up to walk away from WeWork with a $1.7 billion exit while the people who built it watched their stock options evaporate. Same nervous system. Different room.

## What is Adam Neumann's personality type?

### Adam Neumann is an Enneagram Type 7w8

The clean version: he is a [Seven](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-7) with a strong Eight [wing](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-wings-complete-guide), sometimes written 7w8 or "the Realist."

Sevens are the type the Enneagram tradition associates with avoiding pain by chasing options. Their core fear is being trapped — in a feeling, in a consequence, in a small life. Their core strategy is to keep moving so the bad thing can't catch up. That description fits Neumann so cleanly it almost feels lazy. Thirteen homes by twenty-two. A company built around the promise of belonging that he ran like a touring band. A trillion-dollar fantasy life, on the record, in a Wall Street Journal profile, while his actual company was hemorrhaging cash.

The Eight wing is the part that often gets misread as the whole story. It is the brashness, the height (he is 6'5"), the willingness to dominate a room, the demand that the tequila tray come around again. Sevens with a strong 8 wing aren't peacocks; they are bulldozers with vision boards. They can charm a billionaire into a $4.4 billion check in twelve minutes. They can also stage a layoff like a music festival, which we'll get to.

What the Enneagram does for Neumann that no other lens does is resolve the question that hangs over every WeWork postmortem: was he a fraud, or did he believe it? Sevens are almost never frauds in the way we usually mean it. They believe everything they say in the moment they say it. The lie isn't to the audience. The lie is to themselves about what they will still believe tomorrow.

## Adam Neumann's childhood: a psychiatrist who needed a translator

The defining fact of Adam Neumann's childhood is not the kibbutz. It is the moves.

He was born in Beersheba, Israel, in 1979. His parents — both doctors, both Ben-Gurion medical-school graduates — divorced when he was seven. His mother, Avivit Neumann-Orbach, took him and his younger sister Adi to Indianapolis for her medical training. He spoke no English. He has dyslexia, which meant he couldn't read or write fluently in any language until third grade. The psychiatrist scene from the epigraph — his mother translating his sentences into a language she didn't fully speak yet either — is from a 2020 [interview with CTech](https://www.calcalistech.com/ctech/articles/0,7340,L-3802511,00.html).

The Indianapolis years were lean. His mother's salary was $1,800 a month. The paper-route scene from the opening is the one she keeps coming back to in interviews — a ten-year-old calculating half of his mother's rent and handing it to her like a roommate. (His sister Adi, the one in the next room, would later go on to win Miss Teen Israel 1997 and a modeling career; even the family color is high-stakes performance.)

Then the 8 wing began to come in. As a teenager, Neumann's mother sent him from Indianapolis back to Israel — first to Kibbutz Nir Am, three kilometers from the Gaza border, then into mandatory service. He did not do a standard tour. He commissioned as an officer in the Israeli Navy and served five years. This is the part of the biography that complicates the "manic Seven who can't sit still" read; Sevens generally don't make it through naval-officer school. The 8 wing did. The discipline he could borrow from a chain of command was always going to be cleaner than the discipline he could generate on his own — and that gap shows up in every story about him after the structure was gone.

Read the paper-route scene as a Seven and it stops being charming. It is a child trying to transact his way out of debt to the only stable adult in the room — paying for the right to belong to a household that had already proven it could disappear.

The instinct that produces "I earned $300, this is my share of the rent" at age ten is the same instinct that, thirty years later, produces a 350-page IPO filing built around the phrase "elevate the world's consciousness." If you can't trust the room to be there tomorrow, you don't sit in it. You sell it.

## How Adam Neumann (and Rebekah) turned a kibbutz childhood into WeWork

The kibbutz part is the part everyone tells. It is also slightly misleading.

Neumann has called Kibbutz Nir Am both _"the most unbelievable place to grow up"_ and, more often, _"a failed social experiment."_ The thing the kibbutz gave him — friends from morning till night, shared meals, shared rooms, shared school — became the marketing material for WeWork. The thing he resented about it — that the laundry worker and the doctor were paid the same — became WeWork's anti-marketing material.

He has said, on multiple occasions, that WeWork was meant to be _"a Capitalist kibbutz."_ The phrase is more revealing than he probably intended. A kibbutz is a place you live with people who can't be fired. A Capitalist kibbutz is a place where the togetherness is a product and you can leave the lease at any time. He didn't want the kibbutz. He wanted the feeling of the kibbutz, on a 30-day cancellation clause.

He didn't build the spiritual layer alone, which is the half of the WeWork story that tends to fall out. In 2008, Neumann met [Rebekah Paltrow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rebekah_Neumann) — Gwyneth Paltrow's first cousin, a Cornell graduate and aspiring actress who had been studying kabbalah in New York. She became his wife, his co-founder, and the person WeWork's marketing material would later describe as its soul. She is the reason a coworking company had a wellness manifesto, a "chief brand and impact officer," and, eventually, [WeGrow](https://www.thedailybeast.com/weworks-private-school-wegrow-has-stranded-parents-as-adam-neumann-cashes-out/) — a private elementary school she founded in Chelsea in 2017 for "conscious entrepreneurship," where five-to-eight-year-olds did mindfulness, meditation, and farming, because, as she put it on the record, _"children are ready to start creating their life's work when they're five."_

The S-1 filing of 2019 made the partnership uncomfortably literal. In a clause that briefly survived the first draft, Rebekah was named to a "succession committee" empowered to pick the next CEO if Adam died or became permanently incapacitated within ten years of the IPO. She was not on the board. The clause was not legally typical. It had to be removed in [an amended filing](https://www.cnbc.com/2019/09/04/wework-ceo-returns-5point9-million-the-company-paid-for-we-trademark.html), and Rebekah was barred from board service. But the original instinct survived the revision: the company they built was a couple's project the way a kibbutz is a kibbutz — a workplace and a household, a business and a faith.

The structural Seven move underneath both halves is the same. Take the part of the experience that fed you. Strip out the part that constrained you. Sell it back as a service. He is going to run that play more than once.

There is a quieter detail Reeves Wiedeman flagged in [_Billion Dollar Loser_](https://www.amazon.com/Billion-Dollar-Loser-Spectacular-Neumann-ebook/dp/B08CV6GB22): in the early years, before the SoftBank money, Adam and Rebekah Neumann would talk publicly about the "asset-light lifestyle" of the sharing economy while owning five homes. That isn't hypocrisy in a Seven. That is a Seven not noticing the gap, because the story they're telling is true to them in the moment they're telling it.

## How Adam Neumann sold Masayoshi Son on $4.4 billion in 12 minutes

In 2017, Masayoshi Son — the head of SoftBank, the largest tech investor on earth — visited WeWork's headquarters. The tour was scheduled for two hours. It lasted twelve minutes.

Halfway through the walk-around, [Son pulled out his phone](https://www.fastcompany.com/90426446/wefail-how-the-doomed-masa-son-adam-neumann-relationship-set-wework-on-the-road-to-disaster), invited Neumann into the back of his SUV, and sketched out the outline of a $4.4 billion investment on the way to Trump Tower. WeWork's valuation went from a few billion dollars to twenty billion dollars by the time the SUV stopped moving.

The Vision Fund had run months of due diligence in the background. But the human moment — the part Adam Neumann lived inside — was twelve minutes. Twelve minutes is exactly long enough for a Seven with an 8 wing to do what they do best: tell a vision so loud and so certain that the listener forgets to ask how the math works.

Bruce Dunlevie, the Benchmark Capital partner whose firm wrote some of WeWork's earliest serious checks, would later [describe his bet](https://www.bookforum.com/print/2704/how-low-quality-visionary-adam-neumann-gets-it-done-24269) as resting on _"an indefinable feeling that you couldn't quite put your finger on."_ Dunlevie had decades of pattern-matching on founders. The Vision Fund had a thirty-person diligence operation. Both committed on the man, not the math. That is what a Seven feels like across a table — and what makes him so dangerous when he is the one across the table.

The signature of this section isn't the size of the check. It is the rhythm. Twelve minutes of total exposure to Adam Neumann was enough for the most prolific tech investor of the decade to commit billions. By the time anyone slowed down enough to read the prospectus, the company had already grown into the valuation. That is the Seven's trade — get the room to commit before anyone has time to think.

## Adam Neumann's tequila-and-barefoot leadership style

The barefoot stories are real. So are the tequila stories. So is the marijuana-in-a-cereal-box-on-the-private-jet-to-Israel story, which somehow keeps getting buried under the others.

Former employees, [interviewed at length](https://forward.com/fast-forward/431698/we-work-adam-neumann/) after his 2019 ouster, kept circling back to three specific images: Adam barefoot in the office, Adam barefoot on the conference table, Adam yelling at someone from the conference table he was barefoot on. One employee recalled to a reporter, _"He would jump on his desk, or jump on the conference table barefoot and yell at people."_

Maybe that one is just a 6'5" Israeli founder being a jerk and the typology doesn't have to do all the work. The harder evidence is the events.

Every summer through 2018, WeWork ran what it called [Summer Camp](https://benews.co.uk/insight/one-time-at-wework-summer-camp/) — first in upstate New York, later at a country estate in Tunbridge Wells, Kent — where thousands of employees were transported to a festival site for three days of mandatory team-building, tents, headline performances by Lorde, the Weeknd, and Florence + the Machine, and free-flowing alcohol. Drinking was structurally optional and culturally mandatory. The same logic ran the office.

Then there is the layoff. In 2016, $140-a-bottle Don Julio was being passed around in interviews and at staff meetings. It also showed up at an all-hands where Neumann announced that 7% of the company was being cut. As the somber news landed, [trays of tequila shots came around](https://www.spin.com/2019/09/run-dmc-wework-performance/). Then Darryl McDaniels of Run DMC walked out and started performing.

There is a temptation to read the bare feet and the tequila as a costume — a tech founder cosplaying as a guru. That is the wrong read.

Sevens at a high state are genuine adventurers; the energy is real. Sevens [under stress](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-stress-number) borrow the next-most-intense thing in the room because their own system can't generate enough novelty to outrun the dread. The 8 wing makes Neumann willing to be the one delivering the news at the layoff. The 7 core makes him insist that the room be loud enough that nobody — including him — can sit with what is being said.

The tequila isn't a celebration. It is a sedative for a person who cannot tolerate the sound of a quiet office.

## What the WeWork collapse revealed about Adam Neumann

In August 2019, [WeWork filed its S-1](https://www.cnbc.com/2019/08/14/wework-ipo-filing-sells-a-romantic-vision-alongside-losses.html). The world saw the numbers for the first time. A $1.9 billion loss on $1.8 billion in revenue. The mission statement — _"Our mission is to elevate the world's consciousness"_ — sat in the opening sentences of the prospectus.

The numbers were bad. The governance was worse, and the governance is the part that turned a soft IPO into a public unraveling.

Two disclosures landed hardest. First: Adam Neumann had personally trademarked the word _"We"_ through a holding company he controlled, We Holdings LLC, and then sold the rights back to WeWork for **$5.9 million** when the parent company renamed itself "The We Company." He had charged his own company nearly six million dollars to use the first word of its own name. Second: WeWork was leasing four buildings that Adam Neumann personally owned, with future minimum lease payments [disclosed in the S-1](https://wallstreetonparade.com/2023/11/wesuck-first-came-the-hype-then-came-adam-neumanns-self-dealing-then-came-the-ipo-scandal-now-comes-the-bankruptcy/) at approximately **$236.6 million**. He was the tenant and the landlord of his own company.

He returned the trademark money [in September 2019](https://www.cnbc.com/2019/09/04/wework-ceo-returns-5point9-million-the-company-paid-for-we-trademark.html), at his own direction, after the public outrage. The word the company kept wasn't his anymore.

Six weeks after the S-1 dropped, the IPO was withdrawn. The valuation cratered from $47 billion toward $8 billion. Neumann was forced out by his own board. He took a personal exit package reportedly worth around $1.7 billion. SoftBank ate the loss.

Two years later, Neumann sat down with Andrew Ross Sorkin at the [DealBook Summit](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/10/25/cnbc-transcript-wework-co-founder-former-ceo-and-flow-founder-adam-neumann-speaks-with-cnbcs-andrew-ross-sorkin-on-squawk-box-today.html) — his first real public interview since the collapse — and produced the most honest sentence he had ever said in public.

<div class="pull-quote">"The valuation made us feel like we were right, which made me feel that whatever style I was leading at was a correct style at the time." — Adam Neumann, DealBook Summit, November 2021</div>

Read it slowly. There is a Type 7 self-portrait inside it that he probably didn't know he was painting.

_The valuation_ — not Adam, not the founder, not the leadership team. An external object. The number on the deck.

_made us feel_ — a feeling that arrived from outside, like weather.

_like we were right_ — not "we were right." Like we were right. The number gave him permission to feel correct without requiring him to be correct.

_which made me feel that whatever style I was leading at_ — past tense; "whatever" makes the leadership style unspecific, almost incidental, as if the man didn't pick the style so much as receive it.

_was a correct style at the time._ — bounded in time. The style was correct then. The implicit promise is that he is no longer that person.

It is a flawless Seven sentence. Sevens process pain by relocating the cause. The valuation went to my head. The market gave me permission. The number told me I was right. The structure of the sentence is a coping mechanism in real time: the responsibility is real, but it has been gently handed to an external object — the valuation, the market, the moment — so the speaker can carry it without sitting in it.

When Sorkin pushed for an apology to the WeWork employees who had lost their jobs and watched their stock options evaporate, the apology never came. Sevens cannot apologize for a previous self they no longer feel like. They have already moved.

For audiences who first met him through Apple TV+'s [_WeCrashed_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WeCrashed) in 2022 — Jared Leto and Anne Hathaway as Adam and Rebekah — the eight episodes covered most of this terrain. The thing the show could not capture, and the thing the DealBook sentence makes explicit, is that the man inside the collapse never actually conceded he was inside it.

## Adam Neumann's comeback: Flow, the bankruptcy bid, and the boy who can't stop moving

In August 2022, [Andreessen Horowitz announced Flow](https://a16z.com/announcement/flow/). It was a16z's largest single check ever — $350 million — for a residential-real-estate company that had not yet started operating. The valuation was over $1 billion. The pitch deck was, depending on which reporter you ask, slim. Marc Andreessen called Neumann a _"visionary leader"_ who would _"revolutionize"_ residential housing.

What is Flow, actually? Underneath the marketing, it is a property-management company that buys or operates apartment buildings and adds a layer of WeWork-grade amenities and software: an [app](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flow_(real_estate_company)) for booking the gym, the pilates studio, the cold plunges, the sauna, the pool, and the lounges; in-house events; a stated promise that residents will know each other; and — eventually — an "equity component" that lets long-term renters earn participation in the building's upside. By 2025, Flow controlled over 3,000 apartment units across Miami, Fort Lauderdale, Atlanta, and Nashville, with a 40-story Flow House condo tower beginning sales in downtown Miami.

This is the kibbutz again. But notice what is different in the third remix.

WeWork sold the feeling of the kibbutz to companies — togetherness as a 30-day office product. Flow sells the feeling of the kibbutz to renters — togetherness as an apartment lease. The pattern hasn't changed. The audience has gotten more vulnerable. WeWork's customers were small businesses with the money to walk away. Flow's customers are tenants in a national housing shortage who do not have many places to walk to.

Then — and this is the part most postmortems leave out — he tried to take WeWork back.

In February 2024, with WeWork in Chapter 11 bankruptcy, Adam Neumann submitted [a bid through Flow](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/03/25/adam-neumann-submits-bid-of-more-than-500-million-to-buy-wework.html) and his family office Nazare to buy the company he had been forced out of five years earlier. The bid started above $500 million, climbed toward $650 million with debtor-in-possession financing, and was [rejected in May 2024](https://fortune.com/2024/05/28/wework-flow-adam-neumann-bankruptcy-judge-chapter-11-flow/). The judge ruled it infeasible because it failed to address WeWork's $4 billion in liabilities. WeWork emerged from bankruptcy under a $450 million package from Yardi and SoftBank — a smaller deal than the one Neumann offered, but a real one.

That is the most Seven thing about Adam Neumann's career: he has never once allowed himself to be the person things ended for. The IPO collapsed; he reframed it as a chapter break. The board fired him; he took $1.7 billion and bought 3,000 apartments. The bankruptcy court turned him down; he kept Flow. There is no version of his story in his own telling where the ending is final. There is always a next door — even when the door is the one he was thrown out of.

Masayoshi Son, asked about Neumann after the WeWork collapse, said: _"a smart guy"_ with _"a lot of capabilities... I'm a big believer that he will be successful, and that he has learned a lot from his prior life."_ Even the man who lost the most money on the bet refused to write him off.

Which leaves the question the title promised. How does the kid who couldn't stand to owe his mother fifty bucks grow up to take a $1.7 billion exit while the people who built WeWork lost their stock?

He doesn't experience the two as contradictory.

Sevens are intensely loyal to specific people. They are categorically incapable of being loyal to abstractions. The fifty bucks was a debt to his mother — to the only stable adult in the room. The stock options were a debt to a system. The S-1 was an abstraction. The board was an abstraction. Seven thousand employees, in aggregate, were an abstraction. His mother, alone in a Chevy in Indianapolis with two kids and an $1,800-a-month salary, was a person.

The boy who could not bear to owe a person is the same man who could not see what he owed a system. He is barefoot because his feet are still moving — and the kibbutz he keeps trying to build is the one he has not yet been thrown out of.
