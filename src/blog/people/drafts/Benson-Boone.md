## What is Benson Boone's Personality Type?

### Benson Boone is an Enneagram Type 7

Enneagram Type 7s are called "The Enthusiasts" for a reason. Their core drive is the pursuit of experience and the avoidance of pain and limitation. They are the people who always seem to have three projects running, five ideas brewing, and zero interest in slowing down.

The childhood wound of a Type 7 typically involves a sense that their needs wouldn't be fully met — that they had to create their own joy. This doesn't mean neglect. It means they learned early that the world's offerings were finite, and the way to survive emotionally was to pursue everything with intensity before it disappeared.

Sound familiar? Boone grew up as the only boy among four sisters in a tight-knit Mormon household in Monroe, Washington. He learned piano not through formal lessons but by eavesdropping on his sisters' practice. He taught himself guitar, drums, and bass. He joined the diving team. He played tennis. He started flipping off couches at age three.

Nobody told him to do any of this. He just couldn't stop himself.

## Benson Boone's Upbringing

Benson Boone was born on June 25, 2002, in Monroe, Washington — a small town about an hour northeast of Seattle. His parents, Kerry and Nate Boone, raised him alongside four sisters: Kaylee, Natalee, Emma, and Claire.

The household was shaped by two forces: the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints and an unusually deep emotional openness. Growing up "surrounded by girls 24/7," Boone says, made him "more emotional and aware of other people's emotions and my emotions." He credits his sisters for shaping his ability to write emotionally vulnerable songs. Claire sometimes joins him onstage during performances.

His father introduced him to physical fearlessness early. By three, Benson was flipping off furniture. By high school, he was one of Monroe High's first competitive divers — placing sixth at the 2020 state championships despite lacking a dedicated diving coach at his school. He sought outside coaching on his own.

Music came almost by accident. He didn't discover he could sing until his junior year of high school, when a friend asked him to perform at a battle of the bands. Then he attended a Jon Bellion concert that changed something. He described watching Bellion perform and thinking: that's what I want to do.

The religious dimension matters. Boone was raised in the LDS Church but grew increasingly "confused and frustrated" by the gap between what others described feeling — personal spiritual revelations — and what he experienced. He was afraid to voice those doubts for years. Eventually he left the church, though he kept some values (like sobriety) as personal choices rather than religious mandates. His parents supported his independence. "Whatever he comes to and feels is right is what they want for him," he has said.

## Rise to Fame

Boone's rise is a masterclass in Type 7 impulsiveness meeting real talent.

In early 2021, he auditioned for American Idol Season 19. Katy Perry told him, "I see you winning American Idol." He got three yeses and a standing ovation. Then he did a backflip out of the audition room — already the most Benson Boone thing possible.

He advanced to the Top 24. And then he quit.

Why? Because he didn't want to be "the American Idol guy." He wanted to build something on his own terms. "I want to be Benson Boone because I write smash hits and they love my music," he said. A classic Type 7 move: walking away from a sure thing because it felt like a cage.

What happened next was better. Dan Reynolds of Imagine Dragons discovered Boone's singing videos on TikTok, flew him to Las Vegas, and spent three days teaching him the fundamentals of songwriting. Boone signed to Reynolds' label, Night Street Records, under Warner Records.

His debut single "Ghost Town" — written in that three-day mentorship period — has amassed over 350 million Spotify streams. "In The Stars" hit Platinum. Then came "Beautiful Things" in January 2024, which peaked at #2 on the Billboard Hot 100, hit #1 in 19 countries, and won the IFPI Global Single Award with 2.11 billion streams — the biggest-selling global single of the year.

His debut album *Fireworks & Rollerblades* debuted at #6 on the Billboard 200. His sophomore album *American Heart*, released in June 2025, debuted at #2. His first all-arena world tour spans 50 shows across North America and Europe.

All of this happened in roughly four years. For a Type 7, there is no speed limit.

## Benson Boone's Personality Quirks and Habits

What makes Boone fascinating isn't just the acrobatics and chart numbers. It's the patterns beneath them.

### The Adrenaline Addiction

This is the most visible layer of Boone's personality. He doesn't just perform — he launches himself. At the 2025 Grammy Awards, he sang "Beautiful Things" while having his tuxedo ripped off to reveal a baby blue jumpsuit, walked atop a grand piano, and then backflipped off it. At one Salt Lake City show, he had done five flips by his seventh song.

Off stage, he cliff jumps. He once leaped 629 feet from Auckland's Sky Tower. He was a competitive diver. He rollerblades. For <a href="/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-7">Type 7s</a>, physical intensity isn't showing off — it's how they feel alive. When the body is in motion, the mind can't spiral.

### The Sobriety Paradox

Here's where it gets interesting. Boone has never tried drugs or alcohol — and says he never will. Not because of his Mormon upbringing (he's left the church), but because he knows himself.

"I have an addictive personality," he told Rolling Stone. When the interviewer asked how he could know that without ever trying substances, Boone replied: "Dude, candy."

This is deeply self-aware for a 23-year-old. Type 7s are wired to pursue pleasure with intensity. Most discover their addictive tendencies the hard way. Boone recognized the pattern preemptively and drew a hard line. The discipline it takes to maintain total sobriety as a touring pop star — surrounded by industry excess — is remarkable.

### The Ego Check System

Fame has a way of distorting self-perception. Boone knows this and has built a system to fight it. Every two weeks, he does what he calls "ego checks" with a trusted collaborator named Burns. The purpose: to remind himself he is "not the king of the world."

He once snapped at his mother during a phone call and felt so awful that he called her back twenty minutes later to apologize. He cited this as a wake-up call. "It is incredibly easy to get carried away with my ego," he has admitted — a level of candor that most people in his position would never risk.

### The Body Image Battle

Boone's vulnerability about his appearance reveals the darker edge of Type 7 energy. "Because I'm so incredibly hard on myself, and when I start to think about my appearance too much, it just kills me because there's always something more I could do," he told Billboard. "There's always a hairstyle or, like, my arms could be bigger. My shoulders could be bigger. It's never-ending."

He has been explicit that he does not want to be a "sex icon" and doesn't want to "rely on my physical form to be the primary driver of my shows." This tension — between a body he uses as a performance instrument and a mind that relentlessly critiques it — shows the [Type 7 stress pattern](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-types-in-stress) in action. Under pressure, 7s move toward [Type 1](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-1) behavior: perfectionism, self-criticism, and the feeling that nothing is ever good enough.

## Benson Boone's Major Accomplishments

### "Beautiful Things" — A Global Phenomenon

It's one thing to write a hit. It's another to write the biggest-selling global single of an entire year. "Beautiful Things" earned the IFPI Global Single Award for 2024 with 2.11 billion equivalent streams worldwide. It charted at #1 in 19 countries and #2 on the Billboard Hot 100.

What makes this significant through an Enneagram lens is the song's emotional content. "Beautiful Things" is about the terror of losing what you love — a deeply vulnerable topic for a Type 7, who typically avoids confronting loss. The lyrics suggest a 7w6 dynamic: the enthusiasm and joy of the 7 meeting the anxiety and attachment of the 6-wing. Boone wasn't just performing happiness. He was sitting in the fear of its absence.

"I've tapped into how I'll write for the rest of my life," he told Grammy.com afterward. He was proud of "baby me" for writing something emotionally complex even with technical imperfection. This self-compassion — paired with creative ambition — is Type 7 integration at its best.

### The Grammy Performance

Boone's 2025 Grammy performance became a cultural moment. The piano backflip, the outfit reveal, the standing ovation — it was a physical expression of everything his personality contains. Some loved it. The Washington Post ranked it 13th out of 15. Opinions varied.

But here's what matters: Boone fully committed. Type 7s don't do things halfway. Whether the reviews were glowing or brutal, the performance was unmistakably him.

## Controversy and Criticism

### The Authenticity Question

The most persistent criticism of Benson Boone is that he's inauthentic — a "manufactured" pop star doing impressions of <a href="/personality-analysis/Harry-Styles">Harry Styles</a> and Freddie Mercury without a unique artistic identity. Pitchfork gave his *American Heart* album a 3.7/10, with critic Jeremy D. Larson writing that "Benson Boone's flair can't make up for the fact that his songs aren't very good and that he lacks a unique point of view."

The online backlash has been compared to what Nickelback experienced — sudden inescapability meeting baffled cultural derision. TikTok and X have been especially brutal, with one attempted fan campaign ("Mystical Magical" for Coachella) backfiring into a wave of "I hate Benson Boone" posts.

### How He Handles It

This is where the Type 7 pattern becomes clear. Rather than spiraling into defensiveness or retreating entirely, Boone has mostly leaned into humor. He's called out people who "hate him without a good reason" in TikToks, but with a self-deprecating edge rather than bitterness. He plays into the memes. He's pulled back to what he calls a "healthy amount" of social media rather than abandoning it entirely.

Type 7s reframe. It's their superpower and their blind spot. The strength is resilience — they genuinely bounce back faster than almost any other type. The risk is that they can reframe too quickly, skipping over legitimate pain that needs processing. Whether Boone has truly processed the criticism or simply outrun it remains to be seen.

## Benson Boone's Legacy and Current Work

At 23, Boone sits in a fascinating cultural position. His *American Heart* album draws on Bruce Springsteen and Americana, suggesting he's searching for a more grounded artistic identity. His first all-arena world tour (August 2025 through March 2026) proves his commercial power is real. He's single following his September 2025 breakup with actress Maggie Thurmon.

The question hanging over Benson Boone isn't whether he's talented — his voice, his energy, and his songwriting instincts are obvious. It's whether he'll slow down long enough to find the depth that the critics say is missing. Type 7s grow when they stop running and let the uncomfortable feelings catch up. Boone has shown glimpses of this — "Beautiful Things" is a song about sitting in fear, not escaping it. His body image honesty, his ego checks, his sobriety — these are not the behaviors of someone who's purely avoiding pain.

They're the behaviors of a Type 7 who's starting to learn that the most thrilling experience of all might be standing still.
