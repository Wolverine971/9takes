> "All I wanted was to hold on and live."
> — Gal Gadot, Variety, December 2024

<p class="firstLetter">In February 2024, Gal Gadot was eight months pregnant with her fourth daughter when an MRI showed a massive blood clot in her brain. For weeks she had been bedridden by headaches no one could explain and no one could fix. Three in 100,000 pregnant women in their thirties receive that diagnosis (cerebral venous thrombosis), and she was one of them. She was wheeled into emergency surgery within hours. Her daughter was delivered in the same blur of fluorescent lights and dread. They named her Ori. In Hebrew, it means "my light."</p>

That isn't the Gal Gadot the world knows. The Gal Gadot the world knows ran ten kilometers a day as a combat trainer in the Israel Defense Forces, won Miss Israel at eighteen, and became the first actress in a decade to anchor a global superhero franchise — Wonder Woman grossed $822 million on a $149 million budget. The Gal Gadot the world knows answers "what's it like to be Wonder Woman?" with a smile that closes the press junket on schedule. The body in the MRI machine — exhausted, breakable, unable to hold its own pregnancy — was a different person.

The split between those two women is the entire shape of her psychology.

For most of her life, Gadot has done what was assigned. Dance lessons because her parents wanted them. University because her parents required it. The army because every Israeli teenager goes. Modeling because the agencies came calling. Acting because someone said she might be good at it. Each role was rehearsed for, executed cleanly, and moved past. The system worked. Until it didn't.

Then a viral celebrity video became the most-mocked moment of pandemic 2020. Then a Cleopatra biopic she'd attached herself to detonated before a frame was shot. Then her body almost killed her. Then a $270 million Disney movie collapsed at the box office and she pinned the failure on geopolitics. The woman who had executed every assignment with relentless polish was suddenly stuck inside moments that did not respond to polish at all.

That gap — between the role she was rehearsed for and the moments that won't accept a performance — is where Gal Gadot's psychological story actually lives.

## What is Gal Gadot's Personality Type?

### Gal Gadot is an Enneagram Type 3

Type Threes organize their lives around achievement and image. They learn early that love, attention, and safety arrive most reliably when they are succeeding visibly at something. The performance becomes the self. They are often the most disciplined people in any room — not because they were born harder-working than everyone else, but because being seen as capable is a non-negotiable internal requirement. The fear underneath the drive is simple and rarely spoken aloud: that without the performance, there might not be a person there.

Gadot's life carries that pattern in nearly every chapter:

- A childhood structured around discipline (dance, school, modesty) rather than imagination
- Parents who told her acting "wasn't an option" until she had a degree in hand
- A military performance record strong enough to become a casting credential — Justin Lin specifically wanted her IDF weapons training for the Fast & Furious franchise
- A career built on stepping into roles other actresses passed on, surviving by being the most workable person in the room
- A public persona organized around the photogenic, the competent, and the cheerful — Vanity Fair's wholesome morning prayer routine, the Hot Ones interview where she cried through the wings without breaking the bit
- A pattern of confessing softness only after the polish has done its job, never before

Within the Type 3 family, Gadot reads as a 3w2 — the warmer, more service-oriented wing — rather than a 3w4. The 3w2 is the Three who tears up on Hot Ones about her kids and organizes a celebrity sing-along to comfort the world; Beyoncé, by contrast, is the colder, more legacy-driven 3w4. Under stress, Threes drop into Type 9: passive, withdrawn, unable to get out of bed. In growth, they reach for Type 6: finally letting other people carry weight instead of performing for them. Both arrows are visible in Gadot's last five years.

The clearest piece of evidence isn't a single accomplishment. It's the rhythm. Every public chapter of her life looks like a series of executed assignments. The places where the rhythm breaks — Imagine, Cleopatra, the brain clot, Snow White — are the only places where you can see the woman behind the assignments.

## How a Sheltered Israeli Kid Learned to Perform

Gadot grew up in Rosh HaAyin, a small town outside Tel Aviv, in what she has repeatedly described as a "very sheltered" home. "There was no TV-watching," she told Vanity Fair. "It was always 'Take a ball and go play.'" The parents who raised her — a teacher and an engineer — built a household around discipline, family, modesty, and physical activity. Imagination got less airtime than competence.

She danced for twelve years. Ballet, hip-hop, modern, jazz. "I thought that I wanted to be a choreographer," she told Vanity Fair. That detail is worth pausing on. Twelve years is not a hobby. Twelve years is the length of an entire childhood spent learning to hit marks, hold a pose under lights, and move on the count someone else is calling. Threes are not always natural performers. Many of them are rehearsed ones.

Then there was the family rule. "I came from a home where being an actress wasn't an option," she told the same magazine. "My parents told me, 'You need to graduate university and get a degree.'" The rule was absolute. Gadot would later enroll in law and international studies at the Interdisciplinary Center in Herzliya — not because she wanted to be a lawyer, but because that was the route a serious girl was supposed to walk.

In a Glamour interview, she said something quietly revealing about her childhood: "I loved the attention. But I never connected all the dots that maybe I should be an actress."

Read that line again. She knew, at some level, that she liked being looked at. She had spent twelve years learning how to be looked at. And still, she did not give herself permission to want a job that would put her in front of cameras for a living. The attention was acceptable as a side effect of doing other things correctly. Wanting it directly — that wasn't on the menu her family had laid out.

This is one of the most recognizable Type 3 fingerprints. The desire is real, but the desire has to disguise itself as discipline. You don't say "I want to be famous." You say "I'm going to win this competition. I'm going to graduate this program. I'm going to serve in this unit." And then, somewhere along the way, you've spent the entire structure of your young adulthood positioned in front of judges.

## The IDF Years: Where Discipline Became Her Identity

At eighteen, Gadot did what every Israeli does — except she did it harder. She enlisted as a combat trainer for the Israel Defense Forces, a role that required her to teach gymnastics, weapons handling, and physical conditioning to incoming recruits. She has talked openly about the daily ten-kilometer runs across her two years of service. Combat training is not a desk job. The body becomes the credential.

She has credited the army repeatedly with what came next. "I think it really helped me to develop my voice and to know who I am," she told Fox News. The IDF, she has said, gave her the "discipline" and "no-nonsense" attitude that would define her in casting rooms a decade later.

A decade later, that résumé closed her first real movie deal. When Justin Lin was casting Fast & Furious, he wanted Gisele Yashar — the cool, competent woman who knows her way around a weapon — to feel real. Gadot had auditioned for Camille in Quantum of Solace and lost the part to Olga Kurylenko. The same casting director who watched her lose passed her name to Universal three months later. Lin learned she had been a combat trainer. He told her he wanted to use her actual weapons knowledge in the role. She had the part.

The IDF years did two things at once. They built the body, and they built the credential. For a Type 3, this is the dream loop: a hard-won skill that doubles as social capital. Substance _becomes_ image.

But the loop has a cost. When the body is the credential, the body has to keep delivering. Gadot once told W magazine that during her early years she "felt sometimes too serious and smart to be an actress." When the casting director finally got her in the room for Quantum of Solace, she nearly walked away. "Listen, it's all in English," she told her agent. "I'm not an actress. I'm not going to go." She went anyway, out of respect.

## The Husband Who Sold the Hotel

In 2006, between the army and the movie career, Gadot met Jaron Varsano at a chakra retreat in the Israeli desert. He was a hotelier ten years older. On their second date, he told her he intended to marry her within two years. He did. They wed in 2008.

Seven years later, when Hollywood opened, Varsano sold the boutique hotel he and his brother had built in Tel Aviv — to Roman Abramovich, for a reported $26 million — and followed her to Los Angeles. In 2019 they founded Pilot Wave Motion Pictures together. He produces; she stars. By 2024 they had four daughters: Alma (born 2011), Maya (2017), Daniella (2021), and Ori.

This is the part of her biography that almost never gets unpacked, and it should be. A Type 3 marriage is rarely about losing the self in another person. It is more often about finding a partner who will support, protect, and reinforce the performance. Varsano did not just back her career. He liquidated the central asset of his own and built a production house designed to keep her at the center of every project. He is the one person in the world who has seen all four pregnancies, the brain clot, and the Imagine video and is still treating her like the bet of his life.

For most Threes, that kind of partnership is a quiet superpower. It is also a quiet trap. When the person closest to you has tied his identity to yours, the pressure to keep performing isn't external anymore. It is the shape of the marriage.

## Why Gal Gadot Almost Quit Before Wonder Woman

Most of the world thinks Gal Gadot's career started with Wonder Woman. It didn't. It started with a near-miss, then a stitched-together supporting part, then six years of being "the girl from Fast & Furious" who got killed off in the fourth one and brought back in flashbacks. Gisele Yashar appears in Fast & Furious, Fast Five, and Fast & Furious 6. She was killed off in 2013. Gadot was thirty.

For a Type 3, that decade was a slow-motion identity crisis dressed in red carpet appearances. She had crossed an ocean, learned a language, and was still, by Hollywood standards, an upgrade option for action movies. "When I first came to Los Angeles, I couldn't read people," she told Marie Claire in 2017. The discipline that worked in Tel Aviv didn't translate. The IDF didn't teach her LA's coded smiles.

Then Wonder Woman happened. And it didn't happen by accident.

Zack Snyder cast her without telling Warner Bros. who she was. The studio panicked. She was unknown in America. She had been killed off in a franchise that explicitly sold itself on bigger names. She was pregnant during the reshoots. She drilled for nine months in horseback riding, sword work, and capoeira before the cameras rolled. By the time the 2017 film opened, the question wasn't whether she could play Wonder Woman. The question was whether anyone could imagine the part being played by someone else.

It became one of the highest-grossing solo superhero films ever made by a female lead. Gadot, at thirty-two, was suddenly the most recognizable Israeli on the planet.

This is the Type 3 payoff in its purest form. A woman who had been rehearsed — relentlessly, by every institution she had ever been part of — to step into roles that other people defined, finally walked into the role the entire world had been waiting for someone to step into. The preparation matched the moment.

Threes live for that match. They will spend years preparing for it. They will spend years denying that they're preparing for it.

## Wonder Woman 1984: The First Crack in the Polish

Three years later, the sequel arrived. Wonder Woman 1984 opened on Christmas Day 2020, simultaneously in pandemic-shuttered theaters and on HBO Max. The first film had landed at 93% on Rotten Tomatoes and stuck. The sequel opened around 88% and slid to roughly 60%, becoming the first film ever to lose its Certified Fresh status mid-release. Global box office came in around $169 million on a reported $200 million budget — a fraction of the 2017 film's $822 million.

The reasons were not all hers. Pandemic theatricals were a mess. The script asked her to deliver a CGI invisible-jet sequence with a straight face. But the public read it, fairly or not, as the first time her trained performance had failed inside the role she was famous for. The cape and the lasso were no longer enough. The frame had cracked.

A Type 3 absorbs that information differently than another type. Where a 4 might rage at the script and a 6 might catastrophize the franchise, a 3 takes the data and doubles down on the next assignment. Heart of Stone was already in development at Pilot Wave by the time WW84 underperformed. The work doesn't pause to grieve. The work moves to the next role.

## The "Imagine" Video and the Limits of Polish

Earlier that same year, on March 18, 2020, Gadot saw a video of an Italian man playing "Imagine" on his trumpet from a balcony during the early weeks of pandemic lockdown. She was moved. She wanted to do something. So she did what she always does: she organized.

She gathered roughly two dozen famous friends — Will Ferrell, Natalie Portman, Sia, Mark Ruffalo, Kristen Wiig, Amy Adams, Jamie Dornan, Pedro Pascal — and asked each of them to sing a line of the John Lennon song into their phones. She edited the clips together. She released the video on Instagram. She framed it as a gesture of unity in a frightening time.

The internet detonated. The New York Times called it "an empty and profoundly awkward gesture." Twitter spent days dragging the rich, beautifully lit, lyrically incoherent collage of celebrities asking the world to "imagine no possessions" while small businesses collapsed and ICUs filled. Multiple participants would later quietly distance themselves from it. It is now studied as a kind of Patient Zero of pandemic-era celebrity tone-deafness.

What is interesting, psychologically, is not that the video was bad. It is _why_ Gadot thought it would work.

Look at the variables she controlled. The cast was famous. The song was iconic. The lighting was kind. The intention was sincere. By every single metric a Type 3 uses to plan a performance, the package was right.

What she could not optimize was the moment. The moment didn't want to be soothed by celebrities. The moment wanted information about ICU beds and rent freezes. The moment wanted silence from anyone too rich to be afraid in the same way the rest of the audience was afraid. She missed it because Threes tend to assume that if the performance is technically correct, the audience will respond correctly.

Two years later, in InStyle, Gadot finally said it out loud: "Sometimes you don't hit the bullseye. The video was in poor taste. It was premature. It wasn't the right timing, and it wasn't the right thing. It had pure intentions, but sometimes you don't hit the bullseye."

The frame in that apology is still archery. The frame is still performance. It is the most honest she could be without abandoning the language of performance entirely. A Type 3 doesn't crash from laziness or arrogance. A Type 3 crashes from over-confidence in the polish.

## The Cleopatra Project Nobody Wanted Her to Make

In October 2020, while the Imagine video was still being mocked, Paramount announced Gadot would star in a new Cleopatra biopic, produced by Pilot Wave with Patty Jenkins directing. The internet detonated again — this time on the question of whether an Israeli actress should be playing the most iconic North African ruler in history.

Gadot's defense was a Type 3 defense. "If you want to be true to the facts," she told BBC Arabic, "then Cleopatra was Macedonian. We were looking for a Macedonian actress that could fit Cleopatra. She wasn't there." The framing was technical. Casting was a problem of credentials. The right woman wasn't available. She — credentialed, trained, willing — was.

What she could not absorb in real time was that the audience wasn't asking a casting question. The audience was asking an identity question — about who gets to be the face of which story, in what era, for what political reason. By the time Patty Jenkins quit the project in 2023 and Paramount handed it to a different director, the fight had already become bigger than the movie. As of early 2026, Cleopatra remains in development limbo with no announced production date.

The Cleopatra fight matters because it is the missing middle chapter between Imagine and Snow White. It is the first time the polish failed not because of the moment, but because of who she was. Threes can rehearse their way past most things. They cannot rehearse their way past the public deciding they are the wrong body for the role.

## Red Notice and the Years Between

In November 2021, Gadot starred opposite Dwayne Johnson and Ryan Reynolds in Red Notice, a Netflix heist movie in which she played a thief called The Bishop. Each of the three leads was reportedly paid roughly $20 million. Critics were tepid — The Hollywood Reporter called it "empty-calorie entertainment," and Rotten Tomatoes scores hovered in the thirties.

Audiences did not care. The film logged 328.8 million viewing hours in its first 28 days, the most-watched movie release in Netflix's history at that point. For a Three, this is the canonical reframe. The reviews don't matter when the numbers are unanswerable. What you cannot say is true, the data can say for you.

Then came 2022, a quiet year. Then 2023 brought Heart of Stone for Netflix, and four months after that, October 7.

## October 7 and the Year Her Identity Stopped Being Optional

In November 2023, Gadot helped facilitate a Los Angeles screening of "Bearing Witness to the October 7th Massacre," the IDF-compiled raw-footage record of the Hamas attacks. The screening, held at the Museum of Tolerance, drew clashing demonstrators and required LAPD intervention. She was not on site. The reputational damage was. For the first time in her career, her Israeli identity became inseparable from how the next role would be received.

This is the variable a Type 3 cannot control. You can rehearse the press tour. You can rehearse the answer. You cannot rehearse who you are, where you were born, and which uniform you once wore. The only thing the polish can do, when the audience has already decided, is keep showing up.

She kept showing up. She has said in interviews that she was "shocked by the amount of hate" but felt she "could not be silent." Three months later, in February 2024, the headaches started.

## What Almost Killed Gal Gadot in 2024

She was eight months into her fourth pregnancy. She has described the pain as so severe she could not get out of bed. For weeks no one could find a cause. Then an MRI showed cerebral venous thrombosis — a massive blood clot in the venous system of her brain. CVT is rare even among pregnant women in their thirties, and it is fatal in a meaningful percentage of cases when undiagnosed.

She has talked about the moment she understood what was happening. She told Jaron — the same man who had sold his hotel for her career — that when their daughter was born, the baby would be the light at the end of the tunnel. They had not yet picked a name. The Hebrew word for "my light" is _Ori_. She joined Alma, Maya, and Daniella on February 5, 2024.

The neurosurgeons at Cedars-Sinai performed the procedure. Gadot survived. In her own words, posted on Instagram several months later: "In the midst of a difficult year, all I wanted was to hold on and live."

Notice the verb. _Hold on._ The woman who had spent her entire life _executing_ — drilling for sword fights, recording four-language tongue twisters on a chicken-wing show — could only hold on. The performance did not save her. The doctors did. The body cooperated, or didn't. There was no version of this where the IDF résumé was the relevant skill set.

The most revealing thing about how she has talked about that experience is what comes immediately after the gratitude. In the same statement, she pivoted to "the importance of staying attuned to our bodies and listening to the signs." She turned the near-death experience into a public service announcement.

That is not cynicism. The crisis is real. The fear is real. But the public version of the crisis must serve someone. It must instruct. It must produce. The trauma cannot just sit there in its own skin.

She named her daughter "my light." She made the survival mean something. She kept going.

## Why Gal Gadot Still Can't Stop Working

By the time the brain clot story became public in late 2024, Gadot was already on the press circuit for Snow White, the live-action Disney remake in which she played the Evil Queen opposite Rachel Zegler. The film's budget was a reported $270 million. It grossed roughly $205 million worldwide. It was, by any measure, a flop.

The critics did not split the failure across the cast equally. Owen Gleiberman in Variety wrote that Gadot glared "like the world's most furious dominatrix" and wished her vocal performance were "more operatic and less Mata Hari." Other reviewers said her Evil Queen "waffled between campy and scary, never properly executing either." The pattern critics named across WW84, Red Notice, Heart of Stone, and Snow White was the same: an iconic physical presence with a limited tonal range. Perfect for the stoic-warrior register of the 2017 Wonder Woman. Exposed in roles that asked for camp, menace, or comic timing.

That critique IS the Type 3 story. The polish works in the role it was rehearsed for. It strains in the role it wasn't.

Gadot, in subsequent interviews, framed Snow White's underperformance as a casualty of the post–October 7 political environment. "The pressure to speak out against Israel," she said, "had an effect on the box office." The framing is worth examining without taking sides on the underlying politics. Even if every word of her diagnosis is true, notice what it does for her psychologically. The flop is not about her performance. It is not about the script. It is not about the strange middle-distance the movie occupied between fairy tale and IP machine. The flop is about something external — something she could not have controlled.

She would later walk this back, telling Deadline the political pressure "wasn't solely" responsible. But the instinct showed itself first. The instinct was to put a lid on the possibility that the performance had simply not landed.

This is the part of her psychology that is hardest to admire and hardest to look away from. A woman who has nearly died, who has been mocked by half the internet for a video she sang into her bathroom mirror, who has watched her own face become the focal point of culture wars she did not pick, is still — _still_ — wired to protect the performance. Even from herself.

The work doesn't stop. Heart of Stone landed on Netflix in 2023, where she played a covert operative who could be read as a slightly more humorless Wonder Woman. The reviews were tepid. The viewership was enormous. She has Cleopatra in development. She has another Wonder Woman in conversation. She has Pilot Wave. She has four daughters. She has a husband who sold a hotel for this.

When Jimmy Fallon asked her, in early 2025, what she had taken from the medical scare, she did what she always does. She made the answer useful. "Listen to your body," she said. "Get checked." Then she smiled and the band cued and the segment moved on.

## The Performance That Won't Stop

Threes can change. Many do. The growth path for Type 3 is to learn that you are loved without the performance — that the people who matter were never grading the routine. Some Threes get there. Some never do.

Where Gal Gadot is on that path is genuinely unclear. The woman who recites _Modeh Ani_ — the Hebrew morning prayer of "thank you, thank you, thank you" — every morning sounds like a woman who has touched something deeper than the next press junket. The woman who, two months after a craniotomy, was on Jimmy Fallon making sure her near-death moment became a teachable moment, sounds like a woman who has not yet stopped negotiating with the audience.

It is probably both. Threes contain both. The Wonder Woman who closed the press junket on schedule, the bathroom-mirror "Imagine" auteur, and the patient who whispered to her husband about the light at the end of the tunnel are the same person.

She has spent thirty-five years rehearsing her body to hit marks. She has spent the last five years learning, very slowly, that some moments do not have marks to hit.

Ori means "my light." Light, in Hebrew, is something you can give. It is also something you can receive. Gadot has spent her career assuming she has to give it. The question her own daughter's name asks her, every time she says it out loud, is whether she will ever let herself receive it.

She probably won't stop performing. There is no easy way to separate the woman from the rehearsal anymore. But somewhere, in a hospital room in February 2024, between an MRI and a surgery, she said the most un-Type-3 sentence of her life. _All I wanted was to hold on and live._

There was no audience for that line. There was nothing to optimize. There was only a body, and a pulse, and a husband, and a daughter who hadn't been born yet. For one moment, the performance stopped. And the woman was still there.

That is the answer to the Type 3 question. That is also the part she is most likely to forget.
