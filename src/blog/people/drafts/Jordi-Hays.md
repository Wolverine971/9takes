> ["We don't take ourselves very seriously, but we take the work super seriously."](https://jacksondahl.com/dialectic/tbpn)
>
> - Jordi Hays, *Dialectic interview*, 2025

<p class="firstLetter">Jordi Hays entered an October 2025 <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2025/10/11/technology/tbpn-silicon-valley.html">New York Times profile</a> waving a red flag. "The timeline is in turmoil!" he shouted as he ran toward the cameras. He wore the fitted gray suit and polished shoes behind the article's description of his style as Patrick Bateman-core. Then the broadcast began, and the prop became a signal: the internet had produced another crisis, and TBPN was open for business.</p>

The scene makes Hays easy to caricature. There is the gong, the bronze horse, the smashburger breakfast, and the cultivated confidence of a founder playing a news anchor. Yet reporter Kevin Roose also saw the machinery around the entrance. Hays and co-host [John Coogan](/personality-analysis/john-coogan) were building a live show several hours a day with a small team and an unforgiving clock.

Hays explained the method in a long 2025 [Dialectic interview](https://jacksondahl.com/dialectic/tbpn). The hosts worked out early, prepared through the morning, went live, and reviewed the result afterward. Coogan estimated that a three-hour program took about six hours of preparation. Hays described the broadcast as an obligation. A countdown reached zero whether or not either host felt inspired.

The red flag grabs attention. The process explains what Hays does with it. He turns attention into a product, gives the product a public deadline, reads the reaction, and tries again the next day. The showmanship matters because it makes the work memorable. The scoreboard matters because it tells him what to improve.

## What is Jordi Hays's personality type?

### Jordi Hays is an Enneagram Type 3

The public Jordi Hays pattern fits the Achiever, [Enneagram Type 3](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-3). He turns attention into measurable work: a daily broadcast, recurring bits, audience feedback, post-show reviews, and predictable sponsorships. The case rests on adaptation and proof. It does not require pretending to know his private motives.

Type 3 is often flattened into ambition. Ambition is common. The more distinctive Three pattern is identification with the effective version of oneself. Threes notice what a field rewards, develop the form that can win there, and keep adjusting until the result becomes visible.

Hays has done that across startups and media. At Capital, the bright brand helped distribute a serious financial product. At TBPN, entertainment makes dense technology and business news easier to enter. In both cases, presentation is part of the operating system.

The interpretation remains speculative. Hays has not publicly identified his Enneagram type, and public interviews reveal a work persona rather than a complete person. The useful question is whether Type 3 explains repeated choices better than its alternatives.

## The newspaper came before the studio

Hays told *Dialectic* that he had read a newspaper every morning for as long as he could read. His father would wake first and leave it on the coffee table. In middle and high school, Hays refreshed TechCrunch throughout the day. He said he could not imagine a future in which he woke up uninterested in what was happening.

This history changes the meaning of TBPN. The show did not manufacture an interest in technology news after Hays discovered that media could be lucrative. It gave a childhood habit a format, a co-host, and a business model.

Business arrived early too. At 12, Hays used $500 from an aunt to start a skateboard company called J Man. He told *Dialectic* that he sold skateboards around town. The important detail is not that a seventh grader had already become a miniature chief executive. It is that an interest moved quickly into an object another person could buy.

The old version of this article built a mythology around those facts. It invented his parents' occupations, imagined the household's definition of success, and assigned a motive to a year in Shanghai. None of that is needed. A newspaper on the coffee table, a repeatedly refreshed technology site, and a skateboard with his chosen name already show the pattern: information becomes identity through a product.

## Capital made attention part of the product

Before TBPN, Hays and Sarah Chase co-founded Party Round, a fundraising platform for startups that later became Capital. Its marketing belonged to the exuberant startup culture of 2021: internet jokes, designed stunts, and a name that made financing sound social.

The business became more sober as the market changed. In an October 2022 [TechCrunch interview](https://techcrunch.com/2022/10/04/party-rounds-rebrand-is-banking-on-founder-bank-accounts/), Hays said entertainment could help but that the long-term priority was building the best software and products for founders. The rebrand to Capital accompanied a move toward banking and financial tools.

Packy McCormick's [Not Boring profile](https://www.notboring.co/p/capital-and-taste) supplied the clearest account of the strategy. Its conflict matters: the piece was sponsored, and McCormick disclosed that his fund had invested in the company twice. Used with that warning, it still contains direct interviews and contemporaneous documents.

McCormick wrote that Hays had described a media business monetized through financial services well before the full product appeared. Party Round concentrated its early attention on tech Twitter, then used that audience to distribute fundraising tools. McCormick's summary was concise: "While Party Round was fun, Jordi was dead serious."

This is strong Type 3 evidence because the brand was neither decoration nor accidental virality. Hays had connected an image to an outcome. When conditions changed, the image changed too. The effectiveness of the presentation mattered more than loyalty to its first form.

Capital joined Rho in 2023. In his announcement, Hays thanked Chase and the broader team. He then carried the media thesis into a product where attention itself, rather than banking, could generate the revenue.

## Why Jordi Hays reads as an Enneagram Type 3

Hays's strongest Type 3 evidence appears in the feedback loops he builds.

In a 2026 LinkedIn post, he described a brand as the average feeling created by everything a company does. TBPN's recurring bits were intentional. When a joke produced laughter in the room or a clip that people texted to one another, that response became information. The hosts could repeat, refine, or retire the element.

That is adaptation in public. The image is designed, tested, and changed while the underlying product continues. A Three does not merely want applause. At their most effective, Threes are unusually good at detecting which behavior made the applause possible and turning it into a repeatable system.

Hays applies the same logic to business. In a December 2025 [Axios interview](https://www.axios.com/2025/12/05/tbpn-ads-jordi-hays-john-coogan), he said he wanted revenue predictability. TBPN sold annual, category-exclusive sponsorships, which he compared to the integrated commercial structure of a Formula One team. Axios reported that the company was profitable without outside investors and expected roughly $5 million in 2025 revenue.

His focus is just as important as the numbers. Hays told *Dialectic* that TBPN did not want to attach a venture fund or another large side business to the show. A media company distracted by a second project would compete against someone focused only on media.

That choice separates Hays from the generic portrait of a restless serial founder. He has started several businesses, but his stated ideal is concentration. Once a format can compound, he wants the daily repetitions to make it better.

## TBPN makes spontaneity on a schedule

TBPN began as Technology Brothers, a show Hays and Coogan started quickly after discussing a collaboration. By 2025 it had become a daily live program with a newsletter, guests, sponsors, an expanding set, and a four-letter identity that could stretch beyond the original joke.

Hays is the more visibly performative host. Coogan brings his own founder history, research process, and editorial judgment. In the *Dialectic* interview, their answers showed how interdependent the product had become. The morning conversation generated stories. Coogan wrote the newsletter with Hays as its imagined reader. The show then exposed their preparation to immediate audience judgment.

Before the countdown, Hays has to turn an unformed mass of overnight news into a show worth other people's morning. Going live removes the option to wait for a perfect mood. That pressure can look compulsive from the outside. It is also a commitment to a co-host, a team, advertisers, guests, and viewers whose day has been organized around the broadcast.

Hays's ideas about taste support a tentative 3w4 reading. He told *Dialectic* that "technology is too inspired by technology." The better form of creative theft, in his view, reaches outside the immediate industry. TBPN borrowed from sports broadcasting, cinematic newsrooms, and older television aesthetics instead of copying another tech podcast.

The distinction is important. Originality does not mean creating without influences. Hays treats it as choosing references competitors are not watching, then combining them into a recognizable product. His account of taste is practical: find better inputs, combine them with intention, and make the result recognizable.

## Why did OpenAI acquire TBPN?

On April 2, 2026, [OpenAI announced that it had acquired TBPN](https://openai.com/index/openai-acquires-tbpn/). The company said the show would retain independence over programming, guests, and editorial decisions. Hays and Coogan would report to Chris Lehane inside OpenAI's strategy organization.

OpenAI presented the deal as a way to combine TBPN's community and storytelling with its own work. Hays described the move as a transition from commentary toward impact in how artificial intelligence is distributed and understood. Sam Altman called TBPN his favorite tech show and publicly accepted that independent coverage could include criticism of OpenAI.

The purchase price was not disclosed by the companies. Reports have circulated with estimates, but the official announcement and Axios's acquisition coverage did not establish a number. A personality analysis gains nothing by converting an uncertain price into a dramatic fact.

The confirmed structure is already dramatic enough. TBPN built authority as founder-led media operating outside the companies it covered. It is now owned by one of the most consequential subjects in its editorial field.

For the Type 3 reading, the deal produces a real conflict rather than another victory lap. The acquisition is unmistakable proof that Hays and Coogan built something valuable. It may also weaken the independence that made the proof possible. A promise from the owner is meaningful. It cannot eliminate the owner's influence by declaration.

That tension should not be settled by guessing at Hays's inner story. It can be observed. Which guests appear? Which questions become harder to ask? Does the show challenge OpenAI with the same energy it brings to competitors? The next phase has a public scoreboard, though not one that revenue alone can measure.

## How old is Jordi Hays?

The New York Times described Jordi Hays as 29 in its October 2025 profile. That places his birth around 1995 or 1996. A reliable public source for an exact birth date was not found.

The uncertainty is worth stating because search pages often turn inference into a precise birthday through repetition. Age is useful context for the speed of Hays's career. It is not evidence for a personality type, and an invented date does not make a biography more complete.

## Who is Jordi Hays's wife?

Jordi Hays is married to entrepreneur and investor Sarah Chase. She co-founded Party Round and Capital with him, bringing experience in investor relations and startup finance to the company they built together.

Chase is sometimes reduced to a relationship detail in profiles centered on Hays. That treatment distorts the record. The Not Boring article describes her as a cofounder working on the business, and Hays's 2023 Rho announcement thanks her alongside the team.

Their marriage does not tell us whether Hays is a Three, how he behaves at home, or what Chase privately makes of his public persona. Those claims belonged to the old article's invented interior. The sourced fact is more useful: one of Hays's most visible companies was a shared piece of work.

Hays is also a cofounder of the water-filtration company Rorra. In a [2025 founder Q&A](https://www.superangel.blog/p/founder-friday-brian-keller-charlie), he described building the product for two years before launch and named family, startup investing, and fast cars among his interests outside TBPN. The quiet development period adds range to his public record. It does not prove an Enneagram growth arrow or reveal his family psychology.

### Jordi Hays's wing: possibly 3w4

A 3w4 combines the Three's attention to effective performance with the Four wing's interest in distinction, authorship, and taste. Hays's repeated insistence on references from outside technology supports that possibility. The design of Capital and TBPN shows considerable aesthetic intention alongside its commercial purpose.

A 3w2 case remains plausible. Hays is a salesperson, host, and partnership builder whose work depends on reading people quickly. Public media makes the relational Two wing harder to separate from professional skill. The safest conclusion is Type 3 with a possible Four wing, held at medium confidence.

### Why the old subtype claim was too confident

The old profile called Hays a social Three because TBPN pursues status inside a powerful technology audience. That is a reasonable hypothesis, but the evidence also reflects the basic requirements of a specialized media business. Public prestige does not prove an instinctual subtype.

His emphasis on revenue predictability could support self-preservation. His partnership intensity could be read through the one-to-one instinct. Without better evidence about attention and anxiety outside work, a full stacking would create false precision.

See the [instinctual subtypes guide](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-instinctual-subtypes) for how the three instincts modify a core type.

### Stress toward 9 and growth toward 6

Enneagram theory says Threes can borrow the disengagement of [Type 9](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-9) under stress and the loyalty of [Type 6](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-6) in growth. Hays's public record does not show a clean stress-to-9 episode. The daily schedule may prevent drift, but calling that a defense against collapse would be speculation.

The collaborative structure of TBPN offers a modest growth-to-6 signal. Hays's performance works because Coogan, the production team, and the audience participate in a reliable system. Shared obligation has become as important as individual charisma. That is compatible with growth toward Six, though it is not proof.

### Why Jordi Hays might be Type 7 or Type 8 instead

Type 7 is the strongest visual alternative. The props, jokes, multiple ventures, and appetite for novelty fit the Enthusiast. The tiebreaker is his stated preference for constraint: one main product, a fixed live deadline, daily review, and repeated improvement. Sevens usually protect options. Hays keeps narrowing attention into a scoreboard.

Type 8 also deserves consideration because Hays is assertive, comfortable with power, and willing to occupy a room. Eights usually organize themselves around autonomy and resistance to control. Hays's language returns more often to product quality, audience response, brand, focus, and results. The center of gravity looks adaptive and achievement-oriented rather than defiant.

## The next scoreboard is independence

The Type 3 danger is not that every performance is fake. Hays plainly loves business, news, aesthetics, and the act of making a show. A role can be sincere and still become too dominant. The risk appears when the available scoreboard decides which version of the person gets airtime.

TBPN's first scoreboards were legible: publish the newsletter, start on time, improve the set, earn the clip, renew the sponsor, grow the business. OpenAI has added a harder one. The show must remain useful to its owner and credible to an audience that valued its independence.

No gong can resolve that conflict. It will be decided through ordinary editorial choices, one guest and one question at a time.

The red flag remains a good symbol, though not for the reason the old profile suggested. Hays waves it when the timeline is in turmoil, converting confusion into an opening scene. Now the turmoil includes the show itself. TBPN has become part of the power structure it narrates.

Tomorrow's countdown will still reach zero. Hays and Coogan will still have to make the news intelligible and entertaining. Afterward, they can review the clips, the jokes, and the audience response.

The most important result may be the one their own analytics cannot settle: whether people still believe the flag is free to point at its owner.
