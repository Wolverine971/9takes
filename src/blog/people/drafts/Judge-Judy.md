> "I started to wear the lace collar, because I figured, you know what, it's disarming. People walk into a courtroom and think 'Look at that sweet lovely judge that I was lucky to get. She wears a lace collar, how bad could she be?'"
> <cite>Judith Sheindlin, on the costume choice that built a $500 million empire</cite>

<p class="firstLetter">A 60 Minutes camera caught her in 1993, in a Manhattan family court robe, with a small piece of white lace pinned at her neck like a doily on a battleship. Morley Safer narrated her reputation for the country: "To those who confront her, she is the evil queen in the lace collar." Most people heard "evil queen." Judith Sheindlin heard "lace collar." She kept the collar. It is on every robe she has worn since.</p>

She wears it to disarm you. The disarming is the work. By the time you've registered that she dresses like someone's softer grandmother, she has already decided what kind of person you are and how much of your time she is willing to waste.

That is not the persona. That is the woman. America has spent thirty years assuming Judge Judy invented a TV character. She did not. She invented a television set big enough to fit the person she has been since 1965, when she walked into a law school class of 126 men as the only woman and decided very early that she would not be one of the ones who got talked over.

## Why Judge Judy isn't actually angry at the litigants

Watch one episode with the sound off. Watch her eyes when a litigant tries to explain why the rent didn't get paid. Her face does not say _I am annoyed by you_. It says _I am annoyed that the world produced you_. That distinction is the entire shape of a Type 1.

A Reformer aims past the person in front of them. The target is the gap between how things are and how things ought to be, and the litigant has just walked into it. The verdict is for the rule, not the human standing in the room.

This is why Judy can humiliate someone for twenty seconds and then immediately walk through, calmly and without sneering, what they should have done. She has no personal investment in their humiliation. She has total investment in the principle. <a href="/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-1">Type 1s</a> tend to confuse the two only when they are very young or very tired. Judy hasn't been either in fifty years.

The cruelty most viewers project onto her is the projection of a country that does not know what a working inner critic sounds like out loud.

## The Brooklyn dentist who told her every pimple was beautiful

Judith Susan Blum was born in 1942 in Midwood, Brooklyn, in an apartment above her father's dental practice. The ground floor was Murray Blum's office. The upstairs was the household he ruled the way men of his generation were allowed to rule.

He adored her.

"If I had a pimple on my forehead, he said, 'Oh, my God, that is so gorgeous,'" she told Amy Poehler in 2025, eighty-two years old and still smiling about it. "Women find their confidence from their fathers."

The line about fathers gets quoted often. The line that follows almost never does:

"I incurred the disappointment, wrath of my father who never hit me, but I never wanted to disappoint him."

That is the entire formation of a Type 1 in one sentence. The 1's inner critic isn't a critic she was given. It is a critic she developed in order to keep an adoring father from ever having to use his. The standard wasn't installed by punishment. It was installed by the cost of imagined punishment from a man who never delivered the real kind.

Murray Blum was, in his daughter's words, "the greatest thing since sliced bread." Her mother Ethel, an office manager, was by Judy's own remembering "a meat and potatoes kind of gal." The Brooklyn household had one engine, and it pointed at her.

## What is Judge Judy's personality type?

### Judge Judy is an Enneagram Type 1

Every diagnostic axis points the same direction.

**The driver is wrongness, not danger.** Type 8s, the type most casual viewers would guess for her, are organized around _not being controlled_. Their anger is territorial. Judy's anger is moral. It scales with how much a litigant has shirked an obligation, lied, abandoned a child, dodged a debt. It does not scale with how much they personally threaten her. She is fearless in her own courtroom not because she is dominant. She is fearless because she is right, and the rightness is the floor she stands on.

**Discipline as identity.** Same husband for over forty years (with one famous one-year interruption). Same show in the same time slot for 25 seasons. Same opening line. Same lace collar. Same Florida winters (Naples, October through May), same Los Angeles filming weeks. <a href="/enneagram-corner/enneagram-types-in-stress">Under stress</a>, 1s become more rigid before they collapse. Judy never collapses. She tightens.

**The crusade is institutional, not personal.** Her early Manhattan family court career, prosecuting juvenile crime, domestic violence, and child abuse from 1972 onward, produced a specific anger about deadbeat fatherhood, irresponsible parenthood, and women without economic means to leave. That anger became the spine of every book she's written. "These children are entitled to at least one parent to set a good example," she wrote in 1996. That is not a Type 8 talking about power. That is a Type 1 talking about duty.

**The wing reads as 1w2.** A 1w9 would be quieter, more withdrawn, more passive-aggressive. Judy is verbal, fast, oriented toward people, and openly interested in being useful to the women and children she rules in favor of. The 2 wing supplies the maternal layer that the lace collar visually quotes.

She is, in the technical sense, the cleanest celebrity Type 1 in the daytime-television corpus. The persona is not the type. The career _is_ the type.

A 1w9 reading collapses on verbal pace alone. Judy doesn't withdraw, she colonizes the room. An 8w1 reading collapses on appetite: Type 8s want their authority to count for itself, and Judy's authority always defers, in the end, to the rule it's enforcing. The territory she protects is not hers. It's the code's.

## Family court, 1972: where the show was actually built

In 1965 she graduated American University's Washington College of Law, the only woman in a class of 126. She passed the New York bar and took a job at a cosmetics firm, doing corporate work she found beneath her.

She quit within two years to raise her two children, Jamie and Adam.

Then, in 1972, a friend tipped her off to an opening in the New York family court. She walked in to prosecute juvenile crime, domestic violence, and child abuse, and stayed for the next 24 years.

The 1 was already formed. The job gave it scenery.

By 1982, Mayor Ed Koch had appointed her to the criminal court bench. By 1986, she was supervising judge in the family court's Manhattan division. By 1993, a Los Angeles Times reporter named Josh Getlin wrote her up as "one of the toughest family court judges in the country." A 60 Minutes producer read the piece. Morley Safer flew to New York. The lace collar got its first national close-up.

The 1993 60 Minutes segment was titled "Law and Disorder." It did not present her as a folk hero. Safer let lawyers and child welfare advocates call her "needlessly cruel and sarcastic, a loose cannon in the halls of justice." He warned, on air, that her appointment as a mayoral pick might one day be revoked for political reasons. Staten Island state senator Diane Savino, asked about her, called her "the best family court judge I ever worked with."

Both takes turn out to have been correct, which is the most Type 1 thing about the entire segment.

A book offer arrived the week the segment aired. _Don't Pee on My Leg and Tell Me It's Raining_ came out in 1996. A talent agent who saw the same broadcast had already started shopping her as a syndicated daytime concept.

The first episode of _Judge Judy_ taped that year. She was 54.

## How Judge Judy turned reruns into a $100 million annual asset

This is the section where Type 1 readers usually expect the type to break down. Reformers, the textbook says, are conflicted about money. Money is morally compromising. Money is what other people care about.

Judy did not get that memo.

In 2017, with 25 seasons of episodes sitting in a CBS vault, she did something unusual. She sat down at the negotiating table herself, no agent, no business manager fronting for her, and renegotiated. She agreed to extend the show one more season. She also extracted, in the same deal, a separately reported $100 million per year for handing the rerun library back to CBS for unlimited platform rights. According to _The Hollywood Reporter_, her team had been shopping the library elsewhere for as much as $200 million annually.

The press, for years, had been calling her $47 million annual salary "excessive." A judge, of all people, was asked to weigh in. He ruled it was not.

In 2018, she earned approximately $147 million, making her the highest-paid host on television.

She has explained the philosophy on the record: "You have to know your worth. Women often have a problem fully appreciating their worth." And about the table itself: "If I took your envelope and looked at it, I'm a loser. But this will satisfy me."

She didn't abandon her moral framework when she walked into the boardroom. She imported it. <a href="/personality-analysis/anna-wintour">Anna Wintour</a> built _Vogue_ on roughly the same principle: <em>the right number is the right number, and the negotiation is a moral act, not a transactional one</em>. A 1 doesn't negotiate to win. A 1 negotiates because asking for less than you are worth is a form of dishonesty about your own value, and dishonesty is the original sin.

She wasn't grabbing money. She was correcting a misvaluation. From inside the worldview, those are extremely different things.

## What the critics see when they look at Judge Judy

There are two serious critic-side readings of Judith Sheindlin, and both deserve their full weight before any type frame is allowed to absorb them.

The first is interpersonal, and the cleanest version of it belongs to her bailiff. Petri Hawkins-Byrd worked the rotation of court officers in Manhattan family court from 1986 to 1989. When she got the show in 1996, she hired him. He stayed for the entire 25-season run. On the way out he said: "I was never bored in her courtroom. Her get-to-the-point style didn't always sit well with the litigants, and there were times she was definitely glad to have me around."

After _Judy Justice_ premiered on Amazon Freevee in 2021 without him on the cast, he was harsher. He described his relationship with Sheindlin, in a _Deadline_ interview, as "the relationship between a low-ranking mail clerk and Bill Gates." Across 25 years of working together, he said, she had never once invited him to a lunch.

That is not a small thing. Twenty-five years of professional intimacy and not one private hour. Most people defending Sheindlin will reach for "that's just how she is," and the reach is the problem. A man you work with every day for a quarter of a century is, by ordinary human standards, someone whose interior life you eventually have to acknowledge. She did not. Sheindlin's public response was polite and clean: "Byrd is terrific and we had a great 25-year run. This is a whole new program with a whole new cast and an exciting energy." She didn't apologize. She rarely does.

The second critic-side reading is structural, and it is the one the show's defenders almost never engage. In November 2021, Business Insider published an investigation into Randy Douthit, Sheindlin's longtime executive producer and director. Six former staffers told _Insider_ that Douthit had said, on the job, that he didn't want too many Black litigants on the show. Former producers described him referring to litigants as "gross" and "low rent," making comments about their teeth, weight, and disabilities. He had been the subject of at least three employment lawsuits going back to 2001, none of which led to a conviction, all of which he denied. He kept the job.

The deeper critique sits underneath the Douthit reporting. The economic engine of small-claims daytime television is the spectacle of working-poor litigants, disproportionately Black and Hispanic, being publicly corrected by a wealthy white woman for the entertainment of a national audience. A Type 1 with a moral framework who built a $500 million empire on that arrangement is exactly the kind of figure whose internal sense of rightness can quietly outpace external accountability. Sheindlin has not, on the record, addressed the Douthit reporting in any sustained way. The show kept airing. The library kept selling.

None of this disqualifies the Type 1 reading. It sharpens it. A moral standard that is internally coherent can also be a moral standard that never has to defend itself against the cost it imposes on people outside the frame. Many 1s work alongside someone for decades without ever seeing them as a peer. Many 1s preside over an institution for decades without ever auditing whom the institution is built on. The interior rightness is the bug and the feature. The bailiff is the bailiff. The litigant is the litigant. The role is the role.

This is the part of the type her admirers tend to skip, and the part the critics keep returning to.

## The year Judge Judy left Jerry

Jerry was her second husband. Her first was Ronald Levy, a New York prosecutor she married in 1964, the year before law school. They had two children, Jamie and Adam, and divorced in 1976 after twelve years that Judy has described, in interviews, as a marriage that did not survive her decision to keep working. She met Jerry Sheindlin, a fellow attorney, around that time. They married in 1978. He eventually became a judge himself.

In 1990, her father died. The same year, she divorced Jerry.

She has been clear, on the record, that the timing was not a coincidence. Murray Blum's death broke the floor she had been standing on since childhood, and for the first time the inner certainty wavered. The 1 in disintegration moves toward Type 4: pulling inward, melancholic, abandoning the structure that had held her up.

The marriage took the hit. A year later, in 1991, she and Jerry remarried.

She has explained the return in plain, unsentimental language. She missed, she said, having "someone to fuss over."

That sentence is more revealing than it sounds. A Type 1 with a 2 wing organizes her domestic life around _care directed outward_. Fussing over Jerry was, for her, the visible form of belonging to a household. Without it she was, briefly, just a woman in a Manhattan apartment whose father had died.

She has been married to him ever since. She watches Law & Order reruns with him at night. She is in bed early. She arrives in Naples, Florida, every October and leaves in May. The routine is sacred. The routine is, in the 1's vocabulary, what a good life is supposed to look like.

She did not return to Jerry because she could not be alone. She returned because the inner courtroom, in her own private case, ruled in his favor.

## What Judy lets her granddaughter see

In 2021, when Sheindlin moved her court to Amazon's free streaming service Freevee, a calculated departure from CBS that gave her full ownership of the new format, she did something she had never done in 25 years of _Judge Judy_. She invited family in.

Adam came first. Her son, Adam Levy, had served two terms as Putnam County District Attorney before returning to private practice. When _Tribunal Justice_ launched on Amazon in 2023, Sheindlin cast him as one of three judges on its three-judge panel. She is also the executive producer of _Adam's Law_, his own CBS courtroom show announced for the 2026-27 season, the first solo bench any Sheindlin family member will hold outside her own. Then came Sarah Rose Levy: Adam's daughter, NYU Law class of 2022, hired as the law clerk on _Judy Justice_ and sitting beside her grandmother on the show. Asked by Fox News about working with her, she said:

> "The no-nonsense grandmother is still there. But there is a much softer, supportive side. She's the first person I call if I have a problem or a question or something that needs to get solved. She's a fantastic problem solver, especially for the ones that she loves."

Sarah Rose has also said, more pointedly, that she has started disagreeing with her grandmother on camera. "This season, more so than last season, I've gotten a bit more comfortable disagreeing with her or even bringing up points that maybe she didn't discuss in her ruling that I think the audience might have picked up on."

Petri Hawkins-Byrd was never going to get that. The bailiff is the bailiff.

But the granddaughter is allowed to bring up a point. The granddaughter is allowed to push back. The granddaughter is, in the 1's internal hierarchy, _family_, which is the one category in which the rulebook actively requires you to make room.

What Sarah Rose sees is the Type 1 in security: still demanding, still loud, but capable of being interrupted by someone she has decided is real to her. The integration arrow for a 1 points to Type 7: playful, generous, willing to let the rules bend in places that don't matter. With Sarah Rose, the rules bend. With the litigants, the rules do not. The line between those two policies is where the woman lives.

## At 83, still in chambers

_Judy Justice_ is on its fourth season at Amazon. It crossed 150 million hours watched. It won the Daytime Emmy in its first year. The spinoffs, _Tribunal Justice_ and _Justice on Trial_, keep getting renewed. The lace collar is, at this point, a piece of American visual literacy. She is older than every person on her set and she still runs every minute of it.

Most retiring judges leave the bench because they are tired. Type 1s, characteristically, are the last to leave because the rulebook does not care how tired you are.

There is a temptation, watching her at 83, to call this stubbornness. It is not. Stubbornness is what holds when something inside you wants to quit. Judy doesn't want to quit. The inner critic, the one her father almost never had to use, is still issuing verdicts every morning. The choice is whether to issue them in the privacy of her own head, in her own kitchen in Naples, with Jerry, or in front of a camera, where someone might actually have to face a consequence.

She has, since 1996, chosen the camera.

The lace collar is still pinned to the robe. Most viewers still read it as decoration. It isn't. It is a costume note left over from a Manhattan family court bench in 1972, where a young Reformer in a man-shaped uniform decided she would make the system look at her.

The collar is what she wears so the inner critic can wear robes.
