> "I remember in therapy once, I said, 'I'm happiest when I'm somebody else.' I'm an actor. And I think that's how."
> — Madelaine Petsch, Call Her Daddy, 2025

<p class="firstLetter">At eighteen, Madelaine Petsch found a sales job on Craigslist. Windows, door to door, Los Angeles. Her technique: wait outside the house until the wife left, knock, and cry.</p>

"I would make myself cry and get the husbands to buy windows," she told Alex Cooper on Call Her Daddy in September 2025. It worked. The windows sold.

Think about what that job actually requires. Reading a strange man's mood from his doorstep. Producing, on command, the exact emotion that will soften him. Closing before the feeling fades. Acting schools charge tuition for less. Petsch showed up to a Craigslist gig already fluent.

She learned it at home, starting around age eight, in a house where her father's moods set the weather and exactly one person could reliably change them. Her.

The years since look like a career. Seven seasons as television's most famous mean girl. A horror trilogy she helped rewrite from her trailer. A rom-com she produced that went to number one on Prime Video. Underneath the career runs something older: a childhood job she has spent nine years of therapy trying to quit.

## What is Madelaine Petsch's personality type?

### Madelaine Petsch is an Enneagram Type 2

The [Enneagram Type 2](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-2) is the Helper: a personality built around a trade struck young, that being useful to the people you love is how you keep them. Most Twos can only gesture at when their trade was struck. Petsch can date hers.

Her family had a standing arrangement. When her father turned cold, she was the one who went in to bring him back. The diagnostic moment is her answer when Cooper asked, on that September 2025 episode, whether her mother assigned her the role: "Honestly, I don't think she ever asked me to do that. I think I immediately saw a place that I could try to help and I just tried to help every time I could."

Nobody drafted her. She volunteered, at an age when volunteering was the only power available, and the helping hardened into an identity.

The adult evidence stacks the same direction. She financially supports "a lot of my family," she said in that 2025 interview, and her explanation arrives in the same shape: "No one asked me to. I wanted to help." She bought a house in Washington and moved her brother into it so he could get out of their parents' home. She bought it before she bought her own.

Asked on the Angle on Producers podcast in 2025 for the best part of producing movies, she skipped the premieres: "The coolest part of producing so far has been having actors come to me, being like, hey, I'm having this problem with this wardrobe, or the sound thing is this... where actors trust and believe in me enough to do that with me."

Even her vice shows up correctly labeled. Explaining to the same podcast why she turns down honorary credits: "I won't take a producer credit unless I actually produced something, probably because I have too much pride." Pride, in Enneagram terms, is the Two's signature distortion: the quiet certainty that other people have needs and you don't. It took nine years of weekly, sometimes twice-weekly therapy to loosen that certainty. Her verdict on the process, back on Call Her Daddy in 2025: "It's for life."

## Madelaine Petsch's Childhood: The Kid Sent In to Fix Her Father

Port Orchard, Washington. Two South African parents who arrived, by her account, "with I think $1,000 in their pocket" and ran a business from the upstairs of the house for her entire childhood. She heard "business 24/7," and remembers it caused "a lot of fights in the household." At school she was the redheaded, plant-based daughter of immigrants, teased for her hair and her accent. "Maybe it was all in my head, but I definitely felt like I didn't belong growing up."

Inside the house, the weather changed without notice. Her father "would have extreme highs and extreme lows and in the extreme highs, as a child, it's like you have the most fun dad in the world... and then that person is ripped away from you and being cold to you and not responding when you tell them that you love them." The family rule was no diagnoses; the in-house explanations were apartheid-war trauma or a bad mood. She was an adult before she could say the plainer thing: "He has a chemical imbalance in his brain that makes him incredibly high and incredibly low."

She was eight the night the low landed directly on her. Her mother and brother were in South Africa. There was a falling out; she no longer remembers over what. She remembers the rest. "I told him I loved him. I was like crying telling him I loved him. I wanted him to tell me he loved me back and he told me he didn't love me. And he left."

She lay in bed for hours and reached the conclusion every eight-year-old reaches: "My father doesn't love me. Like I did something very wrong."

That math ran forward for decades. "I spent a lot of my life not loving myself," she told Cooper, "because I was like, if the person who made me doesn't love me, how am I supposed to love myself?"

Sometime after that night, the family found its workaround, and the workaround was her. "I would be called to come and fix the problem," she said. "No matter what the issue was, if he was upset with my mother or my brother, I would be sent in to essentially disarm him emotionally." She had a script: "Daddy, can we just have a nice night? Can we just figure it out?" It took hours. It usually worked. "So I think they kept doing that."

<div class="pull-quote">"I wish I would have felt like a kid."</div>

That is her complete answer to what she wishes her family had done differently. One sentence, no elaboration.

Her escape route was volume. An arts high school 45 minutes away, dance from 3:00 to 9:00 every night, community theater on weekends. "I just never wanted to be home. So I immersed myself in the arts and escapism." She moved out at eighteen, "the day I could." The line at the top of this page, the one she said in therapy about being happiest as somebody else, is her own accounting of what those years built: a person most at ease inside feelings that belong to a character.

## Four Phone Calls That Explain Madelaine Petsch

**Call one. Age eight, Washington to South Africa.** The night her father said what he said and left, she picked up the phone and called her mother, ten time zones away. Her mom told her: "This has nothing to do with you. This is fully your dad." It was the first time anyone told her the problem lived inside him. She spent the next two decades behaving as if it lived inside her anyway.

**Call two. High school, sixteen missed calls.** She was out at a ballet. When she finally checked her phone: sixteen missed calls and a summons to come home immediately. What she found there she still refuses to describe, beyond calling it "so incredibly unsafe and so disrespectful as a child to put me in." The anger she kept lands on the whole family: "I had a lot of resentment towards everyone in my family for putting me in that position when they could have just gone to professionals."

**Call three. Her twenties, every friend she had.** She knew her relationship was dead. She polled her friends, and they confirmed it: "Yeah, obviously it's over." She stayed a year and a half longer, "because I watched my mom stay forever." Her math at the time: "Watching my mom be with my dad and just decide to be with him forever made me believe that love is actually just staying no matter what." The boyfriends repeated the pattern she knew best; one of them, after the breakup, "came out with the same diagnosis as my father."

What baited the hook was never the man himself. "Consistency, stability. Even though none of those things were usually true in the relationships, if it felt like security, I was in." Her name for the breakthrough, after years of therapy: "Not dating carbon copies of my father over and over and over again... which is like the biggest success of my life, honestly."

**Call four. Thirty-one, outbound only.** The standing rule today: "With actually both of my parents, they don't call me. I call them. I'm tired of being the one who gets the phone calls." Sometimes she goes six months without speaking to her father, "and I'm very comfortable with that." His place in her life came with a condition: he had to confirm that her memories actually happened. "If my dad wouldn't have given me validation in those moments, he wouldn't be in my life anymore."

The direction of the phone is the entire recovery. The girl in call one waited for love to call back. The woman in call four decides when the line opens.

## Why Madelaine Petsch Can't Stop Working

The phone is one boundary. The calendar has never had one.

The panic attacks came before the fame. "I used to get really bad panic attacks right before I booked Riverdale," she told Refinery29 in 2019, and once the show hit, "really bad social anxiety" followed it. Therapy helped her name both. It has never made her still.

During the pandemic she had her brain scanned. Her reasoning: "I was so anxious all the time that I was like, 'There's something genuinely wrong with me.' That's how bad I am with stillness." The scan came back clean. Her reading of the results: "I'm actually just a crazy person, cuz I'm fine."

In September 2025 she counted exactly one day off in her next two months, then watched her team book something onto it. The volume predates the money. In her audition years she worked, by her own count, 249 jobs, quitting any of them that wouldn't release her for a casting call, and she kept a journal listing every single one. She kept a second journal too: every audition, the feedback, and whatever personal details the casting directors let slip, so she could open the next meeting by asking after their families by name.

The streak of day jobs broke in 2016 with Riverdale. She met [Lili Reinhart](/personality-analysis/Lili-Reinhart) in the audition rooms, the two of them, in her words, "in this thing together where we both hadn't booked it." Once they finally had, they flew out together and lived together through season one.

Riverdale paid the grind back, and she kept receipts. Seven Cartier rings, one for each season renewal. "I literally never wear them... but I have them and I like looking at them." Trophies for an audience of one.

She frames the appetite as artistic range: "I just want to do everything. I want to try everything. Variety is the spice of life, especially for an artist," she told Luxury London in 2026. The plainer accounting came out on Call Her Daddy: "That's probably why I am successful, honestly, is because I did not want to be at home." For a Two, a packed calendar doubles as proof of position; every yes is one more person glad she showed up, and an empty day offers no evidence of anything.

The anxiety does have a documented off-switch, and the switch is other people. "If I'm having a bad day and I'm feeling like my anxiety is through the roof, I will call Camila, and I will go to her place, and we'll eat dried apricots and talk about all of my problems until I have vented it out." Camila is Camila Mendes. She and Lili Reinhart remain her innermost circle two years after Riverdale ended, and their word carries formal power: "There's a couple people in my life that are my north stars. If they love my partner, I'm in. If they hate them, I'm literally out."

## How Madelaine Petsch Earned the Credit Nobody Believed

The producing has its own origin scene in the Port Orchard house. Her parents built her a set of fake car keys when she was small. Her version of playing house: take the keys, "fake driving up the driveway," walk into the home office, sit at the makeshift desk they set up for her, and stick shipping labels on outgoing boxes. "That's how I would play as a kid."

Twenty-five years later, a podcast host asked her what separates an exceptional producer from a mediocre one. Her answer: "I think care. I think it's really almost entirely about care... And detail. Being organized, shipping labels, you know."

Her first film as a producer was Jane, a thriller about, in her words, "the manifestation of anxiety in a woman and how that can eat away at someone." She found the book, the money, and the production company herself. "I was the only person on that film who had been on a film set before. So then I was like, I have to run this ship. I don't know what I'm doing. So I faked it the entire time and it worked."

The Strangers trilogy hired her as an actor. She arrived with notes. "Hey, I have some thoughts on the script if you want to talk about that," she told producer Courtney Solomon, and the two ended up rewriting all three films together in her trailer. The whole trilogy shot in 52 days (The Hollywood Reporter, 2024). Her memory of the rhythm: a 16-hour set day, then five more hours on the phone about tomorrow's pages, "every day honestly, because we cared so much about it."

She knew exactly what people would assume about an actress with a producer credit. "That was what I was always scared of... Other actors, I was like, are they going to take me seriously? Are they going to think this is a vanity credit?" Her answer was attendance. "I will prove them wrong by being on set every single day, even if I'm not working."

The people in the rooms noticed. Director Renny Harlin, on first meeting her: "We were just really impressed by her intelligence, by her passion, by her insightfulness, and her charm." And then: "She very soon became our number one choice and ended up really becoming a partner in this endeavor in terms of working on the script with us." Podcast host Carolina Groppa read the pattern back to her on air: from where she sat, Petsch was "really trying to control your narrative," as an artist first and now as a producer.

The critics were harder on the work itself. Collider's review of Chapter 2 said the sequel "ignores everything that made this series interesting," and the trilogy's notices ran cold across the board. The skeptics have a case; even Petsch calls the first film "just a launchpad... to get to the story I wanted to tell for two and three."

The reviews grade the movies, though, and her reason for taking the job lives somewhere reviews don't look. "Okay, shooting three movies at one time, having to track where my character's at any given moment, what a challenge. I can't not do that. That is my personality." And the payoff she names for producing, again and again, is depth of belonging: "It's just another way to be sewn more into the fabric of the story in a meaningful way." By the third film, the title stopped being honorary. "For the third Strangers, I do have a capital P, because that was earned."

The bet keeps paying. In October 2025, Maintenance Required, a rom-com she produced and starred in, hit number one on Prime Video (Screen Rant). She spent four months in mechanic training for it and says her favorite part of the shoot was fixing brake pads. Lionsgate executives now pull her aside at premieres to ask what she wants to make next, and her own production company is in the works. "I have been a producer on my projects for a while now; it comes naturally to me," she told Luxury London. "I have that kind of brain."

Then, in February 2026, she did something with no precedent anywhere in this story: she quit. The Strangers: Chapter 3 closed the trilogy, and Petsch announced she was done with the franchise. "I think, for now, this is the closing chapter for Maya. I'll never say never, but I think for my own mental health, I need to walk away and have other experiences," she told the Seattle Times.

Playing three films of sustained torment, she said, "definitely spiked my cortisol." To The Hollywood Reporter she admitted: "There were a lot of behind-the-scenes tears on these films because they were hard. The hours were crazy. I was isolated from my family."

Hold that next to the woman who counted one day off in two months and watched her team book it. She has spent a whole career unable to leave a room that wanted her in it. This time she left, and the reason she gave was her own health, a category of reason the eight-year-old in Port Orchard was never issued.

## Why Madelaine Petsch Built a YouTube Channel to Prove She's Kind

The producer skeptics were not her first jury. For seven seasons she played Cheryl Blossom, Riverdale's scarlet-lipped menace, an heiress who greets grief with cruelty and owns every room she burns down. Season one Cheryl was, in Petsch's phrase, "like a bully." Audiences did the obvious math. "It was a time on social media where people thought you were the character you played on TV... And I just wanted to be like, 'Hey, I'm not like that at all. I'm like, I'm not mean, I promise.' But people still think that."

Her correction campaign was industrial. She launched a YouTube channel in 2017 so fans could meet the person underneath the character. She shot it, edited it, and grew it past six million subscribers, winning the Shorty Award for Breakout YouTuber in 2019. Then, years later, she deleted every video. They still exist where only she can see them: "What a beautiful thing I have as my diary."

It is easy to make fun of. An actress so rattled by a fictional reputation that she manufactured six million subscribers' worth of evidence she is nice. She even supplies the counter-quote herself: "I don't really care what you think about me as a human being, because you don't know me as a human being... You have no idea how I carry myself day-to-day. You don't know what my values are, my principles, my morals."

The mockery reads differently next to her childhood. From age eight, her worth in her own house came from a single function: she made the bad moments stop. "Mean" is the one verdict that unmakes that person entirely. When millions of strangers decided she was the cruelty she performed, they were, without knowing it, repeating the accusation she had organized her whole life against. The channel gets read as vanity. It ran more like a years-long appeal of a wrongful conviction. And the defiant quote and the pleading one turn out to be the same sentence at two volumes: you don't know me.

The stakes underneath were never petty. The same fame brought "death threats, the people saying they're going to shoot me at appearances, people doxing me and putting my address online all the time. Like that is the toughest part of this job that nobody talks about." She ran a kindness campaign for an audience that included people threatening to kill her.

The character, in the end, ran a better defense than the channel did. In season two, Cheryl came out as bisexual and fell for Toni Topaz, and a different kind of message started arriving. "The amount of people who have reached out to me telling me that they had a similar story and their parents didn't approve of it, or they have decided to come out to their parents and Riverdale gave them the courage to do so has been wonderful and life-changing," she told Wonderland Magazine in 2018.

She never did convince the internet she wasn't mean. She got something better: strangers writing to say the mean girl had done her old job for them, had made the worst night in their house survivable. Ask her about Cheryl now and there is no grudge in the answer. "I love Cheryl Blossom more than anyone could ever love a character in my life," she said on Call Her Daddy. "She was the most fun psychotic crazy woman of the planet Earth."

## What Happens When Madelaine Petsch Can't Fix Something

All that therapy produced the insight. The reflex outlived it. "I still have a hard time allowing conflict to stew," she says. "When there's unease or conflict, I get really uncomfortable for obvious reasons." In her past relationships, the sequence was automatic: "There was conflict, I was immediately fixing it. To my detriment." Even in fights that had nothing to do with her, "I would just scramble to fix it."

Recovery, at 31, looks like sitting still while every cell in her body files an objection:

<p class="inner-thought">I'm rageful inside, cuz I'm like, I have to fix it, I have to fix it, I have to fix it. It's okay. I'm just going to move my ankle and sit here and listen to you talk. And then I'm going to walk away, even though I don't want to walk away. I want to stay and fix it. And we'll talk about it tomorrow. That's okay.</p>

Rageful is the word worth stopping on. The Enneagram predicts it: [under stress, Twos move toward Type 8](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-types-in-stress), where the warmth flattens and the demands surface. Petsch's version comes with a disclaimer attached: "I'm very blunt, but I'm kind." On set she picks her battles by one criterion, whether the issue will "make or break the film." Everything below that bar: "I will just eat it."

The growth direction is quieter and much stranger to her. Alongside the therapy there is a spiritual healer she flies to Denver to see, three days at a stretch, "and we get really in the weeds." The homework at the end never changes: "You just need to go play. Go be a kid." Her current off-duty self, as she describes it: "I'm just like a little Sim now," on the couch with jelly beans.

Eight months before the Cooper interview, she reversed the largest refusal of her life. She had ruled out children completely: "I refuse to raise a child in a dysfunctional home. I refuse to raise a child where they believe that they are not loved. I never felt like that was possible because of what I saw as a child." Then, mid-session, the frame broke open. She could pick the partner. Pick the house. Pick the weather. "I was like, I don't want to carry on this bloodline. Let it end here. And now I'm like, no, I can change my narrative. I can rewrite history and I can decide what this looks like and I have the power to do that."

There is a colder reading available, a recovering fixer arranging to create the one person whose need for her will be total. Her version points the other way: a house where, for once, the kid gets to be the kid.

## You Can't Fix Him

The portrait she gives of her father now is soft. "He makes little salads from his garden and he loves his little greenhouse... I'm like, 'You're an egg. You're just this wonderful little creature.'" She loves him "with every fiber of my being" and calls him "one of the smartest people on the planet."

His stability arrived late, and its ease is the part that stings. "The only resent that I feel is seeing now how it seemingly seems so easy for him to be normal. That's the hardest part. Because now he's actually a pretty wonderful dad." The repair had exactly one non-negotiable term: he had to confirm her memories. "Saying these things to my father and having him acknowledge that's exactly what happened. That was the most important piece to me to have a relationship now."

So they have one, on her terms. She calls him; he doesn't call her. Sometimes six months pass. "He wishes we spoke more," she says. "I'm doing what I can."

Her mother's file is shorter and more tender. "Do you know people who are destined to be mothers? Like that's my mom's entire purpose on this planet," she told Cooper. But the protector's method left its own mark. Her mom kept "peace and tranquility" by keeping things from her, and when the three of them, mother, brother, daughter, compare memories now, whole events turn out to have been filed under *don't think about it*. "We'll be like, 'Wait, that's not what happened,'" Petsch says.

The outbound-only rule covers her too. Being the gentler parent did not earn an exemption from the new phone policy.

Nine years of therapy built all of it, and she is honest about the floor the work still can't reach. "Even though right now I'm telling you, I'm a badass, I love myself more than anyone could possibly love me on the planet, I still have moments where I'm that little Madelaine who's sitting in her bed crying cuz she thinks her dad doesn't love her."

In September 2025 she sat down with one of the biggest podcasts in the world and told all of it: the night she was eight, the deployments, the ballet, the boyfriends who wore his face. Near the end of the episode she mentioned, almost in passing, that he didn't know yet. "I've not told him I'm going on this, and I'm very curious to see how that goes."

The job, from age eight on, was making sure her father never had a feeling she hadn't already managed. This time he gets to have one on his own.
