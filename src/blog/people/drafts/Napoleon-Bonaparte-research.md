# Napoleon Bonaparte - Enneagram Type 8 Research Brief

## Research Date: 2026-03-01

---

## 1. CENTRAL CONTRADICTION / CORE TENSION

**The man who conquered Europe could not conquer his own need to be loved.**

Napoleon projected absolute dominance, control, and invulnerability to the world -- and yet privately he was consumed by desperate emotional neediness, crippling insecurity about his social status, and a terror of abandonment. He was the most powerful man in Europe writing letters to Josephine like a lovesick teenager: "Without his Josephine, without the assurance of her love, what is left him upon earth? What can he do?" This from the man who commanded the Grande Armee.

### The Specific Tensions:

1. **The Conqueror Who Couldn't Be Alone**: He dominated nations but was psychologically dependent on Josephine, writing obsessive love letters while commanding armies. He once wrote: "I need to be alone. I am tired of grandeur; all my feelings have dried up. I no longer care about my glory. At twenty-nine I have exhausted everything." -- a confession of emptiness from the most ambitious man alive.

2. **The Outsider Who Built an Empire to Belong**: Born Corsican (practically Italian), bullied mercilessly at French military school for his accent and origins, Napoleon spent his entire life trying to prove he belonged -- and then overshot so dramatically he made himself Emperor. But even as Emperor, he was pathologically aware that European royalty secretly despised him as a commoner. He created kings from his brothers, married into Habsburg royalty, and still never felt accepted.

3. **The Controller Who Lost Control of Himself**: He was a master of discipline, propaganda, and image-crafting -- yet he attempted suicide with poison at Fontainebleau when his empire crumbled (April 13, 1814). The man who said "He who fears being conquered is sure of defeat" tried to end his own life rather than face exile.

4. **The Mythmaker Who Was Most Honest in Private**: He manufactured his public image more systematically than any leader before him (the phrase "to lie like a bulletin" became French slang). Yet his private letters and deathbed words reveal a man consumed by love, regret, and longing. His last words: "France, the army, head of the army, Josephine."

5. **The Power Artist Who Wrote a Romance Novel**: Before he was Emperor, Napoleon wrote "Clisson et Eugenie" (1795) -- a thinly veiled autobiographical romance about a soldier who falls in love and is betrayed. The conqueror of Europe started as a heartbroken romantic fiction writer.

## 2. SIGNATURE DETAILS (Small details that crack open who Napoleon really was)

### Detail 1: The Two-Hour Bath Ritual

Napoleon took daily baths that lasted up to two hours -- extraordinarily unusual for the era when bathing was considered risky. Servants continuously added hot water to keep the temperature perfect while he read newspapers and correspondence. In an age of filth, this obsessive hygiene was a private ritual of control and self-soothing. He always traveled with a personal hygiene kit containing comb, razors, nail scissors, toothbrushes, and bottles of his signature rosemary-citrus eau de cologne.

**What it reveals**: The man who controlled empires needed this ritual of private order. The baths weren't vanity -- they were a decompression chamber between the public performance and his inner world.

### Detail 2: The Romance Novel He Wrote at 26

Before any of the conquests, Napoleon penned "Clisson et Eugenie" -- a novella about a brilliant soldier who falls in love, retires to domestic bliss, then returns to war only to have his lover seduced by a comrade. It was transparently autobiographical, based on his failed engagement to Desiree Clary. He was influenced by Goethe's "The Sorrows of Young Werther."

**What it reveals**: The archetype of the Type 8 conqueror started as a sentimental writer processing rejection through fiction. The tender inner world was there before the armor went on.

### Detail 3: The Dog on the Battlefield That Haunted Him

Walking a battlefield after combat, Napoleon encountered a dog sitting beside his dead master, licking the soldier's face and howling. Napoleon later wrote that this single image "stirred me to tears" more than anything else he had witnessed in war. The memory haunted him for the rest of his life.

**What it reveals**: A man responsible for hundreds of thousands of deaths was emotionally undone not by human suffering at scale, but by a single loyal creature grieving. The intimacy of devotion -- not the abstraction of loss -- is what penetrated his defenses.

### Detail 4: The Intercepted Letter That Humiliated Him

When Napoleon learned of Josephine's affair during the Egyptian campaign, he wrote a devastated, raw letter to his brother Joseph -- "I need to be alone. I am tired of grandeur; all my feelings have dried up" -- which the British navy intercepted and published in every newspaper across Europe. His private emotional collapse became public entertainment. The most feared general in the world, exposed as a cuckolded, heartbroken man.

**What it reveals**: The gap between his public mask (invincible conqueror) and private reality (devastated lover) was brutally exposed by enemies who understood that emotional vulnerability was his real weak point.

### Detail 5: His Ability to Sleep Anywhere, Instantly

Napoleon could fall asleep in minutes directly on a battlefield, on a bearskin rug, even with cannons firing. He would take 15-20 minute naps between battles, then wake up with total clarity. He described his mind as having "different drawers" he could open and close at will -- closing one subject entirely to focus on another.

**What it reveals**: This is the Type 8's compartmentalization made literal. He could wall off emotional and mental content with terrifying efficiency -- until he couldn't (see: the Josephine letters, the suicide attempt).

### Detail 6: The Liquorice Box and Nose-Pinching

Napoleon carried a small tortoiseshell box of liquorice and would give pieces to children he encountered, pinching their noses between his fingers. He was also known for pinching the ears and cheeks of his soldiers as an affectionate gesture.

**What it reveals**: Physical touch as a language of connection and dominance simultaneously -- a tenderness that expressed itself through mild aggression, perfectly Type 8.

### Detail 7: Fifteen-Minute Lunches

Napoleon's meals rarely lasted more than fifteen minutes. He ate simply -- roast chicken, pasta (discovered during the Italian campaigns), wine mixed with water. He was uninterested in fine dining or luxury food despite having access to anything.

**What it reveals**: Food was fuel, not pleasure. The man who could have feasted like a king ate like a soldier. The asceticism reveals someone for whom control and efficiency trumped sensory enjoyment.

## 4. TESTIMONY FROM PEOPLE AROUND HIM

### From His Valet, Constant (Louis Constant Wairy, served 15 years)

"On getting closer to him, one enjoyed the surprising charm of his conversation, the entire simplicity of his family life, and the habitual kindliness of his character." The memoir reveals Napoleon in "moments of vulnerability and strength, from the burning of military eagles to secret councils amidst the chaos of retreat."

### From Madame de Remusat (Court Lady)

She noted Bonaparte's "disregard of others and his insolent attitude toward those who served him" -- he "dictated with great ease," "never wrote anything with his own hand," and "utterly lacked patience to do anything whatever with his own hands." She was "astonished and indignant that the Emperor, coming straight from long hours of work with his ministers and with his secretary, could not find soft words for the ladies of the Court."

### From Bourrienne (Secretary and Childhood Friend)

Bourrienne noted that Napoleon as a young man had "a somber and pensive character, never played with any one and ordinarily walked by himself" though "he had much ability; understood and learned readily." Bourrienne "shows us the other and the really important side of Napoleon's character" -- the workaholic, the strategist, the man who functioned rather than performed.

### From His Troops

"His reception by the troops was nothing short of rapturous." It was "well worth seeing how he talked to the soldiers -- how he questioned them one after the other respecting their campaigns or their wounds." He called his soldiers "my children" and knew many of them by name.

### From Talleyrand (Foreign Minister turned rival)

When Napoleon called him "a turd in a silk stocking," Talleyrand coolly responded: "What a pity, such a great man and so ill-mannered." This captures the paradox: even his enemies acknowledged his greatness while noting his coarseness.

### From Metternich (Austrian Chancellor and adversary)

"There is no more useless labour than to point out that Bonaparte was an excellent man. He is in no wise wicked as this word is understood in common life. He has too much practical understanding for that. He is a very strong man, and in the different setting of another age, he would have become a very great man."

Napoleon dismissed Metternich: "Entertain this simpleton, we are wanted elsewhere."

### From Goethe (After meeting Napoleon)

When they met, Napoleon exclaimed "Voila un homme!" (Here's a man!). Goethe later reported that Napoleon "made observations at a high intellectual level, as a man who has studied the tragic scene with the attention of a criminal judge."

### From Ralph Waldo Emerson (Analytical assessment)

Emerson saw Napoleon as conducting "an experiment in the use of the intellect without conscience." He was "far the best known and most powerful person" of the century, but Emerson also called him "dishonest, stagy, unscrupulous, selfish, perfidious, and coarse."

### From Wellington (His conqueror)

On Hudson Lowe, Napoleon's jailer: "A very bad choice; he was a man wanting in education and judgment. He was a stupid man." -- Even Wellington seemed to respect Napoleon more than his own appointed governor.

### From Letizia Bonaparte (His Mother)

The one person Napoleon always treated with respect. She "instilled in him the tenacity and force of will which led him to conquer much of Europe." She never liked Josephine and disapproved of the marriage. Her famous reported skepticism during his height of power: "Pourvu que ca dure" ("Let's hope it lasts").

### From an Italian Officer (First meeting with young Napoleon)

When Napoleon took his first major command in Italy, a local officer began answering his many questions -- initially thinking Napoleon looked ignorant. But as the conversation progressed over hours, the officer's evaluation "slowly reversed into being impressed -- Napoleon seemingly went from being entirely clueless about the political and military situation in Italy to having an expert-level opinion on it, matched only by a handful of specialists in the region, within a few hours and a single conversation."

## 5. CRISIS / FAILURE MOMENTS

### Crisis 1: The Suicide Attempt at Fontainebleau (April 13, 1814)

Having lost his empire, throne, wife, and son, Napoleon tried to poison himself in his room at Fontainebleau in the early hours of April 13, 1814. He took a poison containing opium. His valet Constant and aide Caulaincourt noticed something wrong. Baron Yvan was called and managed to save him by inducing vomiting. One theory: Napoleon had been taking small doses of arsenic as protection against assassination ("mithridatization"), which may have made the poison less lethal.

**Psychological significance**: The man who preached that "he who fears being conquered is sure of defeat" chose death over the humiliation of exile. This was not a calculated move -- it was the Type 8's absolute terror of powerlessness made lethal.

### Crisis 2: The Josephine Divorce (December 14, 1809)

Held in the throne room at the Tuileries, Josephine wore a plain white gown reminiscent of a wedding dress. Both exchanged words of gratitude and love, but Josephine collapsed sobbing and asked someone else to finish reading her statement. That night, the Emperor was already in bed when Josephine appeared at his door "silent but bearing the signs of the profoundest grief." He kissed her several times, and when she fainted, he handed her to his secretary and "hastened out of the room to hide his own emotion from any curious eyes." Josephine then wept violently and begged the secretary to tell Napoleon not to forget her.

**Psychological significance**: Napoleon chose dynasty over love -- the ultimate Type 8 calculation of power over feeling. And it destroyed him. His relationship with Marie-Louise was never the same. His deathbed word was still "Josephine."

### Crisis 3: The Retreat from Egypt and the Siege of Acre (1799)

After 63 days besieging Acre with eight failed assaults, plague ravaging his army, and no reinforcements possible, Napoleon had to retreat. He later said: "I missed my destiny at Acre." French casualties: 1,200 killed in action, 1,000 dead from disease, 2,500 ill or seriously wounded. His response: he abandoned his army in Egypt and sailed back to France to seize political power. He turned military failure into political opportunity.

**Psychological significance**: Napoleon's response to failure was never to grieve or accept -- it was to pivot aggressively toward the next conquest. Classic Type 8: transmute loss into action.

### Crisis 4: The Escape from Elba (February 1815)

Cut off from his wife and son, his allowance unpaid, aware of rumors he'd be sent somewhere more remote, Napoleon escaped Elba with a small force. Reports from French officers convinced him the monarchy was fragile. When soldiers sent to stop him met him on the road, he opened his coat and said "If any of you will shoot his emperor, here I am." Every soldier joined him. By the time he reached Paris, Louis XVIII had fled.

**Psychological significance**: The Hundred Days wasn't strategy -- it was the Type 8's absolute refusal to accept defeat. Even knowing the odds were impossible, he could not remain passive. He needed to move, to act, to dominate. Rest was more terrifying than war.

### Crisis 5: Waterloo and the Aftermath (June 18, 1815)

During the battle, an aide noted "one saw on Napoleon's face a look of disquietude." Toward eight o'clock in the evening, "seeing that his army was almost beaten, Napoleon commenced to despair." After the defeat, when his brother Lucien urged him to "dare" and declare himself dictator, Napoleon replied with perhaps the most self-aware line of his life: "Alas, I have dared only too much already."

**Psychological significance**: The admission is devastating. After a lifetime of believing that willpower could overcome anything, Napoleon finally encountered the limits of Type 8 energy. The engine that had driven him since Brienne -- the refusal to submit, the compulsion to dare -- was finally acknowledged as both his genius and his destruction.

### Crisis 6: St. Helena -- The Performance Ends (1815-1821)

Stripped of power, Napoleon spent his final years dictating his memoirs and rewriting his legacy. He presented himself as "the liberal saviour of Europe, a Prometheus chained to a rock by reactionary monarchies." He gardened, read, argued with his jailer Hudson Lowe, and slowly declined. Over the years, "Napoleon shut himself away in Longwood, refused visits and spent more and more time alone in his room."

**Psychological significance**: At St. Helena, the Type 8's tools were gone -- no army, no empire, no stage. What remained was the mythmaker, the storyteller, the man who could not stop working even in captivity. His final campaign was for his own historical narrative.

## 6. CHILDHOOD TO ADULT THREAD

### The Corsican Outsider

Born August 15, 1769 -- just one year after France conquered Corsica. Napoleon was ethnically Corsican-Italian, spoke Corsican and Italian as his first languages, and didn't learn French until age 9. His very identity was that of a conquered people.

### Carlo Bonaparte: The Father Who Surrendered

Napoleon's father Carlo initially fought alongside Corsican independence leader Pasquale Paoli against the French. But when independence failed, Carlo switched sides and collaborated with the French occupiers -- securing political appointments and social status for the family. He died of stomach cancer when Napoleon was 15, leaving the family in debt.

**Psychological thread**: Napoleon watched his father choose pragmatism over principle -- surrender identity for social advancement. The son would spend his entire life refusing to surrender anything, to anyone. The father's capitulation became the son's founding wound.

### Brienne Military School: Where the Armor Went On

At age 9, Napoleon was sent to French military school at Brienne. He was "routinely bullied by his peers for his accent, birthplace, short stature, mannerisms, and poor French." He was considered "an upstart from a conquered country." Their cruelties made him "withdraw into dreams of personal glory and military triumphs."

Napoleon later said: "As regards my thinking, it is not Corsica but Brienne that is my native land, because it was there that I formulated my first opinions of mankind."

**Psychological thread**: Brienne is where the Type 8 structure crystallized. A sensitive boy with a "somber and pensive character" who "never played with any one and ordinarily walked by himself" learned that the world was hostile, that weakness would be punished, and that the only safety was dominance. He emerged with "much ability; understood and learned readily" -- channeling all his pain into intellectual and military superiority.

### Letizia Bonaparte: The Iron Mother

Napoleon's mother was the dominant influence of his childhood. Her "firm discipline restrained a rambunctious child." She accompanied her husband on guerrilla campaigns while pregnant with Napoleon. After Carlo's death, she "kept the family afloat through a combination of thrift, charity and the small sums Napoleon sent from his meagre lieutenant's salary."

**Psychological thread**: Letizia modeled the Type 8 virtues -- strength, resilience, refusal to break. She was the one person Napoleon always treated with respect. Her influence created a man who equated love with toughness, and who sought in Josephine (an older, sophisticated woman) a replacement for the maternal warmth/authority his mother represented.

### The Adult Pattern

The childhood formula repeats throughout his life:

- **Outsider status** --> Compulsive need to prove, to dominate, to make himself undeniable
- **Father's surrender** --> Absolute refusal to capitulate or compromise
- **Bullying at Brienne** --> Deep distrust of people combined with desperate need for loyalty
- **Mother's toughness** --> Attraction to strong women, difficulty with emotional vulnerability
- **Intellectual compensation** --> Using knowledge and work as armor against the world

## 7. PUBLIC / PRIVATE GAP

### The Public Napoleon

- The first modern leader to systematically use propaganda
- Controlled his visual representation through commissioned artworks (David's "Napoleon Crossing the Alps" -- heroic, divine, on a white horse)
- His military bulletins were so unreliable that "to lie like a bulletin" became French slang
- His maxim: "It is not what is true that counts, but what people think is true"
- He presented himself as the embodiment of the Revolution, merit over birth
- After defeat, he recast himself as "the liberal saviour of Europe, a Prometheus chained to a rock"

### The Private Napoleon

- Wrote obsessive, desperate love letters dripping with neediness and jealousy
- Cried bitterly when divorcing Josephine; sobbed while signing the papers
- Attempted suicide rather than face exile
- Ate simple food in 15 minutes; preferred roast chicken and pasta to imperial banquets
- Was susceptible to cold and always sat next to a roaring fireplace
- Could fall asleep instantly on a battlefield but was kept awake by Josephine's silence
- At St. Helena, gradually withdrew into isolation, refusing visitors, spending more time alone

### What Surprised People Who Met Him

- **His conversation**: People expected a brute and found a man of "surprising charm" and intellectual depth
- **His memory**: He once met a man ten years after a brief encounter and remembered details about the man's children
- **His absorption speed**: An Italian officer watched Napoleon go from "entirely clueless" to having "expert-level opinion" on a region's politics within hours of a single conversation
- **His physical touch**: He constantly pinched soldiers' ears and cheeks, gave children liquorice from his tortoiseshell box -- a rough tenderness
- **His emotional volatility**: The controlled public figure could erupt into rages, then pivot immediately to calm strategic thinking
- **His simplicity**: Despite all the imperial trappings, those close to him noted "the entire simplicity of his family life"

### The Gap at St. Helena

When the performance was finally over, what emerged was:

- An obsessive memoirist rewriting his legacy
- A man who gardened and argued with his jailer
- Someone who shut himself in his room for increasing periods
- A dying man whose last words still circled back to Josephine -- not France, not glory, but the woman he couldn't hold

## 8. YOUTUBE / DOCUMENTARY / PODCAST RECOMMENDATIONS

### Documentaries

1. **"Napoleon" -- PBS Empires Series (4 parts, ~240 min)**
   - Covers childhood through death, with particular attention to his psychological evolution
   - Strong on the Josephine relationship and the private man behind the myth
   - Available on PBS and streaming platforms
   - **Why it matters**: The most balanced long-form documentary treatment; covers both military genius and personal collapse

2. **"Napoleon" -- BBC Documentary with Andrew Roberts (3 parts, 2015)**
   - Historian Andrew Roberts retraces Napoleon's footsteps with access to personal letters
   - "Sheds new light on Napoleon as an extraordinarily gifted military commander and mesmeric leader, whose private life was littered with disappointments and betrayals"
   - **Why it matters**: Roberts is arguably the premier Napoleon biographer of our time; this is the psychological portrait version

3. **"Napoleon" -- TV Mini Series (2000, IMDB 8.0)**
   - 4-hour dramatic documentary covering the full arc
   - **Why it matters**: Strong ratings suggest a nuanced treatment beyond military history

4. **Ridley Scott's "Napoleon" (2023) + Director's Cut (2024, Apple TV+)**
   - Joaquin Phoenix as Napoleon; focuses heavily on the Josephine relationship
   - Director's cut adds 48 minutes of new footage
   - **Why it matters**: While historically imperfect, Joaquin Phoenix captures the emotional contradictions of the character -- the tenderness, the rage, the neediness beneath the crown

### Podcasts

5. **Founders Podcast -- Episodes #294 and #302 (David Senra)**
   - Episode #302 ("The Mind of Napoleon") is built from Napoleon's own words
   - Host reflects on "Napoleon's extreme personality and the implications of his all-consuming ambition"
   - **Why it matters**: This is the entrepreneurial/psychological lens -- Napoleon as a case study in what happens when ambition consumes everything else

6. **The Napoleon Bonaparte Podcast (Cameron Reilly & J. David Markham)**
   - Long-running deep-dive podcast by Napoleon scholars
   - **Why it matters**: Encyclopedic detail with genuine expertise; good for obscure anecdotes and personality details

7. **The Age of Napoleon Podcast**
   - History podcast covering Napoleon's life and the broader European context
   - "Covers big trends and grand sweep of history as well as smaller, individual stories"
   - **Why it matters**: The smaller individual stories are often the most psychologically revealing

### Books (for deeper research)

8. **"Napoleon Against Himself: A Psychobiography" by Avner Falk**
   - A full psychobiographical treatment of Napoleon
   - **Why it matters**: The most focused psychological analysis available in book form

9. **"Recollections of the Private Life of Napoleon" by Constant (Louis Constant Wairy)**
   - Memoir by Napoleon's personal valet of 15 years
   - Available free on Project Gutenberg
   - **Why it matters**: The most intimate source on who Napoleon was when no one important was watching

10. **"The Mind of Napoleon: A Selection of His Written and Spoken Words" edited by J. Christopher Herold**
    - Curated collection of Napoleon's own words organized thematically
    - **Why it matters**: The best single source for Napoleon in his own voice

## TYPE 8 ANALYSIS FRAMEWORK

### Core Type 8 Patterns in Napoleon

**The 8's Core Fear (Vulnerability/Being Controlled)**:

- Childhood bullying at Brienne created the armor
- Father's capitulation to France became the founding wound -- never surrender
- The suicide attempt reveals what happens when the 8 cannot maintain control
- The desperate love letters show the vulnerability he fought to suppress

**The 8's Core Desire (Protection/Self-Determination)**:

- Built an empire from nothing to ensure he could never be dominated again
- His obsessive work habits (16-hour days since military school) were the engine of self-determination
- He called his soldiers "my children" -- the 8's protective instinct scaled to an army

**The 8's Integration to 2 (The Helper)**:

- His genuine warmth with soldiers, children, and close associates
- The tender lover in the Josephine letters
- The man who was haunted by a grieving dog
- His paternalistic care for his family (appointing siblings to thrones)

**The 8's Disintegration to 5 (The Investigator/Withdrawal)**:

- At St. Helena, the withdrawn recluse who shut himself in his room
- The compartmentalized mind with "different drawers"
- The obsessive reading and intellectual absorption
- The retreat into memoir-writing and myth-construction

**The 8w7 vs 8w9 Question**:
Napoleon is often typed 8w7 (more aggressive, expansionist, restless) but some type him 8w9. Evidence for 8w7: the relentless expansion, the inability to stop, the Hundred Days gamble. Evidence for 8w9: the desire for domestic peace (the Bourrienne quote about wanting "a house with friends and a cabriolet"), the withdrawal at St. Helena, the meditative baths.
