> "It felt like getting an A on a test I didn't study for."

<p class="firstLetter">That's how Nikki Glaser describes the day her classmates noticed she'd lost weight. She hadn't been trying to lose weight. She'd been too nervous to eat before a date. But when people told her she looked great, something locked into place inside her brain. The same something that would nearly kill her within four months. The same something that would eventually make her the best roaster on the planet.</p>

In May 2024, Glaser walked off the Netflix stage after roasting Tom Brady and received a standing ovation. The audience was on its feet. Don Praw, who produces Netflix's roasts, called her "one of, if not the, best roaster I've ever seen." By January 2025, she'd become the first woman to solo host the Golden Globes. They asked her back for 2026. Then 2027.

It would be easy to tell this as a triumph story. The girl from Kirkwood, Missouri who clawed her way up through 22 years of comedy clubs and finally got her moment.

But that framing misses what's actually interesting about Nikki Glaser.

The mechanism that put the audience on its feet for the Brady roast is the same one that made a 17-year-old girl stop eating for three months because strangers said she looked good. That compulsive drive to win validation through performance, to test every joke twelve times, to outwork everyone in the room until the applause is guaranteed.

Same hunger. Different stage.

## How a Compliment Almost Killed Nikki Glaser

The Glaser family moved from Cincinnati to Kirkwood, Missouri when Nikki and her younger sister Lauren were small. Their father EJ took a new job. Their mother Julie felt terrible about uprooting the girls from their grandparents and the support system they had in Ohio.

Nikki grew up Catholic, with Irish and German roots, in a suburb where her sister would eventually return to teach high school Spanish. By all accounts, a stable, loving home. Not the kind of childhood that produces tabloid headlines.

But stable homes can teach quiet lessons. And the lesson Nikki Glaser absorbed, the one that would shape everything that followed, was that being noticed felt better than anything else in the world.

Senior year of high school, 2002. A classmate she had a crush on asked her to hang out. She got so nervous she couldn't eat. The next day at school, people noticed.

"They were like, 'You look great. You look like you've lost weight,'" Glaser told Graham Bensinger. "It felt like getting an A on a test I didn't study for."

She kept the streak going. Not eating became a project. A measurable one, with visible results and daily feedback. Within weeks, her hair was falling out. She had mouth sores from malnutrition. She was so thin it hurt to sit down.

"I stopped eating for like three months," she recalled. "I just got anorexic overnight."

When she went in for a physical before college, doctors told her she wasn't going home. She'd lost nearly 60 pounds in four months. Her father EJ, sitting beside her in the Bensinger interview years later, remembered the terror of that period. The family thought they might lose her.

She didn't die. But the anorexia morphed into bulimia, which she carried quietly, "so much shame and hiding," for the next 18 years.

The detail that matters isn't the eating disorder itself. It's the trigger. A compliment. Positive feedback from people whose opinion she valued. That's not a food issue. That's a wiring issue. And it's the same wiring that would eventually make her one of the hardest-working performers in comedy.

## Comedy Saved Her Life (But Not How You Think)

Glaser started performing stand-up at 18, while studying English at the University of Kansas. She'd transferred there from the University of Colorado Boulder. The comedy clubs gave her something the classrooms couldn't.

"Comedy is a way for me to say those dark things," she told Variety in 2025, "and when I get that relational laugh, well maybe I'm not so weird."

Her family had been afraid her honesty would repel people. It didn't. It drew them in. But what was actually happening wasn't just therapeutic self-expression. It was a transfer of addiction.

The eating disorder had given her measurable feedback: pounds lost, compliments earned, visible progress. Comedy offered the same thing. A joke either lands or it doesn't. An audience laughs or stays silent. A roast gets applause or it bombs. Every night on stage is a report card.

"You can be great at things if you just work harder than anyone else," Glaser has said. She took that belief and applied it to comedy the way she'd applied it to not eating: total, consuming focus.

She got sober from alcohol on December 9, 2011. She credits Allen Carr's book _The Easy Way to Stop Drinking_. Before that, drinking had been "the only thing I looked forward to in my life." She used it to manage her social anxiety and her fear of intimacy. "It was the only way I could have sex because I was scared of sex," she admitted to Bensinger. "I would get blackout drunk."

One addiction replaced another. Drinking replaced the eating disorder. Comedy replaced the drinking. Preparation replaced everything.

The substances changed. The mechanism didn't.

What it built, though, was staggering. A sex-themed Comedy Central talk show (_Not Safe with Nikki Glaser_, 2016). Three Netflix specials. Three seasons hosting _FBoy Island_. A daily morning show on SiriusXM (_You Up_) that she ran for four years. An HBO special (_Someday You'll Die_) that earned Emmy, Grammy, Golden Globe, and Critics Choice nominations and a WGA win. The _New York Times_ named her Comedian of the Year for 2024. _TIME_ put her on the 100 Most Influential People list in 2025. She hosted _SNL_ that November. Her monologue ran nearly nine minutes, one of the longest in a decade. She's currently on the Stunning Tour and has a new Hulu special, _Good Girl_, that she filmed at the Fabulous Fox Theatre in her hometown of St. Louis.

That list reads less like a career than a compulsion. A Three's engine running without a governor.

And the comedy itself? She found her lane early: sex, dating, and the gap between what women actually think and what they're supposed to say. Her 2019 Netflix special _Bangin'_ was an hour of forensically honest material about her sex life, the kind of set where half the audience is laughing because it's funny and the other half is laughing because they've never heard someone say it out loud. She's not a storyteller in the Mulaney mold or an absurdist. She's a confessor. Every joke is built from real shame, real desire, real neurosis. Filed down until it cuts clean.

## What is Nikki Glaser's personality type?

### Nikki Glaser is an Enneagram Type 3

Most people see the funniest woman in America: raw, unfiltered, fearless. The comedian who says what everyone else is thinking. The one who will tell you about her eating disorder, her sex life, her intrusive thoughts about death, and then make you laugh about it.

What they don't see is how carefully constructed that rawness is.

Enneagram Threes carry a core wound that forms early: the belief that they are only as valuable as what they achieve. That without external validation (applause, compliments, measurable success) they might not exist at all. The emotion that drives them isn't anger or fear. It's shame. A shame so deep they've built their entire personality around outrunning it.

The evidence in Glaser's life is overwhelming:

- **Format-shifting as dominance**: Stand-up, hosting, podcasting, reality TV, roasting, award show emceeing. Threes don't just work hard; they colonize every format where success is measurable. She didn't just keep doing stand-up. She kept finding new rooms to win.
- **Strategic transparency**: She's built a brand on radical honesty, and she's aware it gives her a competitive edge. As her manager Alex Murray puts it, "Her vulnerability combined with an insane work ethic is what separates her from other comedians." She turned her deepest wounds into her unique selling proposition.
- **Collecting proof of existence**: NYT Comedian of the Year. TIME100 Most Influential. First solo female Golden Globes host. Emmy, Grammy, and Golden Globe nominations in a single cycle. A Three doesn't frame these as nice-to-haves. These are evidence that she's real.
- **Achievement as emotional anesthesia**: She's said it plainly: "the obsession with work is just not wanting to feel." A Three articulating the machine from inside the machine.
- **Fear of being average**: "I just want to be extraordinary. I don't want to be average. I don't want to be forgotten. I don't want to be not seen. Because I felt that way so much."

The 2 wing is strong. This isn't a cold, corporate Three. Glaser leads with warmth, connection, humor that draws people in rather than distancing them. Lisa Lampanelli, herself a legendary roaster, observed that great roasters succeed "because they have an underlying kindness and a good goal. She leads with her heart and her vulnerability." Fellow comedian Esther Povitsky called Glaser's work "a service to the world, especially to women of all ages."

The 3w2 charm is real. But so is the drive beneath it. When she says she doesn't want to be forgotten, she means it the way Threes mean everything: as an existential statement, not a preference.

## The Best Roaster on the Planet Cries Before Every Show

Glaser's preparation for the Tom Brady roast is a case study in how her mind works.

She started months in advance. She assembled a team: writers Sean O'Conner and Jessica Dweck, plus her longtime boyfriend and collaborator Chris Convy. They generated material. She took it on the road. She tested every line in front of live audiences, night after night, tracking what hit and what didn't with the precision of someone running A/B tests on their own psyche.

"I thrive on anxiety," she told CBS News. "I like feeling like, 'Oh, what's gonna happen?' I like that feeling. I kill it when I'm anxious."

But the preparation wasn't just about jokes. After every roast, she has to do "a cleanse" because her mind goes to "constantly thinking the worst thing about someone, looking at pictures of them thinking, what is something I can think about them that is going to haunt them the rest of their life?"

Weaponized empathy, dressed up as craft. She understands people deeply enough to identify their most devastating vulnerability, then says it into a microphone while they're sitting ten feet away.

Indiewire called her "the best roaster on the planet." Then she went home and had to scrub the cruelty out of her thinking.

"Whenever I've done a Comedy Central Roast, I've done three of them and at the end of every single one I tell my friends to never let me do another one," she told Interview Magazine.

She kept doing them. She keeps doing them. The Three can't resist a stage where winning is this measurable.

The Golden Globes followed the same pattern. She studied what had gone wrong when Jo Koy hosted the year before. She prepared like she was defending a thesis, not telling jokes. Then she walked out on January 5, 2025, became the first woman to solo host the ceremony, and pulled 9.17 million viewers. She hosted again in 2026. When they asked about 2027, she said yes with a joke about finally being equal to her sister, who has three kids.

Her _SNL_ debut that November followed the same pattern. Some material drew backlash. She didn't care. Controversy is still attention, and attention is oxygen.

## When the Machine Breaks Down

For all the ovations, there are stretches where the performance couldn't hold.

The drinking, which ended in 2011, had been filling the same role as the eating disorder. Numbing the space between performances. "I drank every single night of my life and it was, like, the only thing I looked forward to in my life."

There was the shoplifting. She's talked about it openly: an addiction to stealing, a compulsion she couldn't explain. One day she got caught by a manager at Urban Outfitters. She went home and punished herself with self-harm.

The eating disorder persisted for 18 years, long past the initial hospitalization. The anorexia became bulimia. The bulimia became an obsessive relationship with food that consumed her mental energy even as her career accelerated.

Growing up, she'd struggled with ADD, OCD, and depression. The OCD manifested as intrusive thoughts, dark unwanted images that cycle through the mind on repeat. She titled her second HBO special _Someday You'll Die_ after the intrusive thought she couldn't shake.

"I was plagued with shame, depression and feeling different," she told Variety.

A reviewer of the special noted that "Glaser is a walking intrusive thought, and her giving voice to her darker musings helps people feel better about having their own."

When Threes are under extreme stress, something disorienting happens. The drive evaporates. The engine that never stopped running just... stops. In Enneagram terms, a Three in stress takes on the unhealthy patterns of a [Type 9](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-9): numbness, paralysis, an inability to connect with what they want or feel. The achiever becomes the avoider.

Glaser's darkest periods map this arc precisely. The drinking wasn't ambition. The shoplifting wasn't thrilling. The eating disorder wasn't control. They were all the same thing: a person who runs on external validation reaching for anything to fill the void when the applause stops.

"When I'm about to do [Howard Stern](/personality-analysis/howard-stern), I'm also preparing for things he's not going to say to me because no one is as mean to me as me."

That line cuts deeper than any roast joke she's ever written.

## Why Nikki Glaser Went Home

When COVID hit in 2020, Glaser did something she hadn't done in years. She stopped.

She moved back to St. Louis. Back to her parents' house in Des Peres. Back to the suburb where she'd grown up, where her sister still taught Spanish at Kirkwood High.

She partly went because she was afraid of being alone and bingeing. The same month she moved home, she enrolled in a 12-step eating disorder program. After 18 years of hiding, she finally asked for help.

"When you stop fighting it, when you stop trying to control it, your body will just be what it needs to be," she said about her recovery.

She lived with her parents for 10 months. As one profile put it, "when she got a taste of a life that wasn't constantly hustling, she wanted more and didn't want to go back." Something shifted.

She didn't move back to New York. She stayed in St. Louis. Got her own apartment in the Central West End with her best friend and podcast co-host Andrew Collin. April 28 was declared Nikki Glaser Day by the mayor. She didn't need to be in a major entertainment hub anymore. She'd realized that the geography was never the point.

But she's still a Three. So she turned the homecoming into a TV show. _Welcome Home Nikki Glaser?_ aired on E! in 2022, cameras following her around Kirkwood, rollerblading at Steinberg, reconnecting with her ex Chris Convy, eating dinner with her parents. Even the quiet personal chapter got optimized into content. The question mark in the title was the most honest part.

Her father EJ, reflecting on the family move from Cincinnati decades earlier, had once said: "I felt so horrible taking them away from their grandparents and all the support system we had."

Thirty years later, his daughter came back to build exactly that. A support system. A community that existed independent of what she achieved.

When Threes grow, they move toward the healthy qualities of Type 6: loyalty, genuine trust, commitment to something beyond personal success. Glaser's return to St. Louis, her entry into a 12-step program, her willingness to stay near family rather than chase opportunities on the coasts. This is what growth looks like for someone wired to measure their worth by the last round of applause.

## Nikki Glaser and Chris Convy

She met Chris Convy in 2013. He was a producer on her MTV show _Nikki & Sara Live_. She thought he was drunk because of how fun he was. Then she found out he didn't drink.

They've broken up and gotten back together "probably five times in 10 years," with breaks ranging from two days to three years. He produces her specials. He co-writes with her. He's the person who sees behind the performance.

Convy is the answer to a question Threes are terrified to ask: _Would someone love me if I wasn't impressive?_

The fact that their relationship has survived a decade of breaking apart and rebuilding says something about both of them, but especially about Glaser. She keeps coming back. Not because he validates her career. Because he knew her before the career mattered.

"I'm so in love," she says about him now. And when she says it, you believe her, not because Nikki Glaser is always honest, but because this is one of the few parts of her life she hasn't tried to optimize.

## The Song She Hasn't Written Yet

In her Interview Magazine conversation with Cara Delevingne, Glaser said something that stopped the interview cold:

"I'd like to write one good song before I die. One that makes people feel 1/128th as emotional as I feel listening to 98% of [Taylor Swift](/personality-analysis/taylor-swift) or Wilco's catalog. I'd like to leave behind something sincere, which is hard to do as a comedian."

_Which is hard to do as a comedian._

She knows. She's always known. The comedy is real, the pain underneath the jokes is genuine, the self-awareness is earned, the honesty is not fake. But it's also a performance. Every dark confession has been tested in front of twelve audiences before you hear it. Every raw moment has been refined until the rawness itself is polished.

Don't call it dishonest. She learned at 17 that the only way to be loved is to be extraordinary at something. The performance is the price.

She wants to write one sincere song. She wants to leave behind something that didn't have to get a laugh first. Something that wasn't workshopped, wasn't A/B tested, wasn't refined into a weapon.

She hasn't written it yet. She's gotten sober, survived an eating disorder, hosted the Golden Globes three times, been called the best roaster alive, moved home, found love, entered recovery, and taped a special at the Fabulous Fox in the city she chose over New York. But the thing she actually wants, the thing that scares her, is something quiet and imperfect and sincere.

That's the paradox. She's healthier than she's ever been. She's also hosting award shows for the third year running and filming specials in sold-out theaters. The machine didn't stop. It runs on cleaner fuel now. Recovery instead of starvation. Preparation instead of alcohol. But it still runs. Growth for a Three doesn't mean the engine shuts off. It means learning to drive it instead of being dragged behind it.

The bravest thing Nikki Glaser could do? Sit alone with a guitar and sing something bad. Something nobody claps for. Something that proves she exists even when the audience is gone.

She hasn't done it yet. But she named it. And for someone who spent 18 years hiding behind achievements, naming what you actually want might be the hardest performance of all.
