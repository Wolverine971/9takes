> "I was the eldest daughter of two, and a child of divorce, so there was a lot of: look at me, love me."
> — Olivia Cooke, The Guardian, 2024

<p class="firstLetter">For two seasons, House of the Dragon fans complained that Alicent Hightower always looks like she is about to cry. Olivia Cooke, who plays her, offered the simplest possible defense. That is just what her face does when she is in distress, she has joked, and Alicent spends most of the show in distress, so short of surgery there was not much to be done.</p>

It lands as a joke, and it is one. It is also the most honest thing a working actor has said about their own casting in years. Directors keep hiring Cooke to play women who are quietly coming apart, and she keeps saying yes, because the wiring is already installed and idling. And the wiring is the whole story: what makes her convincing as a woman who cannot find her own footing is that she has spent a lifetime tracking the loss of hers. The gift and the affliction are the same equipment.

She is very funny about all of it. That is the first thing people notice: the self-deprecating Manchester wit, the reflex to say the unflattering thing about herself before anyone else can, the habit of attaching herself to the loudest jokers on any set and daring them to misbehave. The vigilance underneath the humor takes longer to see.

Cooke has spoken about a stretch of her early twenties that nearly broke her. She deleted her Instagram, twice, because she does not trust herself around strangers' opinions. On a set the size of a small country, she keeps a script advisor on hand to answer a question she cannot always answer alone: where, emotionally, is she today. All of that belongs to one person. And that person is a textbook study in a single Enneagram type.

## What is Olivia Cooke's personality type?

### Olivia Cooke is an Enneagram Type 6

Type 6, the Loyalist, runs on one question: what if the people and structures you rely on quietly give out? Sixes scan for threat, rehearse failure, and bind themselves to the few they decide they can trust. Their fear is having nothing solid underneath. Their wish is to feel safe. You can read Olivia Cooke off that operating system.

Watch how she guards herself on set. She refuses to method-act her way into a character's despair and live there between takes. She has called herself too sensitive to stay in that headspace, and told Who What Wear in 2025 that "method acting is for men usually." An actor chasing prestige marinates in the darkness. Cooke keeps one foot on dry land, because the moment she sinks all the way into the dread, it stops being a performance and turns back into her actual early twenties. She would rather stay slightly outside the character and safe than fully inside and exposed.

Now the skeptic's objection, because it is a fair one. Anxiety by itself is not a personality type. Plenty of people live with dread and never build a whole life around managing it the way Cooke does. That is exactly right, and it is why the anxiety alone proves nothing. The [Type 6](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-6) shows up in the specific moves she makes around the fear: the alliance she forms with a trusted guide, the humor she uses to preempt judgment, the ferocious loyalty to a chosen few, the way validation deepens the doubt instead of easing it. House of the Dragon opened in August 2022 to nearly 10 million U.S. viewers on night one, the biggest series premiere in HBO's history. The more proof of belonging arrived, the louder the voice telling her she had smuggled her way in.

Hold onto that shape. The world hands Cooke evidence that she has made it, and some part of her keeps filing that evidence under not-yet-confirmed.

## Why Olivia Cooke still feels like she doesn't belong

She was born in Oldham, outside Manchester, in 1993. Her father was a police officer, her mother a sales rep, and they divorced when she was young. She started at the Oldham Theatre Workshop at eight and spent years buried in the ensemble before anyone handed her a lead.

That childhood line, "look at me, love me," describes the exact soil a Six grows in. Being loved in that house meant being seen and being good, and once the adults stopped agreeing on the rules, safety became something she had to keep earning.

She earned it the hard way, working service jobs from fourteen, in salons and cafés, before acting paid for anything. Her first real job on camera came young: a BBC drama, _Blackout_, playing Christopher Eccleston's daughter, which took her out of college before her A-levels were finished. The work she was leaving was the precarious kind, where a manager can decide on a Tuesday that they no longer need you, and there is no cushion when they do.

<p class="inner-thought">No reason required, no notice given. That was the lesson under every shift she pulled: anything she leaned on could vanish overnight, and the only way she would ever feel safe was to get good enough that no one could send her home.</p>

The RADA story is the one that never healed cleanly. She has told it since her earliest press: she made the final round of auditions for Britain's most prestigious drama school (Den of Geek, 2018) and did not get in, wondering aloud whether her thick northern accent was the reason. The same week, she landed the lead in a film. She jokes now about which of the two counted as the win, and then, quieter, admits she carried a sense that this was not how it was supposed to go, that she was meant to earn her craft at drama school first. A Three banks the film role and forgets the rejection. Cooke banked the film and kept the rejection, and it still surfaces. She has described feeling under-trained and not quite tuned next to the drama-school crowd, a thinner grounding in the classics, and has talked about giving herself the theatre education she feels she missed, closing a gap only she can see.

Part of that gap is class, and she named it precisely in her 2025 Who What Wear cover profile. Breaking into British acting's inner circles, she said, is "like trying to cut through steel with a twig. It's impossible to penetrate." The Eton and Harrow crowd share a shorthand, a way of smoothing over a first meeting by asking which school you went to, and it was a reference point she simply never had. Her mother now scoffs at the label and tells her, "you're not working class any more." Cooke disagrees: her sensibility is still working class, she says; she has just become, against the odds, very successful in her field.

None of the winning ever quite silenced the doubt. In 2015 she took Me and Earl and the Dying Girl to Sundance, shaved her head to play Rachel, a teenager dying of leukaemia, and came home with the festival's top two prizes and a name. It was the film that made her, and even it handed her a girl whose body was failing.

Her most-seen film looks, at first, like the exception. In Steven Spielberg's _Ready Player One_ she plays Art3mis, the confident rebel leading a revolt inside a virtual world. Then you notice what the character spends the movie doing: hiding a birthmark she is sure makes her unlovable, refusing to let the hero see her real face. The one time the role is a winner, the winner is still braced to be found out.

Cory Finley, who directed her in _Thoroughbreds_, saw the machinery up close. She "takes a very extreme character and makes her very, very human," he said. He also watched her produce real tears on command, then drop straight into deadpan comedy, four takes in a row. "There were no fake tears," he marveled. Cooke can summon the interior weather at will because she lives in it. [Anya Taylor-Joy](/personality-analysis/anya-taylor-joy), her scene partner and closest friend from that shoot, put the intensity another way: "I can't slack in the slightest because I've got to hit her with exactly the amount of force that she's hitting me." By the end of the first day, Taylor-Joy said, "we became symbiotic, almost. I would tuck my hair behind my left ear, and she would be doing the exact same thing."

The speed of that fusion is the tell. A Six does not collect acquaintances; she finds the one or two people who feel safe and bonds to them at the molecular level. With Taylor-Joy it showed up as synchronized hair-tucking. With the Bates Motel cast it became calling them "a family away from home." On House of the Dragon it means gravitating to the "naughty boys," the Rhys Ifanses and Matt Smiths, and egging them into mischief so the tension has somewhere to go.

## Why Olivia Cooke deleted her Instagram

Recruiting a trusted few is only half of how a Six manages the fear. The other half is walling off the inputs she cannot control. Twice now she has removed the app from her life, most recently in 2026 while filming abroad, and both times she has described the same relief: she got her own mind back. Her reasoning is blunt. She does not think she is resilient enough to have access to that many people and their opinions, and she doubts anyone is.

To some people that reads as ingratitude. She wanted a huge career, got one, and then complains about the attention that comes with it. Set that against what she has actually lived through, though, and the move looks like something else entirely. For a woman whose own mind once turned on her hard enough that she wanted out of it, a feed of strangers grading her face and her body is not a mild annoyance. It is a direct line back to the worst version of her own head. Deleting it is not fragility. It is triage. She is protecting the one instrument she performs with, and the deletion is a Six sealing off a known source of threat.

The same logic explains the self-deprecation that charms everyone who meets her. Ranking herself below the drama-school kids, waving off her own success as luck, owning up to "huge impostor syndrome": this is a person saying the cruel thing about herself first, out loud, so that no one else gets to say it to her. Get to the verdict before the room does, and the room cannot ambush you.

## Olivia Cooke's anxiety and the years she felt "tortured from within"

The full picture is darker than the wit lets on. When Cooke moved to America in her early twenties for Bates Motel, alone, the ground she stood on gave way. She told the Sunday Times in 2025 about "panic attacks every day for three weeks," followed by obsessive-compulsive disorder and intrusive thoughts that ran for roughly three years, sliding into isolation and depression. It felt, she said, like being "tortured from within," and at the bottom of it she thought, "I don't want to be here any more." The incessant anxious thoughts only began to lift around 2019.

She has a theory about why it took that shape. "I don't like to display any intense emotion in my personal life," she told the Hollywood Reporter in 2025. "Any hard emotions I push to the side and repress pop out in other horrible ways." The panic and the intrusive thoughts were the pressure finding a valve. Push the fear down far enough and the mind starts manufacturing its own.

The single most Six thing Cooke does happens on set. House of the Dragon is shot out of order across seven-month blocks and multiple years, and she cannot always tell where her character is emotionally at any given moment. So she leans on the show's script advisor to tell her where her character stands emotionally at any given moment. She needs a trusted authority to hand her a coordinate, and once she has it she can act with total conviction. Under real pressure that reliance can tip into overwork, a compulsion to prove competence by never stopping. When she is well, it settles into a plainer wish for calm. The mechanics of both directions live in the rabbit hole below.

### Olivia Cooke's Wing: 6w7

The evidence points to a 6 with a strong 7 wing. Pure Sixes can curdle inward under anxiety and stay there. Cooke reaches for the exit instead. On set she seeks out the jokers, breaks the tension with laughing fits, and treats comedy as survival gear rather than decoration. She gravitates to whoever will make her laugh and refuses to sit in a character's despair a second longer than the take requires. That reflex to escape dread through play, connection, and fun is the 7 wing doing its work. There is a 5-ish undertow too, in the reclusiveness and the retreat from public life, but the warmth and the need for company win out. Read more on how the neighboring numbers color a core type in the guide to <a href="/enneagram-corner/enneagram-wings-complete-guide">Enneagram wings</a>.

### Olivia Cooke's Instinctual Subtype: Self-Preservation 6

This is the warm, alliance-seeking, safety-first variant, and it fits her almost too well. The self-preservation Six manages fear by building a protected nest and a trusted inner circle. Cooke's entire private architecture is a nest: "I don't like to go out, I don't like to be seen," "hiding in my living room," the anonymity she "loved and cherished" and mourned when fame threatened it. Her friendliness is not performance; it is how a phobic Six recruits protectors. She befriends fast and deep, and she keeps the world at arm's length everywhere else. See how the instincts reshape each type in the <a href="/enneagram-corner/enneagram-instinctual-subtypes">instinctual subtypes</a> overview.

### Stress and Growth Arrows

Under stress, a Six moves toward the unhealthy side of Type 3: driven, image-managing, compulsively productive as a way to outrun the dread. You can see it in the version of Cooke who moved countries alone and worked relentlessly while privately falling apart. In growth, the Six moves toward the calm of Type 9. That is the Cooke of the last few years: the anxious thoughts quieting after 2019, the relief of getting her "mind back," and, at 32, telling the Telegraph that after "lots of therapy" she can finally say, "Olivia, just rest." ### Counterarguments: Why Olivia Cooke Might Not Be Type 6

The strongest alternate case is Type 4. She is an actress drawn to melancholy and darkness, and she works from deep emotion. But Fours amplify and inhabit their feelings, cultivating a sense of being singular. Cooke does the reverse: she represses emotion, downplays herself as "low-key and quite non-descript," and treats her own specialness as slightly embarrassing. The darkness she reaches for is dread, the low hum that something is about to go wrong. A Four's darkness runs on longing for what they lack. The One reading has some pull as well, given the inner critic and the drive to fix herself, though what governs her is fear rather than anger at imperfection. The tiebreaker is the alliance-seeking. A Four withdraws into a private world; a One trusts their own standard. Cooke outsources her emotional coordinates to a trusted guide and bonds herself to a chosen few. That reflex to find safety in another person, more than the anxiety itself, is what makes the Six call hold.

## What Olivia Cooke wants now

At 32, on the far side of the fame she spent years trying to sidestep, Cooke has started saying the quiet part out loud, and she is clear that the calm did not arrive on its own. One image sticks. A show shot over years and out of sequence, a character whose feelings even the actor cannot always find, and Cooke turning to the one person she trusts to ask: where am I. She has built an entire career being paid to locate the exact inner weather of women who cannot locate their own, because it is the thing she has practiced longest, on herself, in the dark.

The worried face was never a costume. It is the one part she never auditioned for, and the only one she has never managed to turn down.
