> "I'm a very different person than I was 10 years ago, than I was 15 years ago."
> — Pete Hegseth, The Megyn Kelly Show, 2024

<p class="firstLetter">June 14, 2015. Fox & Friends is live from the plaza, and Pete Hegseth has an axe in his hand. The segment is a stunt: throw it at a target, flex for the cameras, cut to commercial. He winds up and lets go. The axe sails past the target and hits Jeff Prosperie, a drummer in West Point's Hellcats band, who is performing behind it in uniform.</p>

Prosperie needed treatment and eventually sued. The clip still circulates. A television host, dressed up as a frontiersman for a morning show, wounding an actual soldier with a real axe.

Nine and a half years later, the United States Senate deadlocked 50 to 50 on whether to hand that host the entire American military. It took the vice president's tiebreak to confirm him, only the second cabinet rescue of its kind in the country's history (Axios, January 2025).

The clip plays as a joke. The credentials do not. Princeton. Two Bronze Stars. Three deployments: Guantanamo, Iraq, Afghanistan. He has stood post in places his critics have only read about.

So the question that actually explains him is smaller and stranger than the confirmation fight suggested. Why does a man with the real resume still need the cameras this badly?

His personality type answers that question. And once you see it, the axe, the tattoos, the Signal chats, and the purge of the generals stop reading as separate scandals. They become one pattern, repeating.

## What is Pete Hegseth's personality type?

Pete Hegseth's personality type is Enneagram 3, the Achiever. His identity runs on certified, visible winning: the valedictorian speech, the Bronze Stars, the prime-time slot, the cabinet title. The pattern needs witnesses, which is why, since the Army, every job he has held has come with a camera attached.

His boss got there first. Trump slapped his war secretary's bicep for the cameras at a cabinet meeting and delivered the review, as The Independent clipped it in May 2026: "We have great people, and the Secretary of War, Pete Hegseth, Central Casting. He loves war."

Trump meant it as praise, and he chose the exact vocabulary of the pattern. Casting. The look. The part. He hired a war secretary the way a producer books a lead, and the lead has spent his whole tenure proving the casting right.

### Pete Hegseth is an Enneagram Type 3

The [Enneagram Type 3](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-3) is called the Achiever. The math is simple and brutal: somewhere early, worth got fused to winning, and a win only counts if someone sees it. A Three grows into whatever shape the nearest audience will certify.

The ladder is the plainest exhibit. Valedictorian at Forest Lake in 1999. Princeton. Infantry officer with two Bronze Stars. Harvard graduate degree. Prime-time Fox host. Secretary of War at 44. And alongside the ladder, a publishing schedule: four books between 2016 and 2024, each one arriving right as he needed a bigger platform, from In the Arena when he flirted with political office to The War on Warriors five months before Trump picked him.

The diagnosis gets its sharpest evidence from a 32-day window. On November 7, 2024, he told the Shawn Ryan Show: "I'm straight up just saying we should not have women in combat roles. It hasn't made us more effective, hasn't made us more lethal, has made fighting more complicated." Within a week CNN had resurfaced the clip alongside a decade of similar statements, Trump nominated him anyway, and the audience became the United States Senate, which includes women who flew combat missions.

Thirty-two days after the podcast, in December 2024, he sat across from Sean Hannity on Fox News: "some of our greatest warriors, our best warriors are women." Senator Elizabeth Warren did the math out loud at the January 2025 confirmation hearing: "a very, very big about-face in a very, very short period of time." Then she filed it under a genre: "I've heard of deathbed conversions, but this is the first time I've heard of a nomination conversion" (NPR).

The reversal got its epilogue in July 2026, when The New York Times reported that Hegseth had blocked the promotions of seven senior Navy officers, five of them women or people of color, among them Amy Bauernschmidt, the first woman to command a nuclear-powered aircraft carrier. For the first time in more than a decade, no active-duty Navy woman is likely to make admiral this year. No podcast, no hearing, no explanation. The Senate heard the recalibrated answer. The promotion list runs on the original one.

The Type 8 reading, the Challenger, tracks his aggression and misses his mechanism. An 8 plants a costly position and dares the room to take it. Hegseth adjusts the position and keeps the room. (Type nerds: the wing, the subtype, and the best argument that this call is wrong are boxed up in the 🐇 section after the extremist chapter.)

The Senate, for its part, split exactly in half. Lisa Murkowski put her refusal in writing, in a statement posted to X on January 23, 2025: "I must conclude that I cannot in good conscience support his nomination for Secretary of Defense." Fifty votes to fifty, and [JD Vance](/personality-analysis/jd-vance) walked in to break the tie. The only other vice president ever forced to do that for a cabinet seat was Mike Pence, for Betsy DeVos, in 2017.

A man confirmed by the thinnest margin in Pentagon history had two options: quietly earn the room he barely won, or perform for it. Everything that followed answered which.

## Pete Hegseth's Childhood: The Coach's Son Who Kept Score

The pattern was installed early, in a house that kept score. Peter Brian Hegseth was born in Minneapolis in 1980 and raised in Forest Lake, a small town north of the Twin Cities. His father, Brian, coached high school basketball across Minnesota for decades. His mother, Penelope, coached executives. The family trade, on both sides, was measurable improvement in other people.

A coach's house has a particular emotional weather. Effort is visible. Results are posted. Love is rarely conditional in such homes, but attention often is, and a sharp kid learns the difference early.

His father read him accurately even then. Pete looked forward to the Christian basketball camps every summer, Brian told the Minnesota Star Tribune, though he suspected the Bible verses and testimony-sharing ran a distant second to the court time with friends. Even at church camp, the boy had found the part with a scoreboard.

In this house the lesson came with a gym attached: Brian Hegseth coached the boys' basketball team at Forest Lake Area High School, the same school where his son suited up at point guard. Look at the shape of Pete's game there. Undersized and slow for the position, he built his value on the one shot the scorebook weights extra, and left holding school records for three-pointers in a season, three-pointers in a career, and three-point percentage.

"I was a short, slow guy who had no business playing basketball," he told the Forest Lake Press in 2015. "But I did the best I possibly could." The team reached the state tournament in 1999, the same spring he graduated valedictorian, and the school eventually put him in its hall of fame for academics and athletics both.

Then came the choice that quietly runs the rest of the story. He had West Point in front of him, the warrior institution, the uniform. He picked Princeton instead, largely because of basketball. At 18, offered the arena or the Ivy League, he took the prestige.

He spent the next twenty years correcting for it. ROTC commission in 2003. A 90-page senior thesis, submitted that April, on how American presidents talk about war (The Daily Princetonian). Guantanamo in 2004 as an infantry platoon leader. Iraq in 2005, Baghdad and Samarra, and a Bronze Star. Afghanistan in 2011 as a counterinsurgency instructor, and a second one. Each deployment was real. Each also repaired something the Princeton choice had left open.

These are the years the type cannot fully claim, and the record deserves that said plainly. Nobody volunteers for Samarra to polish a resume, the mortars did not care about his brand, and there were no cameras at the checkpoints. He went back anyway, twice. If the Achiever pattern explains anything about those years, look past the courage to what happened to it afterward: every tour became a chapter, every chapter a segment, and the uniform something he could put back on under studio lights. A man can be brave and still need you to watch.

By his early thirties he held degrees from Princeton and Harvard and medals from two wars. The resume was complete. What he needed next was somewhere big enough to display it.

## The Decade Pete Hegseth Fell Apart Off Camera

The display came through veterans advocacy, and then it came apart in private while the cameras stayed friendly.

Hegseth ran two veterans organizations in his late twenties and early thirties, Vets for Freedom and then Concerned Veterans for America. By The New Yorker's 2024 account, both pushed him out, amid reports of financial mismanagement and drinking. A whistleblower report written by former CVA employees in February 2015 described him being repeatedly intoxicated at official events. At a November 2014 event at a Louisiana strip club, staff said, he had to be physically stopped from climbing on stage with the dancers. One complaint described him drunk in an Ohio bar chanting "Kill All Muslims." Hegseth denied the accounts and called them anonymous smears.

What makes the period psychologically legible rather than just sordid is the split screen: while the reports piled up privately, his public trajectory only climbed. Fox brought him on as a contributor in 2014. By 2017 he was co-hosting Fox & Friends Weekend, grinning through the 6 a.m. light. The performer was rising exactly as fast as the man was sinking, and nobody watching could see the second line on the chart. When a man built on visible winning hits that kind of internal collapse, the slide runs toward numbing and autopilot rather than confession, a spiral [the stress research](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-types-in-stress) maps closely.

Even his vices got delivered as content. In February 2019 he announced on air, "I don't think I've washed my hands for 10 years," then explained that germs are invisible and therefore not real. He later called it a joke. Watch the clip and you see something more specific: a man who cannot let even hygiene be private, off-script, unwitnessed.

The private ledger from those years is documented and ugly. His first marriage ended in 2009 after he admitted infidelities. His second marriage produced three sons and ended after he had a daughter with a Fox producer, Jennifer Rauchet, in 2017. That October, a woman in Monterey accused him of sexual assault; he said the encounter was consensual, prosecutors declined to charge him, and he later paid a confidential settlement while denying wrongdoing.

And in April 2018, his own mother put the sharpest version on the record, in an email The New York Times would publish six years later. "You are an abuser of women," Penelope Hegseth wrote to her son, adding that she had "no respect for any man that belittles, lies, cheats, sleeps around, and uses women for his own power and ego."

Power and ego. His mother, the executive coach, had diagnosed the engine.

She has since disavowed the email, saying she wrote it in haste and anger. In December 2024 she sat across from a Fox anchor and delivered the counter-verdict: "Pete is a new person. He's redeemed, forgiven, changed." Hegseth himself dates the turn to 2018, to a New Jersey church service about brokenness, to Jenny, to faith. He remarried in 2019, promised senators he would not drink if confirmed, and framed the whole arc in the line this piece opened with: a different person now, the old one archived.

Maybe both verdicts are true. What is beyond argument is which one got the prime-time slot. In this family, even redemption airs on Fox & Friends.

## What Being Called an Extremist Did to Pete Hegseth

Two years into the redemption arc, the Army issued a verdict of its own. In January 2021, Major Pete Hegseth of the D.C. National Guard was set to help guard Joe Biden's inauguration. Twelve guardsmen were pulled from that duty after FBI vetting. A fellow guardsman had flagged Hegseth as a possible insider threat, citing a tattoo: the phrase "Deus Vult," a Crusades slogan, inked on his bicep near the Jerusalem cross on his chest.

His orders were revoked by phone. "My commander called me a day before," he recalled on the Shawn Ryan Show, "and was like, major, you can just stand down."

<div class="pull-quote">"I joined the army to fight extremists in 2001. 20 years later, that same army labeled me one."</div>

That line, from the same interview, is the closest thing to a skeleton key for his entire tenure at the Pentagon.

Be precise about what happened, because both halves matter. The flag was defensible: a year earlier Hegseth had published American Crusade, a book that closes with "See you on the battlefield. Together, with God's help, we will save America. Deus vult!" A guardsman who connected the ink to the book was doing exactly the vigilance the moment demanded. And the humiliation was real anyway: a man with two Bronze Stars, told by voicemail-level courtesy that his own army would rather he stay home.

For a Three, that specific injury lands harder than almost any other, because the whole psychological economy runs on certification. Grades, medals, ratings, titles: external bodies confirming worth. The Army was the institution whose certification he had bled for, twice, and it looked at him and issued the opposite finding. It stamped him a threat.

<p class="inner-thought">Twenty years. Two wars. Two Bronze Stars. And the only thing they saw was the ink.</p>

He quit the Guard. He wrote The War on Warriors, a book arguing the military had been captured by a woke ideology that hunts its own best men. And when Trump handed him the Pentagon in January 2025, he started firing certifiers. The chairman of the Joint Chiefs, gone in his first month. The chief naval officer, gone. The top military lawyers, the JAGs who rule on what conduct counts as acceptable, gone.

His supporters call it restoring the warrior ethos. His critics call it a political purge. Watch it through the stand-down call and a third reading opens, the personal one: a man who was mislabeled by a system, as he sees it, now deleting the system's authority to label anyone at all. You do not have to excuse a single firing to understand the hunger driving them. Anyone who has been branded the problem by an institution they loved knows the fantasy of walking back in with the power to re-decide.

Hegseth got to live the fantasy. That is what makes him legible, and what makes him dangerous to the institution: the re-deciding never seems to finish.

### Pete Hegseth's Wing: 3w2

The 3w2 achieves through people, and Hegseth's whole broadcast career is warmth deployed at scale: the grin, the first-name banter, the morning-show approachability that made Fox viewers feel he was their guy. A 3w4 builds a portfolio and lets it speak; Hegseth works the couch, the greenroom, and the president. His redemption story is also classically 2-flavored, told through relationships (Jenny, the pastor, his mother's forgiveness) rather than solitary discipline. ### Pete Hegseth's Instinctual Subtype: Social 3

The Social 3 pursues prestige through group-visible markers: titles, insignia, institutional rank. Hegseth's first year in office was a festival of them. ## Pete Hegseth's Signal Chats: The After-Action Review He Won't Write

The Army has a ritual for failure. It is called the after-action review, and every unit Hegseth ever led ran them: what was planned, what actually happened, why, and what changes. The AAR only works if the leader can say the miss out loud.

Here is the one his Pentagon never filed.

**What was planned.** March 15, 2025: U.S. strikes on Houthi targets in Yemen. Compartmented, time-sensitive, the kind of operation where leaked launch windows can kill pilots.

**What actually happened.** The national security adviser accidentally added Jeffrey Goldberg, editor-in-chief of The Atlantic, to a Signal group chat named "Houthi PC small group." Hegseth posted strike timing and aircraft details into it before the jets flew. A month later, The New York Times reported a second Signal chat, this one on his personal phone, where he shared strike details with his wife, his brother, and his personal lawyer.

**What the institution found.** The Pentagon's own inspector general concluded, per officials familiar with the December 2025 report, that his Signal posts had risked endangering troops. His former chief spokesman, John Ullyot, a loyalist who had defended him through confirmation, resigned and went public: "It's been a month of total chaos at the Pentagon." And: "the Pentagon focus is no longer on warfighting, but on endless drama." Ullyot is not a resistance figure. He is the friendly witness, and his testimony is the one that costs Hegseth something.

**What he said.** "Nobody was texting war plans. And that's all I have to say about that." Then he attacked Goldberg as a "deceitful and highly discredited so-called journalist." No miss acknowledged, no change named. The AAR requires the one sentence he has never said on camera: I got that wrong.

And the missing AAR has a prehistory, because the reflex predates the building. In 2019, when his only command was a couch on a morning show, Hegseth privately lobbied Trump to intervene for three men accused or convicted of war crimes: Navy SEAL Eddie Gallagher, Army Lieutenant Clint Lorance, Army Major Mathew Golsteyn. CNN reported the campaign in May 2019. That November, Trump pardoned Lorance and Golsteyn and restored Gallagher's rank over the objections of his own Pentagon, a fight that helped cost the Navy secretary his job.

Trump, during the confirmation fight, told The Spectator that Hegseth used to call him to vouch for men who "did what they were trained to do." So the position was on the record six years before he held any office: the warrior is the victim, the investigation is the enemy, and accountability is something done to fighting men by people watching from the seats.

Six years later, with actual strike authority, the same position ran the Caribbean. After a September 2025 strike on an alleged drug boat, The Washington Post reported that Hegseth had given a verbal directive to "kill them all," and that a follow-up strike killed two survivors clinging to the wreckage. He called the story fake news; the admiral who commanded the operation testified there was no such order; Congress is still fighting to see the full video, which Hegseth has refused to release publicly.

TIME ran a timeline under the headline "Hegseth's Shifting Story." Whatever the eventual findings, the reflex is already on the record: when the outcome is contested, defend the image first, at any cost to the account.

Ullyot's word was "drama." The military's word for what an AAR produces is "truth." A secretary who can survive anything except admitting the miss will always choose the drama.

## The Secretary of War Is Still Auditioning

Strip away the controversies and look at what Hegseth has actually spent his tenure building. The answer, overwhelmingly, is presentation.

He championed the September 2025 executive order rebranding the Department of Defense as the Department of War, a change NBC News estimated could cost up to $2 billion once every seal, sign, and letterhead is redone.

<div class="key-stat">$2,000,000,000: the estimated cost of renaming the Department of Defense (NBC News, 2025)</div>

Twenty-five days later, on September 30, 2025, he summoned hundreds of generals and admirals from commands around the world to a single auditorium at Quantico, a gathering with no operational agenda. "Welcome to the War Department," he told them, "because the era of the Department of Defense is over." He announced that it was "completely unacceptable to see fat generals and admirals in the halls of the Pentagon." He declared, "No more beardos." Every quote survives in the Pentagon's own official transcript, posted to the department's website. The men and women in the seats, many with combat records dwarfing his, sat in silence while a former weekend television host graded their appearance.

Then came the move that gives the whole tenure away. Two weeks later he ordered every member of the armed forces, more than two million people, to review the speech.

That order is its own confession. A commander confident in a message trusts it to travel. This was something else: an audience, compelled by directive. The one thing a performer cannot survive is the possibility that nobody watched.

Above the two million compelled viewers sits the audience of one. At the same May 2026 cabinet meeting where Trump delivered the Central Casting review, Hegseth returned it by comparing him to George Washington and Abraham Lincoln. He has survived the Signal chats, the inspector general, and his own spokesman's resignation because the only certifier with the power to cancel the show stays pleased. For a performer whose worth is certified by the crowd, the compelled audience below and the flattered patron above amount to one project: proof, running on every screen at once, that the performance is landing.

The faith got the same treatment. In May 2026 his Pentagon cut the military's list of recognized religions from more than 200 to 31, Military.com reported, and 22 of the surviving codes are Christian denominations. The tattoo that once got him flagged now files policy.

The pattern is still compounding. On July 15, 2026, he announced mandatory annual testosterone screening for every service member over 30, framing low-T as a readiness crisis (Salon). The force's own bloodwork, conscripted into the aesthetic. Even biology now reports to the brand.

Which brings it back to the book that started his public life. In the Arena, 2016, titled after Theodore Roosevelt's 1910 speech: the credit belongs to the man "whose face is marred by dust and sweat and blood," never to the critic watching from the seats. Hegseth has carried that speech for twenty years, through three deployments, four books, two careers, and one confirmation he won by a single borrowed vote.

He has the dust. That has never been the question.

Roosevelt just never says what to make of the man in the arena who keeps looking up, mid-fight, to check whether the cameras caught it.
