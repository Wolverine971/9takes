> "Midge doesn't struggle in the same way that I do." — Rachel Brosnahan, on playing Midge Maisel

<p class="firstLetter">The woman playing the most luminously self-assured character on television said this in 2020, to Backstage, as if it were a simple observation. Midge Maisel, the explosively confident, motor-mouthed, irrepressibly funny protagonist of <em>The Marvelous Mrs. Maisel</em>, was "the most unapologetically confident woman I've ever played," Brosnahan explained.</p>

Brosnahan, beneath the stage lights, had spent years being told she couldn't do the thing she was now winning awards for.

That gap between the Rachel the world sees and the Rachel who struggles is where this story begins.

## What is Rachel Brosnahan's personality type?

### Rachel Brosnahan is an Enneagram Type 3

Enneagram Threes, called "The Achiever," carry a core wound most of them can't name: early on, they learned that what they _do_ matters more than who they _are_. Achievement became the language of love. Success became the proof of worth. And so they became extraordinary at succeeding. Not because they love the spotlight, but because without the accomplishment, they fear there's nothing underneath.

The signature Type 3 move is shape-shifting: becoming whatever the room requires, calibrating to earn recognition, building a self from accomplishments rather than discovering one from within. The question underneath every goal, every audition, every award: _If I stopped performing, would anyone still care who I actually am?_

Rachel Brosnahan is a [Type 3](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-3) with strong self-preservation instincts, what Enneagram teachers call the SP-3 subtype. The "counter-type" Three: she hates being seen as a show-off, achieves through preparation and craft rather than performance and image, and works in private to be genuinely good rather than merely appearing impressive.

**The evidence pattern:**

- She was told repeatedly (professionally, by industry veterans) that she wasn't funny. Rather than redirecting her career, she studied. She frequented comedy clubs all over New York City to watch how comedians worked: "how they delivered punchlines, received laughter, addressed the audience, held the microphone." She did the work before the room knew she was serious about it.
- She describes herself as "kind of a research nerd" who likes to "do a deep dive" into every role. For Lois Lane, she spent months interviewing real investigative journalists. For Midge Maisel, she studied 1950s comedy history in granular detail.
- She kept her marriage to actor Jason Ralph secret for _years_, not out of embarrassment but as deliberate resistance to the celebrity-image machine. When it came out in 2019 that they'd been quietly married, she called out the double standard in how the press treated women's relationships vs. men's.

Type 3s in their most grounded form build real things rather than chasing applause. Rachel Brosnahan has been doing that since before anyone knew her name.

## The Shy Kid from Highland Park Who Wanted to Be a Psychologist

Rachel Brosnahan was born in Milwaukee in 1990 and grew up in Highland Park, Illinois, a leafy North Shore suburb of Chicago. Her parents worked in children's publishing. She describes herself as having been shy — a reader, an observer, someone who dreamed of becoming a psychologist.

She joined the wrestling team for two years. She taught snowboarding. She played lacrosse. She was, by all accounts, an athletic, curious, somewhat interior kid who didn't announce herself.

Theater came later, and it came quietly. In junior high she appeared in school productions. At sixteen, she took a class with Carole Dibo at the Actors' Training Center in Wilmette — a class that would redirect her life. Dibo became her manager. The psychologist dream quietly retired.

What's notable about Brosnahan's origin story is that acting found the person underneath the shyness, not by changing her but by giving her somewhere to put what was already there.

She grew up adjacent to Highland Park's Jewish community, which she has said helped prepare her for the role of Midge Maisel. She absorbed culture by proximity. She developed an ear for the cadence of communities not exactly her own, which is itself a Three quality: the instinctive calibration to context.

That calibration would be tested years later. On July 4, 2022, a gunman opened fire on the Highland Park Independence Day parade, killing seven people. Brosnahan responded immediately and publicly. "I am sick to my stomach," she wrote. "The definition of insanity is doing the same thing over and over and expecting different results." She called family and friends to check they were safe, linked to gun-control organizations, and has continued advocating for Everytown for Gun Safety since. The "leafy North Shore suburb" had people she loved in it. The advocacy that followed wasn't abstract.

But the thing that stayed, even through all the years of transformation, is the interior life. The bookishness. The psychology-curious mind watching everyone in the room. When she says she relates to Lois Lane because Lois is "obsessive" and "intense," you can see the Highland Park kid, reading in her room, making precise mental notes about the world.

## The Rejection That Became the Reel

Brosnahan went to NYU's Tisch School of the Arts and graduated in 2012. She built her pre-Maisel career in dramatic roles: Shakespeare's Desdemona, the political thriller House of Cards where she played the morally compromised Rachel Posner, smaller films that showed craft without announcing arrival.

By her late twenties, she had found a home in drama. Comedy was not her lane, or so the industry had determined.

"I spent most of my life being told I wasn't funny," she said in January 2019, after winning her second consecutive Golden Globe. "I lost many jobs because people would say, 'We really liked her, but she's just not funny.'"

She heard it enough that she considered listening. "I thought, 'Maybe I should listen to it.'"

She didn't.

When _The Marvelous Mrs. Maisel_ came along in 2017, something happened that's almost too on-the-nose for an Enneagram case study: the role was Midge Maisel, a woman who discovered stand-up comedy after her life fell apart. A woman who gets onstage at a comedy club in the middle of the night, drunk and furious, and sets the room on fire. A woman for whom being told "you can't" is not an obstacle. It's an ignition switch.

Brosnahan won the Emmy. Then the Golden Globe. Then the Golden Globe again.

The pattern holds. For Type 3s, rejection is fuel. Each "you're not funny" was archived. When the moment arrived, the archive became ammunition.

## How She Prepares

Before she could play Midge, Brosnahan had to become comfortable doing something she'd never done: standing alone in front of an audience and making them laugh on command.

She didn't fake her way through it. She went to comedy clubs all over New York. She watched the amateur sets — the open mics, the nervous first-timers — to understand what the attempt looks like before the polish sets in. She studied how comedians hold the microphone. How they absorb a laugh before moving to the next line. How they recover when a bit doesn't land.

She took stand-up classes. She prepared until the role felt less like performance and more like possession.

Her co-star Alex Borstein had one piece of advice when she started: "Take up your space, and ask for what you need. And bring this character into the world. Nobody knows who she is but you."

That's not advice about acting. It's permission. And permission, for someone who has spent years being told to redirect (to be different, funnier, less like what she is), is not a small thing to receive.

This is the signature Self-Preservation Three move: doing the invisible work that makes the visible work look effortless. She doesn't want to seem like she's trying. She wants to have tried so thoroughly, so privately, that when the moment comes, nothing shows except the result.

Director James Gunn said she asks "tons of questions about every little moment in the movie, every line and action." She is, by her own description, "a research nerd." For Lois Lane, she interviewed real investigative journalists, not just to understand journalism but to understand the specific psychology of someone who will chase a story anywhere it leads.

She and Lois are "very similar," she said. Both motivated by the word "no." That's the exact mechanism: the shy bookworm who was told she wasn't funny became the person best qualified to play the woman who could.

## The Marriage She Kept Private

In 2019, the media discovered that Rachel Brosnahan and actor Jason Ralph had been quietly married for _years_. They'd been photographed in matching wedding bands at the 2018 Emmys, but no one had confirmed it. She'd been answering red carpet questions about her relationship status as if it were an open question.

When the secret came out, she explained why.

Both she and Ralph were leads of successful TV series. Yet Ralph was almost never asked about their relationship in interviews. She was asked about it on almost every red carpet. That double standard read clearly to both of them: "problematic and frustrating." So they opted out, redirecting conversations back to their work and keeping the personal life a private fact rather than a public currency.

This is the Three who has examined the celebrity machine and decided which parts she'll participate in and which parts she won't. It's a sharper, more deliberate version of what <a href="/personality-analysis/anne-hathaway">Anne Hathaway</a> went through publicly: the same tension between what the audience expects and what the person decides to give.

It's also something deeper. She'd learned, through experience, that the distinction between public performance and private self matters. That who you perform for the camera is not the same as who you are in the quiet of your own house. And that the latter deserves protection.

She gave her Golden Globes acceptance speech thanking Ralph: "Jason, I love you. I wouldn't be here at all without you."

That's the private self, briefly visible. Then the curtain closed again.

## The Kate Spade Years

In June 2018, Rachel Brosnahan lost her aunt Kate Spade to suicide. Fifteen days later, she lost her grandfather.

Kate Spade, born Katherine Noel Brosnahan (Rachel's father's sister), was one of the most celebrated designers of her era. Her brand had become synonymous with a kind of aspirational brightness: color, wit, optimism in handbag form. She seemed, from the outside, to have everything.

Rachel had known a different Kate. A warmer, more present one. When Brosnahan was making her Broadway debut (_The Big Knife_ at the Roundabout Theatre Company, 2013), Kate "let me crash in their extra bedroom while I figured out my living situation" and "included me in family dinners every night while we were in rehearsals." She described her aunt as someone who "spread stardust over everyone she came into contact with."

The public Kate and the private Kate were different women, as they always are. Rachel knew both. And then the private Kate, carrying whatever pain she was carrying, was gone.

It's hard to sit with this fact and not wonder what it illuminated for Brosnahan about the gap between the brilliant public self and what lives behind it. Kate Spade built one of the most recognizable personal brands in American fashion. She projected warmth and success and optimism. And underneath it, something the brand couldn't reach.

Rachel came through that grief carefully. She became the face of Frances Valentine, Kate's accessories line, because keeping her aunt's spirit alive mattered more than the discomfort of the association. "When you lose someone you love very, very much, you spend a lot of time trying to think about how to keep their spirit alive."

But she also came through it with a harder line between what she performs and what she protects.

## The Lois Lane Arrival

In 2025, Rachel Brosnahan became Lois Lane in James Gunn's _Superman_.

This casting is, on its face, a career leap. From prestige TV comedy to a major comic-book franchise. But it's also, if you've been watching, the logical destination.

Lois Lane isn't an easy role. She's not a love interest, not in the versions that work. Lois is the most important person in the room who isn't flying. She's the one who notices things. Who asks the question no one else will. Who follows the story when everyone else decides it's not a story anymore.

Brosnahan spent months preparing by interviewing actual investigative journalists, talking to women who had chased stories into hostile territory and kept going. She was looking for the psychology of someone who refuses to compromise her journalistic integrity "even for somebody that she loves."

"I understand her passion, her determination, and her feeling motivated by the word 'no,'" she said.

Of all the characters she's played, Brosnahan has said Lois Lane feels most like her _true self_. Not Midge, who is unapologetic and explosive. Not Rachel Posner from House of Cards, who was morally compromised. Lois, who is "obsessive," "intense," and will "go to the ends of the earth for the things she believes in."

The scene critics kept returning to: Lois interviews Superman in her apartment, pressing him on a geopolitical intervention, not softening a single question because of who's sitting across from her. One reviewer called it "perhaps the single best scene of Lois as a reporter in the entire canon of live-action Superman media." When _Superman_ opened in July 2025, Nerdly called her "the best screen iteration of Lois Lane since Margot Kidder." JamRadio named her "the film's emotional anchor." The damsel version, gone. This Lois commands every scene without needing Superman to validate her.

She has been doing this for over fifteen years, and she keeps getting more precise. At some point you stop asking whether she's performing confidence and start recognizing that she became it. The archive became the person. The fuel became the fire.
