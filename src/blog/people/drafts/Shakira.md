> "I had to pick up the pieces of myself and then put them all back together, and music was the glue."
> — Shakira to Zane Lowe, Apple Music, March 2024

<p class="firstLetter">Shakira does not sleep when she is making an album. She told Zane Lowe she had been "going to sleep every day around 9:00 a.m., the next day 9:00 a.m., 11:00 a.m." For weeks. School run, kids, breakfast, studio, home at 7 a.m., breakfast again. "I'm running on fumes." She said it like it was a brag.</p>

This is a 47-year-old woman with two children, a father recovering from a stroke, a Spanish tax case still trailing her, and a public divorce that broke her enough that she described the pain as "someone had stabbed a hole in my chest." And her solution to all of it is to stay awake.

That sentence is the entire blog. Shakira is what happens when a Type 7 — the Enneagram's possibility engine, the personality wired to outrun pain by turning it into the next thing — has no choice but to feel everything, and decides to feel it in front of 14 billion streams.

The story you think you know is the divorce era. The Bizarrap diss track. The "you traded a Ferrari for a Twingo" line. Piqué, humbled, photographed in a Casio. Women everywhere screaming "las mujeres ya no lloran, las mujeres facturan" — women don't cry anymore, they cash in. It is a great story. It is also the surface.

The deeper story is what a Type 7 does when reframing finally fails. When the optimism engine seizes. When every trick that worked since age eight — turn it into a song, dance the pain out, stay in motion, never land — has to be rebuilt from the wreckage in real time. Shakira is one of the most fascinating Type 7 case studies alive precisely because she stopped running, sang from the wound, and somehow set Guinness World Records doing it.

## What is Shakira's personality type?

### Shakira is an Enneagram Type 7

Type 7 is the Enneagram's <a href="/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-7">Enthusiast</a> — a personality wired to outrun limitation by generating possibilities. Sevens are not shallow. They are highly intelligent (Shakira's IQ is reportedly 140, Mensa-eligible per HuffPost), often genuinely optimistic, and unusually good at metabolizing pain into creative fuel. The trap is that the same engine that turns disappointment into a song can also turn a marriage, a city, or a feeling into something to escape from.

Shakira's whole adult life is a Seven case study, but the divorce era is where the type became unmissable. Look at what a Seven does when life delivers a coordinated attack:

- **The pain becomes material.** "I tend to write the most when things are tough," she said. The album _Las Mujeres Ya No Lloran_ is twelve tracks of exactly this.
- **The reframe is fast and public.** Within months of the split, she has turned humiliation into "las mujeres facturan" — a global feminist rallying cry. Sevens do this in private constantly. Shakira does it on Spotify.
- **Motion is the medicine.** Album promo, world tour, three new music videos in a week, sleeping at 11 a.m. The schedule is the coping mechanism.
- **The escape route stays mapped.** She admits she "put my career on hold" for Piqué, then tells Jimmy Fallon "the husband was dragging me down." The exit door was never sealed, even when she was inside the marriage.
- **Joy and pain coexist on purpose.** "I've learned that you can feel joy even while carrying pain — while living with a wound that won't quite heal." That is the Seven's mature integration in one sentence.

A Type 3 case (the alternate hypothesis many sites push) would explain the achievement, the reinvention, the polished image. It does not explain the bleeding. Threes do not publicly describe themselves as having "a hole in my chest." Threes do not let the wound become the brand. Threes hide weakness; Shakira built _Las Mujeres Ya No Lloran_ around hers and called the process "going to the shrink, only cheaper."

The Seven, on the other hand, will turn anything — including the public collapse of a marriage — into the next adventure. The question is only whether the running outpaces the wound or the wound finally catches up.

For Shakira, both happened. Then she made an album about both.

## The Eight-Year-Old Who Wrote Her Father's Grief

Before she was Shakira, she was Shakira Isabel Mebarak Ripoll, the only child of Lebanese-Colombian father William Mebarak Chadid and Spanish-Colombian mother Nidia Ripoll. She was born in Barranquilla, Colombia, on the Caribbean coast, in 1977.

The defining event of her childhood happened before she could remember it. When she was two years old, her older half-brother William — eight years old — was killed by a drunk driver in a motorcycle accident. Her father, devastated, started wearing dark sunglasses everywhere. Indoors. At dinner. For years.

He wore them to hide the grief. Eight-year-old Shakira noticed.

She wrote a song about it. It was called "Tus Gafas Oscuras" — Your Dark Glasses. She would record it six years later as a teenager. Her father, in a documentary years later, confirmed the connection: the glasses were where he kept the pain.

This is the origin story most personality analyses miss. The Seven defense — turning pain into possibility, suffering into something productive — was not invented in Shakira during the Piqué divorce. It was forged at a kitchen table in Barranquilla, watching her father refuse to let his grief be seen, and deciding very young that her job in this family was to put the unspeakable into something you could dance to.

She had asked for a typewriter at age seven, after watching her father write stories on one. She got it. She still uses what it taught her. Her instinct was never to play with dolls. It was to put words on paper before the feelings got too big to carry.

By ten she had decided she would be a singer. In second grade her music teacher told her she sounded "like a goat" and rejected her from the school choir. She has talked about how traumatizing it was. She has also said, in retrospect, "He was probably right, I did have a voice a bit like a goat."

The vibrato the teacher hated became one of the most recognizable voices on Earth.

## Why Shakira Is the Most Restless Pop Star Alive

Most musicians pick a lane. Shakira's career looks more like someone who refuses to be told there are lanes.

She started in Colombian pop-rock as a teenager. Switched to Latin alternative with the breakthrough _Pies Descalzos_ in 1995. Crossed into English-language pop with _Laundry Service_ in 2001 ("Whenever, Wherever"). Reinvented as a global dance-pop force with _Oral Fixation Vol. 2_ and "Hips Don't Lie" — which became one of the best-selling singles of the 21st century. Made the official 2010 World Cup anthem ("Waka Waka") in three languages. Put out a children's TV series. Headlined the Super Bowl halftime show with Jennifer Lopez in 2020. Dropped the Bizarrap session in 2023 that became the most-streamed Latin track in 24 hours in Spotify and YouTube history (four Guinness World Records).

She speaks Spanish, English, Portuguese, some Italian, some French, and a few phrases of Arabic. She belly dances because of her Lebanese father. She learned to belly dance, she has said, by trying to flip a coin with her stomach. At the 2020 Super Bowl she stuck out her tongue and did a zaghrouta — a Middle Eastern celebratory ululation — on national television because she wanted Lebanese kids watching to see themselves.

That is not a person trying to maximize one career. That is a Seven's possibility engine running at full throttle, refusing to leave any door unopened. The pattern echoes other Type 7 musicians who refuse to be confined to a single sound — <a href="/personality-analysis/Bad-Bunny">Bad Bunny</a> bouncing between trap, reggaeton, and pop until he made a love letter to Puerto Rico, or <a href="/personality-analysis/Doja-Cat">Doja Cat</a> shaving her head and writing a solo album to break her own pattern. Sevens collect containers. Sometimes a country becomes one. Sometimes a wound does.

The Type 7 weakness is that all this breadth can flatten into shallowness — knowing a little about everything, mastering nothing. Shakira's exception is that she actually goes deep on every container she enters. She has writing credits on almost every track she has released, which is unusual for a pop star at her commercial volume; her peers at that level are mostly faces for other people's songs. She produces. She co-directs videos. Gabriel García Márquez, her countryman and friend since 1999, wrote that her music "doesn't sound like anybody else's, and she has invented her own brand of innocent sensuality." When Márquez asked her to write songs for the film of _Love in the Time of Cholera_, she gave him "Despedida" and "Hay Amores" — Spanish-language ballads dense enough that Latin American critics put her songwriting in conversation with the literary tradition she came from. The breadth is real, and the obsession inside each project is real too.

Her collaborator Bizarrap — himself a perfectionist — said making the Vol. 53 session was "intense." His exact phrase: "She drove me crazy. She knows about production and knows a lot. It was like she asked more of me than I thought I could give."

That is what happens when a Seven goes <a href="/enneagram-corner/enneagram-types-in-stress">into stress and pulls toward Type 1</a> — the perfectionist arrow. The free-flowing artist who can make joy out of anything becomes a control freak in the studio, demanding take after take, drilling on a single bar until it is exactly right. The same pattern shows up in her tax case — she became famous for her meticulous control over her own contracts and finances, which is partly what landed her in Spanish prosecutors' crosshairs over allegedly unpaid taxes from 2012 to 2014.

The Seven's mind generates a thousand options. The Seven under stress tries to control all of them simultaneously. Anyone who has worked with Shakira will tell you both halves are true.

The 7w8 wing — the assertive, body-confident half of the personality — explains the rest. The cage at the 2009 American Music Awards. The wolf-howl on "She Wolf." The hips that supposedly don't lie. There is no Type 7 hiding inside any of that. Sevens want pleasure. Eight-wings refuse to apologize for it. The combination is what lets Shakira put a Lebanese ululation on the Super Bowl stage and a Casio joke on a Latin Grammy stage and treat both as equally hers.

## The Eleven Years Before the Eleven Years

There is a part of the Shakira story that even most fans forget, and the divorce coverage almost completely buries.

Before Gerard Piqué, there was Antonio de la Rúa.

He was the son of an Argentine president. They started dating in 2000, when she was 23. By 2004, at her request, he had taken over what their team called "the Shakira brand" — running her business, her contracts, her finances. By 2006, they were engaged (they never married). By 2008, he had reportedly negotiated her decade-long, $300 million Live Nation deal — the biggest contract of her career to that point.

They were together for ten years. Then in 2010, she ended it. The breakup got delicate fast. In December 2012 — the same year she met Piqué, the same year she was pregnant with Milan, the same year that would later anchor the Spanish tax case — Antonio sued her for $100 million, claiming he was owed for years of business work he had done as "more than a boyfriend." Shakira denied the formal partnership and filed a counter-suit alleging he had misappropriated $6.6 million without her consent. The courts dismissed his claims.

Read the pattern.

Two consecutive ten-or-eleven-year relationships. Both with men who became deeply enmeshed in her business, her finances, her residency. Both ending with legal proceedings, public discord, and Shakira eventually free, more famous, and back in motion. The Seven's commitment-and-escape pattern, repeated almost identically, with the second iteration playing out in Spanish tax court instead of a Manhattan civil filing.

This is the part the Bizarrap-era press never sat with: Shakira does not have a "first big love" who was Piqué. She has a track record. She tries to commit. She really tries. She gives up career years and brand control to do it. And then the door she never quite closed swings open, and she walks out, and the legal apparatus catches up to whatever was tangled while she was inside.

Sevens are not afraid of love. Sevens are afraid of staying so long that the exit gets sealed shut. Both relationships look, in retrospect, like long arguments with that fear.

## The 28-Year Commitment Hidden Behind All the Reinvention

If the Seven story were the whole story, Shakira would have a closet full of abandoned causes by now. The opposite is true.

In 1997, when she was barely nineteen and just a year into the post-_Pies Descalzos_ fame that would take her global, she founded the **Pies Descalzos Foundation** — Barefoot Foundation — named after the album. The mission was simple and Colombian: build real schools, with real food and real psychological support, for children displaced by the country's internal violence.

Twenty-eight years later, the foundation has built nine schools across Colombia and served more than 152,000 children. She has expanded into Quibdó on the Pacific coast, Altos de Cazucá outside Bogotá, Barranquilla in the Caribbean, and most recently the Catatumbo conflict region. She is still placing first stones at construction sites. She still funds it from her tours.

This is the Type 7 detail that almost no celebrity profile of her gets to: the personality wired against staying has stayed at one thing, in one country, for almost three decades. While she switched genres, languages, partners, residencies, and continents, the schools never moved. The kids in the photos got older. The buildings multiplied.

The Enneagram has a name for this. It is what integration looks like for a Seven — not the absence of restlessness, but the ability to point the engine at one thing long enough for the thing to compound. Pies Descalzos is what Shakira has done with the part of herself that is not running. She made a container that could hold the part of her that wants to land.

The lyric from _Las Mujeres Ya No Lloran_ that everyone quoted is "las mujeres facturan." The lyric from her actual life that no one quotes is the one she has been writing in school cement since 1997: *the kids stay, and so do I.*

## Why Shakira Gave Up Ten Years for Gerard Piqué

Here is the part of the story that does not fit a Type 3 reading at all.

In 2010, Shakira — then arguably the biggest Latin artist on Earth — met Gerard Piqué, a Spanish soccer player, on the set of the "Waka Waka" video before the World Cup. They share the exact same birthday, February 2, exactly ten years apart. She was 33. He was 23. They went public in 2011, she moved to Barcelona for him, and they had two sons, Milan (2013) and Sasha (2015). They never married — Shakira had said in a 2020 _60 Minutes_ interview that "marriage scares the [expletive] out of me" — but they were treated as one of the most public couples in Europe.

And during those eleven years, by her own admission, she stopped.

"For a long time I put my career on hold, to be next to Gerard, so he could play football," she told _The Sunday Times_ in 2024. "There was a lot of sacrifice for love."

She told Apple Music the same year, talking about the studio: "Every time I had to be pulled into the studio to do my work I felt I was leaving my family unattended. There were times when I really didn't enjoy it. I felt guilt. I felt torn."

She released exactly one studio album — 2017's _El Dorado_ — across that whole stretch. For the artist who had averaged a record-breaking album every two years, this was a near-disappearance.

Then she said the line that detonated on _The Tonight Show_: "I don't know why it was dragging me down."

This is the part of Shakira that the gossip coverage rarely sits with: a Seven who actually tried to commit. Type 7s are famously bad at staying. The escape route is always mapped, the exit always rehearsed, "yes" always feels like "no" to infinite alternatives. Shakira chose Barcelona for over a decade. She raised two kids. She suspended the engine that had driven her since age eight.

The internal cost — the cost a Seven pays when they sit in one place too long — bled through everywhere. She has said the relationship was "monotony." She wrote songs about it before the split was even public ("Te Felicito," "Monotonía"). The Spanish songwriter Keityn confirmed Piqué actually heard "Monotonía" before the breakup, walked into the studio, and said, "Wow, I love it." He did not catch what he was hearing.

The Seven was already telling on the relationship. The Seven was already writing the exit. He missed it because he thought it was just another song.

## How a Type 7 Survives Everything Falling Apart at Once

The summer of 2022 was the worst stretch of Shakira's life, and almost no one outside her closest circle knew the whole shape of it.

In June, she and Piqué publicly announced their separation after eleven years. Spanish tabloids almost immediately reported he was already with a 22-year-old PR student named Clara Chía Martí. Shakira was 45.

In late May — weeks before the announcement — her father William had fallen badly. She rode in the ambulance with him to a Barcelona hospital. He recovered, then in October suffered a stroke. The man whose dark glasses she had written about as an eight-year-old, the man who had taught her how to put pain on paper, was now in serious decline.

Throughout this same window, Spanish prosecutors were pressing a tax fraud case against her — the alleged 14.5 million euros she did not pay between 2012 and 2014, the years she lived with Piqué. Conviction could mean eight years in prison.

To compress: in roughly four months, she lost her partner, her father's health collapsed, the press camped on her doorstep, and the state was threatening to put her in jail.

She described the period to _Rolling Stone_: "It felt like someone had stabbed a hole in my chest. And the sensation was so real, almost physical. I physically felt like I had a hole in my chest and that people could see through me."

She told _Elle_ it was "the darkest hour of my life."

She was photographed crying in Times Square the next year. She admitted she could not function at times. The Seven who had spent her entire life turning pain into the next thing was, for once, simply in pain. No reframe. No alchemy. Just a hole in the chest.

This is where the Enneagram framework cuts deeper than the press coverage. Most analyses of Shakira's divorce era treat the Bizarrap song as the story. But the song is not the story. The song is what a Seven does when the running starts working again.

The story is the months in between, when nothing worked.

<p class="inner-thought">If she stops moving, the wound opens up again. If she keeps moving, it is still open — but at least there is a song coming out of it.</p>

In the Apple Music interview, she described the only thing that kept her functional: "I had to pick up the pieces of myself, and then put them all back together. And music was the glue." She has said writing songs is "like going to the shrink, only cheaper." The Seven was finding the only escape route that had ever actually worked — turning the wound into the work.

She wrote the Bizarrap session reportedly in a state of refusal — some on her team, she said, "wanted me to change the lyrics. They were really scared about the reaction. I'm like, I'm not a U.N. representative. I'm an artist. And no one's going to tell me how I'm supposed to heal."

Then she released it. It broke four Guinness World Records. Piqué showed up to work in a Casio watch and a Renault Twingo, exactly the brands she had named in the song to mock his trade-down. The internet treated it as the perfect revenge. Shakira treated it as therapy.

A month later, she did it again. "TQG" with Karol G — _te quedó grande_, "I was too big for you" — landed on the same nerve, this time as a duet of two Latin women laughing at exes who had downgraded them. The song stripped away even the pretense of political distance. Shakira sang directly to "the new one." Karol G sang directly to her own ex, Anuel AA. The pair turned a private wound into a global feminine anthem in the same way 1970s country women's songs did, except this version sold more in 24 hours than most albums sell in a year.

A Type 3 would have treated either song as a comeback strategy. A Type 8 would have treated them as public executions. Shakira treated them as something she had to get out of her body before it killed her — and let the rest of the world do whatever they wanted with what came out.

In April 2023, weeks after "TQG," she finally moved out of Barcelona. The original plan had been to get to Miami earlier; her father William's series of hospitalizations kept pushing the date back. She arrived in Miami with the boys — Milan now 10, Sasha 8 — and started over. New schools. New time zone. New language environment. The Seven who had spent eleven years calling Barcelona home detached the entire life and slid it across an ocean.

The tax case ended a few months after that. On November 20, 2023 — the morning her trial was scheduled to begin in Barcelona — she settled. She accepted a three-year suspended sentence, paid a fine of more than €7.3 million on top of the back taxes she had already wired, and added another €432,000 to keep the prison sentence suspended. No admission of fraud as charged; just the deal. A second tax case Spanish prosecutors had been building was dropped in May 2024. The chapter she had spent five years insisting was unjust closed quietly, on terms she could live with, the same week she went back to making the album.

Two songs, one move, one settlement, in roughly twelve months. The escape route the Seven had spent her whole life mapping was operational again. The difference, this time, is that she had run with the wound out in front of her, not buried under the next adventure.

## What Shakira Won't Hide from Her Sons

The most revealing moment of the entire Apple Music interview is not about Piqué. It is about her kids.

"Society maybe teaches us to conceal our feelings in front of our kids," she told Zane Lowe, "but I think that's a mistake. They know better. They perceive things in many different ways. They can tell when an adult is lying to them."

She had decided, mid-divorce, that she would not pretend in front of Milan and Sasha. They would see her cry. They would see her write songs about what was happening. They would know that the album coming out was a real thing happening to a real woman who was also their mother.

Then she told the story that gives the whole blog its center.

Milan, eleven years old, started writing his own songs on the piano while she was recording _Las Mujeres Ya No Lloran_. Two of them. With lyrics. "Whenever he feels a little down, or he, he goes to the piano, and he writes. He writes music. And that is also his catharsis."

She is teaching him exactly what her father William never could.

Her father wore dark glasses to hide his grief. Eight-year-old Shakira had to write a song for him, because he could not say it himself.

Now her own son sits at the piano when he is sad and turns it into music — because his mother showed him, in real time, in front of him, that this is what you do. You do not hide it. You put it in something. You let other people hear it.

The full line, the one that should be on the wall of every Type 7's therapy office, came from the same conversation: "We have to express how we feel about these losses, because otherwise the body starts talking. The body talks. The body speaks what the mouth doesn't."

The Seven who spent her childhood translating her father's silence is not going to leave that inheritance to her sons. She is breaking the line.

---

## Why "Las Mujeres Ya No Lloran" Is a Love Album

Most of the world heard _Las Mujeres Ya No Lloran_ as a divorce album. Shakira pushed back gently, in the Apple Music conversation. She called it a love album.

"It's better to trust than never to trust. It's better to trust and see and find yourself betrayed than never to trust. There's a lot more good people in the world. It's worth it. To love is the most amazing experience a human can live, and no one should take away that opinion from you."

This is not the manic cheerleader optimism Sevens use to dodge pain. It is the earned optimism of someone who walked through the pain and came out still believing. The possibility engine stops scattering and points at one thing.

The integration is also why she is suddenly more productive than she has been in a decade. "I now feel like working. I feel like writing songs. I feel like making music. It's a compulsive need of mine that I didn't feel before." Translation: when she had the marriage, the marriage was holding the engine down. Now the engine is hers again. So is the pain. So is the work.

There is a wide-open look in her eyes when she talks about the next tour, the next video, the next song with her son on it. The Seven is back. The escape route is fully operational. The difference is that now she knows what is on the other side of trying not to escape — and she is choosing the work anyway.

There is one small detail in the Apple Music transcript that lands harder than anything in the diss track. Talking about whether to release the Bizarrap session, she said, "I've been going through, like, what — how many stages of grieving? Five, seven, whatever. In my case probably twelve or twenty."

Twelve or twenty stages of grief. From the woman whose entire personality is supposed to skip grief.

She did not skip it. She just made a record out of every stage.

---

## The Seven Who Stopped Running Long Enough to Be Seen

There is a moment in the Apple Music interview where Zane Lowe tells Shakira he can hear her voice differently on this record. There are notes, he says, that were "tucked away and hidden until I guess that pain and that vulnerability found those notes."

Shakira's response: "My voice keeps changing over the years. I tell my kids — I sometimes listen to some music of mine with them, not on purpose, but just happens — and I say, _I used to suck. I don't even know why I have fans._"

She is laughing when she says it. But the line has more in it than the laugh.

The Seven spent forty years generating possibilities, learning languages, trying genres, dancing styles, countries, partnerships, songs. She got to a place where her voice could finally do what it could not do at thirty, at twenty, at fourteen with her vibrato that sounded like a goat. The notes, she is saying, were not there before. They are there now. The pain put them there.

She did not get those notes by running. She got them by, for the first time in her life, having no choice but to stand in one place while everything around her burned. The marriage, the father, the prosecution, the wound that would not close. She kept moving. But she also let it land.

Both at once. Joy and pain. Movement and stillness. The Seven and the wound.

Forty years writing songs to translate other people's silence — her father's, mostly — and she finally wrote one in her own voice. The record set Guinness records the day it came out. The schools she started building when she was nineteen are still going up.

And in a house in Miami, the eleven-year-old son of the woman who can't stop moving sits down at the piano whenever he feels a little down, and writes.

That is the only ending the Seven was ever after.
