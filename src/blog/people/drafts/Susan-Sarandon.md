<p class="firstLetter">In March 1993, Susan Sarandon walked onto the Academy Awards stage to present an award, looked past the teleprompter, and started talking about a prison camp. Hundreds of HIV-positive Haitian refugees were being held behind razor wire at Guantanamo Bay. She and Tim Robbins had decided, without warning the producers, to spend ninety seconds of the most-watched broadcast in America saying so.</p>

The Academy's response was immediate. The act was "outrageous." Telecast producer Gil Cates let it be known she would not be welcome back.

Three years later she stood on that same stage and won Best Actress for *Dead Man Walking*.

That is the whole woman in one anecdote. Tell her the cost. Watch her pay it anyway.

Most people file Sarandon into one of two folders. There is the warm one: the earth-mother of *Bull Durham* and *Thelma & Louise*, all wisdom and cleavage and big brown eyes, the actress Ron Shelton called "wise, but vulnerable as hell." Then there is the cold one: the self-righteous radical who "helped elect Trump," got herself fired over Gaza, and won't stop lecturing America from a protest line. Both folders are wrong, because they are the same folder. The tenderness and the steel are not two Susan Sarandons. They are one [Type 8](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-8), and she has been telling us which one for eighty years.

## What is Susan Sarandon's personality type?

### Susan Sarandon is an Enneagram Type 8

Susan Sarandon is an Enneagram Type 8, The Challenger, almost certainly an 8w7.

Type 8 lives in the body, in the gut, in anger. The core fear is specific: being controlled, harmed, or left at the mercy of someone with power over you. The strategy that grows out of it is to get strong, stay in charge of your own life, and put yourself between power and the people it is crushing. Eights don't bury anger the way most people do. They run on it. Justice, to an Eight, is personal, physical, and slightly furious.

Now read her life back through that. The justice obsession started absurdly early. "I've always had a really developed sense of justice," she told *AARP* in 2014. "As a child, I would rotate my dolls' dresses for fear that they might come alive at midnight and one of them would always have the best dress on." A small child losing sleep to make sure no doll got more than her share. The grown woman would spend five decades doing the adult version, except the dolls became death-row inmates, AIDS patients, separated migrant children, and tipped restaurant workers.

The vulnerability and the steel are the same instrument. Eights protect ferociously precisely because they know what it is to be small and at someone's mercy, and they have decided never to be that again. The softness on screen is real. So is the spine. They come from the same place.

A serious case can be made for Type 1, the Reformer, and that argument lives in the Rabbit Hole below. The short version: Ones reform from an inner sense of correctness, and they are restrained, self-critical, and proper about it. Sarandon is none of those things. She owns her appetite, her body, and her anger without apology, breaks rules with obvious pleasure, and goes to jail on purpose. That is the gut center pointed outward. An Eight, not a One.

## Susan Sarandon's childhood as the oldest of nine

She was born Susan Abigail Tomalin in 1946 and raised in Edison, New Jersey, the eldest of nine children in a Roman Catholic family. Her father was an advertising executive and former big-band singer. Her mother was, in Sarandon's words, "Catholic and incredibly fertile," and, she added decades later, "a staunch Republican."

Being the oldest of nine is a Type 8 origin story told in plain demographics. You are responsible before you understand the word. You keep order in a house that is always one tantrum from chaos. "It makes you flexible," she said. "You're not used to privacy and can focus in the midst of chaos. It's a primer for show business." A child who learns to hold the center while eight smaller humans spin around her does not grow up easy to push.

But the engine is not really the birth order. It is what she saw in her mother. On the podcast *Podcrushed* in 2025, talking about her mother, Sarandon broke down crying. "I didn't feel my life was done to me," she said. "My mom definitely felt her life was done to her." She cried recalling her mother telling her, "I never had that much fun with my kids as you do."

Sit with that. A woman who felt her own life had been handed to her by other people, lived on terms she didn't set. And a daughter who looked at that and decided, somewhere very young, that it would never happen to her. Everything else, the arrests, the burned bridges, the refusal to fall in line, grows out of that single vow.

The other half of the wound was the Church. "I was a very quiet kid, a very wanting-to-please kid," she told an OWN interview in 2013. "But certain things didn't make sense to me, and when I questioned them, there was a problem." Original sin didn't make sense to her. Limbo didn't make sense. A God who would condemn someone for their sexual orientation didn't make sense. So she asked, and asking "caused a problem," and the quiet wanting-to-please girl learned the defining lesson of her life: that authority would punish the question, and that she was going to ask it anyway.

## Why the Academy banned Susan Sarandon from the Oscars

Go back to that 1993 stage. She was a Best Actress nominee that night herself, for *Lorenzo's Oil*, where she had just played Michaela Odone, a mother waging total war against doctors, a skeptical support group, and a fatal disease to save her dying son. She had a statue to win and a career to keep open, and she spent her ninety seconds on a prison camp instead. The thing that makes it an Eight story rather than an activist story is the calculation, or the absence of one.

<p class="inner-thought">Ninety seconds. The whole country is watching. Say the safe thing, smile, hand over the envelope, keep every door in this town open. Or say the true thing and watch the doors start to close. There are people in a camp who cannot say it for themselves. You can. So you will, and you will deal with what it costs after.</p>

She dealt with what it cost. The ban came down. The industry's most powerful body told one of its biggest stars she had embarrassed them on their night, and the message to everyone watching was clear: this is what defiance costs. Then she went back three years later and won the whole thing for *Dead Man Walking*, playing Sister Helen Prejean, the nun who refuses to abandon a condemned man. The town that tried to muzzle her handed her its highest honor for a performance about exactly that. And Prejean did not stay on screen. Sarandon befriended the real nun and made death-penalty abolition one of the causes she has carried longest, in every decade since. The protector she plays and the protector she is were never two different people.

The pattern repeated almost on schedule. In 2003, the Baseball Hall of Fame canceled a fifteenth-anniversary celebration of *Bull Durham* because Sarandon and Robbins opposed the Iraq War. Hall president Dale Petroskey wrote that their public criticism of the President "helps undermine the U.S. position." Watch what happens next, because it is the tell. After thousands of letters and calls, Petroskey backed down and admitted "I should have handled the matter differently." The institution flinched. She did not. That is the Eight's entire relationship to a power struggle: they will outlast the discomfort, and they are betting you won't.

## Why people blame Susan Sarandon for Trump

Here is where you are supposed to lose patience with her, and a lot of people did.

In 2016, Sarandon campaigned hard for [Bernie Sanders](/personality-analysis/bernie-sanders). When Sanders lost the primary, she would not transfer her loyalty to Hillary Clinton on command. On MSNBC, asked if she would vote Clinton over Trump, she said, "I don't know. I'm going to see what happens." She floated the idea that a Trump win might "bring the revolution." She wrote, "Fear of Donald Trump is not enough for me to support Clinton, with her record of corruption." In the end she voted for Jill Stein.

The backlash was enormous and it never fully ended. Debra Messing went after her for years. A widely shared 2019 essay was titled "I'm Still Sorry I Listened to Susan Sarandon During the 2016 Election." The charge was simple and damning: a celebrity with a megaphone told the left that the two candidates were equally bad, and helped depress the turnout that put Trump in the White House.

Concede the real part first. In a race that close, public figures telling voters that fear of Trump wasn't reason enough to vote against him may well have moved some margin. Plenty of people will never forgive her for it, and the reasoning is not crazy. Her own response only made it worse. "It's very flattering," she said in 2017, "to think that I, on my own, cost the election."

But here is what the Enneagram makes visible that the outrage cycle never could. To an Eight, voting from fear is not a neutral act. It is submission. It is letting someone else's threat decide what you do with your own power, which is the precise thing an Eight cannot make herself do. Ask her to vote against Trump because Trump is frightening and you have asked her to do the one move her entire psyche is built to refuse. You can think she was reckless and still see clearly what she was protecting: not Trump, and not even Stein, but the principle that fear does not get to author her choices. She would sooner lose an argument with all of liberal America than let it.

## What Susan Sarandon is like when the camera is off

The reputation is a humorless scold. The reality keeps surprising the people who actually meet her.

Geena Davis went into *Thelma & Louise* braced to dislike her co-star and instead, she says, fell in love with her on sight. "Everybody loved her," Davis recalled in 2024. "There was nothing confrontational about her, she just said what she thought." Watching Sarandon change a line of dialogue she didn't like on page one of the script reprogrammed Davis's whole approach to her own career. "Up until that point, I just tried to get along and not make waves. Susan, on the other hand, stood up for herself." That is what an Eight does to the people around her without trying: she gives them permission to take up space.

<div class="pull-quote">"I'm always afraid. I just don't pay attention."</div>

The reputation misses the fear because she hides it well, even from herself. Eights armor up so early and so completely that the soft thing underneath becomes invisible to everyone, including them. But it leaks. "To not have structure panics me," she admitted to *AARP*. "When I took art classes, color just overwhelmed me. That much choice. I can't go into huge department stores. It's just too much choice." The woman who stares down the Academy gets overwhelmed in a department store. The control she imposes on the world is a defense against how much the world can flood her.

The same refusal runs through how she loved. She met Tim Robbins on the *Bull Durham* set in 1988 and stayed with him twenty-three years, raising two sons, and they never married. That was a choice, not an oversight. Marriage scared her, she said, because of the risk of "losing your identity. When you become a couple [it's] so easy to have happen." An Eight will refuse even the institution of marriage rather than hand anyone, including the person she loves, the standing to define her. When the relationship ended in 2009 she explained it the same way she explains everything: she would not stay in a union that had stopped being true, because "you can't do a meditation on death and stay in a situation that's not authentic."

You see the tenderness most clearly in how she mothers and mentors. Her daughter Eva Amurri says the great gift was "the permission to want a lot for yourself, career-wise, as a woman." Her first husband, Chris Sarandon, divorced from her since 1979, still calls her evolution "extraordinarily heartening." Director Stephen Chbosky, describing the set of *Nonnas* in 2025, said he "created this atmosphere, Susan Sarandon took control, and they locked in." She walks in, takes the room, and somehow everyone in it feels safer. That is the Challenger at her best.

## Why Susan Sarandon got fired for a ceasefire

That protective drive does not stay inside warm rooms. It is also what gets her fired. The most recent time came in November 2023. At a pro-Palestine rally in New York's Union Square, Sarandon said there were people "afraid of being Jewish at this time" who were "getting a taste of what it feels like to be a Muslim in this country." Within days, United Talent Agency, which had represented her since 2014, dropped her.

This time she did something she almost never does. She apologized, fast and fully, for the sentence. "This phrasing was a terrible mistake," she wrote, "as it implies that until recently Jews have been strangers to persecution, when the opposite is true." Critics were right that the line landed badly, and she said so herself.

Then watch the Eight reassert. She apologized for the words. She never apologized for the cause. By the time she accepted an International Goya Award in Spain in February 2026, the contrition had hardened into defiance. "I was fired by my agency," she said, "specifically for marching and speaking out about Gaza, for asking for a ceasefire." She said it "became impossible for me to even be on television," that an Italian director who hired her "was told not to hire me." She did not soften. She relocated the fight. "Right now, I kind of specialize in tiny films with directors who have never directed." At seventy-nine, dropped by Hollywood, she went and found smaller rooms where the work could still happen.

This is the Eight's signature move with an apology: concede the wording, defend the ground. The thing she can be moved to regret is hurting people she didn't mean to hurt. The thing she cannot be moved to regret is having spoken at all.

### Susan Sarandon's Wing: 8w7

The wing is where the energy lives. An 8w9 ("The Bear") is steadier, more grounded, slower to move. Sarandon is plainly the 7-wing: appetitive, restless, gregarious, enterprising. She co-founded a chain of ping-pong social clubs in her sixties because she loved that the game "crosses all gender and age barriers, you can have little girls beating muscley 35-year-old men." She had her first child at thirty-nine and her third at forty-five over everyone's objections. She says she chooses roles "that will frighten me. Then I know I'll be awake." The 7-wing adds the hunger for more life, more stimulation, more rooms to walk into, which is why Geena Davis remembers fun rather than fury. The Seven keeps the Eight from curdling into pure grievance. Read more on the <a href="/enneagram-corner/enneagram-wings-complete-guide">wings</a>.

### Susan Sarandon's Instinctual Subtype: likely so/sx

The social instinct is loud. She literally became president of an advocacy group, One Fair Wage, and gets arrested in organized actions of hundreds, not solo stunts. The social Eight turns the protective drive toward the group, the cause, the collective under threat, and builds alliances around it. But the sexual instinct is right behind it, and you can see it on screen: the one-on-one magnetism, the unembarrassed sensuality she carried into her seventies, the intensity that makes a camera lean toward her. So/sx fits a woman whose activism is communal but whose presence is electric and personal. More on the <a href="/enneagram-corner/enneagram-instinctual-subtypes">instinctual subtypes</a>.

### Stress and Growth Arrows

Under sustained pressure, the Eight <a href="/enneagram-corner/enneagram-types-in-stress">disintegrates toward Type 5</a>: withdrawal, secrecy, cutting off, retreating into a fortress. You can see traces of it in the post-2023 period, when, by her own account, she pulled back from the American industry and relocated her career to smaller and more private rooms in Italy and England. When an Eight gets backed into a corner she can't win head-on, she goes quiet and conserves.

In growth, the Eight integrates toward Type 2, and the raw protective force becomes genuine, generous care. That is the Sarandon her collaborators describe: the woman who emboldens younger actresses, mentors first-time directors, mothers a set. The same gut that will torch a relationship over principle, pointed in the healthy direction, becomes the warmth Geena Davis fell for on sight.

### Counterarguments: Why Susan Sarandon Might Not Be Type 8

The strongest alternate is **Type 1, the Reformer**. The justice obsession, the moral certainty, the "need for justice" she traces back to her dolls, all read as One. But Ones are contained. They suppress anger as unseemly, criticize themselves harder than anyone, and follow rules even while reforming them. Sarandon does the opposite: she expresses anger freely, voices "no regrets," breaks rules with relish, and treats her own appetites as nothing to apologize for. The moral fire is real, but it runs through the gut, not the inner critic.

The second case is **Type 2, the Helper**, given the caretaking and the mothering. But a Two shapes herself to be needed and fears rejection above almost everything. Sarandon does the reverse. She will alienate her agency, her party, and half the country rather than soften to keep them. The caretaking is real, but it is an Eight in growth borrowing the Two's generosity, not a Two's need to be loved. When the warmth and the principle collide, the principle wins every time. That is how you know which center she lives in.

## Eighty, and still on the line

In October 2026 she turns eighty. She is still getting arrested. She is still doing the tiny films and the protest speeches and the interviews where she refuses to give the comfortable answer. The agency is gone. A lot of the doors that were open in 1993 are shut now, and she shut some of them herself, on purpose, with her eyes open.

Her mother felt her life was done to her. That is the sentence under everything. The camera tried to set her terms, and the Academy, and a political party, and a talent agency, and each time she answered the same way, by deciding she would rather lose on her own terms than win on someone else's.

She is not fearless. She told us that. She is afraid all the time. She just doesn't pay attention.
