<p class="firstLetter">She was seventeen. Someone had pasted her face onto sexual images and posted them where anyone could find them. Then she said the part almost nobody expected.

That someone typing her name into a search bar might find them next to the superhero and, eventually, the dance trophy.

Sit with how strange that reaction is. A minor describes one of the ugliest violations a person can suffer, and the first place her mind goes is what it does to her image. You could call that misplaced. It might be the most honest sentence she has ever said about herself.

## What is Xochitl Gomez's personality type?

### Xochitl Gomez is an Enneagram Type 3

Xochitl Gomez is an Enneagram Type 3, the Achiever. For a Three, the public image and the private self fuse into one thing, so protecting the image starts to feel like protecting your life. That is the only frame in which her deepfake response stops sounding shallow, and we will come back to it.

The shorthand for Type 3 makes it sound like it is about trophies. It is really about that fusion, the one most people never make. The version of you that the world sees and rewards stops being a presentation you manage and becomes the self you actually believe you are. You can read the full pattern in the [Type 3 profile](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-3).

Everything about her lines up behind that. The film set was the family business, so performance arrived as both inheritance and chore. The warmth is real, and it also keeps the work coming. The rituals she uses to control her own face are precise. Even her wins get routed outward, offered up as gifts to the family who built the life around her. Marvel producer Victoria Alonso, who cast her at thirteen, said it plainly to Marvel.com in 2022: "There was a spark in her eye. We saw the defiance of America Chavez in her."

Here is the honest objection, and it is sharper than the usual one. She does not read as image-managed at all. Her online reputation is the opposite of polished: she is the goofy, unfiltered one, the "I mean" girl of the TikTok interview clips fans pass around because she is refreshingly unrehearsed. How is that a Three obsessed with image? Two ways, and they point the same direction. For a Three the winning image is whatever wins the room, and being lovably real is a far better look than being slick, so the unpolished charm may be the most effective presentation she owns.

The real tell comes when the image is actually hit. A Seven, the type all that buoyancy suggests, scatters toward new and brighter options to outrun the pain. Xochitl goes quiet, stays fixed on the work in front of her, and reaches for one instinct only: take it down. That instinct is the engine, and the deepfake is where you can hear it running.

A Helper (Type 2) is the subtler miss, because the warmth is so real. But a Two organizes the self around other people's needs, while her frame stays on her own image and her own visibility, with the giving pointed at family as tribute. The warmth sits on top of the Three. It is not the engine.

## Xochitl Gomez's childhood: raised inside the family trade

Xochitl Gomez was born in Los Angeles in 2006 and moved to Echo Park at five. Her father came from Morelos, Mexico, and worked construction. Her mother decorated film sets. That second detail matters more than it looks. The movie business was not a dream she had to sell her parents on. It was the family's actual trade, the thing that put food on the table, and she was walking onto real sets as a little kid.

By her own account, she started performing at five, in a local production of The Little Mermaid, and by twelve had done 22 full-length musicals. She was an only child, and she has said she misses having a sibling, which means the theater and the audition circuit were also where a lot of her social world lived. Her mother quit her job to manage it and spent her days behind the wheel. "You end up spending your days in the car driving around from audition to audition," Xochitl has said, sometimes four or five in a single day.

In that house, love and attention were made of showing up, being ready, doing the thing well, and being seen doing it. A kid raised on movie sets does not have to be taught that being watched is normal. She has to be taught, much later and usually the hard way, that there is a self underneath the watching at all.

She once named her professional inspiration as Jennifer Lawrence in The Hunger Games. It is a telling pick. Lawrence was a young actress who turned discipline into a franchise and a family's fortunes. At an age when most kids idolize pop stars, Xochitl was already sorting the world by who had performed their way to safety.

## The girl who went back into the harness after lunch

Here is a sentence she dropped about filming a Marvel movie, the way you would mention running an errand: "I would literally have lunch and then go back into a harness."

She is fifteen, sixteen, flying on wires for hours, breaking for a sandwich, then getting strapped back in. She trained in kung fu from age eleven and did stunt work "every other day for hours" to be ready. All of that labor exists so that none of it shows. What reaches the audience is a girl who wandered, apparently, into superpowers, because hiding the effort is the whole point.

There is a reason the seams never show. Xochitl has dyslexia, and her fix is total preparation: "I memorize everything I do and I memorize my character and my lines and the other person's line so that I know what I'm going to say and I don't mess up the order of the words." She learns everyone's part so she can never be caught out.

The people around her felt the poise and read it as maturity. Elizabeth Olsen, who played her aunt-figure Scarlet Witch, put it this way to Screen Rant in 2022: "She's way more prepared than I was at 25 for the Marvel Universe." Olsen also described her as "really a light and an energy that was amazing to be around, just pure kindness and goodness." Both things are true at once. The light was genuine, and it was also the most reliable tool she had for making a set glad she was on it.

Even her nerves got folded into the performance. Auditioning opposite Benedict Cumberbatch on a live set ringed by cameras, a kid who had grown up on Doctor Strange and Sherlock, she coached herself in the moment: "Don't be a fan right now. This is work. Be cool, be chill, be confident." She could be the star-struck kid and the composed professional in the same breath, and she never let the first one slow the second.

## Why playing America Chavez made Xochitl Gomez a target

In 2022 she became America Chavez in Doctor Strange in the Multiverse of Madness, a movie that grossed 955.8 million dollars worldwide and finished as the fourth-highest earner of the year (Box Office Mojo). She had auditioned at thirteen and joined the cast at fourteen. Benedict Wong, defending her later, noted she was "one of the youngest actors to join the MCU of a film of that magnitude" (via ComicBook.com, 2022).

For a young Latina who grew up short on people to point to, the role was the answer to a wish she had said out loud: "When I was growing up, I didn't really have too many people to look up to." Now she was the person other kids messaged about feeling seen. "I belong with the superheroes now, too," she said. Sam Raimi, who directed her, framed the casting as a mission to Screen Rant in 2022: "This young Latina can now represent a heroic figure for many people out there that were underrepresented before."

A small choice inside the part shows how she thinks. America Chavez wears a denim jacket covered in doodles. Asked why, Xochitl invented a backstory: "If you're traveling through the multiverse, you're going to get lonely and kind of bored, so what are you going to do? You write poems, and you do little drawings and kind of do little diary entries." Handed a superhero, she reached for the private, lonely coping life underneath the powers. She gave the character the interior world that a public kid learns to keep tucked away.

There is a small object that carries the rest of the story. When Marvel produced an America Chavez action figure, Xochitl cried the first time she saw it. "There's an action figure of me. I literally just cried." She started carrying a miniature version with her. "Whenever I'm depressed, whenever I go places, I literally have my little mini America with me everywhere I go. It reminds me how far I've come." The sunniest kid in the cast keeps a tiny plastic proof of her own worth in her bag, for the days the sun does not come out on its own.

America Chavez was not the first queer coming-of-age she was handed. Two years earlier, in the Netflix series Gentefied, she played the young version of Ana in a flashback to the moment the character first realizes she likes girls. Then came the superhero with two mothers. An actress who has never claimed a queer story as her own kept being cast to carry one, made into the public face of a queerness she was representing rather than living.

The visibility came with a cost she did not get to vote on. Her America Chavez is Latina and has two mothers, and the film drew homophobic trolling and was reportedly pulled from several international markets partly over her. She was a teenager absorbing organized hate for a role she was proud of. The cast closed ranks around her. Her own posture was to keep radiating the representation and let the ugliness go unanswered.

## What Xochitl Gomez was actually upset about

The deepfakes were a different order of thing, because they used her exact face.

On a podcast in early 2024 she described finding sexually explicit AI images of herself circulating on X while she was still a minor. She told her mother. She learned her team had already tried to get them removed and hit a wall. She kept returning to one question. "Why is it so hard to take down? That was my whole thought on it, was, 'Why is this allowed?'" And then, more simply: "That was my main thought process was, 'Down. Take this down. Please.'" What unsettled her most was the permanence and the attachment to her name: "You can't take them down." "It freaked me out that someone thinks that's cool."

<p class="inner-thought">Take it down. That is the entire thought. Never mind who did this or why me or whether it is even legal. Just: that is on there with my face, and when someone types my name it is going to be sitting right there, and I need it gone, and they are telling me it cannot be gone.</p>

Here is the honest skeptic's version of Xochitl Gomez, and it deserves a fair hearing. A cynic hears a girl respond to a sexual crime with worry about how it looks and calls it vanity. The same cynic watches the bottomless positivity and calls it a media-trained child-star brand. Concede the surface: her stated hurt really is about image, and even the ease is partly a tool. She has talked about setting boundaries so she worries less about what she cannot control and leaning on a tight friend group as a "positive distraction." Some of the calm is built on purpose.

The Enneagram is what turns that read from an accusation into an explanation. For a Three, the image is the load-bearing wall of the self, built brick by brick since age five on movie sets that were the family's livelihood. So when something appears that is "on here with my face" and yet "has nothing to do with me," the violation is not abstract at all. It is the hijacking of the one thing she was raised to author. Her instinct, "take this down, please," then "I just try not to think about it and put my phone away," is the only move a kid taught to protect the performance was ever handed. She controls her response with total precision, in the exact arena where she was allowed no control over the event. Her remarks resurfaced in January 2026, when a wave of AI deepfakes generated through xAI's Grok swept up other young women too, [Millie Bobby Brown](/personality-analysis/millie-bobby-brown) among them, and a violation she had already filed away came back around without her consent, again.

## The two things Xochitl Gomez does with her own face

The clearest window into how Xochitl Gomez runs her own image is a pair of habits she has mentioned in passing, and they only make sense together.

<div class="contrast-panel">
<div>
<h4>The face she keeps</h4>
<p>"A lot of people don't realize but I wear the same lipstick everywhere I go," she told Brit + Co in 2024, "with a little brown lip liner." Red carpets, ordinary days, all of it, the same fixed mouth. One reliable, repeatable public face, standardized so it never has to be reinvented on the spot.</p>
</div>
<div>
<h4>The face she scrubs</h4>
<p>Getting makeup off has become, in the same 2024 interview, a "pet peeve" and close to a "hyperfixation." She cannot stand to leave it on too long. Lately she pairs the ritual with meditation "to kind of align my body." The second the performance is over, she wants every trace of it gone.</p>
</div>
</div>

Both habits are the same program running in two directions. Keep the public face locked and predictable. Then, in private, strip it off as fast as possible and get back to whatever is underneath. It is the portrait of someone who has near-total command of her presentation and a real, physical need for a place where the presentation ends.

That second habit is also the part the type does not fully account for, and it is worth saying so. The pull to scrub it all off and sit with the body underneath is the part of her that senses there is a self worth returning to. In Xochitl, the compulsion to end the performance may be the most hopeful thing about her.

## The Xochitl her fans fell for

The composed girl this analysis keeps describing is real. She is also, a lot of the time, unfiltered and giggly and incapable of getting through a sentence without pulling a face. She interrupts herself. She laughs at her own answers before she lands them. On the Dancing with the Stars floor she beamed and bounced at the judges' scores the way a kid does at a birthday cake, and none of it looked managed, because none of it was.

This is the version her fans actually fell for, and it is worth enjoying at face value instead of decoding. Not every bright thing she does is a maneuver. Sometimes a buoyant young woman is exactly as buoyant as she looks, and the delight is just delight, with nothing underneath that needs explaining.

That loose, unguarded girl is the clearest look we get at the self she scrubs back down to every night.

## The Dancing with the Stars trophy that belonged to her nana

The self under the makeup stays private. Her public wins run the other way, handed outward to the people who built the life around her. When she won Dancing with the Stars in December 2023 with partner Val Chmerkovskiy, the couple posted a perfect 120 out of 120 in the finale and lifted the first Len Goodman Mirrorball Trophy. She was seventeen, the youngest competitor of that season. She was not, for the record, the youngest champion the show has ever crowned; Laurie Hernandez won at sixteen, and Shawn Johnson also won at seventeen. But the run had a detail that tells you more than the score.

The show was not really her dream. It was her grandmother's. "My nana always was like, you need to go on the show," she explained. "It would just be a really crazy full-circle moment getting to bring my nana and being like, hey Nana, I'm a star on this, how crazy is that."

That is the whole flavor of her ambition. The achievement is not for a shelf. It is a thing she can carry back to the people who built her, proof offered up as love. Chmerkovskiy, who spent a season with her at close range, gave away how hard she actually works while sounding like he was bragging about himself, telling ET in 2023: "My student this season makes me look like a brilliant teacher because she is very talented, picks things up very fast." He also let the protective note slip, the thing partners of a Three often feel: "Most of my fear is because I'm nervous for you."

She won the way she does everything. Enormous preparation, delivered so smoothly it reads as gift.

## How Xochitl Gomez handles what she can't control

Put the three hardest moments of her young career side by side and the same response repeats.

She lost the role of Dawn Schafer on The Baby-Sitters Club when Doctor Strange scheduling made it impossible to do both. Her reaction was to bless the replacement: "I think Kyndra Sanchez is going to do an amazing job." No visible resentment, no fight for the part. When the homophobic mobs came for America Chavez, she did not engage them; she kept performing the representation. When the deepfakes came, she said her piece once and put the phone away.

That is the classic Three under pressure. When the achievement engine cannot fix the problem, the drive stalls into [withdrawal](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-types-in-stress): she goes still, looks away, refuses to keep staring at the thing. In Xochitl it looks like grace, and some of it genuinely is. It is also a way of not feeling what she cannot solve.

The growth version is already visible, and it points the other direction. On the Doctor Strange set she noticed she was holding back. "I was afraid to fully go a hundred percent," she said, while Olsen "was constantly going there and wasn't afraid, and I was like, I just need to do that too." Olsen also handed her advice that clearly stuck, which she recounted to Brit + Co in 2024: "Don't be afraid to speak up, just don't be afraid to say it and just be a part of the conversation." Talking about the deepfakes at all, plainly, on the record, at seventeen, is Xochitl taking that advice.

She is twenty now, no longer the seventeen-year-old the deepfake story keeps freezing her as. In 2026 she carried her first grown-up lead in the horror film HIVE and turned up in a Johnny Orlando music video, the work of someone starting to pick her own projects instead of a prodigy driven from audition to audition. The work keeps coming. The open question is whether the girl who scrubs her face clean every night can learn to let people see what is under there before she has it perfectly composed.

### Xochitl Gomez's Wing: 3w2

The 2 wing is what keeps her Three from ever reading as cold or purely careerist. Textbook Threes can come across as calculating; Xochitl comes across as sunlight, and the people who work with her reach for words like "kindness" and "goodness" (Olsen) and "a force" (Chmerkovskiy). ### Xochitl Gomez's Instinctual Subtype: Social 3 with a self-preservation undercurrent

The image-consciousness is loud enough to suggest a social slant: becoming the visible face, the represented hero, the one who "belongs with the superheroes now." Social Threes track how they land with a whole audience, and her deepfake distress, framed around what "pops up when you look up my name," is an audience-level worry. The self-preservation undercurrent shows in the grind itself, the harness hours and the family-trade discipline, the part of her that wants to actually be good and secure rather than only look good. The counter-type flavor of the self-preservation Three, which hides its effort and distrusts show-offs, fits the way she narrates enormous labor as if it were nothing. ### Stress and Growth Arrows

Under stress, Threes take on the qualities of an unhealthy Nine: the drive stalls into withdrawal and avoidance. Her pattern of going quiet and looking away from what she cannot control (the trolls, the deepfakes, the lost role) is that arrow in a mild, functional form. In growth, Threes move toward the healthy side of Type 6: honesty, collaboration, letting themselves be held rather than only admired. Olsen's "speak up" advice, the tight friend group she leans on, and the plain on-record honesty about the deepfakes all run in that direction, toward being real with people instead of only being impressive to them.

### Counterarguments: Why Xochitl Gomez might not be a Type 3

The loudest rival is Type 7. She is bright, buoyant, and quick to move past pain, which is the Enthusiast's whole signature. Watch what she does with pain, though. A Seven scatters toward new options; Xochitl stays fixed on the projects in front of her and grinds through them. Rather than chase novelty to outrun the dark, she composes herself and keeps performing the same thing, which reads as heart-triad behavior more than head-triad.

Type 2 is the subtler rival because the warmth is so real. But a Two organizes the self around other people's needs and can lose track of their own wants entirely. Xochitl's frame stays on her own image and visibility, with the giving pointed at family as tribute. The service runs downstream of the Three rather than serving as the engine of a Two. Type 6 (the read on someone like Zendaya) is worth a glance too, since both are prepared young women, but Xochitl's anxiety is image-shaped rather than threat-shaped: a Six asks who is coming for me, while Xochitl asks how does this look on me. Confidence in the typing: high.

## The one thing she couldn't take down

Xochitl Gomez has spent her whole life learning to control the picture. The lipstick is always the same. The harness work never shows. The wins get wrapped and handed to her nana. When homophobes and lost roles and organized cruelty came at her, she managed her face and her voice with a discipline most adults never reach.

Then a stranger made a picture of her she never posed for, pinned it to her real name, and made it permanent. Everything she was raised to author, someone else authored for her, in the ugliest way available, while she was still a child. What should stop you is how she answered. She filed it. She put the phone down. She went back to work and kept the light on for everyone who looks up to her.

She can control her response to anything. The image, it turns out, was never fully hers to author.
