> "If everyone thinks you are normal, and your dream is imaginable by everyone, then you have failed to expand the dreamscape of humanity." — Yang Zhilin, interview with Zhang Xiaojun, 2024 (translated)

<p class="firstLetter">Near the end of the longest interview Yang Zhilin has ever given, the journalist Zhang Xiaojun asked him about the white piano at Moonshot AI's front door. Someone had propped a Pink Floyd record on it: <em>The Dark Side of the Moon</em>, the album Yang named his company after. He had been walking past it for days. "I don't even know who put it there," he said in that 2024 interview.</p>

The record sat in plain sight at a company built on noticing things. Moonshot's meeting rooms are named for Led Zeppelin, Deep Purple, the Rolling Stones, Metallica. The mythology assembled itself around him, employee by employee, while the founder looked somewhere else.

Two years later, in July 2026, a model he shipped in the middle of the night wiped billions off American chip stocks, and bankers began repeating a number: fifty billion dollars.

In Bloomberg's 2026 coverage, the standing description of the man behind all this is "a reclusive founder." His scientific creed, stated in nearly every interview he has given, is lossless compression: intelligence is whatever survives when nothing gets thrown away. His career reads differently. He turned down Apple. He walked out of his first company and into a five-investor arbitration. He spent 200 million yuan a month on ads, watched half his users leave anyway, then gave his best model away for free.

A man who believes nothing should be lost keeps losing things on purpose. Either the creed is a lie, or the losses are buying something he values more, and he has never said what.

## What is Yang Zhilin's personality type?

### Yang Zhilin is an Enneagram Type 5

Yang Zhilin is an Enneagram Type 5, the Investigator: a mind that treats understanding as safety and attention as a budget. The evidence: a one-axiom worldview, a single-sentence reply to a five-investor arbitration, and a habit of vanishing between model releases while the models speak for him.

Western profiles keep reaching for "China's [Sam Altman](/personality-analysis/sam-altman)." The comparison flatters the valuation and misses the man. Altman narrates his industry in public, essay by essay, hearing by hearing. Yang surfaces a few times a year, says something about scaling laws, and goes back inside. The British press settled on "baby-faced." Neither label explains why this particular person keeps detonating the market from behind a closed door.

The comparison a 2026 reader actually carries is Liang Wenfeng, the DeepSeek founder whose open-weight model set off the first of these chip-stock panics in January 2025. Two reclusive Chinese founders, two market-detonating releases, and profiles that keep swapping one for the other. But the resemblance stops at the money. Liang owns 84 percent of DeepSeek (Forbes, 2025), funds it from his own hedge fund's trading profits, and told a Chinese interviewer in 2023, "I don't want to share control of my company" (via TechCrunch, translated). Yang sold pieces of his company to Alibaba and a queue of venture funds, and nearly everything that goes wrong in this story, the ad war, the arbitration, the investor pressure now steering him toward a stock exchange, walks in through that door. Liang bought his silence up front. Yang has been paying for his in installments.

His worldview compresses to a single axiom, and he says so. "Machine learning and AI have been researched for 70 to 80 years, and the only thing that actually works is the scaling law" (Overseas Unicorn interview, 2024, translated). Everything else derives from it. Ask about products, he returns to scale. Ask about AGI, he prices it: "Strict AGI will still need tens of billions of USD" (Overseas Unicorn, 2024, translated). One idea, held with total conviction, deployed against every question. The record backs the confidence: his XLNet paper alone has been cited more than 10,000 times (The Wire China, 2025), and Chinese press has called him the most cited NLP researcher in China under 35.

Then there is the line that doubles as a self-portrait. Asked to explain his research philosophy, he offered: "Lossless compression is actually learning certainty in a chaotic environment" (Tencent Tech, 2024, translated). He was describing model training. The sentence describes the speaker just as well: chaos outside, certainty manufactured inside, and a lifelong preference for the smallest number of ideas that can hold the largest amount of world.

The behavior matches the psychology: attacked, he goes quiet and produces evidence; rival founders spent 2025 giving speeches while he shipped models. But force a Five to fight for mass attention and he will fight in a borrowed style, expensively. The middle of this story is about exactly that.

## Yang Zhilin's early life: an olympiad, a transfer, and a drum kit

Yang Zhilin was born in 1992 in Shantou, a port city on Guangdong's coast. The public record of his childhood consists of roughly three facts: the hometown, the year, and a prize in a regional informatics olympiad (The Telegraph, 2026). No family lore, no origin-story interviews, no childhood-bedroom photo essays. For most founders that gap would be a PR oversight. Here it reads as policy.

He entered Tsinghua University in thermal engineering and worked his way sideways into computer science, where he finished ranked first in the department (The Wire China, 2025). On the side he played drums in a rock band named after a data structure. Sources disagree on which one, a splay tree or a skip list, which may be the most Tsinghua dispute ever recorded. The chair he picked is its own data point: the drummer sits at the back, holds the tempo, and lets other people be looked at.

In 2015 he began a PhD at Carnegie Mellon under Ruslan Salakhutdinov and William Cohen. He finished in four years; six is typical. Along the way he co-authored Transformer-XL and XLNet with collaborators including Google's Quoc V. Le, and XLNet beat BERT, then the field's reigning model, across 20 benchmarks. Salakhutdinov calls him an "absolutely brilliant student" (The Wire China, 2025). Jian Ma, another CMU professor, remembers him as "sharp, open-minded and genuinely curious" (The Telegraph, 2026). Yang's own summary of the run is six words: "Not really talent, just working hard" (2024, translated).

When he graduated in 2019, Apple tried to recruit him (The Wire China). He said no and flew back to Beijing, where Recurrent AI, the company he had co-founded mid-PhD in 2016, was waiting. His advisor remembers the calculus:

> "I remember him telling me that if he didn't at least try starting his own company, he would regret it for the rest of his life." — Ruslan Salakhutdinov, The National, 2026

<p class="inner-thought">Take the offer and the next thirty years compile cleanly: Cupertino, a beautiful campus, other people's products. Decline it and nothing is guaranteed except an answer. He could live with the company failing. Living without knowing was the one outcome he had no way to price.</p>

By 2021 he had helped build two of China's earliest large language models, working on Huawei's PanGu and leading a team on Wu Dao at the Beijing Academy of Artificial Intelligence. Both projects proved the technology worked. Neither belonged to him.

In March 2026, on a Beijing stage, he compressed his whole career into one line: "The essence of building large models is converting more energy into intelligence" (Zhongguancun Forum, translated). The teenager who enrolled to study energy conversion is still at it, except the output now is intelligence instead of heat.

## Why Yang Zhilin named his company after the dark side of the moon

Moonshot AI was incorporated in March 2023, fifty years almost to the day after Pink Floyd released <em>The Dark Side of the Moon</em>. The Chinese name, 月之暗面, is the album title, literally. Asked why, he gave the least corporate naming rationale in the industry: "You usually only see the illuminated side of the moon, but the dark side remains mysterious. It's challenging, yet full of potential. That aligns with our mission" (First Push podcast, translated). To Zhang Xiaojun he put it with more heat: "We may be the earliest people to explore the dark side of the moon."

The same interview produced his description of what founding the company felt like: "It's a bit like driving on the road, with continuous snow-capped mountains in front of you, but you don't know what's inside."

The first product shipped in October 2023: a chatbot that could hold 200,000 Chinese characters of context while most rivals managed a fraction of that. Long context was the entire thesis. "Lossless long context is everything," he told Tencent Tech (2024, translated), and then went further:

<div class="pull-quote">
"If you have a context length of 1 billion, today's problems will cease to be problems."
</div>

Investors wired money at the idea. By February 2024, Alibaba was leading a round of more than $1 billion that valued the ten-month-old company around $2.5 billion, the richest large-model startup in China at the time.

The chatbot is called Kimi because he is. It is Yang's own English name (The Wire China, 2025). The most private founder in the industry shipped his first product with his own name on it, and the secret stayed in plain sight for years, because the name he actually guards is 杨植麟. Tens of millions of people keep Kimi on their home screens and tell it things they would not tell their families. They ask it everything. It has never told them anything about him.

## The interview where he answered feelings with numbers

Yang's unguarded record is small: a few long Chinese-language interviews, the longest with journalist Zhang Xiaojun in early 2024, translated into English by volunteers later that year. All quotes from it appear here in those translations. Read straight through, the interview develops a pattern worth replaying in slow motion.

<div class="dialogue">
<p><b>Asked whether the pressure makes him anxious:</b> "More excitement and thrill."</p>
<p><b>Asked about the odds of the whole thing collapsing:</b> "I accept the probability of failure."</p>
<p><b>Asked whether he is lonely:</b> "Hahaha... I think it's okay, because we still have dozens, or 100 people fighting together."</p>
</div>

Watch what each answer does. A question about fear comes back relabeled as appetite. A question about failure comes back as a probability, pre-accepted, filed. A question about loneliness comes back as a headcount. Every probe aimed at a feeling returns a quantity.

He audits desire the same way: "If you know something is not what you want, you won't FOMO. Because even if you get it, it's nothing." Want, evaluated down to a boolean.

The feeling is findable anyway. It lives in the vocabulary he reserves for the work: dark hemispheres, snow-capped mountains you cannot see inside, humanity's imagination. Ask about the man and you get arithmetic. Ask about the mission and you finally hear emotion. That allocation, everything for the problem, nothing for the self, is the clearest Type 5 signature anywhere in his record.

And the fear he never names directly has a shape in his own words. It is the imaginable life. The dream everyone can already picture. The quote this profile opened with sets his bar exactly there: if your dream fits inside other people's imaginations, you have failed. Most founders fear losing the company. Yang talks like a man who fears something quieter, a life small enough to be fully understood.

## How Kimi burned ¥200 million a month and lost half its users

By mid-2024 China's model war had become a marketing war, and Moonshot fought it with money. Monthly ad spend approached ¥200 million (36Kr). The cost of acquiring one user climbed from roughly ¥10 to more than ¥50. It worked, briefly: Kimi peaked around 36 million monthly active users in October 2024.

<div class="key-stat">¥200,000,000 a month: Moonshot's approximate peak advertising spend during the 2024 user war (36Kr)</div>

He had told everyone the plan earlier that year: "we can become a company that combines OpenAI's technology idealism with the business philosophy of ByteDance" (Overseas Unicorn, 2024, translated). The blitz was the ByteDance half of that sentence, executed with Alibaba's money.

Then January 2025. DeepSeek released R1: free, open-weight, viral, marketed by nothing except its own quality. Kimi's monthly actives fell to somewhere between 10 and 15 million by mid-2025 (36Kr). The company preaching losslessness had lost more than half its users, at a cost per head no spreadsheet could defend.

The playbook was never his, and that was the problem. Under sustained threat, a Five's energy scatters outward into borrowed motion, more spend, more channels, more speed ([how each type falls apart under stress](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-types-in-stress)), and that is a fair description of Moonshot's 2024. He was running someone else's company with both of his names on the door.

## The lawsuit Yang Zhilin answered with one sentence

The bill for the founding came due in November 2024. Five of the seven investors in Recurrent AI, his first company, filed arbitration against him in Hong Kong: GSR Ventures, Boyu Capital, Eminence Ventures, WestSummit Capital, and Zoo Capital (TechNode, 2024). The charge: Yang and Zhang Yutao, his co-founder at Recurrent who became Moonshot's CTO, started Moonshot in March 2023 without first securing consent waivers from Recurrent's backers, and the board resolution blessing the spin-off arrived only in January 2024, ten months after the company existed. By the account of GSR's Zhu Xiaohu, the loudest of the five, Zhang alone held around 14 percent of Moonshot while Recurrent's investors were collectively offered about 9.5 percent.

Zhu wanted a public reckoning and said the case should set a precedent for China's entire venture industry. Yang's full public response was one sentence: "I have gone through all necessary formalities to leave Recurrent and start a new business." Zhu's review of that sentence: it "dodged all the key questions" (SCMP, 2024).

Zhu is right about the sequence. The waivers were late. The split looks lopsided. The people who funded Yang's first act watched him build a twenty-billion-dollar second act on momentum their money helped create, and the paperwork requesting their permission showed up after the fundraising did. That grievance is real, and no personality analysis dissolves it.

What the grievance cannot explain is the order of operations, and the order of operations is the man. ChatGPT had detonated in November 2022. Yang had spent two years building large models inside institutions that owned the results. He knew how long paradigm windows stay open the way he knew a training run's schedule, and in February 2023 the window was open. He had already priced the regret once, out loud, to his advisor: not trying was the one loss he could not recover. So he moved the way he schedules compute, mission first, formalities queued behind it. To a compression thinker, process is redundancy, and redundancy is the first thing you cut. The cut had a cost, and other people paid part of it. Both things are true: it was the fastest path to the company, and the toll landed on people who once bet on him.

As of this writing there is no verified public resolution. The tribunal formed in early 2025, reporting has since gone quiet, and the IPO machinery now moving in Hong Kong implies some kind of peace that nobody involved has confirmed.

Zhu, for his part, reviewed K3 the week it shipped: "Both online reactions and our own initial use of the model have pointed to impressive reasoning and asset generation capabilities" (Yahoo Finance, July 2026). The skeptic conceded the model while the case he brought stayed open.

## How Kimi came back: a scrapped model and open weights

The recovery started before the crash. At Kimi's first-anniversary press session in November 2024, weeks before DeepSeek hit, Yang was already backing away from the growth war: "Focus first, then globalize. We need to be more patient" (KrASIA). After the collapse, patience became the whole strategy. Marketing spend was cut hard. Consumer growth stopped being the scoreboard.

Then came the bet. Partway through building a 200-billion-parameter model, he scrapped it and restarted at a full trillion parameters (Bloomberg, 2026). Weeks of sunk training became a write-off in service of scale.

Kimi K2 shipped in July 2025 as an open-weight, trillion-parameter model under a modified MIT license. Within days, Perplexity's chief executive posted that the model was "looking good on internal evals" and that Perplexity would "likely begin post-training on it pretty soon" (X, July 2025). Cursor went further and later built its own coding model on a Kimi base (2026). American products in front, Chinese weights underneath: the hemisphere doing the work stays out of view. K2 Thinking followed in November, built to chain hundreds of tool calls. K2.5 arrived in January 2026, multimodal. His stated rationale for giving the work away: open weights put frontier intelligence in anyone's hands at almost no cost (Zhongguancun Forum, 2026).

Open-sourcing was the first growth strategy he ever ran that behaves like him. No ads. No spokesman. Depth, distributed, allowed to argue for itself. The pattern reads healthier too: fewer hedges, bigger commitments, resources spent instead of guarded.

It also settles the question this profile opened on. A compressor's first move is deletion: decide what counts as signal, discard the rest, and the signal survives without loss. The Apple offer, the first company, half the users, now the weights themselves: every loss in the ledger was redundancy, spent to keep one thing intact. The creed was never a lie. He has been running lossless compression on his own life the whole time, and the only thing he has never been willing to lose is the work.

His old advisor watched from Pittsburgh as the strategy peaked:

> "What a huge win for the open-source community! It feels like just yesterday Zhilin was graduating from my lab at CMU." — Ruslan Salakhutdinov, Fortune, July 2026

That standard, originality as a survival requirement, is the <a href="/enneagram-corner/enneagram-wings-complete-guide">4 wing</a> talking.

## The hemisphere Yang Zhilin keeps dark

Kimi K3 appeared on July 17, 2026, in the middle of the night, China time: 2.8 trillion parameters, open weights, released hours before Xi Jinping opened the World Artificial Intelligence Conference. American chip stocks slid the next morning as analysts reached for the only available comparison, a second DeepSeek moment (CNBC, Fortune). Arena's chief executive called it "the single biggest release of the year." [Elon Musk](/personality-analysis/elon-musk), building the rival model Grok, allowed one word: "impressive."

Moonshot now sells subscriptions faster than it can serve them; new sign-ups were frozen in late July because demand ran past what its data centers could carry (Bloomberg). Annualized revenue tripled inside four months. Bankers are circulating a Hong Kong IPO within six months, at valuations that started at $31.5 billion and have been repeated as high as $50 billion (SCMP, Qz). Yang turns thirty-four this year. The British shorthand, baby-faced, at least records the fact the bankers skip past: the man they are pricing at fifty billion dollars finished graduate school seven years ago.

Every profile written this month calls it his arrival. Read them closely and notice what is missing: him. The fresh quotes come from bankers, analysts, and his old professors. The Yang quotes are the same 2024 translations this profile has been drawing on, because that is the last time he sat still and let someone keep asking.

One exchange from that year belongs here. Asked whether he worried about a soft exit, some acceptable landing for the company if AGI stays out of reach, he said: "I'm not at all anxious about making a safe landing" (Tencent Tech, 2024, translated). The entire financial world is currently staring at Moonshot's landing gear. The IPO. The valuation. The lockups. He has already said, in the only long interview anyone has, that the landing is the part he does not think about.

The white piano is presumably still there, just inside Moonshot's door, the record still propped on it, whoever put it there still unasked. The album it advertises has a famous cover: a prism on a black field, a thin beam of white light entering one face, a band of color leaving the other. Thirty-one billion dollars, thirty-six million users, one long interview. The spectrum keeps widening. The glass stays black.
