> "I am subject. I am not object." — Zooey Deschanel

<p class="firstLetter">Zooey Deschanel is forty-six years old, engaged to a property-television host, the mother of two children, the co-founder of three companies, and she still lies awake some nights replaying things she said when she was eight.</p>

She told Dax Shepard this on Armchair Expert. She wasn't being cute. She wasn't workshopping a bit. She was describing a normal Tuesday.

"I think about stuff I said when I was eight years old," she said. "I think about [it] nonstop."

This is not the woman the internet has been arguing about for fifteen years. The internet has been arguing about a character. The character wears Peter Pan collars and bangs and a ukulele and a smile that the internet eventually decided was too much smile. The character has a name. Her name is Jess Day, and she lives on Fox.

The woman who plays Jess Day spent her twenties submitting her music to critics anonymously, because she didn't trust them to listen if they knew her name. She spent her thirties giving notes on a sitcom shooting schedule that drove her, by her co-stars' own description, to be _the boss_. She has spent her forties politely, repeatedly, on every magazine cover she lands, refusing the only word the culture seems to want to use about her.

That word is _quirky_. Or _adorkable_. Or worse, _manic pixie dream girl_.

The cuter the costume, the bigger the gap between the costume and the body inside it. And the body inside it is anxious.

## What Is Zooey Deschanel's Personality Type?

### Zooey Deschanel Is an Enneagram Type 6

[Type 6](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-6) is the Loyalist. The Six's core question is _can I trust this?_ — the situation, the person, the praise, the criticism, the offer, the audition, the script, the future. The Six's anxiety isn't theatrical despair. It's a low, constant scan for what could go wrong, paired with an enormous appetite for evidence that something probably will.

A casual fan would type Zooey as a Type 4 — the Individualist, the artistic one, the quirky outsider. A Four would embrace _quirky_. A Four would build an identity around being misunderstood. Zooey rejects _quirky_. She has been rejecting it on the record since New Girl premiered. She has said, repeatedly, that what she actually wanted as a kid was _to be normal_.

That is a Six tell. Fours mythologize their difference. Sixes are uncomfortable with it.

The 7 wing is what the public has confused for a Four. The 7 wing is the part that loves vintage dresses and bubblegum pop and the Christmas album and the ukulele and the cardigans. The 7 wing is the optimistic, plural, scattered part of the Six that wants to enjoy things. The Six core is the part that audits which things are safe to enjoy in the first place.

Most people see the wing. The engine is the type.

## Zooey Deschanel's Childhood: The Cinematographer's Daughter Who Hated Travel

Zooey grew up in two houses. The literal one, in Los Angeles, with her older sister Emily and her mother Mary Jo. And the metaphorical one — the airplane, the location set, the borrowed apartment near whichever movie her father, Oscar-nominated cinematographer Caleb Deschanel, was shooting that year.

She has been very specific about which one she preferred.

"She lived in Los Angeles, but spent much of her childhood traveling because her father shot films on location, which she hated as it involved leaving her friends and interacting with unfamiliar cultures."

That sentence is from the public biographical record. Most kids in Hollywood orbit beg to come along on the location shoots. She begged to stay home. The thing the airplane represented to her was the loss of the things she had built a life out of: the same friends, the same room, the same bedtime, the same week. Novelty was not a treat. Novelty was what the parents were doing _to_ her.

The school she settled into — Crossroads, in Santa Monica — became the answer. She has talked about Crossroads with the kind of loyalty most people reserve for family. Her two closest school friends, <a href="/personality-analysis/jake-gyllenhaal">Jake Gyllenhaal</a> and <a href="/personality-analysis/kate-hudson">Kate Hudson</a>, are still in her life four decades later. She does not graduate out of friendships. She graduates _into_ them and never leaves.

She started auditioning young. Her father read lines with her. Her mother, an actress herself, coached her. She told Dax Shepard auditions always made her nervous. She didn't say _used to_ make her nervous. Auditions, at forty-six, with twenty-five years of credits, still made her nervous. The fear didn't go away with practice. She learned to work _through_ the fear instead of past it.

> "Having anxiety and having fears is a good thing in that when you face those fears and anxiety it gives you strength."

That is the Six's version of optimism. Not _it'll be fine_. _The fear is the gym._

## How Almost Famous Got Zooey Deschanel Out of Northwestern

In the fall of 1998, Zooey enrolled at Northwestern University. For a kid raised on movie sets — by Caleb Deschanel, the cinematographer who shot _The Black Stallion_, _The Right Stuff_, _The Natural_, _The Patriot_, and _The Passion of the Christ_, and by an actress mother, in a household where her older sister Emily was already auditioning for the leads that would later make her the face of Fox's _Bones_ for twelve seasons — college was the structured path. The not-show-business path. The path her parents had not taken.

She lasted nine months.

Cameron Crowe cast her as Anita Miller in _Almost Famous_. Anita is the older sister who, in the first fifteen minutes, packs a copy of _Simon &amp; Garfunkel's Greatest Hits_ into a bag and walks out of her mother's house with one line that the movie hangs on for the next two hours: _One day, you'll be cool_. Anita is the sister who escapes the family scan. The character launches the protagonist's life. The actress played her, dropped out of Northwestern, and started auditioning full-time.

Then came the eight-year hole between recognizable hits. _The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy_ in 2005. _Failure to Launch_ in 2006. _Tin Man_ in 2007. _Yes Man_ in 2008. Roles that paid, roles that didn't quite stick. The cultural moment in that stretch was _Elf_, in 2003 — the shower duet of "Baby It's Cold Outside" with Will Ferrell, the small scene that planted _Zooey can sing_ in the public mind years before any of her own records came out. It was a four-minute karaoke scene in an otherwise unrelated Christmas movie. It was also the audition reel that produced the rest of her career.

The role that fused her face to a label was _(500) Days of Summer_, in 2009. She played Summer Finn, a character who tells the male lead, on the record, that she doesn't believe in love and isn't looking for a relationship. He projects his fantasy onto her anyway. She breaks his heart. She marries someone else. The character is, on the page, the explicit refutation of the manic pixie dream girl trope — a woman who refuses to be the dream girl.

> "To anyone who thinks Summer is the villain, I say: think a little deeper."

The culture watched her play that part — the woman who said _I am not your fantasy_ — and decided her face _was_ the fantasy. That is the seed of the fight she has been having ever since. She was cast as the rebuttal. The audience saw the costume.

## How She &amp; Him Got Reviewed Without Anyone Knowing It Was Zooey Deschanel

This is the signature detail. If you remember one thing about Zooey Deschanel that is not the bangs, remember this.

In 2008, she and the indie folk musician M. Ward put out a record called _Volume One_, under the band name _She &amp; Him_. _She_ was Zooey. _Him_ was Ward. The name was deliberately bland. The cover art was deliberately retro. And the promotional copy that went out to music critics deliberately did not mention that _She_ was a working actress with two studios willing to back any movie she wanted to make.

She has explained why, in language that is too casual for what she's actually saying:

> "We talked about liking the idea of a name that's sort of anonymous and sort of humble. I also like the name, the band, because it's not about being like the star of anything, it's about the music."

Listen to what's inside that quote. _Anonymous_. _Humble_. _Not about being the star of anything_. Those are the words of someone who has already imagined the bad review. Who has already pictured the critic squinting at the back cover, seeing _Zooey Deschanel_, and writing the line _the actress tries her hand at music_ before the needle drops.

A Three would put her face on the cover. A Four would lean into the autobiographical narrative. A Six runs the album to the door of every reviewer in town with no name attached and waits to see if anyone says anything nice before claiming it.

M. Ward, who'd first heard her sing on the karaoke scene in _Elf_, told NPR that hearing her in a studio for the first time blew him over. _"This is someone that has a bunch of records already recorded,"_ he said he'd thought. _"I was amazed to find out that she didn't have any records."_

She has been amazed-into-existence by Ward for eighteen years now. _Volume One_ in 2008. Six more albums. A Christmas record. A live album. A holiday tour that became its own institution. Same partner. Same project. Same voice. That kind of loyalty is structural, not romantic.

In September 2009, eighteen months after _Volume One_'s release, Zooey married Ben Gibbard, the frontman of Death Cab for Cutie — indie rock's most prominent voice during the exact window she was building her own indie music career. Two years later, they separated. By the end of 2012, the divorce was finalized. The musical partnership that survived the period was not the marriage. It was the one with Ward. Sixes' loyalty isn't unconditional — it is structural, built around the work, in containers the Six has direct authorship over. The album cycle survives. The household doesn't always.

## The Boss of New Girl Was Quieter Than Jess

In 2011, Fox bought a pilot called _Chicks &amp; Dicks_ from a writer named Liz Meriwether. The pilot needed a lead. Meriwether interviewed every quirky young actress in town. When she met Zooey, she said something that has been quoted in every New Girl retrospective since:

> "I didn't think I could find someone as weird as I am."

The show became _New Girl_. The character Zooey played, Jess Day, became the most public version of her face for seven years. Jess sang to herself. Jess made up jingles. Jess wore Peter Pan collars. Jess was _adorkable_. Fox put _adorkable_ on the marketing.

Behind the camera, the woman playing Jess was running notes on the script.

Jake Johnson, the actor playing Nick — Jess's love interest for seven seasons — has said it plainly. _"Zooey was our boss. She really cared about tone."_ He has described how the two of them would text each other before scenes, talking through the moment, deciding how to land it, before they got to the soundstage and the directors caught up.

Reports started leaking, around season three, that Zooey was _difficult_. That she was making schedule demands. That she would push back when the production tried to stack her scenes in ways she didn't think served the show. The trade press loved the word _diva_.

The trade press misread the word. A Six who doesn't trust the room she's in starts running the room — not for the throne, but because she can't stand to watch anyone else mishandle it. The way Liz Meriwether put it later — pushing back on the _adorkable_ marketing she herself had inadvertently licensed — was telling: _"I don't think the show is adorkable."_ The show's two weirdest women, the creator and the lead, both kept trying to walk back the brand the network had built around them.

Jess looked like she was making it up as she went. The actress playing Jess was building in two extra hours every morning so she could leave the house without the panic of running back inside. _"If I don't wake up two hours before I have to leave, I'll be like, oh my God, running back in the house like 40 times,"_ she told Dax Shepard. _"I need more time than other people to do things. I need to build in more time."_

That is not the work of an adorkable woman. It is the work of a woman with a checklist that does not stay checked.

## Why Zooey Deschanel Will Not Stop Fighting the Manic Pixie Label

In 2007, the film critic Nathan Rabin coined the phrase _manic pixie dream girl_ — using, partly, Zooey's character in _Elizabethtown_ as the prototype. Two years later, _(500) Days of Summer_ came out, and the phrase fused to her face. By 2011, the New Republic ran a whole essay headlined _Enough Quirkiness! Why I Can't Stand Zooey Deschanel_. By 2012, she was responding to it in print.

In 2026, she is still responding to it.

This is the Six pattern at its most public. A healthier Four would have shrugged. A Seven would have laughed it off. A Three would have rebranded. The counterphobic Six does not let it go. The counterphobic Six writes the rebuttal, and writes it again, and writes it again, on every cover, in every podcast, in every Q&amp;A, for fifteen years.

> "It's a way of making a woman one-dimensional, and I'm not one-dimensional." (_The Guardian_, 2022)

> "I want to be a fucking feminist and wear a fucking Peter Pan collar." (_Glamour_)

The epigraph at the top of this piece — _I am subject. I am not object._ — is hers, from the same Bustle profile that gave us _adorkable_. It is the line that doesn't sound like her. It sounds academic. It sounds like a thesis defense. That is because, for a counterphobic Six, the label is a threat to be answered, and the answer has to be load-bearing enough to stand up the next time the threat shows up. _Do not write me. I write._

Read in isolation, fighting a fifteen-year-old film-criticism term for fifteen years looks slightly unhinged. Read through the Six lens, it is what loyalty looks like when the thing the Six is being loyal to is _her own continuity_. She isn't defending a brand. She's defending the version of herself that she can recognize when she looks in the mirror.

The Peter Pan collar is real. So is the rage at the Peter Pan collar being used against her.

## Three Companies Is a Lot of Companies for an Actress

Most working actresses, asked what they're doing with the New Girl money, will say _real estate_, _producing_, or _nothing_. Zooey said: _a media company, an indoor farming startup, and a grocery-rewards app_.

In 2011, with friends, she co-founded **HelloGiggles**, a women's media site she sold to Time Inc. for a reported $30 million in 2015. In 2019, with her then-husband Jacob Pechenik — a film producer she'd married in 2015 and would divorce by 2020 — she co-founded **Lettuce Grow**, an indoor farming company that sells hydroponic _Farmstands_ made from recycled ocean-bound plastic. In 2021, she joined **Merryfield**, a sustainability-focused grocery rewards app, as Chief Creative Officer.

Three companies. Three industries. Three sets of co-founders. None of them in entertainment. One of them outlasted the marriage that built it. She and Pechenik co-parent two children — Elsie Otter and Charlie Wolf — and still co-run Lettuce Grow. The marriage ended. The venture didn't.

A Three diversifies for the optics. A Six diversifies because the airbag in the car has an airbag — what if the audition pipeline dries up, what if the network cancels, what if the next role doesn't come, what if the marriage ends and the lifestyle goes with it. Each company is a different kind of insurance for a different kind of rainy day.

When _Entrepreneur_ asked her about the businesses, she said the kind of thing Sixes accidentally say when they're being honest about their motivations: that she likes _quirky_ businesses, and businesses should embrace _quirky_, because the alternative is being indistinguishable from every other company. Read that twice. The same word she rejects when applied to her _person_, she _embraces_ when applied to her _portfolio_. _Quirky_ is dangerous when it makes her dismissible. _Quirky_ is a moat when it protects the businesses she built to never need anyone's permission again.

## How Zooey Deschanel and Jonathan Scott Met

In summer 2019, Apple TV+ filmed a Carpool Karaoke episode pairing the Deschanel sisters — Zooey and Emily — with the Scott Brothers, Drew and Jonathan, of HGTV's _Property Brothers_. The producers, Zooey has since said, did not pick the four of them at random. _"It wasn't random,"_ she told the Drew Barrymore Show. _"I wasn't thinking I'd find romance, but I thought we'd find a fun afternoon."_

She and Jonathan Scott started dating not long after. They got engaged in 2023. They are, at the time of this writing, still engaged.

It is worth pausing on the conditions under which this relationship started. Zooey met her current partner _on camera_. With both of their siblings present. With a producer's clipboard nearby. With multiple cameras rolling. With a podcast-radio host between them in the back seat. With a karaoke playlist running. _"How many people can say they have them meeting their partner — the whole first meeting on video?"_ she said, marvelling at it later. _"I don't think that many people can say that — with our siblings present."_

Almost nobody, Zooey. That is the point.

This is the most controlled environment imaginable in which to meet a stranger. Witnesses. A pre-vetted social context. A producer who already cleared him. A blood relative on either side. A schedule. A wrap time. None of the unstructured uncertainty of _meeting someone at a bar_. None of the ambient threat of _swiping_. By the time the cameras stopped rolling, she had a documented record of every word he'd said to her, in case she ever needed to check it.

Jonathan Scott's first impression survived the playback. _"It was simultaneous sparks,"_ he later told Parade. _"It was unlike anything I've ever experienced."_

She wasn't looking for romance that afternoon. She was looking for evidence. The cameras provided it.

## The Things She Said at Eight

She is a working actress. She is a working musician. She is a co-founder three times over. She is, by net worth estimates, somewhere around twenty-five million dollars. She has been on the cover of every magazine that publishes covers. She has a fiancé who tells daytime television, unprompted, that meeting her was the most special moment of his life. She has two children she is trying very deliberately to teach that the world will not always treat them this nicely.

> "I need them to know we're getting treated nicer than you're supposed to get treated. I also need to prep them for the reality of life, which is people are not excited to wait on you."

That is the inner voice talking. That is the Six who, even from the top of an industry, is still scanning the exits, still preparing the children for the day the warmth runs out, still teaching the lesson she clearly learned somewhere herself.

The last line of the Bustle profile reads: _"I'm not really ever, in my life, trying to be someone else."_ Read in 2023, it sounds like a feminist statement. Read in the context of everything else she has said about her own mind, it sounds like an admission. She is not trying to be someone else. She is, however, trying very hard to be someone the eight-year-old version of her wouldn't be embarrassed by.

The bangs are real. The cardigans are real. The ukulele is real. The Peter Pan collars are real.

So is the woman who lies awake remembering what she said in the third grade.
