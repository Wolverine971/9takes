> "I released the anxiety I was going through. I released depression and I also released some weight on my body."
> — Lizzo, 2025, on her weight loss

<p class="firstLetter">In September 2022, the United States Library of Congress loaned Lizzo a 200-year-old crystal flute that once belonged to President James Madison. Two Capitol Police officers walked it onto a stage in Washington, D.C. Lizzo, in sparkly silver, took the flute, lifted it to her lips, played a few bars, twerked once, and handed it back. The whole moment lasted maybe ten seconds. Republicans called it desecration. The internet called it the most American thing that had ever happened. Lizzo called it "a moment."</p>

<span class="key-stat__number">209 years</span>
<span class="key-stat__label">that the crystal flute sat in the Library of Congress untouched. Lizzo was the first person ever to play it.</span>

A year later, three of her former dancers — Arianna Davis, Crystal Williams, and Noelle Rodriguez — filed a complaint accusing the four-time Grammy winner of sexual harassment, weight-shaming, religious harassment, false imprisonment, and creating a hostile work environment. The dancers in question were fat dancers and Black dancers, hand-picked from her own body-positivity reality show, _Watch Out for the Big Grrrls_.

The brand was joy. The complaint was that the joy was a performance.

You cannot understand either of those moments — the crystal flute or the lawsuit — without understanding what Enneagram theory calls the **Type 7**, the Enthusiast. She did not choose joy because she felt it. She chose it because she could not afford to feel anything else.

## What is Lizzo's Personality Type?

### Lizzo Is an Enneagram Type 7

Lizzo is a Type 7 with an 8 <a href="/enneagram-corner/enneagram-wings-complete-guide">wing</a>. The core type is the Enthusiast — the one whose mind metabolizes any disappointment into the next possibility. The wing is the Challenger, which is why her Type 7 doesn't read as flighty: it reads as loud, armored, and willing to fight for the right to feel good. A 7w6 Lizzo would be anxious and team-loyal. A 7w8 Lizzo, the one we actually have, runs the joy engine at industrial scale and dares the world to interrupt it.

Most public Type 7s — Robin Williams, Anthony Bourdain, Cameron Diaz — manifest a specific tell: their optimism is so insistent it implies what it's running from. Lizzo's tell is the same. The flute solos in the middle of breakup songs. The body-positivity anthems built on a year of homelessness. The signature greeting — "Bitch!" — yelled as a hello, not a threat. The relentless production of feel-good content from a person who, in March 2024, posted "I'm starting to feel like the world doesn't want me in it" and announced, "I QUIT."

She did not quit. Four days later, she clarified: "I quit giving any negative energy attention." That is the Type 7 in two sentences. The pain registers. The reframe arrives before the pain can land.

The Type 7 lens makes one thing immediately legible: Lizzo's brand and Lizzo's pathology are the same engine. The thing that built her — turning every limitation into a launchpad, every loss into a possibility — is also the thing that, by 2023, was being described under oath as a hostile workplace. <a href="/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-7">Type 7</a>'s defining wound is the inability to stay with pain long enough to learn what it has to say.

## How Houston Built Lizzo's Joy Engine

Melissa Viviane Jefferson was born in Detroit in 1988. The family moved to Houston when she was nine, joining the Pentecostal church her grandfather had founded. Her mother sang gospel. Her father, Michael Jefferson, was a music engineer who handed his daughter a flute in fifth grade. By high school she was in marching band. By college she was on scholarship at the University of Houston, majoring in music, playing piccolo in the Cougar Marching Band.

The Type 7 pattern is laid down early, and Houston laid hers down clean. Pentecostal church meant performance as devotion — joy as a religious obligation, not just a feeling. Marching band meant that the way you participated in your community was by being a coordinated source of energy. Classical flute training meant she had to practice the same passage one hundred times in a row, which is the one thing Type 7s structurally hate, and she became excellent at it anyway.

Enneagram theory groups Type 7 with Types 2 and 9 in what's called the Positive Outlook harmonic — the three types who instinctively reframe, minimize, or redirect away from anything that threatens the room's good feeling. The mechanism is not denial. It is real cognitive work, fast and creative, performed in the space between hearing bad news and feeling it. Lizzo's childhood gave her every reason to develop this faster than most.

Years later, on Beyoncé's _Renaissance_ tour stop in Poland, Lizzo described what watching Beyoncé felt like from the audience: "In that moment I was thinking about baby me, listening to _Happy Face_ by Destiny's Child and crying myself to sleep because of bullies." The bullying is mentioned. It is also routed instantly into the next sentence, which is about possibility, lineage, and how cyclically connected everyone is. The grown woman has not stopped doing what the child invented. She just has a louder microphone.

## Why Lizzo's Father's Death Started the Pattern

Michael Jefferson died in 2009. Lizzo was 20.

"I actually stopped talking," she said years later, on the _On Air with Ryan Seacrest_ retrospective. "It was like an actual kind of breakdown that I had, and it was really, really difficult. I felt like my life didn't really have purpose anymore."

For the next year, she lived in her car. A used Subaru first, then the floor of her band's rehearsal space. In a 2019 _Teen Vogue_ interview she summarized that year in one of the most Type 7 sentences a Type 7 can say: "I was addicted to the gym, I didn't eat, and I was sleeping in a dusty car, all for music."

Read it again slowly. That is not a sentence about recovery. That is a sentence about escape. The gym addiction, the not-eating, the dusty car — these are the symptoms of a 20-year-old who has just lost her father and cannot afford to sit with the loss for one second. The phrase "all for music" is the reframe in real-time. The grief gets repackaged as artistic dedication. The body gets used as a tool for outrunning what the mind cannot face.

The pattern did not pay off for ten years. _Lizzobangers_ came out in 2013. _Big GRRRL Small World_ in 2015. The _Coconut Oil_ EP in 2016. "Truth Hurts" was originally released in 2017 and disappeared. Pitchfork ignored her. Radio ignored her. She kept building the next thing on top of the failure of the last thing because that is what a Type 7 does when grief is the alternative. The treadmill ran at low altitude for a decade before the brand caught up to it.

When it finally did, the architecture was already built. _Truth Hurts._ _Good as Hell._ _Juice._ _About Damn Time._ Every hit ran on the same circuit: a feeling that should hurt, redirected at high speed into a feeling that feels good.

Her producer Ricky Reed, who signed her to Nice Life Recording Company in 2015 and made every major record with her, told _Variety_ in 2022:

<blockquote>"I think of Lizzo's music almost as a sort of service-related art. Often its primary job, aside from her getting to express herself, is us being able to help the people that need some healing through music."</blockquote>

It is worth noticing what Reed actually said. The healing is for other people. The expression is for her.

## How Lizzo Turned Body Positivity Into a Brand

The thing that makes Lizzo a phenomenon and not just a pop star is the second product. She didn't just sell songs. She sold a feeling: that fat Black women, specifically, were allowed to take up space, dance hard, wear thongs at the BET Awards, twerk with the most powerful objects in American history, and be loved for it.

By the time _Cuz I Love You_ hit #4 on the Billboard 200 in 2019, the brand was fully assembled. The flute solos. The leotards. The "100% that bitch" tweet that became "Truth Hurts" became a #1 song that took three months to climb the chart after originally being released in 2017. The interviews where she addressed every reporter who asked "isn't body positivity a fad" by simply existing harder. The _Watch Out for the Big Grrrls_ reality show, where she auditioned plus-size dancers from across the country, picked the ones she wanted, and built them into her live show. She launched the Yitty shapewear line in 2022. She became the most visible "love your body" figure of her generation.

A Type 7's most efficient escape is forward motion, and a public Type 7 with a brand has unlimited fuel. Every album promotes the next album. Every interview promotes the next product. Every product reaffirms the original feeling. The treadmill is the point.

The first time the treadmill stalled was in June 2022. Lizzo released the single "Grrrls" with the line "Hold my bag, bitch / Hold my bag / Do you see this shit? / I'm a spaz." Within twenty-four hours, Hannah Diviney, an Australian writer with cerebral palsy, posted that "spaz" is a slur tied directly to her condition. The disability community joined in. Within 72 hours, Lizzo had apologized, recut the lyric, and re-released the song on every streaming service. "I never want to promote derogatory language," her statement read. "As a fat Black woman in America, I've had many hurtful words used against me, so I overstand the power words can have." She did the right thing. She also did it at speed Type 7s reserve for closing emotional tabs. By Monday the conversation was over.

This is the architecture's hidden cost. Body positivity, sold at scale, is a promise that the seller has solved the problem the buyer is paying to solve. Lizzo had to perform "I am free in my body" for a decade in front of a camera while privately running the same Type 7 escape program she had been running since she lost her father. The brand and the wound were sharing the same machinery, and it ran twenty-four hours a day.

Her favorite stage flute, Sasha Floot, is named after Sasha Fierce — the alter ego Beyoncé invented on _I Am… Sasha Fierce_ so the "real" Beyoncé didn't have to be on stage. Hold that detail. The crystal flute makes more sense with it.

## Sasha Floot, the Crystal Flute, and the Possibility She Borrowed

On September 27, 2022, on the third night of her Special Tour, Lizzo became the only person ever to play the 200-year-old crystal flute owned by James Madison. The flute was offered to Madison in 1813 by Claude Laurent, a French craftsman, for his second inauguration. The Library of Congress had loaned it to no one in the intervening two centuries.

The detail that matters is not that she played it. The detail that matters is what she did with it.

She played a few measured notes — classical, technically clean, the work of someone who had spent her childhood drilling scales in a Houston marching band. Then, while still holding the flute, she twerked. One small, deliberate motion. A wink at the camera. The Capitol Police flute-handlers stood next to her trying not to laugh. The internet exploded. Right-wing commentators wrote columns. Lizzo posted a thank-you to the Library of Congress and immediately released a clothing drop for Yitty the next day.

That sequence — _play the relic, twerk on the relic, monetize within 24 hours_ — is the Type 7 mind operating at peak capacity. Lizzo did not stop to ask what it meant that a Black woman was the first person ever to play a slave-owning president's flute. She didn't even stop to play more than a few measures. Forward motion was the whole point: meet the relic, transform the relic, get a Yitty sales drop out of it by morning.

The crystal flute moment is also the brand at its most coherent. The Houston marching-band kid who slept in a Subaru is on a Washington stage playing a presidential artifact and turning it into both a meme and a sales channel. Every chapter of her life is in that single ten-second clip. None of the chapters are sitting still.

She told an Instagram caption afterward: "I JUST TWERKED AND PLAYED PRESIDENT JAMES MADISON'S CRYSTAL FLUTE FROM THE 1800s!! I'M THE FIRST AND ONLY PERSON TO EVER PLAY IT… WE MADE HISTORY TONIGHT!"

The all-caps is the wing. Type 7w8 doesn't whisper its triumphs.

## Why Lizzo's Dancers Said the Joy Was a Performance

In August 2023, three former dancers filed suit. Arianna Davis, Crystal Williams, and Noelle Rodriguez accused Lizzo and her dance captain of weight-shaming, religious harassment, racial harassment, false imprisonment, assault, and creating a sexually charged hostile work environment. The specific allegations included a night in Amsterdam's Red Light District in which Lizzo, the suit said, pressured Davis to touch a nude performer's breasts as the crew chanted her name.

Lizzo's first statement called the allegations "as unbelievable as they sound and too outrageous not to be addressed." Within days, Sophia Nahli Allison, the Oscar-nominated filmmaker who had begun directing a Lizzo documentary in 2019 and walked off after two weeks, posted her own account. "I was treated with such disrespect by her," Allison wrote on Instagram. "This kind of abuse of power happens far too often." She added that Lizzo "creates an extremely toxic and hostile working environment and undermines the work, labor and authority of other Black and brown women in the process."

The sentence that detonated the public conversation came from Arianna Davis, in an ABC News interview shortly after the suit was filed.

<div class="pull-quote">"I know it's alarming to hear that Lizzo, a plus size woman who preaches body positivity, would be fat-phobic, essentially. But I want to challenge people to understand that hurt people hurt people."</div>

That line is the entire analysis in fourteen words, delivered by someone who lived inside the brand.

In February 2024, Judge Mark H. Epstein dismissed the fat-shaming claims, ruling that the dancers were fired for secretly recording rehearsals, not for weight gain. The remaining claims — sexual harassment, hostile work environment, assault, false imprisonment, religious harassment — survived. A jury trial was set for December 2025. As of late 2025, the appellate phase was still active. Lizzo, in a public statement that month, said: "I am not settling. I will be fighting every single claim until the truth is out."

The fairest version of the Type 7 defense is real. The fat-phobia claim did not survive. Allison's "abuse of power" account is a single ex-collaborator's read, not testimony from a tribunal. A creative star with a demanding rehearsal room is not, by default, a hostile workplace. Sevens insist on their own innocence with a force other types do not have, and some of that force is correctly placed.

But "I am not settling" is not the joy talking. It is the part of the Type 7 that <a href="/enneagram-corner/enneagram-types-in-stress">slides toward Type 1, the Perfectionist</a>: rigid, righteous, unwilling to entertain that the room they ran might have looked different from the inside. The woman who has spent fifteen years selling "release the bad vibes" cannot release this one. The dancers said the joy was a performance. The instinct is to dispute the framing, not to ask what about the workplace produced it. That instinct is also a tell.

Whether the remaining claims survive trial is a legal question. The psychological question is different. The brand had a structural problem long before the lawsuit: a Type 7 cannot reliably deliver, in private, the feeling she is being paid to manufacture in public. Hurt people hurt people. The Type 7 who has not metabolized her pain is also a hurt person, just one moving so fast nobody can see the wound.

## How Lizzo Reframed Her Own Body Into Healing

Then she lost the weight.

By 2024, Lizzo had visibly slimmed down. The internet, predictably, said she was on Ozempic. She denied it, sharing before-and-after photos in late 2024 with the caption: "All these magazines and blogs wanting me to be on Ozempic soooo bad." She explained the weight loss had come from a calorie deficit, cardio, and protein.

But the more revealing sentence came on the Jay Shetty podcast and on Instagram, repeated in slightly different forms across 2025: "I released the anxiety I was going through. I released depression and I also released some weight on my body."

Read it once for the surface meaning. Then read it again.

She is not saying she got healthier and lost weight. She is saying the weight is the depression. The body becomes the same material the music was always made of — feelings she could not metabolize, repackaged as something she could release. The Type 7 reframe, applied for the first time to her own flesh.

This is also the most honest sentence Lizzo has ever given a microphone. She did not say "I love my new body." She did not say "I love my old body, too." She said: _the bad stuff came off, and the weight came off with it._ A reader can argue with the metaphysics. They cannot argue with the self-knowledge. The woman who built a billion-dollar movement on "love your body as it is" just admitted, on tape, that she had been releasing her body the same way she had been releasing every other feeling she did not want to keep.

She also told Kelly Clarkson, in early 2025: "I think when you are a pop star, they put you in a box, and you're never supposed to change. You're never supposed to get older… At this point in my life, this was the change I chose. I chose to release weight from my body for my own reasons."

The pop-star answer is "I chose." The Type 7 answer is the verb. _Release._

Meanwhile the music has stuttered in a way the press has not. Her planned 2024 album _Love in Real Life_ released two singles in early 2025; both missed the Billboard Hot 100. Pitchfork, which had ignored her for a decade and then trashed _Special_ in 2022, was actually kind to the title track — "Lizzo is back." The aesthetic critics were not the problem. The crowd was. In September 2025 she told _New York_ that she had written the album in 2022, that she "no longer felt aligned" with it, and that she had sat down with Atlantic Records to shelve the project. A mixtape called _My Face Hurts From Smiling_ followed instead — a title that, to anyone who has spent any time around a Type 7, is the closest thing to a confession the brand has ever permitted.

Ricky Reed, asked about that period, told _Variety_:

<blockquote>"It was the most vulnerable I've ever, ever seen her in the studio. She was saying things I'd never heard her say and being really honest about her feelings."</blockquote>

The producer who has been in the room with her for a decade is saying, in 2022, that he had not yet seen her be honest about her feelings. That is the cost of running the machinery at industrial scale. The room you make for everyone else's emotions has, at some point, to come back to you. Sevens, more than any other type, can defer that bill for a very long time.

There is one place where the bill might be coming due. In December 2024 — between the dismissed fat-shaming claims and the shelved album — Lizzo got engaged to her partner of four years, the comedian and designer Myke Wright. They had met in 2016, started dating quietly around 2021, hard-launched the relationship in February 2023 (a month before the dancers' suit), and married in September 2025. Four years is not a long marriage. It is, however, the longest Lizzo has stayed in one place since she stopped sleeping in the Subaru.

The Seven who has spent her whole career routing pain into the next possibility has, for the first time, chosen a possibility she did not reframe out of. That is either growth or another reframe dressed in a wedding dress. The honest read is that it is too early to know. Marrying someone four years in is a staying act. Calling weight loss "release" the same year is not. The two facts live in the same person and the same calendar quarter.

## What Lizzo Won't Let Herself Feel

Crystal Williams, one of the dancers suing her, said in 2023: "You can see a public figure and all you know about them is what they present to you when the cameras are on and the cameras are rolling."

That is true about any pop star. It is more true about a Type 7 pop star. The Positive Outlook engine is, by design, optimized for what the cameras catch. The work the Seven cannot do — the slow, joyless, unglamorous work of letting a feeling land before deciding what to make of it — happens, if it happens at all, in the cracks. In a Subaru in 2009. In the studio in 2022 when Ricky Reed says, for the first time, he heard her be vulnerable. In an Instagram post in March 2024 that read, "I QUIT," and was scrubbed of its meaning within four days.

Lizzo is 38 years old. The lawsuit is unresolved. An album is shelved. A husband is new. The body she has visibly reshaped has been described, by the woman who built it, as a place she put her depression and then released. The brand whose central promise — that joy is something you can produce on demand and sell at scale — has been publicly stress-tested by the people standing closest to the machine.

The mixtape is called _My Face Hurts From Smiling._

Somewhere underneath the smile is the Detroit kid who lost her father, lived in a car, decided that the only way out was forward, and built the loudest version of forward that American pop has ever permitted a fat Black woman to build. Whatever the courts decide about the workplace, the psychological case is something else. The forward motion bought her every good thing she has — the Grammys, the marriage, the platform, the right to play the President's flute. It also kept her from ever having to ask whether the thing she was selling was the thing she actually had.

That is the question she has not answered yet. It is also the only question that matters.
