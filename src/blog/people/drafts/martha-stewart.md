> "I am a perfectionist. And it runs in the family. And it started with my father." — Martha Stewart, *Martha* (Netflix, 2024)

<p class="firstLetter">The slap landed when she was nineteen, halfway up the stairs to tell her parents she was engaged. The man she was about to marry, Andy Stewart, was a Yale Law student. Andy was Jewish. Her father — Edward Kostyra, a Polish-Catholic salesman who could not quite hold a job and ran his six children like a failing inspection — hit her once across the face. Then he went back downstairs.</p>

Sixty-five years later, Martha Stewart still opens her own life story with him. The first sixty seconds of the 2024 Netflix documentary *Martha* are the line above — delivered to camera with the calm of a woman reading the founding charter of her business out loud.

A casual fan hears a brand statement. Read it as a confession instead. She has spent the entire second half of the twentieth century, and all of the twenty-first so far, building America's most polished domestic surface on top of a verdict her father handed down before she was old enough to vote. The empire is not a refuge from him. It is a sixty-year cross-examination of him, conducted in fitted sheets, hand-piped icing, and federal indictments.

## What is Martha Stewart's personality type?

### Martha Stewart is an Enneagram Type 1

Type 1 is the Reformer, sometimes called the Perfectionist. The core wound is the early belief that you yourself are not quite right and have to earn the verdict of "good" through correctness — of folding, of phrasing, of moral conduct, of dinner. The internal experience is a courtroom that never adjourns. Every choice is on trial. Every verdict is *not quite*. (See the [full Type 1 profile](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-1).)

The reason this typing earns its keep with Martha is that it explains the contradiction the documentary itself can't quite resolve. The cultural read of Martha Stewart is *control freak who got too greedy and got humbled*. Under Type 1, the picture rearranges. Greed never had to be the driver. What drives a Reformer is the intolerability of being caught wrong — and an obstruction-of-justice charge is the federal-court version of exactly that. A Reformer can absorb almost any outcome except the one where the verdict against them is on the record.

The 1w2 wing is the part of the type that makes Martha into a teacher rather than a recluse. The 1w2 wants to fix the world by showing other people how to fix it; the resulting voice is part instructor, part scold, part mother who cleans up after you while narrating what you should have done. *Martha Stewart Living* — note the verb, the imperative, the fact that the magazine title itself is a model someone is supposed to copy — is a 1w2 magazine before it is anything else.

## The Slap That Started the Empire

In the documentary, Martha's longtime friend Lloyd Allen describes what she inherited from Edward Kostyra in nine words: "the strong hand of her father was passed on to Martha." It is the most useful line in the film. The father is the first system she had to please and the first one she could never quite finish pleasing.

The signature details from her childhood in Nutley, New Jersey, are not the Catholic-Polish-immigrant texture; they are the small corrective ones. Her father taught the six Kostyra children to garden. Her mother, Big Martha Kostyra — a former schoolteacher whose Polish recipes seeded the empire and who later co-hosted her daughter's early cooking segments — taught her to cook. Neither parent, she has said, was particularly affectionate. A Type 1 daughter of one perfectionist is a precarious thing. A Type 1 daughter of two has nowhere in the house that doesn't grade her. The garden and the kitchen become the two rooms where the child can win — because the standards are visible, the work is finishable, and approval is on the table the moment the bed of seedlings comes up even.

The slap on the stairs is the other formative detail. The man her father hit her over — Andy Stewart — is the same man she is still publicly arguing about in 2024. They married in 1961, when she was nineteen and he was at Yale Law School. They separated in 1987 after Andy began an affair with Robyn Fairclough, Martha's personal assistant — twenty-one years his junior — who had been living on the Stewart property. The divorce went through in 1990. The marriage shaped the empire as much as the parents did, because the empire began in earnest after it ended.

## How Martha Stewart Built an Empire on Correction

In 1990, the year of her divorce, Martha Stewart was already a successful caterer, model, and author. She had signed a $200,000 lifestyle-consultant deal with Kmart in 1987 — the first time a perfectionist's domestic aesthetic was sold to the mass-market public on the same shelf as the rest of the discount store's life. Over the next decade she would become the first self-made female billionaire in American history through a public offering. The throughline of the work is a single sentence she has been saying since the early 1990s: *It's a good thing*.

Read it as a Reformer's verdict in disguise. *It's a good thing* is not a compliment paid to the recipe, the technique, or the centerpiece. It is the judgment Martha is allowed to give once a thing finally meets her standard. The audience hears the seal of approval. What Martha is actually doing is closing a tiny case file: this one passes. The fact that she eventually trademarked the phrase, sold the trademark, joked "I invented 'It's a good thing' before you were even born," and then collected the best of them into a book called *Martha Stewart's Very Good Things* — that is the 1w2 wing turning correction into curriculum.

Inside the work she is, by every credible account, brutally exacting. Archival footage from the *Martha Stewart Living* set has her holding up the wrong knife at a staffer for cutting an orange — *"Well, isn't that a stupid knife?"* — like the offense is the staffer's and the evidence is the tool. A former Martha Stewart Living Omnimedia executive once told *Vanity Fair* the office air could change the moment a witness walked in: "Martha is delightful, charming, brilliant, fascinating — if you are in the room alone with her. You bring a third person in and it becomes poisonous. She has to dominate." The closest mainstream analog is [Anna Wintour](/personality-analysis/anna-wintour) — another perfectionist who built a publishing empire on the premise that someone has to set the standard or it will not get set. The two readings of Martha — world's best mentor, world's worst boss — are the same impulse heard at different volumes by different listeners.

## The Daughter Who Got the Same File

The empire had a household price, and her name is Alexis. Martha's only child was born in 1965 — four years into the marriage to Andy, in the middle of the years when Big Martha's standards were quietly becoming Little Martha's. In 2008 Alexis launched a Fine Living Network show called *Whatever, Martha!* whose entire premise was that she and her co-host Jennifer Koppelman Hutt would watch old episodes of *Martha Stewart Living* and roast them in real time. The mother who had built an empire on instruction got, in her own daughter's format, a child narrating which of her segments were ridiculous.

The 2011 memoir Alexis co-wrote with Hutt — *Whateverland: Learning to Live Here* — sketched the childhood underneath the magazine. Martha used to hand her gifts pre-wrap with the instruction, "Now wrap these but don't look inside." The line that did the rounds: *"I grew up with a glue gun pointed at my head."* A One who tries to raise a child the way they fix a recipe produces a child fluent in the verdict and allergic to it. The fertility journey that followed — Alexis became a mother at forty-five, by surrogate, to a daughter named Jude and later a son named Truman — was its own slow rebuilding of a family on her own standards, on the other side of public estrangement and quieter reconciliation.

Asked at the time about *Whateverland*, Martha publicly called it "hilarious." It is the Reformer's only honest move when the file comes back from her own daughter: concede the laugh, keep the line moving. The 2024 documentary shows the two of them in the same room again. It does not show them agreeing about much.

## Why Martha Stewart's Prison Sentence Cut Deeper Than the Crime

The facts of the case are smaller than the fallout. On December 27, 2001, Martha sold roughly 4,000 shares of ImClone Systems stock for about $230,000, one day before the FDA's negative decision on the company's cancer drug — saving herself somewhere around $45,000 in losses. She was tipped off by her broker, Peter Bacanovic. In March 2004 she was convicted not of insider trading itself but of obstruction, conspiracy, and lying to federal investigators about why she sold. She served five months at the Alderson Federal Prison Camp in West Virginia, then five months of home confinement, then probation.

To a Type 1, those are not equivalent charges. *Insider trading* is a charge about money. *Lying to investigators* is a charge about character. One has a fine. The other goes on the permanent record. Stewart has said, with what reads as residual frustration twenty years later, that her legal team's advice to "keep quiet" during the media fallout was "bad advice" — meaning she still believes the version of events where, given the chance, she could have explained herself out of it. The money she could lose. The stock price she could rebuild. The editorial seat she could re-take. What a Reformer cannot redo is the line in the permanent file that reads *she should have known better.*

She did not get the chance to talk her way out. The judge sentenced her. The cameras caught her walking into Alderson with the ankle-monitor handover already prearranged. The story she has since told about prison is, characteristically, that she ran it like a campus — observed the other women, took notes, learned ceramics, planted a vegetable bed. The crisis response of a One [under acute stress](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-types-in-stress) is to turn the punishment into a curriculum. *If I'm here, I might as well be the best inmate they have.*

## Martha Stewart and Snoop Dogg: The Friendship That Shouldn't Work

Before Snoop there was Trump. In 2005, the year of her prison release, NBC gave Martha her own *Apprentice* spinoff in which she tried to deliver Donald Trump's signature execution scene with the softened line *"You just don't fit in."* The show ran a single season, averaged six to seven million viewers against Trump's ten, and was cancelled. Trump publicly blamed Martha for the decline of his own ratings. The Reformer learned what a Reformer always learns on camera: her standards do not translate to spectacle when the spectacle is humiliation. Domination, for a One, is private business. She needed a different way to be on television.

By the time [Snoop Dogg](/personality-analysis/snoop-dogg) first walked onto Martha's cooking show in 2008, she had been out of Alderson for three years and was rebuilding. The friendship that has since become its own line of business — *Martha and Snoop's Potluck Dinner Party*, joint Olympic coverage, his appearance as the first guest on her podcast, her in his music videos — formally began at the 2015 Comedy Central Roast of Justin Bieber.

Snoop's own description of that night is the second key piece of named-third-party testimony in any honest Martha profile. "She sat next to me, and she stole the show," he later told the documentary's crew. "She was the funniest roaster that night. In that moment I knew I wanted to be alongside this lady for the rest of my life." There is no equivalent line about her from a corporate collaborator. There is a reason.

A One does not get to be funny in their own kitchen. Funny is what other people are while the One is correcting. Snoop is the rare public space where the standards do not belong to her. He laughs first. He pours the wine before she can pour it. He makes jokes about her ankle monitor. The Reformer borrows the Seven's permission slip — for a few hours at a time, around someone whose entire orientation toward life is *let it be funny* — and is suddenly allowed to be a person whose mistakes are part of the show rather than the cause of it. Martha has said the first thing that drew her in was Snoop's laugh: "what was really charming about Snoop, first of all, was his giggle. He giggles in a very, very nice way." A One does not say that about a peer. She says it about an antidote.

Snoop is the gateway, not the whole gate. The decade since their first cooking segment has produced its own genre of late-Martha output: the *Sports Illustrated* swimsuit cover at eighty-one (the oldest cover model in the issue's history), the thirst-trap selfies, the CBD partnership with Canopy Growth, *The Martha Stewart Podcast* with guests from Drew Barrymore to her own surgeon, the cameo appearances aimed at a generation that grew up watching her on basic cable while their parents folded laundry to her instructions. The Reformer who could not be funny in her own kitchen has built a third act in which she is, half the time, the joke she is letting be told. The standards did not leave. They finally found an audience that thinks they are funny.

## The Critics She Can't Out-Polish

In November 2024, three weeks after the Netflix documentary went live, Andy Stewart's current wife, Shyla Nelson Stewart, posted a statement co-signed by Andy on Facebook. It called the documentary's portrayal of the marriage "sensationalized," described the union itself as "a painful and abusive marriage that ended almost forty years ago," and accused Martha of continuing "to publicly relitigate the marriage." Shyla described Andy as "one of the gentlest, most soft-spoken, kind-hearted men" she had ever known. The post said it would be the only public statement.

Stewart, who had spent thirty minutes of a *New York Times* interview the same month criticizing the documentary for showing her at "the ugliest angle" and ending with her looking like "a lonely old lady," did not publicly respond to Shyla. There is no clean place for her to. The Reformer cannot win an argument about a marriage from forty years ago. The harder she presses her version, the more the structural complaint — *you keep relitigating the verdict* — proves itself.

This is the unresolved pressure point in any honest Martha profile and the one most likely to define her remaining years in public. The Andy and Shyla rebuttal opens up the one argument the Reformer cannot adjudicate herself — the question of *who was the difficult one* is not hers to settle alone. She is eighty-three. She landed the cover of *Sports Illustrated* swimsuit issue at eighty-one. She is funnier than she has ever been on a podcast next to a rapper. And she is still, in the version of her life she most wants the record to show, trying to make the wrong people admit she was right.

## The Verdict She Can't Acquit

The Federal Bureau of Prisons released Martha Stewart on March 4, 2005. The slap on the stairs had landed forty-five years earlier. The Sports Illustrated cover was eighteen years away. The first episode of the Snoop show was eleven years away. The Netflix documentary, and the public argument with the man whose Jewish surname her father once slapped her over, were nineteen years off.

She has spent the time since release the way a Reformer spends time after a ruling they didn't write. She has built better. She has folded the towels and pressed the linens and made the centerpiece and posted the swimsuit cover and let Snoop tell the joke and let Alexis tell hers. She still opens her own movie with her father's voice.

The empire was always about one client. The first standard she ever failed to meet was the one written upstairs in a Polish-Catholic salesman's handwriting, in a house in Nutley, New Jersey, in 1960, the day she came home engaged to the wrong kind of boy.

She has not won the case. But somewhere between Nutley and Cantitoe Corners — the hundred-and-fifty-three-acre Bedford farm where she now plants, by choice, the gardens her father once made her plant — the appeal changed register. From grievance to performance. The file is still open. She just spends most of her days now reading it out in better and better light, next to a man who laughs at her ankle monitor and a daughter she has finally agreed to let make jokes about her on television.
