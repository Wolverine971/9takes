> "I didn't even really want to record 'Gold.' The label that I was with at the time really wanted me to do it." — Victoria Justice

<p class="firstLetter">In 2013, Victoria Justice released the lead single to a debut album that never arrived. The song was called "Gold." It charted nowhere and sold roughly 33,000 downloads (Billboard, 2014). Years later she explained the failure in one sentence that should stop any fan cold: "I didn't even really want to record 'Gold.' The label that I was with at the time really wanted me to do it."</p>

Sit with that. The single meant to launch her out of child stardom — the most consequential song of her music career — was a song she didn't choose, didn't want, and made anyway because someone else wanted it.

Now remember the name of the show that made her. *Victorious.* Nickelodeon built it around her, custom, after creator Dan Schneider discovered the kid he'd cast could sing and dance as well as act. She was the lead. The title. The name on the marquee.

The person who walked out of that show into stadium-filling, Grammy-winning superstardom was the supporting player — the girl who sang back-up. [Ariana Grande](/personality-analysis/ariana-grande).

So how does the one placed at the dead center of everything end up at the edge of her own story?

It isn't a talent gap, and it isn't a feud. It's a quiet, almost invisible pattern in how Victoria Justice makes decisions — the same pattern that recorded "Gold," absorbed a decade of manufactured drama without firing back, and kept her from saying the obvious thing about Dan Schneider until a documentary forced the whole room to look.

## What Is Victoria Justice's Personality Type?

### Victoria Justice Is an Enneagram Type 9

Type 9 is usually called the Peacemaker, and the label oversells the calm. The engine of a [Type 9](/enneagram-corner/enneagram-type-9) is not serenity — it's self-forgetting. Nines blend so completely into the agendas of the people around them that their own preferences fade into background noise, sometimes for a decade at a stretch. The Nine doesn't fight you. The Nine doesn't even disagree out loud. The Nine simply goes along, and the going-along is so frictionless that you forget to ask what she actually wanted.

You can watch the mechanism operate in one decision. A label hands a 20-year-old a single she doesn't believe in. A different type pushes back — argues, stalls, walks. The Nine records it. "I didn't even really want to record 'Gold,'" she said later. Not a no. Just the absence of a yes that nobody noticed was missing. The song flopped, the album evaporated, and the most-watched window of her music career closed around a choice that was never hers.

That is the tell, and it points with conviction. Picture the obvious alternative — a thwarted Type 3 who chased the crown and lost it on talent. That isn't her. Victoria Justice was handed the crown, found the weight of insisting on it faintly distasteful, and let it slide toward whoever reached for it harder. "I wasn't always confident," she has said. "I still have so many insecurities that I'm trying to grow out of." The self-doubt is real, but it sits on top of something quieter: a reflex to keep the room comfortable, even when the room is costing her the thing she's standing in the middle of.

## How a Kid From Hollywood, Florida Talked Her Way Into Hollywood, California

She was born in Hollywood — the one in Florida — to a Puerto Rican mother from the Bronx and a father of English, German, and Irish descent. At eight, she saw a kid in a commercial, decided *I can do that,* and screamed for her mother. At ten, the family moved to the other Hollywood.

By twelve she was inside Dan Schneider's orbit, auditioning for the role of Lola Martinez on *Zoey 101* in 2005. She stayed three years. Then, near the end of that show's run, Schneider, having clocked that she could sing and dance as well as act, floated building a series around her. Here is the part that complicates the whole portrait: Victorious was, in part, her idea. She mentioned she'd attended a performing-arts middle school in Los Angeles, and the show grew out of that world. A Nine who supposedly can't advocate for herself authored the vehicle that made her famous.

That contradiction is the key to the type, not the exception to it. Her drive was real — it simply lived in private, in the dreaming stage, before there was anyone in the room to defer to. The teenager who could pitch a show to a network was the same young woman who, four years later, couldn't tell that same network's golden-boy creator that a single was wrong for her. Ambition a Nine can summon alone; what she can't do is keep her grip on it once a stronger will walks in.

> "Everyone wants me to be perfect, but I am so far from perfect!"

Her co-stars saw the warmth without always seeing the cost. "She's so adorable. She's a good egg, Victoria, and she's wonderful to work with," Elizabeth Gillies said in a Nick-era interview. "I think she has an extremely bright future ahead of her." Good egg. Wonderful to work with. Every word true, and every word the kind of thing people say about the person who never makes the day harder.

## Why Victoria Justice's Career Stalled While Ariana Grande's Exploded

This is the question fans have circled for over a decade, usually answering it with the laziest options: she wasn't as talented, or she was sabotaged, or she didn't want it badly enough.

The talent option dies fastest, because she'd already proven it. Victoria Justice was the lead vocalist of the *Victorious* soundtrack. "Make It Shine," "Freak the Freak Out," "Best Friend's Brother": those were her vocals, and the first soundtrack album debuted at No. 5 on the Billboard 200 and hit No. 1 on the soundtracks chart. She could front a hit. The catch is who the hit belonged to. Inside the show, the music was Tori Vega's, the ensemble's, the franchise's. The voice was hers; the ownership was shared. And shared ownership is the exact climate a Nine sings freely in.

Then came the one launch where the song had to be hers alone. Justice signed with Columbia, released "Gold" in 2013, then left the label the same year over what was reported as creative differences, the single she's since admitted she didn't want to make. No album followed. No new label materialized despite her saying one would, "next year sometime." The franchise's voice could not become a solo artist, and the reason wasn't range. It was that claiming a thing as solely, defiantly hers is the one move the type is built to avoid.

Meanwhile Grande — the supporting player — chased her own sound with a ferocity that read as almost rude by comparison, and built one of the defining pop careers of the era. The cruelty of the contrast isn't that Grande was more gifted. It's that Grande wanted it in a way she was willing to be unpleasant about, and the Nine, constitutionally, was not.

Here's the part that's easy to miss: a stalled Nine doesn't experience the stall as failure in real time. She experiences it as fine. Things are okay. And the résumé stayed busy enough to keep the okay-ness convincing. She headlined the MTV thriller *Eye Candy* in 2015. She played Janet in Fox's *Rocky Horror* remake in 2016. She led the Netflix comedy *Afterlife of the Party* in 2021. These weren't bit parts; they were lead vehicles, and they came and went without pulling her back to the center. That's the more honest and more unsettling version of the stall: not that she stopped getting cast, but that she kept landing leads that quietly failed to stick. The flattening is gentle and gradual, which is exactly why it's dangerous. By the time a Nine notices she's drifted to the margin of her own field, years have passed and the momentum belongs to someone else.

The critics had their own verdict, and it stings precisely because it's fair. Reviewers called her romance films "vintage Victoria Justice" and "same old" — pleasant, professional, forgettable. Concede the point. The Hallmark-grade lead who never disrupts a frame is a real limitation. But notice that "pleasant, professional, forgettable" is not a talent diagnosis. It's a personality diagnosis. It is the exact occupational hazard of a performer whose deepest instinct is to not be a problem.

The people who actually work with her describe something the box office doesn't capture. Her *A Perfect Pairing* co-star Adam Demos praised her "drive" and "initiative" on set and called her "very courageous" (2022). That's the quiet paradox of a high-functioning Nine: privately disciplined, reliably prepared, genuinely game — and still allergic to the one move, self-assertion in front of a stronger will, that converts competence into a career.

## The Feud Victoria Justice Never Started

For ten years, the internet insisted Victoria Justice was locked in a bitter feud with Ariana Grande. There was no feud. There was a narrative, and it was assigned to her.

"I think the biggest rumor is this whole narrative that was created with me being jealous of her," she said. She and Grande have texted about it and agreed it was, in Justice's word, "stupid." Grande, for her part, has kept the door open warmly: "The love is still there, and I hope we get to do something soon together," she told People in 2024, "because we have so much fun, and that was such a special time."

The most revealing artifact is a resurfaced clip from their teens. Asked about singing, Gillies noted Grande "sings absolutely everything," and Justice added, mildly, "I think we all sing." Fans read it as a dig. Justice received death threats. Her defense was almost painfully on-brand: it was "factual," she said, because "we did all sing."

Look at the shape of that. A Nine makes the most peace-keeping, ego-flattening statement available — *we're all equal here, no one's the star* — and the culture converts it into an act of aggression. This is the strange burden of the type. Because the Nine projects no sharp edges of her own, she becomes a blank screen, and people paint their own dramas onto her. The jealousy story was never about Victoria Justice's feelings. It was about an audience that needed a villain for Ariana's hero arc, and a Nine is the easiest possible casting: present, agreeable, and unlikely to fight the script.

## Why It Took a Documentary for Victoria Justice to Say Dan Schneider Owed Her an Apology

In 2024, the docuseries *Quiet on Set* reopened the question of how Nickelodeon treated its child stars. Justice responded — and the timing is the whole story.

"Dan had a very large ego," she told Marie Claire, "and sometimes that ego clouded his better judgment and affected the way he treated people, and there were times I felt like I was being treated unfairly." Some moments, she said, "were in poor taste," though nothing sexual occurred. When Schneider conceded he owed some people an apology, she was direct: "I would say I'm definitely one of the people on that list."

Read those quotes for what's *not* in them. There's no scorched earth, no decade-old grievance finally detonating. Even naming the harm, she immediately balances the ledger: "I met him when I was 12 years old, and he's the person that gave me this big break. He completely changed my life." She had, by her own account, not spoken to him in years — not from rage, just from drift.

This is conflict-avoidance with a slow fuse, and the fuse ran long partly because the stakes were total: every credit of her childhood, *Zoey 101* and *Victorious* both, carried Schneider's name. The man she couldn't confront for a decade wasn't a passing boss; he was the architect of her entire early career. The Nine will name the wrong — but typically only after the environment has already named it, when speaking up no longer means *starting* a fight, only joining one that's safely underway. For more than a decade she carried a grievance she was treated unfairly and said nothing, because saying it would have disturbed a peace she was wired to protect. It took a documentary, a cultural reckoning, and the cover of a crowd before she'd put it into words. That delay isn't weakness. It's the precise signature of a person for whom disturbing the peace feels worse than absorbing the harm.

### Victoria Justice's Wing: 9w1

The evidence leans 9w1 over 9w8. Her public register is composed, image-clean, and self-correcting — the "everyone wants me to be perfect" anxiety, the diligent performing-arts-kid discipline, the measured, non-inflammatory way she frames even genuine grievances. That's the One wing: a quiet conscientiousness layered over the Nine's ease. A 9w8 would carry more visible heat, more willingness to make the room uncomfortable on purpose; Justice's edges, when they finally appear, are reluctant and carefully worded. The faint moralism — "treated unfairly," "poor taste" — is the One wing supplying the vocabulary the core Nine would rather not use at all. Read more on <a href="/enneagram-corner/enneagram-wings-complete-guide">wings</a>.

### Victoria Justice's Instinctual Subtype: Social (so) Nine

The Social Nine "fuses" with the group — the ensemble, the cast, the fanbase — and finds identity in belonging rather than in standing apart. It fits. Her warmest self-descriptions are collective ("I see Ariana all the time… adding chapters to our book"), the *Victorious* group chat reportedly still runs, and her most catastrophic PR moment came from a line designed to dissolve hierarchy within the group ("I think we all sing"). The Social Nine sublimates personal ambition into the comfort of the collective — which is exactly the instinct that can leave a lead standing modestly inside an ensemble while a more self-prioritizing castmate steps to the front. See <a href="/enneagram-corner/enneagram-instinctual-subtypes">instinctual subtypes</a>.

### Stress and Growth Arrows

Under stress, Nine moves toward Six: anxiety, worst-case scanning, self-doubt. You hear it in "I still have so many insecurities that I'm trying to grow out of" and in the years of quiet uncertainty about whether the music would ever come back. In growth, Nine moves toward Three — focused, self-prioritizing, willing to claim the spotlight. The bitter irony writes itself: Ariana Grande reads as a natural Three, and the healthiest version of Victoria Justice is a Nine reaching *toward* exactly the trait that made her former co-star a superstar. Her 2025 comeback — choosing her own songs, finally — is what integration to Three looks like in practice.

### Counterarguments: Why Victoria Justice Might Not Be Type 9

The strongest alternate case is Type 2 (the image-conscious, well-liked helper) or even a wing-quiet Type 6 (the anxiety, the loyalty to the group, the slow-to-confront caution). The self-described insecurity could be read as Six fear rather than Nine self-forgetting. What tips it back to Nine: Twos actively pursue connection and want to be needed, while Justice's pattern is receding, not reaching; and Sixes test and question authority, while Justice deferred to authority (the label, Schneider) for years without the probing skepticism a Six brings. The defining datum is the "Gold" decision — not anxiety about a choice, but the quiet disappearance of her own preference inside someone else's. That's Nine, not Six.

## Victoria Justice's Comeback Is Built on a Song She Wrote at 17

In October 2025, Justice released a single called "Love Zombie." It was not new. It was a vault track she co-wrote at seventeen, pulled forward more than a decade later. "Never in a million years would I have expected that a song that I wrote when I was 17 would continue to have life in 2025," she told Us Weekly. A Christmas single followed in November. She turned up on *Suits: L.A.* in February, playing — note the casting — a "young, confident attorney."

There's something almost too neat about a comeback that runs on material from before the self-erasure began: the songs she's reaching for now are the ones she wrote when the wanting was still private and still hers, before the labels and creators and narratives arrived to reorganize her around their plans.

And the language she uses about the lesson is, line for line, the Type 9 growth path. Asked what surviving child stardom taught her, she didn't say *work harder* or *trust no one.* She said she learned "when to say no." For most people that's a tactic. For a Nine it's the entire spiritual curriculum — the single hardest sentence in the language, the one whose absence wrote "Gold," swallowed a grievance for a decade, and let a feud get pinned on her without a rebuttal.

The show was called *Victorious.* It was named for her — for a quality everyone but Victoria seemed certain she possessed. The story of her thirties is whether the woman can finally claim the noun that was printed over her head at sixteen, before she'd ever once had to say no to anyone.
