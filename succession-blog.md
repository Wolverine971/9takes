Ah — that is much more specific. You’re looking for **succession-pressure relationships**, not just “great partnerships.”

The pattern is:

> **An elder architect built a machine. A chosen or emerging successor has history with them. The public can see the handoff, but nobody knows whether the successor can preserve the magic under a new era of uncertainty.**

That is exactly why the Morris Chang → C.C. Wei / Jensen ecosystem is interesting. TSMC is not just asking “who runs the company?” It is asking: **can the next generation preserve an operating religion while AI, geopolitics, and global fab expansion change the game?**

Here are the best places to look.

---

## 1. Warren Buffett → Greg Abel at Berkshire Hathaway

**Domain:** capital allocation / conglomerates
**Relationship type:** patriarch + quiet chosen operator
**Future uncertainty:** can Berkshire work without the Buffett aura?

This is probably the cleanest Western business analogy to TSMC.

Buffett built Berkshire as a trust machine: founders sell to him because he promises autonomy, long-term ownership, and no Wall Street nonsense. Greg Abel is the chosen successor, but his test is not merely “can he operate the businesses?” It is: **can he preserve the spiritual contract that made Berkshire unique?**

As of 2026, Abel has taken over as CEO after Buffett’s 60-year run, while Buffett remains chairman. Reuters notes that Abel now has to steer a trillion-dollar conglomerate, deploy a massive cash pile, and manage investor expectations after the transition. ([Reuters][1])

The special dynamic: Buffett is still alive, still chairman, still the myth. Abel has the job, but not yet the legend.

**The unknown:** Berkshire’s edge was partly financial discipline, partly Buffett’s personal reputation. If Abel becomes too interventionist, too private-equity-like, or too eager to prove himself, he could damage the very culture he inherited.

**Why this is like TSMC:** both are succession stories where the successor must preserve an invisible operating culture, not just run assets.

---

## 2. Bernard Arnault → the five Arnault children at LVMH

**Domain:** luxury / family empire
**Relationship type:** patriarch + competing heirs
**Future uncertainty:** can a family committee preserve one king’s taste and control?

This one is spicy because it is not a clean “chosen successor” story. It is closer to a royal court.

Bernard Arnault has five children involved in the LVMH orbit: Delphine, Antoine, Alexandre, Frédéric, and Jean. At LVMH’s April 2026 shareholder meeting, all five publicly appeared in a way that looked designed to project unity and calm succession speculation. Arnault, 77, has permission to remain CEO until age 85 and avoided naming a successor. ([Le Monde.fr][2])

The ownership structure makes this even more fascinating. Reuters reported that a new Agache Commandite structure gives each of the five children a 20% stake, and absent specific instructions from Bernard Arnault, decisions would be made by a majority of three. ([Reuters][3])

That is a huge dynamic.

Luxury is not just operations. It is taste, aura, scarcity, designer selection, China exposure, brand discipline, and cultural timing. Arnault is not just a CEO. He is the integrating mind across dozens of maisons.

**The unknown:** can five heirs govern what one imperial taste-maker built?

**Why this is like TSMC:** both are empires built around discipline and trust. But LVMH has more family-politics risk, while TSMC has more geopolitical/engineering risk.

---

## 3. Rupert Murdoch → Lachlan Murdoch

**Domain:** media / politics / family control
**Relationship type:** patriarch + ideologically aligned son
**Future uncertainty:** what happens when the founder’s political instincts leave the stage?

This is the most openly dynastic and dramatic.

Rupert Murdoch tried to secure Lachlan Murdoch’s control over Fox and News Corp rather than have voting power shared equally with several siblings. A Nevada court initially ruled against Rupert and Lachlan in 2024, but the family later reached a settlement in 2025 giving Lachlan control of Fox and News Corp until at least 2050, while other siblings received large payouts. ([Wikipedia][4])

This is a relationship where succession is not only about business competence. It is about **ideological inheritance**.

Murdoch built a media empire that shaped politics in the U.S., U.K., and Australia. Lachlan is not just inheriting companies. He is inheriting an influence machine.

**The unknown:** can Lachlan maintain the empire’s political/cultural force without Rupert’s animal instincts for media warfare?

**Why this is like TSMC:** both involve a founder whose judgment became part of the institution. But Murdoch succession is much more family-conflict-heavy and politically combustible.

---

## 4. Jamie Dimon → JPMorgan’s internal contenders

**Domain:** banking / financial system stability
**Relationship type:** dominant CEO + bench of lieutenants
**Future uncertainty:** can anyone replace the “adult in the room” of Wall Street?

Jamie Dimon is not gone yet, but this is one of the biggest unresolved succession dynamics in American business.

JPMorgan has multiple internal contenders often discussed, including Marianne Lake, Jennifer Piepszak, Troy Rohrbaugh, and others. Recent reporting and analyst commentary have framed Marianne Lake and Troy Rohrbaugh as leading candidates, while Dimon continues to play down succession speculation. ([Fortune][5])

The special relationship angle here is not one heir. It is Dimon’s relationship with an elite internal bench.

Dimon’s role is unusual because he is not just a bank CEO. He has become a kind of private-sector financial statesman. In crises, people listen to him. Regulators listen. Markets listen. Presidents listen.

**The unknown:** JPMorgan has talent, but does anyone inherit Dimon’s crisis authority?

**Why this is like TSMC:** both companies have deep benches, but the top person carries unusual external trust. The key-man risk is not technical execution. It is **credibility under stress**.

---

## 5. Anna Wintour → Chloe Malle at Vogue

**Domain:** fashion media / cultural gatekeeping
**Relationship type:** queenmaker + selected editorial successor
**Future uncertainty:** can Vogue matter in a post-magazine world?

This one is culturally very interesting.

Anna Wintour stepped back from the U.S. editor-in-chief role after nearly 40 years, while remaining Condé Nast’s chief content officer and Vogue’s global editorial director. Chloe Malle was named head of editorial content for American Vogue in 2025. ([Reuters][6])

This is not a normal retirement. Wintour still controls the larger universe: global Vogue, Condé Nast content, the Met Gala, and cultural power. Malle gets the U.S. editorial seat, but Wintour’s shadow is still everywhere.

That creates a delicate relationship dynamic: Malle has to be new enough to make Vogue feel alive, but not so rebellious that she breaks the Wintour machine.

**The unknown:** can a successor modernize an institution while the old monarch is still in the palace?

**Why this is like TSMC:** both involve a powerful incumbent stepping away from day-to-day control while remaining symbolically and structurally influential.

---

## 6. Samsung: Lee Kun-hee → Jay Y. Lee

**Domain:** electronics / chaebol dynasty / AI chips
**Relationship type:** incapacitated patriarch → heir under legal and governance pressure
**Future uncertainty:** can Samsung regain strategic edge in the AI semiconductor era?

Samsung is a major one because it mixes family succession, national industrial strategy, legal risk, and technology uncertainty.

The transition from Lee Kun-hee to Jay Y. Lee was not clean and ceremonial. Lee Kun-hee’s 2014 heart attack forced leadership responsibilities toward Jay Y. Lee, and the family later faced massive inheritance tax and governance complications. A 2026 Korea Herald report said the family was wrapping up a 12 trillion won inheritance tax payment, reducing uncertainty and reinforcing Jay Y. Lee’s leadership. ([Korea Herald][7])

The future uncertainty is huge: Samsung has to compete with TSMC in foundry, SK Hynix in HBM memory, Apple in devices, and Chinese firms in hardware ecosystems.

**The unknown:** can Jay Y. Lee make Samsung feel strategically inevitable again?

**Why this is like TSMC:** both are Asian semiconductor giants deeply tied to national power. But TSMC’s succession looks technocratic; Samsung’s is dynastic and more legally/politically entangled.

---

## 7. Mukesh Ambani → Akash, Isha, and Anant Ambani at Reliance

**Domain:** Indian conglomerate / telecom / retail / energy
**Relationship type:** patriarch + three-child domain split
**Future uncertainty:** can Reliance manage a multi-sector empire after Mukesh?

Reliance is like India’s version of a national-scale family-capital machine. Mukesh Ambani built or expanded a sprawling empire across energy, telecom, retail, media, and digital infrastructure. His children have taken increasingly visible roles: Akash in telecom/Jio, Isha in retail, and Anant in energy/new energy themes.

The relationship dynamic is less “one successor” and more **portfolio partitioning among heirs**. That can work if domains are clean and the family stays aligned. It can become fragile if capital allocation, control, or public markets start demanding a single center of gravity.

**The unknown:** can Reliance’s next generation maintain coherence across unrelated strategic domains?

**Why this is like LVMH:** both are family-controlled empires where the next generation is being tested in public before the patriarch fully exits.

---

## 8. Ray Dalio → Bridgewater’s next generation

**Domain:** hedge funds / macro investing / management philosophy
**Relationship type:** founder-philosopher + institutional successors
**Future uncertainty:** can a cult-like decision system survive without the cult founder?

Bridgewater is interesting because Dalio did not just build a hedge fund. He built a doctrine: radical transparency, believability-weighted decision-making, principles, pain + reflection = progress, etc.

That makes succession extra hard. When a founder builds a normal company, the successor inherits processes. When a founder builds a doctrine, the successor inherits a religion.

**The unknown:** were Bridgewater’s principles a transferable operating system, or were they inseparable from Dalio’s personality?

**Why this is like TSMC:** both are culture-as-operating-system cases. But Bridgewater is more psychological; TSMC is more engineering/manufacturing.

# The deeper pattern

The really interesting cases have four ingredients:

**1. A founder whose personal judgment became infrastructure.**
Morris Chang, Buffett, Arnault, Murdoch, Wintour, Dimon.

**2. A successor who is qualified but not yet mythologized.**
C.C. Wei, Greg Abel, Chloe Malle, Lachlan, Jay Y. Lee.

**3. A world that is changing right as succession happens.**
AI, luxury slowdown, post-cable media, post-magazine fashion, bank regulation, geopolitics, semiconductor nationalism.

**4. A question nobody can answer yet:**
Was the institution strong because of its system, or because of the founder’s presence?

That is the exact tension you’re picking up.

The most interesting ones to study right now are probably **Berkshire, LVMH, Samsung, JPMorgan, and TSMC** because they combine real succession mechanics with massive uncertainty in the external environment.
