## Analysis

### Quick summary
- Harry's framework is a quality gate for copy, not just a style preference.
- A line is strong when it is:
  1. Visual (`Can I see it?`)
  2. Falsifiable (`Can I verify it?`)
  3. Ownable (`Can only this brand say it?`)
- If a line fails any one of these, it usually underperforms.

### The 3 rules, broken down

| Rule | What it means | Why it matters | Rewrite move |
|---|---|---|---|
| Can I visualize it? | The reader can picture a scene/object. | Concrete language is easier to remember. | Replace abstractions with sensory specifics. |
| Can I falsify it? | Claim could be proven true/false. | Specific claims create trust and attention. | Replace adjectives with evidence, examples, or measurable facts. |
| Can nobody else say it? | Line is tied to your positioning. | Distinctiveness protects against commodity messaging. | Add unique context, mechanism, audience, or proof competitors cannot use. |

### Key tactical insights
- "Don't talk, only point" is a high-value writing heuristic.
  - Instead of saying someone/something is "good," point to concrete evidence.
- Avoid abstract corporate lines.
  - Example of weak copy from transcript: "Change an entire industry."
- Favor contrast and juxtaposition.
  - Example discussed: "supermodels in London and dads in Ohio" works because it is vivid and distinctive.

### Practical rewrite framework
Use this fast sequence for headlines, hooks, and hero copy.

1. Write first-pass line.
2. Make it visual.
   - Ask: what can the reader literally picture?
3. Make it falsifiable.
   - Ask: what proof/fact can I point to?
4. Make it ownable.
   - Ask: could a competitor copy-paste this?
5. Run speed test.
   - "One Mississippi, Two Mississippi": does it click immediately?

### Copy checklist
- Is there a clear mental image?
- Is there a concrete proof element?
- Is this claim unique to our product/position?
- Is the line understandable in under 2 seconds?
- Did we remove vague adjectives and inflated claims?

### Common failure patterns
- Abstract nouns with no image (`innovation`, `transformation`, `better way`).
- Subjective praise with no proof (`best`, `world-class`, `premium`).
- Generic positioning that any competitor can sign.

### My key takeaways
- The framework acts like a pre-publish QA pass for copy.
- The biggest lift usually comes from specificity, not clever wording.
- Distinctive copy is strategic: it reflects real positioning choices.
