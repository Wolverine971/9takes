## Core Themes

1. Interviewing as craft: prep is invisible, but the visible payoff is the subject relaxing the moment they realize you've actually done the work.
2. The pre-interview matters more than the interview. The 90 seconds before the camera rolls determines whether the guest opens up.
3. Sourcing is built on understanding motivation, not avoiding bias. Every source has an angle; the journalist's job is to triangulate, not to vet for purity.
4. Distribution channels (newsletter, TV, summit, book, scripted TV) are not strategy — they're different canvases for the same lens.
5. "Take everything personally" is reframed as the marker of people who actually care about the work.
6. Volume isn't a productivity hack — it's a saying-no discipline. The 8 years on 1929 is the giveaway.

## Analysis & Key Insights

### What This Episode Reveals About Sorkin

1. **He's a structure thinker who pretends he's a feel thinker.** The "flight path" metaphor (JFK to LAX, willing to divert through Atlanta or O'Hare) is a planned-improvisation framework. He drafts the path, then says the trick is "listening." Both are true; the path is what makes the listening useful.

2. **The "depersonalize the question" technique is the most replicable thing in the episode.** Read someone else's quote aloud. Two effects: removes the journalist as the accuser, and the subject has usually already grappled with that exact criticism for weeks. It bypasses defensiveness and gets a real answer.

3. **He treats the pre-interview as the interview.** The two minutes in the wings, the commercial break with someone in the chair, the sweaty handshake as a tell — this is where he claims most of the work gets done. The on-stage portion is the rendered output of an off-stage settling process.

4. **His sourcing model is post-trust.** He doesn't try to find sources without ulterior motives — he assumes everyone has them. The work is to confirm 80–90% of a story before going to the principal, then let the principal shape the remaining 10%. The "jilted lover" archetype (banker who lost the deal, junior who hates the boss) is named explicitly as a primary channel.

5. **DealBook's origin is a generational distribution insight, not a content insight.** In 2001, bankers told him they read the front page and the sports section, then left the paper at home and took the WSJ on the train. His response wasn't to write better; it was to bypass the physical paper entirely and land in the inbox. This is a Substack thesis 15 years before Substack.

6. **He's allergic to scaling the wrong things.** Asked about more DealBook Summits or a podcast, his answer is essentially "those would be bad versions." The 8-year arc on 1929 is presented as proof that depth is the constraint, not time.

7. **Billions started as a procedural insight, not a Wall Street insight.** His agent said hedge fund people would be boring. The unlock came watching his wife rewatch Law & Order: legal procedurals work. So he set the hedge fund world against the prosecutorial world. That structural move (two worlds in conflict) is a screenwriting frame, not a finance one.

8. **He frames "too close to sources" as a strength, not a defense.** The standard journalism critique of him gets reframed: knowing people is the only way to tell the human-nuance version of the story. He explicitly accepts the trade-off.

9. **The cumulative answer to "how do you do it all" is: he doesn't.** He says no to almost everything. The work is a small set of high-prep activities (newsletter, Squawk, summit, occasional book) that all reinforce each other rather than a sprawling output portfolio.

### Practical Takeaways

1. If you're prepping a hard interview, draft the path you'd take if everything went smoothly, then accept that the weather will change.
2. Read a critic's quote aloud instead of asking the question yourself when the subject is sensitive.
3. The two minutes before the interview starts is not warmup — it's the interview.
4. Sources don't need to be unbiased; they need to be triangulable.
5. The honest version of "how do you do it all" is "I do five things and say no to a hundred."

### 1) Cold open: how do you actually do all of this?

Ben and David frame the conversation. Sorkin runs DealBook (the daily newsletter at the New York Times), hosts CNBC's Squawk Box from 6–9 a.m. Eastern, runs the DealBook Summit every December, co-created Billions, and has written Too Big to Fail and 1929. The angle: not the new book, but the operating system underneath all of it.

Sorkin opens by joking that he wore a sweater for the podcast — "Usually I'm in a suit basically all day. I kind of sleep in a suit with a tie."

### 2) The morning routine

- Wakes around 4:30 a.m. Phone under the covers. Reading email and Slack from the DealBook team.
- Writes the "Good morning, Andrew here" lede for the newsletter himself, every morning.
- About an hour before Squawk Box starts, he does last tweaks on the newsletter — which doubles as his "read-in" for the show.
- The send button for DealBook is pressed in Europe by Michael de la Merced (~20 years on the team) and Bernard Warner, who lives in Italy. They edit overnight; Sorkin and team Slack edits in the morning before publish.

### 3) How Squawk Box happened

Around 2011, Mark Haines (original Squawk Box host) had passed away, and CNBC reshuffled. Carl Quintanilla moved to Squawk on the Street, which opened a Squawk Box chair. CNBC asked Sorkin if he'd host. He thought it was crazy — couldn't read a teleprompter, hadn't done it before — but agreed when CNBC let him keep DealBook and the rest of his work.

He was already up early to publish DealBook, but had been doing it in pajamas. The hard part wasn't the hour, it was being on camera at it.

### 4) Origin story: ads at 15, the New York Times at 18

- 15 years old, Scarsdale, NY: started a magazine called Sports Page distributed inside high schools nationwide. Wanted to be in the business side of media, not journalism. Sold ads at $900–$1,200 a page; mom drove him to NYC ad agencies. NYT wrote a small article about the venture.
- Made classic rookie errors: shipped via FedEx to look professional; mis-budgeted the move from black-and-white to color printing. Business failed by the time he was 18.
- Read Stuart Elliott (NYT advertising columnist) religiously to fake credibility in pitch meetings. Cold-called him, eventually got lunch, and convinced him plus Glenn Kron (running the business section) to let him hang around with a visitor pass.
- "I'd stand and I'd xerox and staple. I had no intention of putting two words together let alone a sentence."
- 1995: an editor mistook him for a college-grad news clerk because he wore a suit. Overheard him talking about "the internet" and assigned him a piece. The piece was about the noise that modems make — Sorkin had to explain to her that the noise was two modems "mating."
- Stayed through Cornell, freelanced from London during a junior-year semester at LSE, published nearly daily.

### 5) Founding DealBook in 2001

- Posted to NYC to cover M&A. Bankers and lawyers told him: "I read the front page, sometimes sports, and leave the paper at home. I take the WSJ on the train."
- Realized the play was to bypass the physical paper entirely and go straight to the inbox.
- Pitched it inside NYT during the dotcom bust. Internal "TAM" estimate: 30,000 free subscribers. Today: over 1 million.
- Pitched it as ad-supported, profitable on day one. First advertiser: Brooks Brothers. Plus a currency exchange.
- Conceit included linking to *competitors* — WSJ, FT, local papers. NYT newsroom found this uncomfortable for a while.
- Early subscribers asked their secretaries to *fax* DealBook to them.

### 6) The newsletter as feedback channel

- Subscribers reply with PDFs of decks, leaks of pending deals, corrections from people inside the transactions: "I'm involved in this and you didn't get it right."
- Sorkin: "Back then 25 years ago there was no feedback loop in that way. … We didn't have Twitter or X or any of these other social media platforms."

### 7) Sourcing methodology

- Every source has motivation. The job is not to verify motivation, it's to confirm the information from 2–4 independent sources or via documents.
- Stories often start junior, not senior. "A great story starts with what I call the jilted somebody" — banker who lost the auction, lawyer who thought a 10-year client relationship was secure until Goldman got the deal.
- Rule: get to 80–90% confirmed *before* asking the company for comment. At that point, the company often wants to help shape the narrative.
- Anecdote: 11-member board, 10 want a deal, 1 thinks it's terrible — the dissenting member leaks because public exposure will kill it. Inverse case: CEOs leak past their own board because they think the public will cheer.

### 8) Reporting today vs. reporting on 1929

- For 1929, he had to work from letters, diaries, memos, archival material — no live sources.
- Tries to put himself in the shoes of people who weren't alive when he wrote about them. What were they thinking? Why? How does that map to someone alive today?
- Ben and David note this rhymes with their work as "business historians."

### 9) The DealBook Summit

- Launched 2011, ten years into the newsletter. Conceit: bring the pages to life.
- First year speakers: Jamie Dimon (opener), Lloyd Blankfein, Marc Andreessen, Eric Schmidt, Dick Costolo (then Twitter CEO). Year two: Ken Griffin, Dan Loeb, Preet Bharara.
- The other big conference at the time was the WSJ "D" conference (later Recode), which was tech-only. Sorkin saw an opening at the intersection of finance, tech, and Washington policy.
- Goal of summit interviews isn't breaking news — it's understanding why people think what they think. Quotes from summit interviews often surface in NYT stories months later, when those people make decisions.
- Example: Bezos's comments about AI being "like fire" at a recent summit, which made more sense once Bezos announced a new AI venture and a return to a co-CEO role.

### 10) Set, room, and audience design

- Held at the Appel Room at Jazz at Lincoln Center. Overlooks Columbus Circle, has natural light, holds ~400–500 people without feeling that big.
- Set design is intentionally sparse: a massive LED screen behind the host that can be rebranded for any moment.
- Audience is curated to be peer-level to the speakers — business leaders, political class, academics, public-intellectual journalists.
- Tickets: readers can apply. A pool is set aside for the public.
- Distribution: full interviews go free to NYT, YouTube, and other platforms within 24 hours.

### 11) Prep and interview craft

- 15–30 hours of prep per Summit interview.
- Keeps a notes app on his phone with running notes on people — quotes, articles, things to circle back on. Includes people on a wish list who haven't yet appeared.
- Talks to others who know the subject before the interview. Less about facts than about disposition.
- Never shares questions in advance. Will share themes.
- Tries to meet the subject before the day if he hasn't met them — a quick Zoom, brief intro via PR.

### 12) The two minutes before the interview

- Most important part of the interview, in his telling.
- Equivalent on Squawk Box: the commercial break with the guest already in the chair.
- Goal is to settle the guest, meet them where they are emotionally — anxious, scared, or over-energized.
- Tell: "You get a sweaty sweaty hand, you know what's about to happen."

### 13) The "flight path" metaphor

- Plans interviews like a flight: JFK to LAX, with a planned routing through O'Hare, Atlanta, Denver, Dallas.
- "I know the weather's going to change." May have to divert.
- The point of the path is so listening becomes useful — when something surprising comes up, prep lets you drop a known beat and chase the new one.
- Tell that the guest has joined you: Bill Gates slouched in his chair until it was a hammock. "Game recognize game" moment when the subject realizes you've done real research.

### 14) The depersonalize-with-a-quote technique

- For sensitive or critical questions, Sorkin reads a quote from someone else aloud rather than phrasing the criticism himself.
- Two effects: (1) it's not the journalist accusing them, it's a public-domain critique; (2) the subject has usually already wrestled with that exact quote for weeks.
- Ben confesses he's stolen this technique and calls it the "Andrew Ross Sorkin phrasing."

### 15) Tennis-rally framing

- "I'm still rooting for them to hit the ball back."
- Hard questions, but placed so the subject can return them. The goal is a real exchange, not a gotcha.

### 16) DealBook 2025 lineup (recorded just before the event)

Sorkin lists his upcoming Summit slate, all of which he interviews himself:

- Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent — economy, Washington, tariffs
- David Ellison — Paramount acquisition, the Warner Bros. Discovery chess game, Oracle/AI/TikTok ties via his father
- Erika Kirk (Charlie Kirk's widow) — taking over Turning Point USA
- Mary Barra — GM, manufacturing, tariffs
- Dario Amodei — Anthropic, AI, criticism from Trump and Nvidia
- Alex Karp — Palantir
- Brian Armstrong + Larry Fink ("the young OG and the old OG of finance") — joint conversation
- Jimmy Donaldson (Mr. Beast) — "the most influential, powerful influencer in the world"
- Gavin Newsom — counterpoint to the Trump administration
- Halle Berry

Roughly 9 a.m. to 6 or 6:30 p.m. on stage, all in one day. Goes on Squawk Box for ~5 minutes that morning to tease.

### 17) The business of DealBook

- Newsletter publishes 6 days a week to over 1 million subscribers.
- 100% advertising / sponsorship-supported. No paid subscription tier.
- Goal was always reach, not direct revenue.
- Summit follows the same model — sponsorship-driven, with the public able to apply for a curated subset of tickets.
- All summit interviews go free to YouTube and NYT within 24 hours.

### 18) Leaps

Sorkin says he doesn't think of himself as a big risk-taker, but the things that felt like leaps:

1. Writing Too Big to Fail. "I remember telling my wife, 'We're going to have to give the money back.'"
2. Writing 1929. Eight-year project; first couple of years he wasn't sure he'd actually finish.
3. Co-creating Billions. Wrote the pilot on spec with Brian Koppelman and David Levien — no studio, no development deal.
4. Going on TV in the first place.

### 19) Becoming a public figure

- Has been on TV since 2002–2004 (Today Show with Katie Couric and Matt Lauer; Charlie Rose during the Martha Stewart trial).
- Says he doesn't think about being recognized. Kids used to ask, "Dad, is that a friend of yours?"
- Treats the audience interaction as energizing rather than burdensome.

### 20) Billions

- Came out of co-producing the Too Big to Fail HBO film. Loved being on set.
- His LA agent told him a hedge fund show was a terrible idea: "people hate Wall Street, they just look at computer screens, it'll be boring."
- Unlock came when his wife was rewatching Law & Order. Realized legal procedurals work. Set the hedge fund world against the prosecutorial world.
- Reporting context: had covered white-collar crime, including prosecutors who weren't always doing things right.
- Says he didn't see the Sorkin/Press character as himself.
- Credit for the show's quality goes to showrunners Brian Koppelman and David Levien.

### 21) The "what is the business of Andrew Ross Sorkin" question

- Rejects "media empire."
- Frames it as: he's a journalist applying one lens — people, characters, what motivates them — across multiple formats.
- "An economist might look at 1929 in terms of cycles and systems. … I think people make decisions, some good, some bad, that lead to the cycles."
- Different formats (newsletter, TV, summit, book, fictional TV) are different canvases for the same lens.

### 22) The discipline of saying no

- Asked if he'd add a podcast or run more DealBook Summits around the world.
- Answer: he can only do anything well by spending an extraordinary amount of time on it. 8 years on 1929 is the proof point.
- "I do a lot of things and then I say no to a lot a lot a lot of things."

### 23) Take everything personally

- Inverts the standard "don't take it personally" advice.
- "The people that I know who are the most successful people in the world take everything personally. And it's because they care."
- Ben extends: if the goal is great work, you have to take it personally; if the goal is anything else, you don't.

### 24) "Too close to sources"

- Acknowledges this is the standard critique of his work.
- Defense: the only way to tell the human-nuance version of a story is to know the people. Subjects respect that more, including when he writes critically of them.
- "A great journalist in a way is a fair weather fan. … If they do something right, you should say attaboy. And if they do something wrong, you should explain why it's wrong."

### 25) Close

Ben thanks Sorkin and acknowledges he hadn't read enough of 1929 in advance to interview him about the book itself, so they pivoted to the operating-system episode listeners just heard. Sorkin says the conversation has been "a ball" and that he's listened to Acquired for a long time.
