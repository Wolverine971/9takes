## Core Themes

1. **The script is the “truth.”**
   He treats source material as nourishment, but draws a hard line: the script is what you’re actually making.

2. **Specificity over safety.**
   He repeatedly prefers directors with clear taste and sharp feedback over vague consensus.

3. **Archetypes as magnets.**
   Heathcliff, Frankenstein’s creature, Elvis — he gravitates toward characters with cultural weight and internal contradiction.

4. **Pre-production is the sweet spot.**
   He describes the private research window as the most joyful part: references multiply until the world starts “speaking” in the role’s language.

## Parsed Conversation Flow

- _Wuthering Heights_: first exposure via a stage production; Emerald Fennell texts him the offer; book vs script prep.
- Working with Emerald: shorthand, humor, and a binary feedback style (“gorgeous or not”).
- Adaptation pressure: he references watching earlier versions while not feeling trapped by them.
- _Priscilla_: working with Sofia Coppola; the weight of playing Elvis and making interior choices where footage doesn’t exist.
- _Frankenstein_: del Toro’s influence, favorite films, and the creature as an “every person” mirror.
- Joy in process: the “detective with the red string” research phase, then the terror of showing up to shoot.

## Analysis & Key Insights

### What Stands Out

1. **He’s building a repeatable method for big roles.**
   The method isn’t “be intense” — it’s research, references, and then committing to the script as the final map.

2. **Director trust is his main risk-management tool.**
   He’d rather be pushed by a specific artist than float in ambiguity; the feedback style matters more than reassurance.

3. **He likes the unseen interior.**
   The Elvis segment is a clear example: he looks for the gap where the audience never had access (what happens after the door closes).

### Practical Takeaways (Non-Clinical)

- Use the source text to build intuition, but don’t confuse it with the movie you’re making.
- Choose collaborators who can say “yes/no” clearly; specificity beats politeness.
- Treat pre-production as a real phase of creation (not waiting time): build reference density until your perception shifts.

### 1) Heathcliff and adaptation prep

He describes first encountering _Wuthering Heights_ through a stage production, then being offered the role directly by Emerald Fennell. His prep pattern: read the book for cohesion, then return to the script as the final authority.

### 2) Working with Emerald Fennell (specificity + fun)

He describes Emerald as both funny and sharply specific — someone whose “gorgeous or not” feedback keeps momentum. The point isn’t harshness; it’s clarity.

### 3) The Elvis problem: what happens after the door closes

In discussing _Priscilla_, he describes the weight of playing someone massively documented, then narrows into the useful creative space: the moments you don’t have footage for. He frames that gap as where acting choices can actually live.

### 4) Frankenstein’s creature as mirror

He frames the creature as an “every person” metaphor and talks about preparation under time pressure. The focus is less on makeup and more on philosophy: why the creature reads as human suffering made visible.

### 5) Joy: the private research phase

He describes the pre-shoot window as his favorite part: collecting references until everyday life starts feeling like the role (colors, songs, locations). The punchline is self-aware: you show up to set and fear it was all coincidence (“why did I study how a cheetah walks?”).
