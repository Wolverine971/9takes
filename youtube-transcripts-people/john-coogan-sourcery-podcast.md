## Analysis

### Key Insights from This Transcript

**1. The Serendipity Engine**
Coogan describes his YouTube channel as a "serendipity machine" (David Perell's term). Evergreen content grows for a long time, people find it, realize you're interested in something, and reach out. Congress people, startups, potential hires - all came through YouTube. This is how he connected with Founders Fund: making videos about their portfolio companies (Flexport, SpaceX, Palantir) without even realizing the pattern.

**2. The EIR Role Was Organic, Not Transactional**
His path to Founders Fund: COVID boredom → YouTube videos about tech companies → chatting with Mike Solana (CMO) on Twitter → speaking at Hereticon (talk on nicotine misconceptions) → getting introduced to the team → "hey, want to come hang out and figure out what's next?" It's a 30-40 person firm where "everyone's kind of off doing whatever they want."

**3. Soylent's Distribution Problem Explained**
Nutritional supplements have "very very few barriers to entry." Success depends on cornering influencers (exclusive Rogan or Huberman deals). But products have short shelf lives - Amazon creates generics, every MBA grad starts a DTC brand. There was a "DTC industrial complex" that emerged with Dollar Shave Club and Warby Parker. This insight directly informed why Lucy was more attractive - regulatory barriers create an oligopoly.

**4. The Juul Cautionary Tale**
Coogan studied Juul's rise and fall deeply:

- Early employees were saying "I'm going to work here for 4 years and then start a family office" - and it actually happened, they became billionaires
- Peaked at ~$60B valuation, Altria bought 30% for $13B
- Two critical mistakes: (a) youth marketing perception (school programs that looked like advertising), (b) EVALI lung injury crisis (actually caused by vitamin E acetate in illicit cannabis vapes, not Juul)
- FDA issued marketing denial order
- Lesson for Lucy: be "much much more conservative and much much less aggressive"

**5. The Continuum of Risk Framework**
Scott Gottlieb (Trump's FDA head, former VC at NEA) introduced the "continuum of risk": cigarettes on one end, Nicorette on the other. Products in between (dip, vapes, pouches) should be regulated proportionally. UK's NHS said Juul was "95% safer than cigarettes." The US government has never made a comparable statement - creating massive public health confusion.

**6. The Menthol Ban Paradox**
The Biden admin rolled back a ban on menthol cigarettes while restricting vapes - "you ban vapes but allow menthol cigarettes which are clearly worse." Coogan argues the playing field should be TILTED against cigarettes: "everything you do for the continuum of risk, double it and put that on cigarettes."

**7. The Master Settlement Agreement Shadow Economy**
The 1990s tobacco lawsuit created a massive financial ecosystem: states receive payments, create derivatives, sell settlement bonds. Organizations exist just to trade these bonds. Anti-smoking groups funded by the settlement have "kind of won the battle against cigarettes" and now "look for a new target" - creating perverse incentives against harm reduction products.

**8. Nicotine Myths Debunked**

- Nicotine is NOT a carcinogen (but ~50% of Americans believe it causes cancer)
- The "nightshade/potato" nicotine fact is meaningless - trace amounts requiring "a million potatoes to feel any effect"
- Peter Thiel's "10 IQ point" claim is overblown - tolerance builds quickly, it's comparable to caffeine
- "Don't talk to me until I've had my coffee" is basically what regular nicotine use becomes
- Soldiers smoked in trenches because it "breaks up the monotony of life"

**9. Dimensions of Complexity in Business**
Every new SKU × channel × country multiplies operational work. Lucy went aggressive on product innovation during the narrow regulatory window, then expanded channels (Shopify, Amazon, distributors, direct-to-store, retail partners). Deliberately stayed focused on America. "It can be very tempting because you're like 'Canada is 10% of the US population, I could get a 10% revenue bump' but there's a ton of fixed costs."

**10. Hot Take: The Attention Industrial Complex**
Coogan's meta-take on tech Twitter: "The hot take industrial complex on Twitter right now is like you really got to turn that thing off." People fighting about the Apple iPad ad while "no one's talking about the menthol ban which is gonna kill lots of people." The discourse is "so surface level and so meaningless" - driven by quote-tweet dynamics and internet points.

### Insights About John Coogan

- **8 years deep in nicotine industry** - "it's been like every day for the last eight years" researching tobacco regulation
- **Motivated by misaligned incentives** - fascinated by how regulatory capture, government incentives, and industry lobbying create irrational outcomes
- **Systems thinker** - traces cause-and-effect chains across decades (MSA → settlement bonds → anti-tobacco orgs → perverse incentives)
- **Deeply competitive but patient** - "we want to take a run at these people [big tobacco]... they're weaker than people think... not hiring the best people... the quality of the companies has really really degraded"
- **Father/family influence on business** - being "older dads" means less inclination toward youth-appealing marketing, which is a regulatory advantage
- **Allergic to hype** - dismisses the Peter Thiel nicotine "Limitless pill" narrative as "such a ridiculous thing to me because it's just not true"

During COVID I started chatting with Mike Solana a bunch, who was the chief marketing officer there, and was just talking about crazy stuff on Twitter. From there I started getting introduced to more and more of the people and they were like "hey, you look like you might be thinking about what's next, want to come and hang out and figure out what's next and make some cool content and hang out with the team?" And I was like yeah, that sounds awesome. It's a really tight-knit firm - like 30-40 people and everyone's kind of off doing whatever they want. A lot of people run companies. It was a really good opportunity to go inside the firm, see the other side of the venture startup ecosystem, make some cool content, and plan out my next move.

**Molly**: I love how you got bored and yet all of your YouTube videos have hundreds of thousands if not millions of views and that was just a bored activity.

**John**: I think David Perell has this term for it - the "serendipity engine" or the "serendipity machine." If you put content out on the internet it grows for a very very long time, especially if it's evergreen. People can find it and then they know that you're interested in something and they can reach out and you just get weird connections. ### The Soylent Journey

**Molly**: To get back to your experience as a founder and entrepreneur - you also kind of ride if not create these cultural waves within Silicon Valley. You founded Soylent. How did you come up with this idea?

**John**: It's one of the funniest Silicon Valley startup stories. It started very normally - a bunch of guys who wanted to move to Silicon Valley and build the next technology company. A few software engineers, electrical engineers. The original idea was a wireless mesh network, kind of like Helium later did with crypto, but crypto barely existed at the time. Actually, Coinbase was in our batch at YC and was giving anyone who signed up a full Bitcoin, which is insane - it was like five dollars back then. Their signup reward was "here you go, here's a Bitcoin."

But building wireless base stations is really expensive. We didn't really know what we were doing, didn't have a financial model. No one wanted to pony up millions of dollars to build what were essentially 5G towers pre-Huawei. We failed to fundraise for it, were working on a bunch of other companies too, but nothing was working. We weren't making a dime. I think the best launch we ever had was 500 people signing up for a free app. It was terrible - we just did not understand distribution at all.

My co-founder was writing blog posts about mostly technical subjects, but he knew how to make them really punchy and go viral on Hacker News. He did this one that's really controversial now, but at the time - R. Kelly had this "Trapped in the Closet" series. He wrote a blog post about how to use a graph tool to visualize nodes in a social graph using R. Kelly as the raw data. It was funny but it taught you something, so that went pretty viral. He was starting to build up a little following.

Then he got really into nutrition. Rob was a computer engineer, real computer scientist type, and one of our other co-founders was studying biology at Caltech. There was a cross-pollination there and Rob started thinking about biology from a computer scientist perspective. Like, "what if we could just break down the bare nutrients and consume exactly what we need - just live on this protein shake essentially?"

When he was talking about it I was like this makes no sense, why are you worried about this. But we were broke - we were running out of money. Back then YC gave you like $170K and we were down to maybe $50-60K. We paid our rent in advance, owned our laptops, so there were really no expenses to just keep hacking. But we were spending money on food, and in San Francisco food's really expensive. You don't have a car so you can't go to Costco, you don't have space so you can't grow your own food. If you're eating fast food all the time that's really unhealthy. A very simple protein shake was actually a pretty material upgrade if you're living in squalor.

As soon as he wrote about it, it just went mega viral. He Brian Johnson-like click-baited it - the blog post was called "How I Stopped Eating Food for 30 Days." That's just viral fuel. Back then there weren't as many YouTubers - it was like a Ryan Trahan experiment type thing. Rob lived on this new food for 30 days and it's immediately human - anyone can click on that because everyone eats food, everyone has opinions about it, and there's this inherent drama of "will he survive, what's going to happen to him, who knows?"

It was also a great stress test - if this guy says he can live on this for 30 days, you could probably try a sip of it. People were really interested in trying it and just overnight - remember I said we had 500 signups for our first thing - we had 10,000 people fill out this really really long intake form asking them "would you send us your 23andMe data, would you send us your blood?" and they were like "yeah" - super fans immediately.

So we dropped everything, did a crowdfunding campaign, and it was just off to the races. The news was picking it up, tons of organic media. Vice made a whole documentary about the company. We were on the cover of the New Yorker. Rob went on the Colbert Report - mainstream late night TV, which you don't see from a lot of tech founders. The only tech founder who had done the Colbert Show before Rob was Brian Chesky at Airbnb, which was a $10 billion company at the time.

It was insane - anytime we'd do one of these press hits we'd just make a million dollars in a day. Growth was just nuts. Grew to like $60 million in revenue in a few years. And then just over-raised, brought in bad team, it was just a disaster. Everyone left and the company sold for not a great outcome last year.

### Lucy and the Nicotine Opportunity

**John**: But it led me and my co-founders to launch a new company in 2016 which we have become much more optimistic on because it has a few fundamentally different characteristics from Soylent.

One of the biggest problems with Soylent was that in the nutritional supplement space there are very few barriers to entry. Companies that have done well have done a really good job of cornering influencers - exclusive deals with Joe Rogan or Andrew Huberman. But a lot of these products have a short shelf life because eventually Amazon creates the generic, every person out of an MBA program starts the latest DTC thing. There was a DTC industrial complex that popped up.

At the same time my co-founder was quitting smoking and he didn't like any of the alternatives. He was very skeptical of vapes, but our other co-founder was at Caltech and had studied the nicotinic receptor in the brain and understood the science behind nicotine - that while it was extremely addictive, it wasn't a carcinogen. This was kind of an unknown, just under-discussed.

So we said okay, this is a product that feels controversial, sounds controversial, but is actually not controversial at all. And if we can get into this space - there were new laws going into effect that would make it extremely highly regulated. You could either launch a company in 2016 or you would never be able to launch a new company because the door was closing on this regulatory situation. Anyone who wants to launch one of these products now - it's tens or hundreds of millions of dollars to even get started. Very difficult to underwrite as a venture capitalist because you have to put all this money up front, wait maybe five years for the FDA to get back to you before you can sell a single unit. How do you know if you have product-market fit? You don't.

We were attracted to it because it leveraged all of the consumer experience we had - shipping DTC, Shopify, all the basic stuff - but it had a monopolistic, oligopolistic market structure at the end of the tunnel. It's been a very long road but it's finally starting to work and it just feels awesome.

### The Juul Lessons

**Molly**: What are the biggest misconceptions for nicotine products and this market?

**John**: The Juul story is super informative and we're very lucky they kind of went first. There's a ton of lessons.

Even from day one - some of the early employees were like "I'm going to work here for four years and then start a family office." That's not something you hear from early stage startup _employees_, not founders. And it happened - they're billionaires now. At their peak I think they were valued in like $60-ish billion. Altria bought 30% for about $13 billion, and the majority of that went to pay a dividend to investors and employees. There were people that started a month before the dividend and got a check for $120,000 on basically day one.

But there were mistakes. The two main ones: First, youth initiation. Juul had this program where they went to schools trying to pay public schools to do education about how bad smoking is. But because it was paid by Juul and the people doing it were using Juuls, it looked like they were marketing directly to kids. From day one with Lucy we were like "how can we age-gate more aggressively, advertise to even older people?" It helps that we're all older dads at this point. We've never advertised on TikTok.

The second issue was EVALI - the e-cigarette and vapor acute lung injury crisis. People died. Everyone thought it was because of Juul. It actually turned out to be vitamin E acetate in illicit cannabis vapes. The FDA literally raided someone - kicked down his door - and it's a guy with a syringe plunging vitamin E acetate into vape pods with marijuana. A sketchy basement operation. But the damage was done to the whole category.

Meanwhile, youth vaping skyrocketed from 10-20% to 30-40%. Interestingly, cigarette use plummeted - cigarettes were no longer cool. But you always have to weigh that against youth initiation. Juul eventually got a marketing denial order from the FDA. Watching that company go through all of that was very informative. It's why we've taken a much more conservative strategy.

### The Continuum of Risk

**John**: The biggest cognitive dissonance publicly still remains the idea that nicotine gives you lung cancer, which is just not true. The government's acknowledged this, everyone's acknowledged this, but it hasn't worked its way into public consciousness. If you poll Americans, about 50% will say nicotine gives you cancer.

That creates problems because people think "if all these products are just as bad, I might as well use cigarettes."

There was a guy, Scott Gottlieb, who was the head of the FDA that Trump appointed. He's a former venture capitalist from NEA - extremely sharp, talking to the press, tweeting, laying his cards on the table. He acknowledged this idea of the continuum of risk: cigarettes on one side, Nicorette on the other. Nicorette's been on the market for 40+ years - the side effects are hiccups and sore throat, not cancer. Then you have cigarettes with 100 years of data that kill half of lifetime users.

He acknowledged that products will exist in between. Like dip - loose leaf tobacco that you pack in your jaw. Doesn't go in your lungs so no lung cancer, but can cause mouth cancer, though not at the rate cigarettes do. The FDA actually approved one of those moist snuff products as a "modified risk tobacco product" which allows Swedish Match to put on their labels that it's less harmful than cigarettes.

But then the youth epidemic changed everything and Gottlieb changed his tune, then left, and other people came in.

The UK's National Health Service said they believe Juul was 95% safer than cigarettes. The US government has never said anything comparable. They have approved multiple e-cigarettes now, which is interesting.

### The Regulatory Labyrinth

**John**: Right now the Biden Administration just rolled back a ban on menthol cigarettes. Those products are just as harmful as other cigarettes.

It's very hard for the government to outright ban cigarettes because of the Master Settlement Agreement from the 90s. The government sued all the big tobacco companies collectively. The economics weren't "you're killing Americans" - it was that by addicting Americans to cigarettes which cause lung cancer, those users strain public hospital resources, causing a negative externality and economic loss to taxpayers.

The MSA happens - it's a wild story, there's movies about this, "The Insider" is really great. All the big tobacco companies were sticking together saying "nicotine's not addictive" which is ridiculous. Then one company broke from the coalition and was like "give us a carve out, we'll give you the dirt on all of them."

The money flows into the government and pays for anti-smoking education. But it creates weird incentives where people getting paid by big tobacco through the MSA run anti-tobacco advertising - but they've kind of won the battle against cigarettes. Everyone knows cigarettes are bad. So now they're looking for a new target.

States took these revenue streams and created derivatives, sold them as bonds. There are whole organizations dedicated to trading these settlement bonds. It's this massive shadow economy. Tobacco is America's first cash crop - it's what America was built on.

### Debunking Nicotine Myths

**Molly**: Could we debunk nicotine a little bit more?

**John**: The potato/nightshade thing is so ridiculous because it's in such trace amounts that you would need a million potatoes to feel any effect. That's just a fun fact.

The biggest misconception is that nicotine gives you lung cancer, which is just not true. Still about 50% of Americans believe it.

There's also a NEW narrative to debunk - nicotine as this "Limitless pill." People think because Peter Thiel said the "10 IQ point" thing... like, no one studied the effect on IQ. It's a mild stimulant similar to caffeine. If you were sleepy and took a general knowledge test, you'd probably do better on a stimulant. But nicotine builds up a really quick tolerance. If you're using it every day it's very much like "don't talk to me until I've had my coffee" - you're really not getting some incredible benefit.

That's such a ridiculous thing to me because it's just not true. Certain segments of tech have overcorrected because they think it's so edgy and different. Really, these products are mild stimulants, highly addictive, very easy to lose their potency. Most importantly, they're better substitutes for cigarettes if you're already smoking.

More people died of cigarettes during COVID than from COVID. The peak COVID deaths were 430,000 that year. Smoking killed 460,000 that same year.

### Business Strategy: Dimensions of Complexity

**Molly**: How do you construct and build this type of company?

**John**: There's this dance around what I call dimensions of complexity in a business. When most DTC companies start, it's a single SKU, single product, single channel - Shopify, single country, America. Then over time they add more SKUs, more countries, more channels. But every time you add an extra dimension you're multiplying the work.

If you have two SKUs on Amazon and Shopify, every revenue breakdown needs to be four cells. Add Canada and it's eight. That multiplies like crazy as you get more product lines and flavor variations.

For Lucy we went aggressive on the product side during the narrow regulatory window. Then we expanded into multiple channels - Shopify, Amazon, third-party e-commerce, distributors, direct-to-store deliveries, retail chains. Our channel analytics is insane. But we're only really doing this in America. If you try to do omni-channel, omni-product, AND go global, you can really hamstring your FP&A function.

The guy who sold Vita Coco launched just in New York yoga studios. Got insane penetration, everyone loved it. Then he went into New York CrossFit gyms and Austin yoga studios - proving it worked in different markets in the same channel AND the same market in a slightly different channel. He left so much easy expansion on the table, but all the metrics looked amazing in the narrow area. Sold to Pepsi for hundreds of millions even with modest revenues because it was so obvious it was working.

### Storytelling as Marketing

**Molly**: You're a remarkably good storyteller. How do you think about the storytelling of the business?

**John**: It's tricky for this company. We did one TechCrunch article earlier and they were just like "this is the next Juul" - very negative. So we've mostly tried to let the product speak for itself and work with people in our demographic who have podcasts and genuinely love the product.

I think we should do more business storytelling. It would help with FDA advocacy. A lot of people don't know anything about Zin, but it was started by a big tobacco company from day one, sold to an even bigger one recently. Literally all of our direct competitors above us are owned by big tobacco. We're the one company in the space that's not big tobacco.

We looked at doing some owned media - considered starting a podcast - but we can't really talk about nicotine for an hour. We tried interviewing people in any psychoactive entrepreneur space - caffeine, alcohol. The George Clooney Casamigos story, Teremana... there's enough to feed off of but it wasn't great.

So now we focus on storytelling to retailers. We were on the cover of some retail distribution magazine that no one outside the industry has ever seen, but it sold a ton of product because convenience store owners get this in the mail and think "oh, this is the one on the cover, I should order this." That storytelling is much more dollars and cents - this product outperforms, it makes you more money.

**Molly**: This sounds like a good business.

**John**: It is. After the market crash we were thinking about raising money, we canned that, right-sized the business, and got super profitable. Now it's been growing a ton. It's so much different as a founder - actually opening up a P&L and being like "this is what matters" instead of some wishy-washy KPI. I still do cohort analysis but I'm like I don't care about that, I just want cash in the bank.

We raised a seed round, went through YC with this company too - second time through - which was cool because it was a signal that YC was taking a bet on something "controversial." Then we raised a Series A before the pandemic with RRE. It was enough. This is definitely a capital intensive industry to get set up because of all the regulatory stuff, but once you're set up it's more of a working capital problem - your monthly revenue roughly equals your needed inventory. It's not like there's some massive R&D expense.

### Hot Takes

**Molly**: What are your hottest takes in tech right now?

**John**: Trae Stephens, partner at Founders Fund and co-founder of Anduril, just posted this essay in Pirate Wires about how by the time there's a market trend you can build a VC fund around, it might be too late because the power law company has been established. I thought that was very thoughtful.

But my actual hot take is: the hot take industrial complex on Twitter right now - you really got to turn that thing off. People were freaking out about this Apple iPad ad. If you asked someone on the street, I don't even know if they know Apple launched new iPads. It might just be a culture war happening on your phone. Maybe you should log off and go play an actual instrument.

Logging off is undervalued right now. It could be a negative signal if you even know the cultural significance of this ad and why people are fighting about it.

I have a hot take about the Rabbit R1 - I really think it would be good for kids. I want to get one for my son. It's bright orange, he'll love it, and I'll say "all you can do is ask it facts about rabbits." He'll just talk to this thing and learn prompt engineering essentially. Who cares if it hallucinates? Kids don't care about latency.

I want more specific AI gadgets, more niche. I want the "Flappy Bird story" of this AI cycle. There's one guy making a million dollars a month with a rizz app that helps you talk to girls on Tinder. One of them called me asking "should I raise money?" and I was like "sounds like you can just make a ton of money with this, just monetize it." He texted me back a couple weeks later: "I turned on monetization, I'm making $100K a month." If you're doing this at 18, you'll go on to start a great company one day.

The whole ChatGPT wrapper meme is a total psyop by VCs who have scaled up their funds too much to ruin fun for young entrepreneurs. Just let the 18-year-old build a ChatGPT wrapper and make $100K a month. Who cares if GPT-6 puts him out of business? In that time he'll make a million dollars, learn a ton about building products and dealing with consumers. The skills are super valuable. We've scared ourselves into thinking that's low status. It doesn't matter. You probably shouldn't raise a ton of VC, but you can still build a great business and have a great life and make money.

VCs have completely de-statused this path because the funds are too big. Unless they can put a billion dollars into it, it's bad.

**Molly**: Well John, it was a pleasure having you on.

**John**: Yeah, this is great. I'm looking forward to it.
