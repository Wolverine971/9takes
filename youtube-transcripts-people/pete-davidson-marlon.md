## Analysis & Key Insights

### Major Themes

1. **Childhood Trauma and the Loss of a Father on 9/11**: The central defining event of Pete's life. At age seven, he lost his father -- a New York City firefighter who was among the first responders at the World Trade Center. The loss came only nine months after his parents' divorce, compounding the abandonment and instability. His mother initially told him his father might still be alive, trapped in the wreckage, extending the uncertainty for weeks.

2. **Social Isolation and Bullying on Staten Island**: Pete describes a childhood of persistent social rejection. He attended three different schools, had virtually no friends, and was bullied relentlessly -- even in the immediate aftermath of his father's death. He describes Staten Island as an insular, culturally stagnant place filled with "people who peaked in high school," and credits leaving the island with realizing the problem was not entirely him.

3. **Compulsive and Self-Destructive Coping Mechanisms**: Pete's response to trauma manifested physically (trichotillomania -- pulling out his own hair until he was bald in fourth grade), through substance use (daily marijuana from age 16 to 23), and through behavioral patterns associated with BPD. He describes an all-or-nothing relationship with substances: "Why would you not do it every day?"

4. **Borderline Personality Disorder Diagnosis and Treatment**: At 23, Pete received a BPD diagnosis after months of blind rages, memory blackouts, and emotional breakdowns. He describes the core symptom as a pervasive fear of abandonment directly traceable to losing his father. He was actively in DBT (dialectical behavior therapy) at the time of the interview and describes it as slowly working.

5. **Comedy as Salvation and Identity**: Stand-up comedy gave Pete his first real community and sense of belonging. He explicitly states that comedians were "the first people exactly like me" and that the comedy world accepted him in a way Staten Island never did. His path from the Loony Bin bowling alley comedy club to SNL is remarkably compressed.

6. **The Healing Power of Shared Experience (America's Camp)**: The 9/11 children's camp represents one of the few purely positive memories in Pete's early life. The camp's approach -- distraction, community, and a "Volcano Room" for physical catharsis -- gave him a model for healthy coping that anticipated the DBT skills he would later learn.

### Key Insights

1. Pete's parents divorced approximately nine months before September 11, 2001, meaning he experienced two major losses of his father in rapid succession -- first the divorce, then the death.

2. His mother told him his father might come home and they would get back together, using hope of reunion as an emotional buffer. This created weeks of false hope and extended the trauma of loss.

3. Pete developed trichotillomania (compulsive hair pulling) after his father's death, leaving him nearly bald in fourth grade -- a visible marker of internal distress that made him a further target for bullying.

4. He was already in therapy before 9/11 due to the divorce, and researchers from Columbia University came to his home to interview him as part of studies on 9/11 children, asking questions he found disturbing ("Do you think your dad's alive?").

5. Pete did not visit Manhattan until he was 14 years old, despite living in New York City. His world was confined to Staten Island and Brooklyn, reflecting the insularity of his community.

6. He describes an all-or-nothing addictive personality: with nicotine, alcohol, and marijuana, his immediate thought is "Why would you not do it every day?" -- a trait consistent with BPD's impulsive tendencies.

7. He was diagnosed with Crohn's disease around age 16-17, was misdiagnosed and mistreated by Staten Island doctors for years, and only found relief (Remicade infusions) after going to a specialist in Manhattan.

8. Pete had never watched SNL before being recruited for the show. He watched SpongeBob and Drake & Josh growing up and only YouTubed SNL sketches the night before his writers' meeting.

9. Bill Hader recommended Pete to Lorne Michaels after seeing him in a small role in Amy Schumer's Trainwreck -- a chain of lucky connections that Pete himself calls "ridiculous."

10. Pete was high during his meeting with Lorne Michaels. He describes himself as "a high person from 16 to 23."

11. His BPD manifests primarily as fear of abandonment, which he directly connects to his father's death: "One day he was here and then the next day they're gone." He describes anxiety when his mother says she's leaving the house.

12. He describes manipulative conversational patterns typical of BPD: engineering conversations to get specific reassurances, then disbelieving those reassurances, creating exhausting cycles for partners.

13. Pete expresses a wish that he had no memories of his father at all, like his younger sister (who was three at the time), saying he would prefer to "just start without a dad" rather than carry the memory of loss.

14. His stated goal in therapy is strikingly modest: "I would love to be okay, Marc. That would be incredible. Just to be okay."

15. At the time of this interview (2017), Pete describes the year of his BPD diagnosis as "the worst year of my life" -- notable given everything else he had already been through.

### Summary

This 2017 WTF interview captures Pete Davidson at 23, in the middle of SNL's 43rd season and at one of the most psychologically turbulent periods of his life. He had just received a borderline personality disorder diagnosis after months of blind rages, blackouts, and a stint in rehab. The conversation is remarkable for its rawness -- Pete speaks with very little filter about the worst moments of his life, from watching his world collapse at age seven to pulling his hair out in fourth grade to being the perpetual outcast on an island he describes as culturally dead.

The through-line of the interview is abandonment. Pete's father -- a firefighter he loved, whose firehouse he visited regularly -- was killed on 9/11 when Pete was seven. His parents had divorced just months before. His mother extended hope that his father might return. When it became clear he would not, Pete was left with a template for the world: people you love can vanish without warning. This became the core of his BPD, which he describes with clinical precision and dark humor. He details the fear of abandonment, the manipulative conversational loops, the all-or-nothing thinking, and the constant dread that everyone secretly hates him.

What makes the interview compelling from a personality analysis perspective is Pete's extraordinary self-awareness paired with his inability to fully control his patterns. He can articulate exactly what is happening in his brain -- the manipulation, the testing of partners, the assumption of the worst -- but describes these patterns as still active and overwhelming. His comedy career, which took him from a bowling alley comedy club to SNL in just a few years, serves as both escape and identity. Comedy gave him his first real community, his first experience of being accepted, and a framework for processing pain. His therapeutic goal -- "I would love to be okay" -- is delivered not as a punchline but as a sincere, almost desperate aspiration, and it captures the emotional register of a young man who has already survived more than most people encounter in a lifetime.
