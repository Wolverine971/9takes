### Highest ROI Resolutions

**Chris:** I reflected last year on the highest ROI resolutions that I've ever done.

**No phone in the bedroom at night.** Charge it outside. It's an instant 15% quality of life increase. Because when you start your day, if you use your phone as your alarm, you roll over, turn the alarm off, and immediately you're looking at your phone. You haven't got up, you haven't got moving, you're not hydrated, you're not seeing sunlight in your eyes. **Morning walk every day.** Morning sunlight in your eyes, even if you don't have the sun. Getting up and doing ambulation -- walking through an environment while your eyes scan left and right -- seems to tune down your fear response, makes your amygdala a little more calm. 5 minutes, 10 minutes.

**No caffeine within 90 minutes of waking.** The adenosine system isn't dominant during the first 90 minutes of the day -- your adrenal system is. If you just push that caffeine a little bit later and see what happens, at least for me, I know that that works well.

**No alcohol for 6 months.** If you take alcohol out for about six months, what it forces you to do is think, "Do I really want to go to that party? ## Analysis & Key Insights

### Major Themes

1. **The Deferred Life Hypothesis** -- The pervasive human tendency to believe that "real life" hasn't started yet, that happiness sits on the other side of the next achievement. Chris identifies this as one of the most dangerous psychological traps, where people permanently put off living while waiting for conditions to be perfect.

2. **Subtraction Over Addition** -- A counterintuitive approach to goal-setting. Rather than piling new habits and aspirations onto an already full plate, Chris argues that in order to pick something up, you must put something down. The buffet metaphor (your stomach won't expand to fit more food) captures this perfectly.

3. **The Unteachable Lesson** -- The paradox that the most important life lessons (money won't fix happiness, fame won't fix self-worth) can only be learned through direct experience. People who haven't achieved these things dismiss the wisdom of those who have, and those who have achieved them evangelize the same insights.

4. **Problems as a Feature, Not a Bug** -- The recognition that problems will never disappear from life, and that railing against their existence only compounds suffering. This connects to the Stoic idea of accepting what is rather than fighting reality.

5. **The Lonely Chapter** -- The transitional period during personal growth where you've outgrown your old peer group but haven't yet built a new one. This is presented as an expected, normal, and necessary part of change rather than a sign that something is wrong.

6. **Suppression vs Strength** -- Chris's personal revelation that feeling and expressing emotions is not weakness. This is especially significant coming from someone in the men's self-improvement space, directly challenging the "hustle and grind until your eyes bleed" mentality.

7. **Fuel Sources for Motivation** -- The rocket ship metaphor: use whatever fuel you have at the start (including self-hatred, chips on your shoulder, need for validation), but recognize that those are toxic fuels that must be replaced as you gain altitude.

8. **The Population Decline and Meaning** -- Both hosts discuss their own childlessness despite being in their 30s, connecting it to broader cultural trends including the paradox of freedom and the "vitality curve" problem.

### Summary

This is a two-hour conversation between Steven Bartlett and Chris Williamson recorded at the end of 2025, focused on how to approach the new year with intentionality. Chris presents a comprehensive framework for annual reflection and goal-setting, anchored by his free annual review template. The core argument is that people fail at New Year's resolutions because they overload their plates without subtracting anything, rely on motivation (an unreliable fuel source) instead of systems, and aim at observable metrics of success while sacrificing hidden ones that actually determine happiness.

The conversation takes a deeply personal turn when Chris reveals that 2025 was the hardest year of his life. He contracted toxic mold poisoning that attacked the three pillars of his identity -- energy, mood, and cognition. He spent most of the year in survival mode, going to bed at 7 PM, forgetting how to tie his shoes, forgetting friends' names, all while continuing to produce his podcast. His only two goals for the year were "don't let the show drop and fix my health." This experience stripped him down to what remained when everything he valued was taken away, and what he found was kindness, sensitivity, and resilience in the most ordinary sense -- what he calls "boring victories."

Both hosts also engage in a surprisingly vulnerable discussion about their own childlessness, the population decline, and the tension between freedom and meaning. Chris describes planning to become a father within the next 12 months despite no emotional pull toward it, framing it as an unteachable lesson he must experience. The conversation weaves between practical productivity advice (no phone in bedroom, no caffeine for 90 minutes, 10-minute walks after meals) and existential reflection on the nature of striving, the deferred life hypothesis, and the recognition that "the best men are those who have been broken." The through-line is that personal growth is messy, lonely, full of doubt, and nothing like the hero's journey montage -- but showing up anyway, even tired, even sad, even without self-belief, is what ultimately matters.
