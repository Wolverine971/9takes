### How Do We Prepare for a Loss in Motivation?

### The Tall Girl Problem and Hypergamy

**Steven**: Where did we go wrong and how do we go right?

For a man to increase his rating on a 10-point scale by two points, he requires around about a tenfold increase in his salary. For a woman to achieve the same two-point improvement on a 10-point scale, a salary would need to increase by 10,000 times.

My point being that women are concerned about a partner's socioeconomic status significantly more than men are. Now you can start to see that if you have a world in which women are attending university at high rates, they are achieving more success in employment (at least in that sort of 21-to-29 range, which is when most people are perhaps looking for potential partners), and yet the socioeconomic status of a partner to a woman is a big determinant of their level of attraction -- you can start to see how this imbalance could cause a problem.

Similarly, when we talk about education: a man with a master's degree on Tinder gets 90% more right swipes than a man with a bachelor's degree.

Now, in a world in which, quite rightly, women have finally been able to achieve parity in education and employment and status and have independence and not be financially reliant on their partner -- that's great for them. But it does cause some challenges for their dating.

And this is what I've called **the tall girl problem**. Everybody knows what it's like to have a girlfriend who is six foot without heels. You go -- if you want to wear heels, you're looking at professional athletes. Because on average, women want to date a man who is at least as tall or a little bit taller than they are.

So as women rise up through their own competence hierarchy in education and employment, they further shorten down the potential pool of eligible men that are as educated or more educated and as employed or more employed than they are. This is a challenge. This is just a straight-up imbalance.

What this causes is a very large group of men toward the bottom of this distribution to be essentially invisible to women. It causes a very large number of women -- an increasing cohort -- to compete for an increasingly small group of turbocharged super-performers at the top.

These guys, the super high-value guys, have a wealth of options. So they are commitment-averse. Why would they decide to sit down with one girl for the rest of time when they have this wealth of options? Which can cause them to use and discard many of these women. "No, no, all men are whatever it might be -- they are users and abusers, we don't need them, where are all the good men," etc. It's a big group of men that feel like they are good men that are invisible. ### Dealing with Regrets

**Chris**: Regret is something that I've been thinking about an awful lot. And reframing it has been one of the most useful mental models I've gained.

Douglas Murray, British writer, was telling me a story about Christopher Hitchens. Douglas was lamenting to Hitch about having to choose a thing and, by choosing it, not being able to do a different thing. And Hitchens says: **"Douglas, in life we must choose our regrets."**

And I reflected on it so much. What I'd always presumed was that the only reason I had a regret is because I made a sub-optimal decision. If only I'd made the perfect decision, I could have avoided the regret.

But when you accept that opportunity cost is baked into the fabric of life -- me and you can go to the gym or we can go to the theme park. By going to the gym, we don't go to the theme park. Even if the gym was the right decision, we're always going to have the open loop of "I wonder what the theme park was like."

So: regrets aren't a bug. They're a feature. They are a natural byproduct of us always being curious about what could have been. That's liberating. It makes you feel less culpable.

So what does "choose your regrets" mean? If regrets are inescapable, you have to choose which one you want. Which of these two could I bear living with the regret of? And that makes decisions an awful lot easier. It switches us from scarcity and fear about the future to: which of these could I not bear myself to live without?

For instance, with me moving to America -- it was a big move at 33, 34. But if I had the opportunity to become one of the best in the world at what I do, to pursue my curiosity, and I didn't do it -- I couldn't have lived with that regret. I can live with the regret of trying and failing. I couldn't live with the regret of wondering "what if."

### Choose in Advance What You're Going to Suck At

## Analysis & Key Insights

### Major Themes

1. **Fear of Insufficiency as the Engine of Achievement**: Chris identifies a crippling sense of insufficiency as the primary driver of most high performers. Through the rat experiment metaphor, he argues that running from fear generates more force than running toward desire -- but this fuel is toxic long-term. He frames the central tension of ambitious people as: how do you deprogram the fear without losing the drive?

2. **The Happiness-Success Paradox**: We sacrifice the thing we want (happiness) to pursue the thing that's supposed to deliver it (success). If you remove success from both sides of the equation, you're left with happiness. Yet as social creatures, we can't simply renounce all worldly ambition.

3. **The Masculinity and Dating Crisis**: The centerpiece of the conversation. Chris presents a data-rich analysis of modern dating dysfunction: rising male sexlessness (8% to 28% in a decade), declining friendships (zero close friends increasing fivefold), and the "tall girl problem" -- as women rise in education and employment, their pool of acceptable male partners shrinks due to hypergamy (the tendency to date across and up).

4. **The Loneliness Epidemic**: Men's close friendships have collapsed -- from 55% having six or more close friends in 1990 to 21% in 2020. Social media, technology, and the nerfing of social rejection have created a generation that is more connected but more atomized than ever.

5. **The Male Sedation Hypothesis**: Chris's original theory that porn, video games, and social media are sedating young men out of the dangerous "young male syndrome" that historically destabilized societies. This keeps the peace but at the cost of male vitality, ambition, and relationship-seeking behavior.

6. **Identity, Masks, and Authenticity**: Chris's personal journey from chronically unpopular child to club promoter to Love Island contestant to podcaster is a story of playing roles to earn belonging, then slowly discovering that authentic self-expression -- not performance -- is the only path to genuine connection.

7. **Building Confidence Through Action, Not Affirmation**: Confidence comes from a "stack of undeniable proof" (Alex Hormozi), not from mirror affirmations. Action must come first for the chronically unconfident person, because their negativity will crush any positive self-talk.

8. **Choosing Your Regrets**: Drawing on Christopher Hitchens via Douglas Murray: regrets are a feature, not a bug. Opportunity cost is baked into reality. The question isn't "how do I avoid regret?" but "which regret could I live with?"

### Key Insights

1. **The three traits of hyper-successful people**: (1) Crippling sense of insufficiency, (2) Superiority complex, (3) Ability to have maniacal focus.

### Summary

This two-hour conversation between Steven Bartlett and Chris Williamson on The Diary Of A CEO is one of the most data-rich and emotionally candid explorations of modern masculinity, dating dynamics, and the loneliness epidemic available in podcast form. Chris draws on personal experience -- from a bullied, lonely only child in Newcastle to a Love Island contestant playing a role he never truly was, to building Modern Wisdom into one of Europe's largest podcasts -- to illustrate how fear of insufficiency drives achievement but poisons the soul if left unexamined.

The heart of the conversation is Chris's systematic analysis of why men and women are increasingly unable to form relationships. He introduces the "tall girl problem" -- the mathematical consequence of female hypergamy meeting female educational and economic achievement. As women (rightly) rise in status, the pool of men they'll consider shrinks, creating a competition funnel where a small number of high-value men are commitment-averse with too many options, a large cohort of women compete for access to them, and a massive group of invisible men retreat entirely from the mating market. The stats are stark: male sexlessness tripled from 2008 to 2018, 50% of young men aren't pursuing relationships at all, and 80% of childless women past their fertility window didn't choose childlessness.

Perhaps most provocatively, Chris introduces his "male sedation hypothesis" -- that porn, video games, and social media are chemically and psychologically pacifying young men out of the dangerous "young male syndrome" that has historically destabilized societies with large populations of unpartnered males. This keeps the peace but leaves men diminished. His proposed solutions are notably non-adversarial: turn down the volume of gender warfare, red-shirt boys in school, re-pedestalize motherhood, re-encourage in-person dating, and above all, raise men up without bringing women down. Throughout, he insists that compassion is needed for both sides -- the high-achieving woman who can't find a partner and the invisible man who's been told his problems don't matter -- and that framing the sexes as enemies is the single biggest obstacle to progress.
