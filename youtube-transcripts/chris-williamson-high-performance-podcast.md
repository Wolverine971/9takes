### What Is High Performance?

**Jake**: Chris, welcome to the show. Gentlemen, thank you for being here. Look, you know the way this show starts -- what is high performance to you?

**Chris**: I think I would have given you a very different answer about five or six years ago. I think I would have told you about optimizing my day -- time efficiency, Ebbinghaus forgetting curves and spaced repetition, time blocking, Pomodoro techniques. And now it feels a lot more like just doing work that I'm proud of. I still want to crush it. I still want to leave it all out on the field of play. But my goal is to try and do things that, in retrospect, I'm glad that I did.

And I think once you've spent a bit of time accumulating skills and becoming world class or close to world class at anything, you then get the opportunity to start to play with the rules that previously you followed, and I'm really enjoying that. So for me it's creating work that I'm proud of. The performance is going to come along for the ride because I've worked so hard at it. But it's strange how that arc sort of comes into land.

**Jake**: So whereabouts on that arc are you then? Have you reached the bit where you say, "Yeah, okay, we've had the mastery moment, now it's about the enjoyment and the play and the exploration"?

**Chris**: Yeah, it's around about there. The question I have for myself this year -- the theme of this year -- is: can you be world class and have fun at the same time?

**Jake**: So you think that the world-class bit's been achieved but the fun hasn't?

**Chris**: Correct, yeah. I've sacrificed the fun in order to be world class.

### The Fear of Enjoyment

**Jake**: So how are you bringing the fun back in?

**Chris**: Trying to find play and ease, and not put too much pressure on myself. You know, a lot of people can achieve phenomenally great things by ambient anxiety and just grinding their nose into the floor -- hours and hours and hours of reps and fear and vigilance and concern. How much am I better on the competition floor, whatever your chosen pursuit is, because of this permanent concern that I will not be good enough if I don't do all of this stuff in advance?

And then the even more difficult question is: wow, maybe I could be _better_ if I actually found a sense of ease and joy and play in the things that I do. But I do think that you need to go through it -- it's a mountain that you can only realize was kind of a false peak. You know the concept of false peaks in mountaineering? It's a false peak you can only see once you're at the top of it. And I think that it's important -- if you're going to become world class at something, you actually have to become world class. You have to achieve some degree of competence and learn the rules before you can then start breaking them and playing with them and realizing that maybe they weren't there to begin with. ### What Are You Running From?

**Chris**: People can be driven largely by two different things. They can run away from something that they fear. They can run towards something that they want. There's a great experiment done by starving rats in a tube and attaching a spring to their tail which has a force measure on it, and how hard they pull is a proxy for how much they want the thing. So these rats are starving, they waft the smell of cheese in from the front of the tube, and these rats pull. You think, "Well, these rats are starving, they've got to be running pretty hard."

Then they do a second iteration where they waft the smell of a cat in from behind and the smell of cheese in from the front, and the rats pull harder. And the lesson from that is that in life, not only do you want to run towards something that you want, but away from something that you fear. That being said, the running away from something that you fear -- insufficiency, a requirement for validation and success and recognition from the world around you, prove to yourself that you are worthy of love or acceptance or praise -- it's a very potent fuel, but it's toxic over too long of a time. So I think it's very important to realize when that fuel is spent.

**Jake**: So tell us a little bit then around -- because that "running away from something" is a powerful fuel at a certain stage. I'm interested in your own journey, Chris. What was it you were running away from?

**Chris**: Initially, I think I had a very high need to be validated by the world. So growing up was super unpopular in school, was an only child. Parents are quite introverted as well, so in terms of socialization that was relatively low. And then when I got to university, started becoming a club promoter and basically supplanted people wanting me for people _needing_ me. If you're the guy that has the VIP wristbands and knows who all of the pretty girls are and can get you queue jump at the busiest nights in town, functionally it's the same as people wanting you. But what it actually is, is people needing you -- it's a very transactional relationship.

And then from there I realized, "Oh, if I do something which is socially rewarded and held in high esteem, that makes people positively disposed to me. So I'll just keep running this same script." So I became a male model -- was a male model for a decade -- became a DJ, ran one of the biggest events companies in the north of England, went on Take Me Out, went on Love Island. So I did the Champions League of being a professional "I need attention, please give me validation" thing. And some may argue that I'm still doing the same.

But what I realized sort of going through that was that I just desperately wanted connection with the people around me. I just wanted people that felt like they had my back. Because, you know, a lot of the time, even murderers have got people that will visit them in prison -- brothers and sisters and family and stuff like that. But when you don't go through your childhood with perhaps that safety and security of a tight social net, you try to supplant that need for validation elsewhere. And yeah, that was a big sort of trend, that was a big theme I think coming up.

**Damian**: What are your emotions now when you think back to that period? We have a not dissimilar story -- I had similar issues at school. They still trigger me a little bit. How do you feel when you think back to those days?

**Chris**: I feel sad for that little boy. I am able to make myself the villain, and this is something that a lot of people may resonate with -- which is, even in a situation where it wasn't your fault, you somehow managed to find a way to make it your fault.

And I spoke about this with Jocko Willink -- the Navy SEAL, scary guy, big hands. He came on the show and his whole thing is extreme ownership, extreme responsibility. And I understand why -- this is a heuristic which probably works for a victimhood culture where everybody wants to externalize their locus of control. Here's an example. **Jake**: So if anyone's listening to that thinking, "Right, I want to do it" -- would you draw the direct line between the benefits that ensued from that in terms of addressing that sense of connectivity?

**Chris**: So it wasn't just connectivity. The social skills and stuff is more about practice. What this is, is the forming of a foundation -- I think of you understanding yourself. And then therapy layered on top of that foundation has just been like a supercharged catalyst.

**Jake**: What's the biggest takeaway from the therapy?

**Chris**: I people-please. That's the big one -- that I take on other people's... it is my responsibility for somebody else's emotional state almost at all times. So if I do an episode with a guest -- you guys and me do a similar kind of show in that we're supposed to be a platform for great ideas, right? You want the guests to come on and just be at their absolute best. You want it to be a springboard for them. And in peak flow -- there are other shows out there that are more cantankerous, and that's not necessarily my style -- but if I drop a question to a guest or a friend and I see that it makes them uncomfortable, as you're watching them sat across from them, I want to throw them a life buoy and save them in some way or another.

The same thing goes for friends' emotional states. For me taking responsibility for the way that other people feel when I shouldn't. And it sounds noble -- "I care about someone else's feelings" -- but the place that it comes from isn't a choice. It's a compulsion. And this is a big difference. And this is something that people that are listening should be assessing in themselves: am I _choosing_ to do this because I want to make this other person feel better? Or am I _compelled_ to do this because if I don't, I feel worse? And that's, again, running away from something that you fear versus running towards something that you want.

### Instinct Over Information

**Jake**: I'm interested -- you spoke about connection, we've spoken about being able to embrace you as a geek and that curiosity of what you want to talk about. How did these initial conversations that you were having address the needs that you'd started to recognize were important?

**Chris**: I just followed my curiosity. There's a myth in content creation that you're supposed to niche down, dominate a niche, and then broaden out from there. Because if you scattergun yourself across too many audiences, you can't own any one audience. And I think that it's fundamentally flawed, because there is no one that is only interested in one thing. You love '80s jams and yoga and Renaissance philosophy and knitting, right? Like that's your thing.

And then you don't know what you like, either. So the way that I see the job of any content creator is like a museum curator. You walk in and you don't know what all of the exhibits are going to be. Some of them are brand new to you and you can't believe that you didn't know about it and it's so amazing. Some of them you already kind of knew or maybe had even seen before but were fans of. And other stuff you're like, "Hmm, you know, that's maybe not quite so much for me." But it's your job to curate that.

So all I did was follow my instincts. And the other advantage of following your instincts is that you're always going to have fun, because you're only ever talking about something that you are interested in. So it sounds -- people look back on any come-up story and look at the flatline before the rise and they say, "It's so impressive to stick at this thing where no one was listening." It's like, yeah, but that presumes that the reason I was doing it was because people were listening. It wasn't. It was because I wanted to understand myself and the world around me, and I wanted an excuse to speak to people.

I always had this resentment about university. I did two degrees -- a bachelor's and a master's, both in business. Neither of them I can remember at all. Can't remember anything from them. Maybe they're like in there somewhere. But I always resented not having the bravery to go and do philosophy or psychology, because I didn't know what a philosopher or a psychologist would do as a job. And then realized that basically what I did with the show was create my own perfect university syllabus -- only with the very professors that I wanted to, only talking about the specific microcosm of their work that I wanted to, on my schedule. And I didn't have to do work for it other than the stuff that I chose. So I basically recreated the education that I never had.

**Jake**: When did you prioritize instinct over data collection and following the crowd and doing what everyone else has done and leaning into all of the incredible amounts of information you can get from podcast providers and YouTube and everywhere else?

**Chris**: That's still an ongoing process, because the things that are measured get managed more easily. That's not you being flaky to your audience; that's you being true to yourself.

### The Benefits of Suffering

## Analysis & Key Insights

### Major Themes

1. **The Paradox of Achievement and Happiness**: Chris articulates a central tension in high performance -- that people sacrifice happiness to achieve success, when success was only ever supposed to be the vehicle for happiness. He frames this as a mathematical equation where if you cancel "success" from both sides, you are left with "happiness."

2. **Running Toward vs. Running Away**: Using the metaphor of starving rats pulling toward cheese (and pulling harder when a cat's scent comes from behind), Chris explores how fear-based motivation is potent but ultimately toxic over the long term. His entire career arc -- from club promoter to Love Island to podcaster -- was initially driven by running away from a fear of being unlovable.

3. **Identity and Validation**: A deeply personal thread runs through the entire conversation about Chris's childhood social isolation as an only child with introverted parents, and how that created a lifelong pattern of seeking external validation. His progression through nightlife, modeling, reality TV, and eventually podcasting all served as mechanisms for connection-seeking.

4. **Mastery and the Pursuit of "World Class"**: Chris discusses the evolution from grinding optimization (Pomodoro techniques, time-blocking) to a more playful, confident approach to work. He identifies the concept of "false peaks" -- you can only see that a peak was false once you're standing on top of it.
