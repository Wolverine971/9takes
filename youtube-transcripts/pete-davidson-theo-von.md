The loss didn't just create sadness -- it became the lens through which he understood himself, relationships, and the world. His comment that photos of him show "different eyes" before and after his father's death reveals how he sees the event as a total identity fracture.

2. **Fear of Abandonment & Self-Sabotage (BPD)**: Pete explicitly names his BPD diagnosis and traces its roots to his childhood loss. The pattern he describes -- pushing away people who love him, then panicking when they actually leave -- is a textbook abandonment cycle. His insight that "everybody left me so long ago in my mind" that anyone still present loses his respect reveals a deeply entrenched protective mechanism.

3. **Sobriety as Identity Reconstruction**: Pete's sobriety (over a year at the time of recording) is framed not as quitting substances but as learning to exist without external validation. His statement that he had to "want to be okay" independent of standup, movies, relationships, or even family represents a fundamental reorientation from external to internal self-worth.

4. **The Comfort of Suffering & Fear of Happiness**: Both Pete and Theo articulate a shared terror of happiness. Pete says he is "so afraid to be happy because it's uncomfortable." Theo describes his negative feelings as "best friends" he doesn't want to desert. This theme -- that familiar pain feels safer than unfamiliar joy -- runs through the entire conversation.

5. **Fatherhood as Redemption**: Pete frames impending fatherhood as both his greatest fear and greatest hope. It represents a chance to break the cycle -- to give his child the stable, present father he never had. His relief at being able to "be unselfish" and no longer be "the focal point of my life" suggests fatherhood offers an escape from the exhausting loop of self-focus that addiction and mental illness create.

6. **The Celebrity Trap & Tabloid Destruction**: Pete describes a specific and devastating cycle: public embrace followed by public vilification, with no therapist or even other celebrities (Sandler, Stiller) able to relate. His coping mechanism of replacing his name in headlines with "Hugh Jackman" to test whether anyone actually cares is a practical cognitive reframing technique born of desperation.

### Summary

This episode of This Past Weekend (#612) is one of the most emotionally raw and psychologically revealing conversations Pete Davidson has given publicly. Recorded in New York City in September 2025, with Pete now sober for over a year, expecting his first child, and preparing for his first international comedy tour, the conversation covers the full arc of his life -- from losing his fireman father Scott on 9/11 at age seven, through childhood suicide attempts and psychiatric hospitalization, to his rise on SNL, his public spiral through addiction and tabloid exposure, and his current emergence into something resembling stability.

The most striking psychological thread is Pete's articulation of the BPD cycle he's lived in for decades: a fear of abandonment so deep that he would preemptively destroy relationships, punish those who loved him most, and then experience genuine shock when people finally walked away. He describes this not abstractly but with specific emotional granularity -- the moment his mother called him in rehab with the fear she would see his death on the news, the realization that calling his comedy heroes (Sandler, Stiller) for advice about tabloid pain yielded nothing because they never experienced anything comparable, and the cognitive reframing technique of replacing his name in articles with "Hugh Jackman" to defuse their power.

What emerges is a portrait of a man in active reconstruction. Pete's sobriety, his attendance at multiple recovery groups (including SLAA), his therapy work around BPD and abandonment, and his relationship with his partner Elsie all represent deliberate moves toward a life not governed by external validation. His excitement about fatherhood is framed explicitly as relief from the exhausting self-focus of addiction and fame -- "I get to be unselfish." The conversation between Pete and Theo is notable for its mutual vulnerability; Theo shares his own parallel struggles with work addiction, fear of happiness, and difficulty with commitment, creating a dynamic where both men push each other toward honesty rather than performance. The movie Frequency -- about a man who speaks to his dead fireman father through a radio and changes the past -- serves as a perfect metaphor for Pete's deepest wish and the fantasy that has shaped his emotional life since childhood.
